Silkworms and the Work of Algiers

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/adew-hy48
← Back to Essays

Abstract

The author-practitioner’s detailed description of how to raise silkworms according to conventional early modern sericultural methods in “Silkworms” on fol. 53v is related by material and timing to a quite unusual entry on fol. 52r, “The work done in Algiers.” This entry involves raising a silkworm in a flask to produce a powder that transforms antimony into gold. “The work done in Algiers” also relates to another entry in BnF Ms. Fr. 640 for creating a strong poison by putrefying snails in a flask. Taken together, these recipes fall into a genre of alchemical recipes for producing strong agents by putrefying small but symbolic creatures in flasks. This annotation explores the movement of this complex of ideas and practices from China to North Africa to Toulouse. My reconstruction suggests that “Silkworms” and “The work done in Algiers” serve as examples of Ms. Fr. 640 as both a practical record and as encouraging its user to tread into realms of as yet untested knowledge.

Introduction

Folios 52r–v and 53v present an unusual set of entries in BnF Ms. Fr. 640, both containing processes involving silkworms. Fol. 53v contains a detailed and evocative description of raising silkworms, while fol. 52r has a recipe apparently connected to alchemical transmutation. Careful examination of these two entries reveals a series of surprises. First, the two entries are connected: “The work done in Algiers” on 52r and “Silkworms,” one folio away on 53v, both involve growing silkworms from eggs, and the dates mentioned in “Algiers” for starting up the process match up with the end date given in the “Silkworms” entry for producing eggs in order to start the cycle of growing the worms. Next, the level of detail that the author-practitioner provides for raising silkworms, as well as his references to an external source (a didactic poem published in 1527 by Marco Girolamo Vida, Bishop of Alba and Cremona),1 shows familiarity with early modern sericulture practices. Yet compared to contemporary works on sericulture (which are usually exhaustive treatises), the author-practitioner’s account of raising silkworms is quite brief. What is most unusual about this pair of entries, however, is the author-practitioner’s description about how to use silkworm eggs in creating a gold-producing powder, “as they do in Algiers” (fol. 52r).2 This is a marked departure from contemporaneous literature on silkworms.

This essay explores the relationship of the entries for “Silkworms” and “The work done in Algiers” to Easter and the summer solstice. I also reconstruct the author-practitioner’s instructions for growing silkworms in order to explore this connection. This essay also considers the possible alchemical nature of the author-practitioner’s interest in sealing small creatures in a vessel to produce a powerful transformative agent, as well as the relation of these entries to the movement of materials and practices across Eurasia.3

The Relationship Between “Silkworms” and “The work done in Algiers”

The “Algiers” recipe on fol. 52r and the “Silkworms” entry a page and a half later, on fol. 53r, are separated by a few short entries about colors, plants, and metals.4 The proximity of the two entries in the manuscript, the materials (silkworm eggs) involved in both entries, and the overlap of timing around the Easter holidays and summer solstice seem to indicate that the purpose of “Silkworms” is not to extract silk threads from cocoons (the manuscript contains no information on how to perform the process of unravelling cocoons to pull out the threads, known as “reeling”), but to prepare freshly laid silkworm eggs for the “work done in Algiers.” Close examination of the essential elements laid out in “The work done in Algiers” provides support for this connection:

  1. a young colt fed a special diet to produce manure to heat an enclosed vessel,5 which imitates the warmth of the weather of Algiers;

  2. chicken egg yolks that are poured into the vessel serve as a base and food source for the hatched silkworms;

  3. the silkworm eggs themselves, whose hatchlings are expected to consume each other until there is only one survivor;

  4. the surviving silkworm is fed fresh egg yolks mixed with gold leaf on an hourly basis for several months until it is burned in the vessel and its ash is used to transmute metals into fine gold.

Taken together, this information from “Algiers” establishes a timeline for the production of the surviving silkworm, which, once burned, will form an agent of metallic transmutation. The “Algiers” entry concludes, “The term of the work is nine months from the feast of St Jehan [June 24th, near the summer solstice] until the 25th of April.” This statement provides the link for understanding “Silkworms” as a preparatory entry for “Algiers,” as a corresponding reference in “Silkworms” states: “they [the hatched moths] have completed spinning & making their eggs in three weeks & around Saint Jehan’s Day. And at that time one keeps their eggs & seed until Holy Week” (the week immediately preceding Easter which is always the first Sunday after the first full moon occurring on or after the spring equinox). While the occurrence of Holy Week shifts each year, April 25th is the latest possible date upon which Easter can fall.6

Silkworms and Resurrection

The correspondence of the dates mentioned in the two entries betrays a deeper connection to a cycle of growth and rebirth both in nature and in the Christian liturgical year. John the Baptist’s Day, occurring close to the summer solstice—the longest day in the year—is followed by the significant period of nine months—the length of human gestation—to the feast of the Resurrection, which occurs near the spring equinox, the turning point of the natural year and time of rebirth of the natural world. This timing indicates additional dimensions to these two entries on silkworms.

For a start, the author-practitioner refers to resurrection when he writes about the silkworms on fol. 52r, “each time they remain 4 or five days resting without eating, as if they were dying so as to be born again.” This reference is also present in Bishop Vida’s poem on the nature of silkworms, which the author-practitioner cites in the margin on fol. 53v. The following excerpt from Vida’s The silkworm: a poem in two books sheds light on the author-practitioner’s interest in this work:

Imprison’d dark within the silky ball

Compleat their labours, and resign their breath

Exhausted, sinking in the shades of death.

Go wond’rous race, thrice happy artists go

Meet fate content, to you the sisters owe

A second birth; kind Venus shall requite

Your toils, and raise you to the realms of light…7

The “second birth” cited in the passage above is reminiscent of the author-practitioner’s words about the silkworms, “dying so as to be born again,” on fol. 53v. Both Vida and the author-practitioner share the metaphorical use of silkworms to invoke resurrection.

A later account by seventeenth-century sericulturist Christophe Isnard also connects silkworms with the process of resurrection of a phoenix in his poem:8

This celebrated artisan, of an immortal life,

Comparable to no other being in its fate

Leaves and takes up life again, as soon as it emerges,

And the end of its course, is followed by another.

If for this the Phoenix, is the envy of others,

This worm would have occasion to raise itself with greater strength,

For being beyond the reach of death

Its form is ravished [i.e., carried away] from its body four times.9

Silkworms were, then, not just a prized commodity for the material they produced. They could also act as a metaphor for regeneration and resurrection. A miracle involving silkworms was even recorded in Spain during the reign of Alfonso X (1252–1284) in which the Virgin Mary compelled silkworms to spin a complete wimple in less than an hour.10 As the embodiment of regeneration and rebirth, this lowly worm could simultaneously bring temporal commercial wealth and signify eternal resurrection and immortality.

Silkworms and Regeneration

The “Algiers” recipe involves hatching silkworm eggs in an enclosed flask where they are to feed on egg yolks and each other. When the sole surviving silkworm grows to resemble a serpent, it is burned and produces a toxic vapor—its ash is to be used to transmute metals into gold. I provide a sketched description of this process: Fig. 1, Fig. 2, Fig. 3, Fig. 4, Fig. 5, Fig. 6.

Ms. Fr. 640 contains another recipe that also produces a potent agent, a “horrible poison,” by closing lowly creatures—in this case, snails—within a flask. This recipe, which spans folios 55r–v, calls for snails and results in a poison that will kill a person when “spread on a board over which the person walks, or on stirrups.” Although snails rather than silkworms are involved in this particular recipe, it contains several parallels to “Algiers:” the snails are sealed in a glass bottle, fed boiled egg yolks, and kept in the bottle buried in horse manure until the substance turns into a powerfully toxic substance. Like “Algiers,” the preparation of this poison also happens around midsummer, “in the month of June and July.”11

The recipes on fols. 52r and 55r–55v appear to be related to those in a twelfth-century metalworking account by the pseudonymous Theophilus in which basilisk powder is the key ingredient for making “Spanish Gold.”12 Here, basilisks are raised in sealed brass vessels. They develop from chicks hatched from eggs laid by cocks, growing serpent tails in the process, before being finally burned into ash from which red gold can be produced.13 Similarly, as Pamela Smith has shown, there are related accounts in another metalworking text, the Rechter Gebrauch der Alchimei (1531), which outlines a process of sealing lizards in a vessel to create gold:

In a pot wide at the bottom and narrow at the top, with a cover that has air holes in it, place nine lizards in the milk, put the cover on, and bury in damp earth. Make sure the lizards have air so that they do not die. Let it stand… The lizards will have eaten the brass from hunger, and their strong poison will have compelled the brass to “transform itself to gold.”14

There are many similarities between the recipes in Ms. Fr. 640 for the work of Algiers, the “horrible poison,” and the processes of creating gold and gold-producing agents in the texts of Theophilus and the Rechter Gebrauch: enclosing low creatures in a flask, feeding them agricultural foodstuffs that are normally inedible for them (eggs for silkworms and snails; milk for lizards), and transforming the animals and substances into a highly valuable or inordinately poisonous substance through a process of putrefaction and regeneration. On fol. 98r, the author-practitioner also notes a relationship between metals, lizards, and gold: “Leadsmiths say that making a lizard die in the melted tin makes the tin become very golden,” adding more prosaically, “Or else by adding sal ammoniac.”

These recipes for the production of agents of transformation from various types of low creatures seems to be connected with their physical properties: snakes and lizards shed their skins, lizards can regenerate their tails when detached, lizards, snakes and frogs emerge from the ground fully grown after freezing winters, and insects, salamanders, frogs, toads and other such creatures were regarded as generating spontaneously from putrefying materials.15 These associations with generation and regeneration make sense of practices such as dried frogs being employed as birth amulets.16 We have already seen that the author-practitioner and his contemporaries made the silkworm a metaphor for spiritual resurrection, but none of the earlier metalworking texts make use of the silkworm as a powerful alchemical agent that can be used to produce gold.

Silkworms and Gu

Instead, the author-practitioner’s use of silkworms has an intriguing correspondence to a body of knowledge from China that dates back to 1400 BCE: the concept of Gu (written Ku in the Wade-Giles system).17 Gu refers to procedures associated with certain—usually minority—cultures in the southwestern regions of China. These procedures had the aim of producing wealth or poison, and worked by sealing creatures such as poisonous snakes and insects in a vessel, “until there is but one survivor, which is called the ku.”18

The traditional Chinese character for Gu is succinctly captured in the strokes of its written form. Gu, in fact, is formed from two radicals, Chong (蟲) and Min (皿), to form Gu (蠱). Chong is a term that refers to small, residual creatures somewhere between worms and insects,19 and Min literally means vessel or receptacle. The Gu character is formed by the character for Chong in its upper register while Min constitutes the lower register. Thus, the character itself embodies the placing of worm-bugs in a vessel for the process of Gu.20

In the twelfth century, Gu began to be associated with another word: Jin can (金蚕), which translates to Golden (金) Silkworm (蚕). The concept of the Golden Silkworm predates its associations with Gu as “the silkworm that generates silk thread, literally spitting out money.” Jincan 金蠶 from Xu Xuan’s 徐鉉 (916–991) Jishen lu 稽神錄 (“Record of the investigation of spirits”) tells of a man finding (and discarding) a gold-colored silkworm larva inside of a perfectly round stone.21 Twelfth-century Chinese scholars identified several morphological varieties of Gu based on the surviving creature from the vessel, of which the Golden Silkworm (jincan) was the most powerful.22 The twelfth-century author Cai Tao, a scholar and official in the Northern Song Dynasty, was the first to record the practice, noting that the Golden Silkworm originated from Sichuan and spread to provinces including Hunan, Hubei, Fujian and Canton.23 From that point on, a variety of texts began to identify and classify the Jincan, including medical texts that provided antidotes for its harmful properties. One text from the Southern Song Dynasty, most likely from the first half of the thirteenth century, contains a concise and informative account of the practice:

Southern people rear golden silkworms. The silkworm has a golden color and feeds on silk textiles from Sichuan. Its excrement is used to poison people by adding it into food. Whoever eats this will die. The silkworm can bring fortune to its owner, and make him rich. It cannot be killed by water and blade. [If one wants to get rid of it, one must] place the silkworm into a gold or silver vessel and leave it on the road. Whoever picks it up will become its new owner. This is called ‘marrying out the golden silkworm.’24

Various sources discuss the duality inherent in the Gu practice: it is capable of bringing both fortune and failure, of being both a poison and benefit, similar to the processes in European texts.

Another similarity of the “Algiers” process and Gu practice can be found in the timing of the processes. Recall that in “Algiers,” the silkworm eggs are to be sprinkled on top of chicken egg yolks in a flask and buried under manure around the Feast of Saint John, the date associated with the summer solstice in the Gregorian Calendar. The traditional Chinese Gu preparations occurred on the “fifth day of the fifth month,”25 which is the Duanwu festival, the date associated with the summer solstice in the Lunar Calendar.26

The Movement of Knowledge

In the essay “Knowledge in Motion,” Pamela Smith explores the movements of materials, processes and texts across Eurasia, focusing on the complex of ideas and practices around vermilion making and alchemical transmutation, including the uses of lizards in processes that aim to produce a transformative agent. She follows this complex from China through the Islamicate world to Europe.27 The intriguing recipe, “The work done in Algiers,” seems to indicate yet another trace of this complex and the movement of practices and ideas from China, through the Islamicate world of North Africa and Spain, to Toulouse.28

Reconstruction

This essay began with my proposition that the “Silkworms” entry on fol. 53v should be seen as a preparatory step to provide the silkworm eggs necessary for “The work done in Algiers” on fol. 52r, the entry that aims to create a giant, gold-dust-producing silkworm. While I contend that the author-practitioner most likely did not undertake the “Algiers” recipe, I sought to reconstruct both of the recipes in order to see what insights I might gain from cultivating the silkworms and going through the initial steps of “Algiers.”

At the time of this writing (May 2017), “Algiers” has yielded only a moldy flask (Fig. 7). For the “Algiers” reconstruction, I used both ready-to-hatch eggs (just like those used in the “Silkworms” reconstruction below) and freshly laid silkworm eggs between 2-3 days old. In order to fulfill the conditions of “Algiers,” I used a cooler padded with foam insulation to imitate the ground in which the reaction flask would be buried. Instead of manure, I used two ice/heat gel packs.29 During the work, I reheated the packs twice a day, every 12 hours. I did not close the cooler lid all the way in order to leave allow air to circulate in. I loosely stopped up the flask with a paper towel as the embodiment of the “I do not know if it is at all necessary for the generation that there be air” that the author-practitioner expresses on fol. 52v (Fig. 8 and Fig. 9). After approximately eight days, when the eggs should have been hatching, the entirety of the flask was taken over by a violent green mold. It covered large patches of the thickened egg yolk and covered the places where the silkworm eggs lay half-submerged (Fig. 7). Luckily, I was able to observe this since I was using a glass flask, but if one were following the author-practitioner’s steps precisely and simply changing out the manure around a clay vessel, it might be months before one realized that nothing but mold and decay were happening inside the vessel, and that no gold powder-producing silkworm was forthcoming, although the author-practitioner might have regarded such putrefaction as simply a first step toward regeneration.30

The silkworms under my care, in the habitat I constructed for them according to “Silkworms,” are approximately one week away from the point at which they should begin spinning cocoons in order to transform into moths. On the mulberry leaf diet, they are growing rapidly each day—the largest worms now have eight distinctly visible legs (legs now surrounded by fine, short bristly hairs) and are approximately the length of three-quarters of my ring finger; it will not be long until we reach the author-practitioner’s assurance that, “when it begins to spin, it is as long as a ring finger and has eight legs” (fol. 53v). Obviously my experiment will continue beyond the submission of this essay (perhaps there will even be a sequel to this work at a later date).

Although it cannot be determined definitively, it seems quite possible that the author-practitioner cultivated silkworms or at least observed the process firsthand. Following his instructions, I began the reconstruction by locating a room such that, “if during the day there is any storm or rainy weather, cloudy and cool, one needs to keep in the room three or 4 embers and with glowing coal” (fol. 53v). This took the form of the furnace room in my home in Brooklyn. On a shelf above the boiler that maintains a consistent temperature—stormy weather or fine—of 75–82 degrees Fahrenheit, which is the approximate temperature recommended for hatching silkworm eggs,31 I built a habitat for the silkworms in the form of a box of pine wood as instructed by the author-practitioner. (Fig. 10, Fig. 11)

Unlike the author-practitioner, I sourced the silkworm eggs from LadySilkworm.com, a company run by Nancy Ho, a silkworm enthusiast in the NYC area. I purchased 100 white silkworm eggs (Fig. 12), 100 hybrid South African silkworm eggs, and two tubes of Mulberry Chow,32 an advancement in sericulture (Fig. 13). Alternatives for mulberry leaves have been sought by all those hoping to establish a silkworm industry in an inhospitable environment. For example, Tsar Alexis of Russia (1629–1676) wrote a command, never fulfilled, “to find such a master, however much it might cost, who might be able to… feed the worms with such food that would be comparable to mulberry, perhaps by extracting oil from the mulberry tree, or oil from the leaves, or grass soaked in such oil, to feed the worms, so with God’s help we can produce silk in Moscow.”33 Although the worms happily fed on the Mulberry Chow when it was provided, my field notes record the first time one of my silkworms tasted real mulberry leaves: “One little rascal, after taking his (or her!) first tentative bites from the leaf material rolled away as if in an orgasmic spasm and rocked on his (or her!) back several times like a dog hoping for a tummy rub; then, he (or she) got back to the business of chewing leaves.”34

My field notes document in detail the various stages of silkworm development I have observed. What was most notable about the experience was understanding just how labor intensive and delicate the process of raising silkworms was. For example, Ester Geraci of the Como Silk Museum writes, “since silkworms require a constant, mild temperature, entire sections of farmhouses were turned over to them and whole families would often pitch in, stoking round-the-clock fires to maintain the proper warmth.”35 And sixteenth-century writer Leonardo Fioravanti noted that “no one is to be found who is capable of doing on his own the many tasks that it (sericulture) involves.”36 I too found that I had to rely on my family to cover the demanding feeding schedule when I was running late. Because of this investment of effort and time, I experienced multiple moments of acute fear that I had completely failed. First, I feared that the worms would never hatch. And, when they did, I feared that they had died within the first week. Here, the author-practitioner’s advice that, “they remain 4 or five days resting without eating, as if they were dying so as to be born again” (fol. 53v) came as a great source of comfort and relief. The worms did indeed become more active after their death-like states, and my worries came to naught.

Many of the milestones in the process of silkworm rearing occurred according to the author-practitioner’s description. The rest periods and the color changes coincided almost exactly with the 4-5 day intervals described on fol. 53v. However, some points were not fully discussed in the manuscript. For example, the instructions in “Silkworms” state that, “three times per day, it is necessary to change the leaves for fresh ones” (fol. 53v). Yet, this process was much more difficult than it seemed. Even though I had to remove thin noodles of dried Mulberry Chow instead of leaves, it was difficult to get all the silkworms off. They not only tenaciously stuck to the food, but even after I plucked them off, they remained attached to the hardened, dried old food by invisible silky strands (Fig. 14). I nearly lost several worms in this manner, almost accidentally throwing them away. If my consternations seems absurd, consider how frightening it was to pluck the worms off of their food after reading the following seventeenth-century account by natural philosopher and sericulturist Olivier de Serres (1539–1619), which warns against breathing on the worms, or even looking at them the wrong way:

The governour shall not forget to drinke a little wine earlie in the morning before he goes to worke, for that in communicating the smell of such licour to the Wormes, it preserues them from all stench, specially from the naughtie breath of folkes (more strong being fasting, then after eating) which these admirable beasts feare much…[T]he supersticious vulgar, sottishly attribute to the eye, beleeving that there are people with by their lookes brings ill lucke to the Wormes; but it rather is, nay, assuredly, the breathing of the ill breath which causeth them, indispositions.37

The author-practitioner also does not provide instructions for how to properly arrange “the dry heather branches that we prepare and attach” (fol. 53v), from which the silkworms spin their cocoons. The use of texts contemporary to the Ms. Fr. 640 (particularly the illustrations from the work of Olivier de Serres cited above) was instrumental to understanding how to arrange twigs and branches into a facsimile of a small forest within the incubation trays (Fig. 15).

Conclusion

Overall, the entire process of obtaining the eggs (finding a dealer, negotiating price, gauging quality), constructing the right environment, and caring for the silkworms on a daily basis has instilled in me both sentimental and grandiose ideas. In building a little world, a reproduction of nature (Fig. 16), I found myself feeling like a god to these creatures under my care, as is obvious from my early field notes: “every now and then they lift up half of their bodies like birds with hungry mouths or like some sort of acolytes performing a salutation to the sun, which in their case is a naked bulb in the furnace room (Fig. 17).”38

I look forward to experiencing the curiosities of the further development of my silky friends. I look forward to seeing whether it is true, as the author-practitioner notes, that “two or three and up to eleven worms put themselves in a cocoon” (fol. 54r). I have certainly seen some worms being quite friendly and intertwining with each other, and it is possible that they will continue their affair in the death and rebirth of metamorphosis.

Coda

The silkworms began spinning cocoons within days of each other.39 I checked to see whether “when it begins to spin, it is as long as a ring finger and has eight legs” (fol. 53v), and found that the silkworms were indeed quite large, approximately 3/4 the length of my ring finger and a little thinner around. Silkworms actually have six legs (three pairs) and five pairs of “pseudopods,” which may account for the author-practitioner putting the number at eight. To spin a cocoon, a silkworm situated itself within a few small sticks close together, then attached a single silk thread to one such branch and began pulling the string out of itself, much like a magician pulls handkerchiefs tied together out of a pocket. First, each silkworm used its single thread to form a wide hammock, an area of approximately 8-10 centimeters in circumference, in which it could be suspended. Several hours later, the silkworm reclined and continued pulling the unbroken silk thread, attaching it to anchor points throughout this ample hammock, still visible within the first struts of what would be the cocoon. Even after the metamorphosis chamber seemed to be fully formed, the silkworm evidently continued its labors as the cocoon shifted and moved for two or three hours more. Then it grew still.

Approximately a week after the first spinning started, they were all encased in their own threads. I did not observe “two or three and up to eleven worms put themselves in a cocoon” (fol. 54r), but it is not unimaginable that multiple silkworms might get trapped in each others’ “hammocks” and form such a multiple cocoon. About half the cocoons were yellow as sulfur, the others white as cotton. The first moth hatched on the tenth day after cocooning, and the others hatched in the following five days. To hatch, the moths opened a small hole at the tip of the cocoon and emerged—they do not have mouths, but they probed and pushed their way out. The silkworms hatched quite beautifully and enjoyed approximately two weeks of wonderful, hedonistic life—during this period they emerged from their cocoons, flapped their wings, enjoyed the warm summer air in my backyard, mated, and laid eggs (Fig. 18). They do not have the ability to eat as moths, so mating was their primary activity, which they did with gusto.

I released most of the moths into the wild and kept ten for observation. Three pairs seemed to mate by attaching their tails together. Two of the females laid eggs in the box in which they had been larva. One of the pairs could not detach after mating and after attempting to move around—wildly buzzing their wings—for two days, both finally died, still attached, having laid no eggs. The remaining four silkmoths did not mate.

I attempted to preserve the eggs in my garage, but when I checked on them in mid-February, far too early for them to have hatched, I found that the eggs had indeed hatched, perhaps due to an early warm spell, and the dry larva had perished from lack of food.
