Daily Life and Material Culture in Early Modern Europe

Abstract

This essay explores what we can learn about daily life in early modern France through a close reading of BnF Ms. Fr. 640. Reading across instructions, observations, and marginal illustrations, it reveals everyday practices, local proverbs, beliefs, and material culture, contributing to our understanding of the cultural, physical, conceptual, and material environment of early modern Europe.

A form of regimen

Every morning, take two or three eggs laid one or two days ago. Heat them until & do not cook them. Take the centers well dusted with sugar. And next drink one finger of wine. And apart from being nourishing, it makes a good stomach. (fol. 20v)1

 

What was the daily life of the author-practitioner of BnF Ms. Fr. 640? What objects did he interact with as he went about his work and life somewhere in or near Toulouse in the 1580s? Did he follow his own advice for a daily regime and wake up each morning, take eggs that he had collected in advance and crack them open, dust sugar over the yolks, and sip a small amount of wine to settle his stomach? And what does his inclusion of such information in the manuscript tell us about his interests and preoccupations? Information about the rhythms and practices of everyday life and material culture in early modern Europe is spread thinly across a wide range of archival, printed, visual, and material sources. Ms. Fr. 640 is not primarily about daily life or everyday material culture in early modern Europe. Here, as with most manuscript sources from the sixteenth century, mentions of daily practices and material culture are found as asides, they are a thin dust of sugar on the yolk of information about craft practice. But by closely reading the manuscript for what it tells us about material and quotidian life in early modern Toulouse, we can learn much about the rhythms of social, religious, and economic life for artisans and the material surroundings of people across Europe in the late sixteenth century. Such insights contribute to our understanding of the cultural, physical, conceptual, and material environments of early modern Europe, and they cast more light on the still shadowy figure of the author-practitioner.

Writing

An unattributed pen-and-ink sketch of a bearded man, now stored in the collections of The Metropolitan Museum of Art, offers an image of the educated male writer in late sixteenth-century France (Fig. 1). He is hunched forwards to write at a desk in an elegant room, shutters flung open to let in the light. On the table, a fashionable beaver hat has been placed to his left, marking him out as a stylish dresser, confirmed by his neat ruff and pinked hose. At the table, a leather pen case is open, and its writing utensils have spilled out and lie next to a sand blotter, several bound books, and loose leaves. The room is rather sparsely furnished, the ceiling has bare wooden boards and a beam, but the chair is patterned perhaps in tooled leather, and the wall to the right is decorated perhaps with a tapestry or stucco, wood carving, painted decoration, or fresco. While we do not know the economic or social status of the author-practitioner and so cannot conjure up a clear image of his milieu, as so many of the materials depicted in this sketch are also mentioned throughout the manuscript, this image offers a visual account of many of the objects created and used by the author-practitioner and gives us an impression of what a writer might look like in late sixteenth-century France.

We get limited insight into the author-practitioner’s life as a writer, as there is little self-reflexivity about the practice of writing in Ms. Fr. 640. What tools he used, and where and at what time of day he wrote, are still unknown to us. It seems as if the text was not compiled in one sitting but over a period of time, so for at least part of his life—days, months, or even years—the author-practitioner made writing part of his routine.2 Perhaps he took notes on a tablet, as he describes in one entry how it is possible to write with silverpoint on a tablet and erase your writing by rubbing it with a cuttlefish bone. While this would have been a practical tool, he also writes down that tablets can be decorated with a range of materials, “Some are made from boxwood inlaid with ebony, sandalwood, ivory, or gold and silver like damascening” (fol. 15v).3 He mentions preparing quills to make paintbrushes, and it seems likely that he gathered and shaped his own quills for writing (fols. 66v–67r). He does not include a recipe for a standard iron gall ink, which is likely what he used to write the manuscript, suggesting that the recipe was so commonplace that it was unremarkable to him, but he does offer recipes for unusual inks, such as one for printers (fol. 51r).4 In an entry entitled “Scribe,” he describes how a writer can quickly clean dried ink from his quill using oil of sulfur, sulfur being a material that appears to have been significant to him not only for its many uses across the manuscript’s entries but perhaps also as a material metaphor for experimentation itself (fol. 46v).5 On the same page, he suggests using heated oil of sulfur for “writing without ink,” following this instruction with a statement and mark of proof: “& the letter will turn as black as this one,” next to which he left an ink stroke on the page. As this mark bleeds through the paper, while the rest of the ink does not, I suggest that he has tested this recipe on this page of the manuscript (Fig. 2). Here, the text itself calls attention to the act of writing and making marks on paper as one of material as well as conceptual investigation. As we do not know the purpose of the manuscript—perhaps it was for publication, to give to a patron, or an aide-memoire of working practices—there is no reason why the act of writing this collection of observations and recipes cannot also be considered an experimental act in itself. It may be that the author-practitioner did not have a clear outcome for his text in mind, and we should regard his act of writing the manuscript as a form of practical experimentation as well as a means of recording information.

Daily Interactions in the Workshop and the Home: Lighting, Food and Drink, Weather, Women

Despite a concerted effort, still very little about the identity of the author-practitioner has been discovered through the Making and Knowing Project. Obviously experienced in metalworking and mold making, knowledgeable about artillery and weaponry, and versed in a diverse range of craft practices, he was clearly an experienced craftsman with hands-on knowledge of at least some of the many processes about which he wrote. Writing in the first person about the process of whitening cornaline (which he spells backwards as it is a “secret”), he mentions that he experimented in “my goldsmith’s forge” (fol. 12v). However, we cannot say for sure if he was a member of a guild, which would have provided him with a defined professional identity and enabled us to speculate about his craft training and economic status. His familiarity with many German techniques and descriptions of their visual effect suggests that he might have traveled there, perhaps as a journeyman.6 He twice recommends having “your boy” help with managing the fire in the workshop (“have the fire managed by a shop boy;” “make your boy blow on it”), suggesting that he worked with apprentices or day laborers in a workshop setting (fols. 123v and 135v). He also mentions “the one who taught me to mold” (fol. 110r), but does not refer to him as a master, which might have confirmed an official apprenticeship. What the precise meaning of two entries, entitled “For the workshop” (“Pour la boutique,” fols. 162r and 166r), might be is still under debate. Perhaps La boutique was the intended title of the finished manuscript, and these two entries with their references to classical texts and proverbs were intended to form the prefatory material. Whether or not this is the reason for these two headings, clearly the workshop was of conceptual importance, not just a physical space, connected in his mind with discovery and invention.

Nevertheless, the workshop was also a real place, populated with tools, materials, and people. We get glimpses of the material realities of this space through some of the assumptions the author-practitioner makes.7 For example, under the heading “Painter,” the reader is instructed to “clean your workshop well” before grinding colors, “for when walking, if you stir up dust, this will damage your colors” (fol. 56v). Lighting the workshop was also an issue, and candles are clearly of concern to the author-practitioner. He seems to have experience in making them, and of trying to improve their quality. On fol. 16v, he suggests:

They keep well in bran which makes them white & long-lasting, so say some. I believe, however, that it is enough to keep them in a cool & dry place, in such manner that they are not pressed together. It is better if there is a hemp thread throughout the wick, which gives it more light & makes the wick hold up straighter. Otherwise, if it is all of cotton, as those of Montauban make, it is necessary to snuff it out often, for after it has burnt il a little, the cotton droops & makes the candle drip.

A dripping candle was of such concern—probably because it could damage the careful work of the craftsperson and also wasted precious wax—that the author-practitioner even provides advice “For keeping candles from dripping and making them white,” suggesting to dip them into water beaten with bran (fol. 37r). Candles were not only used for light, but could be useful tools at hand in the workshop, used to burn spiders’ “hairy feet” (fol. 129v), attract passerines (fol. 160v), or to smoke a mold in preparation for casting (fols. 82v, 158v). They could also be luxury goods, adding to the sensory ambience of a space, as we are reminded in an entry about a “Scented candle from Le Mans” (fol. 166v). Candles are also used at home, to “read and write,” even at some distance, with the use of a concave mirror (fol. 5v). 

While this text is clearly not intended to help with household management, the space and materials of the home and the workshop often intersect, showing how domestic and working life overlapped and posing the question as to whether the author-practitioner lived and worked in the same space.8 He catches passerine birds by luring them into his house (fol. 160v). He suggests that to dry preserves, one should use a chafing dish and a “bedsheet folded double” (fol. 54v). The skillets “where one makes the mush for little children” are useful for casting (fol. 136v). He even suggests using house tiles to mix with sand for molding (fol. 106v). While some processes required highly specialized and sizeable equipment, many could be easily achieved in a domestic kitchen or living space with tools at hand. When he advises that chestnuts should be kept in a “humid place,” he suggests the roof gutter (fol. 166v), and he claims that oranges can be kept in a closed “tin vessel” for up to six months (fol. 16v). Fir boxes, “like those where one puts sweetmeats,” are suitable for keeping silkworms (fol. 53v). We get a hint of the implications of making at home when he mentions that mortars of fine copper are good “for a private home” because “they do not ring so much & do not carry as much noise” as the ones for the apothecaries (fol. 85v). While professionals like apothecaries could use the sound made by their tools as a form of auditory promotion for their trade, men and women working at home perhaps tried not to disturb their neighbors. 

While the manuscript only contains a small number of recipes for food (see, for example “Making millas” (fol. 20r) or “Excellent mustard” (fol. 48r)), workshop practice intersects with food and drink on several occasions. The author-practitioner describes how to taxidermy animals by drying them in the oven, “after the bread has been taken out,” suggesting that food preparation took place alongside craft practices (fol. 129v). He also uses bread as a molding material (fols. 140v and 156r) and as an ingredient in making black varnish (fol. 4v).9 Food might also mitigate the inherent dangers of working with toxic material. To avoid suffering from inhaling toxic fumes when making silver run, the author-practitioner advises to “take in the morning good buttered toast, and hold the said butter, or zedoary, or gold coins, in your mouth, and cover your face with a cloth from the eyes down” (fol. 123v). He describes a mixture made from vinegar mixed with rue and juniper berries to extinguish burning bricks which can also be used to cleanse “corrupt air” or to “remove infection” from a garment, room, or house (fol. 170v).

The local environment could be a generative source of materials, and the author-practitioner advises that artisans can increase their profit by “seeking things already prepared in nature” (fol. 90r). But while natural materials and the heat of the sun were helpful for making objects, the weather could pose a great challenge for craftspeople. He warns that painted panels repaired with glue can rot in damp weather, and wax will melt in the sun (fol. 59v). When reheating molds in a forge, you must “[m]ake sure the wind from a window does not beat upon it” (fol. 132). We hear how windows can be made opaque if shade is required by rubbing them with calcined saltpeter (fol. 100r). Rainy or damp conditions in particular posed problems. Crucibles made in winter or humid weather are deemed “not good” (fol. 47v), painters are warned to avoid working when it is “overcast” because it makes flesh colors too brown (fol. 31v), and gold color can turn black in “damp & humid” conditions (fol. 100r). It was imperative that the craftsperson respond to the specific opportunities and challenges of their environment. When discussing “The work done in Algiers,” the author-practitioner explains that you should cut and feed barley and straw to the horse “in the manner one feeds horses in Spain.” However, he adds a suggestion that perhaps “it would be good” to also give the horse sulfurous baths and feed it fenugreek or “other hot foods” because the “intention of the worker” is to use the heat of a horse’s dung which might compensate for the fact that “the climate here is cooler than that of Algiers” (fol. 52r). Such a claim demonstrates how in order to successfully employ craft techniques from other locales, the skilled craftsman must be familiar with both his own surroundings and those from which the technique originated as well as understand not just what to do but what the intention of each step is, in order to adapt it to fit his local conditions. The large numbers of entries concerned with seasonality and animal husbandry are understandable, given that the success or failure of craft practices relied on a practical knowledge of how to exploit and mitigate the challenges of the local environment.

Women are largely absent from the world conjured up by Ms. Fr. 640. The author-practitioner offers advice about the best pigments to use when painting the shadows of women (fols. 65r–66r) and mentions that concave mirrors can be used by women to “see the secret places that they do not want to show surgeons” (fol. 5v). He also tells us that “ladies, wanting to color their cheeks, grind Florence lake very finely, then fill a little cotton with it, which they next wrap in a little fabric of Cambray which is clear. And thus they pounce the lake on their cheeks & then, with another clean cotton, they soften it.” (fol. 58r). We get glimpses of their working lives when he mentions that starch water is made by women (fol. 113v), and he notes that women in Flanders do gilding work, suggesting that this was not common practice in Toulouse (fol. 66r). Women’s milk is also said to be better for making crayons (fol. 57v). We are also told, without further explanation, that if a sheep shearer should want to castrate his sheep, “he must not have been with his wife for this would make the sheep die” (fol. 41v).

Proverbs and Sayings

Such a claim suggests a certain kind of proverbial wisdom, even if the reasoning is not explained. Natalie Zemon-Davis has written about the importance of local proverbs in recording the culture and mentalities of early modern France.10 As Piero Camporesi explains, “the proverb consolidates the unattributed wisdom of a group,”11 but the collecting of proverbs from peasants was also a most appropriate activity amongst learned men in early modern Europe, and it may be that the author-practitioner collected proverbs both for their practical information and as a means to record local sayings and interesting information for an elite reader.12 For example, he records under the title “Observation from rustics,” that “when it freezes on Palm Sunday, it freezes all the months of the year” and “when the first flowers fall & are lost from the frost, the others are usually lost also” (fol. 93r). Such advice both informs an elite reader about the wisdom of a “rustic,” offering a form of armchair travel or access to an otherwise unfamiliar social stratum, but it can also potentially offer useful information about predicting the weather and harvests ahead. Similarly useful cultivation wisdom is offered for planting trees: “One says that rarely a tree planted on Saint Paul’s day fails to take” (fol. 38v). On the other hand, the author-practitioner dismisses the beliefs of founders who think only “three days a week are good for melting, namely Tuesdays, Wednesdays Thursdays, and Saturdays. The others, for them, are unlucky,” as no more than “superstition” (fol. 87r). Proverbs are also used as evidence supporting his claims, such as for taking care of fluxes in the ear, when he reminds the reader “to be very careful not to put anything inside,” specifically referencing “the proverb, one ought only to touch the ear & the eye with the elbow” (fol. 15v).

Other statements record information about practices from further afield. The author-practitioner writes that there are no spiders in Ireland, as the local wood makes them die. Due to this, he suggests, rich people in England make their ceilings of Irish wood to avoid cobwebs (fol. 71v). This information could be of use to an enterprising craftsperson with a clear interest both in making things from wood and also catching spiders for casting, who might seek to investigate what kinds of wood repel spiders. While it is not clear how the author-practitioner came upon this information, this belief was still circulating in 1646 and widespread enough for Thomas Browne to challenge it in his “Enquiries into Vulgar and Common Errors:” “most men affirme, and few here will believe the contrary, that there be no Spiders in Ireland but wee have beheld some in that Country, and though but few, some cobwebs we behold in Irish wood in England.”13 On the same page as his claim about Irish wood, the author-practitioner also notes that the Irish do not drink wine but convert it into eau-de-vie, “which they use almost as habitually as we use wine” (fol. 71v). Such a statement gives us insight both into the local practices of wine drinking and an acknowledgement of cultural difference.

Certain proverbs and advice give us more specific insights into Toulousain beliefs and practices. For example, he mentions that “candlemakers never make a good candle when the Autan wind blows,” referencing the strong south-westerly wind still said to make people go mad.14 He also sees fit to record and challenge or amend local claims. Under the title “Common saying,” he writes, “bell tower of Rodez, church of Albi, bells of Mende. However, one holds the [bell] of Toulouse named Cardaillac as one of the most beautiful in France, all the more since it weighs two hundred and fifty quintals” (fol. 20v). This list of local architectural masterpieces presumably marks them out because of their exceptional sizes and manufacture. At 87 meters, the pink sandstone bell tower at Rodez, added in the early sixteenth century, is still the highest in France. The church of Albi he refers to is presumably the Cathedral of Sainte Cécile, built between 1282 and 1480, supposedly the largest brick building in the world. The bells of Mende might refer to the “Non Pareille” bell, added to the cathedral in 1517, which was the largest bell in the world, and so loud it could be heard from around 16 kilometers away before it was destroyed in 1579 by the Huguenots. To this list, the author-practitioner writes that he wants to add the great Cardaillac bell in the Cathedral of Saint-Étienne in Toulouse, citing its weight as well as its beauty. As someone interested in casting, founders who make bells (fols. 15r, 32v, 77r, 85v, 99v, 142v), and the sound of bells (suggesting to cast with more copper “to give it a bigger voice” or tin for a “clearer sound” [fol. 24r]), it is not surprising that the author-practitioner is impressed by the weight of the bell in his local cathedral.

Religion

Bells provided an aural identity for Catholics, audibly setting them apart from Protestants who tended to worship with song, and so the author-practitioner’s fond mention of bells as beautiful, loud, and clear suggests that he was a Catholic.15 However, apart from the extensive discussions about weaponry and warfare tactics, we get no hint that Toulouse was in the midst of often bitter religious fighting and wars between Catholics and Protestants from the 1560s onwards.16 Nevertheless, religious comments pepper the manuscript. God is only directly invoked once, in a recipe for making water to heal eye disease, which is said to work “with the help of God” (fol. 79r). Another medical recipe also suggests that spiritual efficacy is required to heal burns. The author-practitioner suggests that you should stir wax and linseed oil together over the fire “for as long as it takes you to say one 9 paternoster, and as you say them, wash this composition with holy water, stirring all the while.” Then, the process is repeated with 8, 7, 6 paternosters (and so on), until the mixture turns white, which will prove to be an “excellent” remedy (fol. 103r). As Xiaomeng Liu has argued, this process employs a spiritual power in its material transformation.17

In a unique example, the author-practitioner explicitly makes clear his specific understanding of religious teachings, which informs his investigations into snakes for molding. In describing their movements, he notes, “I have noticed that when they want to bite or eat something, they do not bite with a direct attack, but with sinuous turns & from the side, as do Satan & his disciples” (fol. 109r–109v). He also explains snake physiognomy (that they have a “small head, but a very long body”) as a manifestation of the dangers of evil: “the entryway to sin seems small & inconsequential, but the consequences of it are very great” (fol. 109v). These brief statements give a rare insight into the ways that an early modern experimenter might combine his investigations of the natural world with his religious beliefs.

The author practitioner also made religious objects. He explains that he has “experimented with sand from Thoulouse” and “with wine boiled with elm root, and molded with it a large piece of a portrait of Jesus” (fol. 85v). He also tells us how to mount Agnus Dei—wax discs imprinted with the image of a lamb and blessed by the Pope (Fig. 3)—on small boxes (fol. 15v). We also learn about the environment of the church, hearing how older glass windows in churches were made thicker than “those of today” and in “more lively colors” (fol. 38r) and that “small works” of cast lead “are sold in front of churches,” likely referring to small pilgrim badges or ex-voto (fol. 49r).18

Religious objects provide a common point of reference with his intended reader and as such are useful for measurements. For example, he advises to take “two or three rosary beads’ worth of rocaille” to make blue paint for glass (fol. 31v). He also suggests using paternoster beads of jet to make black varnish (fol. 4v). Such a description might be a qualitative statement referring to the size of the rosary beads (as with the rocaille example), but paternosters made of jet were made in France at least since the 1260s, as when Étienne Boileau compiled his Livre des métiers he divided the trade of making paternosters into three, one of which specialized in making paternosters of amber and jet.19 Surviving examples of jet rosary beads exist from Scotland and Spain, demonstrating that these objects were known across Europe (Fig. 4).20 The author-practitioner also used ground jet to make black pigment (fol. 63r). Perhaps the deep black of the material, combined with its spiritual efficacy, was thought to offer a depth of tone to black varnish, or maybe the material of the rosary held no enduring spiritual potency for the author-practitioner, who was happy to make practical use of materials, however holy, in the service of color making. In his recipe for making millas, he refers to an earthenware mold made in “the fashion of the crown of a Catholic hat,” potentially the rounded Camauro hat worn by the Pope (fol. 20r).21 Taken together, these references reinforce the suggestion that the author-practitioner was himself a practicing Catholic, familiar with the material culture of the church community.

Clothes and Accessories

What did the author-practitioner look like? While we do not know, the manuscript contains two sketchy marginal drawings of faces on fols. 61r and 169v, which are not explicitly referenced as self-portraits but may have been (Fig. 5, Fig. 6). A couple of body parts are also illustrated. One image, showing a makeshift brace to hold “small work” needing repairs (such as the turtle cast from life shown in the illustration) depicts a seemingly naked leg, simply outlined although with a hint of musculature, and foot holding a rope around a cushion (fol. 152v) (Fig. 7). Elsewhere I have posed the question about what the author-practitioner might have been wearing in his workshop, or as he sat writing up his experiments.22 In a charming illustration accompanying a trick to relight a candle, a depiction of hands lighting a “wicker,” we see a pair of hands decorated with ruffled cuffs, which would have been a fashionable way to edge one’s linen undershirt in the late sixteenth century (fol. 33r) (Fig. 8). This is the only depicted dressed body part in the manuscript, but it reminds us that whatever overgarments he wore, the author-practitioner would have been wearing linen next to his skin. All men and women in the early modern period regardless of social status (excepting the very poorest who wore rags) would have worn a long linen shirt or smock under their clothing. This was a comfortable and cool layer, which absorbed dirt and sweat and was easy to wash, bleach, and starch (he mentions starch water made by women on fol. 113v, probably for this purpose), unlike the wools and silks worn on outer garments.23 If a “garment” becomes stained, the author-practitioner recommends rubbing it with eau-de-vie (fol. 29v). While ruffs and cuffs were fashionable and decorative, they were also practical, as they protected the more valuable sleeve from being dirtied while at work. The author-practitioner often mentions using linen cloths to clean materials in the workshop or as strainers to separate liquids from solids. In his instructions on “Sand from pulverized rock salt and sand from the mine finely ground on marble,” he even suggests passing the sand “through a double sieve or through the sleeve of a shirt,” confirming that he owned plain weave linen shirts and expected the same of his reader (fol. 88v; see also fol. 72v).

The author-practitioner was familiar with textiles colored in deep or vibrant hues, as he mentions several recipes for dyeing and discusses black and crimson textiles. He seems to have spoken with textile or dye merchants, which is not surprising for a craftsman living in Toulouse, the center of the sixteenth-century woad trade.24 When he gives an example of what would be written in a merchant’s “waste book,” he uses the examples of two sales of cambric, a light linen named after its place of manufacture in Cambrai (fol. 9v), and he notes that merchants who sell velvet or other stuffs do not use double-entry although those who sell woad do (fol. 38v). He also writes at length about silkworm cultivation, a growing industry in early modern Europe that led to an increased market for silk goods (fol. 53v).25 He mentions that pearls that have “been applied in embroidery on some garment” are likely to be “greasy” but can be cleaned with soapy water, suggesting that he was familiar with very finely decorated clothing (fol. 13v). His instructions for pouncing designs onto satin with chalk or flour, and then tracing them with glair before scratching with glass or a penknife, demonstrates just how easy it was to decorate textiles with the fashionable small cuts known as “pinks” and “slashes” (fol. 42v).26 Whether or not he himself wore pinked silks, embroidered pearls, and velvet and cambric clothes dyed in crimson or black, the author-practitioner expected that his readers would be interested in the making of these kinds of fashions.

Other items of clothing, particularly accessories, are mentioned in passing. Shoes are useful tools, mentioned as a handy way to stop a snake (fol. 110r), though the author-practitioner notes that you should take your shoe off when you step on a cuttlefish bone mold lest it break (fol. 145v). Perhaps he wore tanned and decorated black leather shoes, for he mentions that a shoemaker can “dip a writing quill in oil of sulfur and paint with it what he would like on shoes, boots, and colets.” (fol. 46v). He also suggests that those who lack boots can wrap their legs in rope made of hay, and “the rain will never pass through” (fol. 54v). He suggests that burnt hat felt is good for making sand molds (fol. 83r). A small illustration accompanying instructions for a trick “For transmuting grain from one vessel to another” depicts a simple cloche hat that can be used as a cover (fol. 35v) (Fig. 9). Such a shape was quite common in the late sixteenth century, easily made with felted wool or beaver fur, and worn by men and women across the social spectrum, as is seen in the depiction of a French “rustic” in Hans Weigel’s Trachtenbuch (1577) (Fig. 10).27 He also mentions that “villagers” in Toulouse wear “fine [...] straw” hats (fol. 32v), an example of which might be seen in Weigel’s depiction of a “rustic woman” (Fig. 11). When describing at length the process of making leather scabbards (sword sheaths), he mentions that “common people” can buy them ready made, though this is inferior to getting a custom one, “for they are never carried as well.” A scabbard of “good cow” is “better than any and also costs more, except the velvet kind” which is the “best scabbard of all” if waxed (fol. 94r). However, while a scabbard is valued here for its luxury materials, when he describes at length the material hierarchies of sword grips, other concerns eclipse social signaling through fine decoration. He lists a range of materials from which sword grips can be made, including silk, dogfish skin, iron wire, gold threads, “fine & false silver,” or velvet. He first recommends iron wire, for it “has a lower price & is the most durable. Next is the grip of silk, if one does not have the convenience of being close to the sea” to procure dogfish. Dogfish skin, made from a sand shark, is a “beautiful skin,” he says, and costs “fifty or lx sous & 4 or five grips are made from it. This kind gives a good grip even if the hand is sweating” (fol. 94r). Such claims demonstrate how economic, practical, and logistical concerns are combined into a nuanced hierarchy of materials. While gold and velvet might be associated with elites, the practicality of a dogskin handle in a sweaty palm makes it superior.

These examples show how important accessories, textiles, dyestuffs, and garments were to the early modern economy, and illustrate that men and women dressed for both practicality and style. The hierarchies of materials used in clothing and accessories signaled the expense and time invested by their owners to get the look and function right, as well as the ingenuity of the craftsmen who made them, who exploited the practical propensity of materials to last well or act as a waterproof layer while making their owner look good. These objects sustain social and cultural identity, they set “villagers” and “common people” apart from those who could afford fine things, but there is no simple binary between rich and poor. Rather, there are nuanced levels of material qualities, valued for both their luxurious beauty and ability to perform their job well.

The Material World

What else can we learn about material culture of the every day from this manuscript? We discover that people in Toulouse make finely woven mats “to hang on the partition walls of rooms,” dyed green, red, and violet (fol. 32v). We learn that the “tiles with which one covers houses” are hard and firm, and can be used in making molds (fol. 106v). The exteriors of houses can also be decorated with stamped metal plaques painted with gold leaf that will “hold up in the rain” (fol. 7r). These stamps might also be glued onto smaller items like trunks, mirrors, valances, and bedposts, on top of “colored velvet or satin” (fol. 7r). Such decorations are different from those found on houses in Germany, which the author-practitioner explains are cast medals made with a reheated plaster mix that also “do not fear the rain” (fol. 125r).

The interiors of homes can also be “ornament[ed] with little expense” through the use of stucco made of tragacanth gum and rye flour formed into whatever shape you like (he suggests “masks and garlands”) and can be painted or gilded, a technique said to be used for ceiling ornaments in Rome.28 He suggests making bed ornaments with the rye-flour stucco (fol. 29r). Decorating beds seems to be a preoccupation for the author-practitioner, who mentions on several occasions casting mixed tin ornaments and decorating them with gold leaf, small rubies, orpiment, and colored grains for “adorning beds, mirrors, and suchlike” (fol. 131r) and “the edges of mirrors, the tops of chests, or the friezes of bed valances” (fol. 6v). Counterfeit jasper, made of horn, pigments, and wool, can also be used as an inlay to “encrust beds with” (fol. 10r). We find out about writing tablets made of boxwood inlaid with ebony, sandalwood, ivory, or gold and silver for writing on with silverpoint and erasing with a cuttlefish bone (fol. 15v). The author-practitioner is interested in decorating boxes with softened horn (fol. 15v), and he notes that the Germans make boxes covered with painted and varnished paper (fol. 67r). We are told about the process by which sheath makers imprint “some history” or can even imitate statues and delicate medals on leather, perhaps explaining the process by which molded leather étuis have been formed (Fig. 12) (fol. 57v). He offers instructions for a cheap way of making imitation damask or embroidery by resist dyeing (fol. 15r), explains how one can back gemstones with gold (fol. 37r), and describes the process by which “common people” can make emeralds from pebbles, vinegar, copper, minium, sel de verre, and sandiver bought from glassmakers or apothecaries (fol. 100v).29 While it is clear that his interest in imitation is bound up with his fascination for studying nature and pushing the properties of materials to their limits, his instructions are often explicitly about economic ways of creating decorative effects.30

In Ms. Fr. 640, we see how someone who could not afford silver or gold ornaments might still mimic their luminosity or even decorate their homes with fairly successful imitations of jasper inlay. We also learn about quotidian material culture, presumably familiar to the author-practitioner’s contemporaries, such as “an earthenware bowl, from which the peasants eat their soup” (fol. 121v). We discover that kitchen pots are made “with the same metal the bells are made of” to avoid giving meat a bad taste, and that latten is added to make them more yellow “in order to sell them better” (fol. 36v), showing how makers had to entice both the eyes and the tongue with their wares. The author-practitioner also describes how sieves made of horsetails are woven in Coutances, Normandy, and are brought to Spain via Toulouse, where they are traded for silk tammy (fol. 44v). Such details offer a uniquely rich image of the decorative arts and material culture made and owned by the artisan classes of early modern Toulouse.

While fine luxury items from the region survive in collections in France and around the world, demonstrating that Toulouse was populated by highly skilled craftspeople and discerning wealthy patrons who collected objects of glass, gold, silk, wood, and marble that represent some of the finest Renaissance decorative arts, the material culture made for and used by the non-elites has hardly survived.31 As Daniel Roche has observed, “we are better informed about the King’s furniture than about that of the peasantry.”32 The reconstructions of many of these objects in the Making and Knowing Laboratory, as well as by collaborators of the project, has already recreated material otherwise unknown, ephemeral, or underrepresented objects.33 Still more are likely to be created by readers of the online edition. The manuscript therefore evokes a culturally, economically, and materially nuanced picture of daily life and material culture of Toulouse specifically, and Europe more generally, and enables twenty-first-century researchers and makers to bring these processes and objects to life once more.

How-to texts like Ms Fr. 640 are often mined by historians for what they can reveal about the intellectual life of their authors, but this essay has aimed to show that we can also learn much about daily life and the making, use, trade, and cultural meaning of material culture through these kinds of sources. That the author-practitioner chose to collect and record information about domestic interiors and proverbs alongside his observations and instructions for weaponry use, mold making, and metal casting, suggests that he considered this information to be notable and useful to his reader. His understanding of the material world was informed by his quotidian experiences, and in texts like this we see how material, intellectual, religious, and experimental life was inseparable from the mundane. He integrated these aspects of daily life and material culture in early modern Europe into his understanding of the material world, and they informed his craft knowledge. In other words, instructional texts enable us to see how material culture and everyday life informed artisanal epistemology. More close readings of how-to texts might offer us a far richer picture of the economic, religious, social, cultural, and material surroundings of early modern men and women.
