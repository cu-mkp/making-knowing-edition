Abstract

Andrew Lacey and Siân Lewis spent two weeks in spring 2015 with the Making and Knowing Project leading students in lifecasting experiments. In their essay, they provide an account of Lacey’s experimentation and analysis of lifecasting techniques in advance of the Making and Knowing Residency, the real-time x-ray videos of metal casting they produced, and the whirlwind of Making and Knowing Lab activity in spring 2015.

Setting the Scene

Some time ago, the poet Alice Oswald asked if she and I could talk around the material culture of the “bronze age,” specifically of the heroic Greek period that she was researching at that time for “Memorial,” her epic re-imagining of Homer’s Iliad. This conversation covered everything from copper mining and smelting to the shaping and forming of bronze sculptures, all the way through to how the infamous flame-shaped sword favored by the Greeks felt like in the hand. We became aware that this conversation provided a back door into the past that allowed her to imagine her way into what could not be sensed directly. Since then, I have used this process to help me in the experimental reconstruction of objects or recipes from history (Fig. 1). Simultaneously allowing me to explore landscapes of the past while witnessing from the present has created an experience of time travel. I applied this method in reading Ms. Fr. 640 which revealed an astonishing vivification of seventeenth century life, a rich weaving of every concern and inquiry emerging from the landscape of southern France. Through deeper reading of the text, it was possible to sense the seasons through the descriptions of animals and flowers listed in the volume, and, when remembering the smell of wax and pine resin in my studio, I imagined the landscape of Toulouse. My explorations of the manuscript followed this indirect route of the imagination through the lens of “maker” and my own practical workshop knowledge; I later dissected this vision with the tools of scientific analysis.

Stepping into the Text

In 2014, I was invited to join the Making and Knowing project in the role of “Expert Maker,” a curious title that suggests perhaps too much. This invitation gave me the opportunity to engage with the unknown author-practitioner in a form of “time-distanced” conversation. The aim was to explore the lifecasting recipes within Ms. Fr. 640 and, if possible, to experimentally reconstruct them with a view to better understand the materials and processes therein. By some degree of serendipity I had also been asked to create a lifecast lizard for the new Enlightenment Galleries in the Victoria and Albert Museum, London. Such alignment in the constellations is rare, and the combination of the V&A’s art commission with Making and Knowing Project’s theoretical experimental reconstruction was too good to be missed, thus I spent the summer of 2014 reading and imagining my way into the manuscript. Although I made many lifecasting experiments during this time, few were recorded (Fig. 2).

What follows describes one experiment that asks questions of the author-practitioner while reflecting back physical realizations thrown up by the experiment. It also hints at the author-practitioner’s habit of self-questioning and realignments, a point brought to light in Giulia Chiostrini and Jef Palframan’s essay “Molding a Rose.”1 The essayists also recognize the author-practitioner’s method of using practice as a guide to understanding and refining each recipe. In this same manner, I set out to use reconstructive analysis to challenge the author-practitioner’s observations and my own preconceived ideas on life casting.

Interrogating the Recipe for Molding and Casting a Lizard

The author-practitioner gives great attention to the capturing of lizards, and on fol. 111v suggests “When one takes them with two fingers from the sides of the head, at the level of the ears, one makes them open their mouth, then one takes the end of its lower mandible and next, with the upper mandible, he bites the nail, but he cannot pass through, but he clenches very strongly. And if he were to bite living flesh it would not let go, and there would be no better remedy than to promptly cut off his head. In the spring, as they have changed skin, they are more beautiful.” Next the author-practitioner moves on to killing and preserving the lizard: “put it to death in this way. Place it in a clean bottle or vessel so that it would collect no dust. And put inside such a quantity of good vinegar and urine that it can be covered. And shake it and torment it therein until it is dead, which will be in an hour or a half.” Thankfully in both instances, I did not have to carry out these actions. The sample lizard used for the experiment was a Bearded Dragon and acquired from a pet shop, where, after a short and awkward conversation, the owner revealed they had a dead lizard that had been gifted to a school and returned deceased. The school in their care and wisdom had managed to forget to feed it over the summer holidays, hence it became the unexpected participant of the experiment. Specifically, the Bearded Dragon is a denizen of the southern hemisphere not a native of Britain, but lizards are a protected species and choice was limited, so the Bearded Dragon passed muster (Fig. 3).

On fol. 112v the recipe goes on to describe how the lizard is placed on a flat slab of clay (of an almond shape), and posed in an interesting and naturalistic manner: “Thus, the head will be held high, which will have better grace, and being secured, next, arrange the gesture of the body and the feet and the entanglement as you think will look best.” My lizard’s appearance was dictated by its last pose in life, after which it was frozen, in a state of rigor mortis. It had clearly suffered from starvation and with its sagging body creating folds of skin, it still appeared beautiful in death (Fig. 4).

Next, a wall of clay was made to surround the lizard, with a shorter section arranged toward the area where the gate will be formed. Surprisingly, this arrangement for molding was something I still use today when making molds for both wax and metal casts. In fact, I became aware of many instances where descriptions of practices from the past are still in use today. This long reach back in time reveals continuity of transmission covering centuries, whether oral, textual or from practice. However, the next stage challenged my received wisdom and current workshop practice. The author-practitioner clearly states directing the flow of the metal via the gate and into the body of the animal, as visualized in the margin of fols. 115r and 124v, through the tail. I consulted my peers (other founders, scientists, and makers) on this feature, and the universal conclusion was that it was wrong. The received wisdom today suggests that, when metal is fed into a body like this, the widest part of the form (in this case the belly) should be used. The rationale follows that when the liquid metal enters the mold, the largest parts of the cast will radically contract on cooling, therefore if the body is fed directly, then any contraction will be preferentially directed toward the gate instead. This fact is true but highlights my first mistake and some of the conflict the work of reconstructing past processes creates when viewed from a twenty-first-century perspective. My attitude toward casting was focused on metallurgical concerns, i.e., that of flow and contraction, however the author-practitioner’s concerns lay in preserving the aesthetics of the animal, hence he used the tail as the point through which the mold is filled. Molding in this way suggests that the author-practitioner’s control of the process was highly attuned to the physical demands and complexities of the materials. Seen elsewhere in the manuscript, the practice of casting via the smallest and most discreet entry point, as with the stems of flowers or tail of crayfish, indicates the universality of his technological principle.

I now arranged the gate and sprues, in this case, erroneously toward the head and body of the lizard (Fig. 5). However, the general design for the sprue system follows the author-practitioner’s advice in creating a comprehensive direction of flow throughout the whole mold. Put in simple terms, the liquid metal flows first into the gate where it divides into three streams, then enters the body of the lizard. In theory, the metal then flows through the entire lizard, exiting via the limbs and out through the smaller vents that surround it, simultaneously venting off any combustion gases, steam or air that have evolved in the mold. As we will see later, this idea of metal flow is over-simplified but it still represents the way we tend to visualize the unseen fluid dynamics in the casting process. It is worth noticing that the author-practitioner alters the rather staccato design for the gating of the sprue system, first visualized on fol. 124v by then changing to the gate design on fol. 125r, with the more recognizable fluidity of the funnel divided into three fine feeds.

My second challenge to the author-practitioner was to question whether the wax sprue system he specified could be substituted by one of clay, the premise being that if the mold is designed to be opened, then why not remove the clay sprue and lizard then. The problem with this, as we shall see, became apparent later. Note the close attention to the claws, where very fine sprues connect the outer tips of the claw to other nearby elements, such as the tail or body, further enabling the flow of metal.

Now, having arranged everything and not forgetting the wetting agent, I poured in the plaster investment, following the instructions “And as soon as you have thus moistened everything with eau-de-vie, cast in your wetted sand without letting the eau-de-vie dry, which evaporates promptly” (Fig. 6). Again, the recipes and proportions for making plaster investment are very similar to those today. In fact I use a near exact version of this recipe with brick dust replaced by molochite powder or very finely sifted dry sand, and exchanging “feathered alum” for chopped glass fiber. The eau-de-vie was new to me and worked wonderfully in allowing the liquid plaster to penetrate the finest details of the scales.

On removing the clay surround, the more vulnerable underside of the lizard and attached sprue system was clearly visible (Fig. 7, Fig. 8).

With the mold now facing upward, it was again ringed with clay as before, creating a hollow that will become the bottom half of the mold. It is worthy of consideration that the plaster, a liquid, changes viscosity over a short time and soon begins to stiffen, eventually hardening into a solid block. This principle can be used to one’s advantage in this part of the process. When first mixed with water, the plaster is highly fluid, and, if poured or flicked onto the lizard first, will capture the finest features of the scales. As the plaster becomes more viscous, it traps air bubbles in its matrix, but crucially away from the surface of the lizard, forming a porous body which later will enable moisture to be released from the mold and casting gases to be expelled in exchange for metal. The author-practitioner however has a cautionary note on fol. 133v stating that porosity “readily comes about when you cast two or three small molds, one after the other, in the same bowl, for the last one is cast from the bottom and thickest [sand] of the bowl, which readily becomes porous. The thinnest [sand] casts more finely and more neatly, provided that one blows strongly enough when casting on the tempered and cast sand in order to dissipate these small bubbles.”

On opening the mold the second time (Fig. 9, Fig. 10), I had hoped that the clay sprues and lizard would be compliant enough to allow for their removal. The author-practitioner on fol. 114v recommends “extract gently and with patience and method, the half of the animal remaining in one of the molds, pulling it sometimes from one side and sometimes from the other, to avoid breaking any of it, or also spoiling something from the mold.” However, on investigation I sensed that pulling the body of the lizard would disrupt the mold, fracturing the smaller plaster elements of the mold holding it tight; therefore I chose to leave the lizard in the mold with no expectation of its reuse. The clay sprues came out with ease but to get the channels clear required washing out all the fine sediment, introducing more moisture to the mold at a time when the mold needs to dry.

The mold was ready for firing, first to dry it out, then to heat it until the lizard body turns to ash. The same would apply to any fully embedded animal or plant. On fol. 115r, we are directed to “Reheat the thickest part, where the body of the animal is, at the top, so that, if there is something inside to burn, it falls to the bottom.” A key question here would be, how long is the mold fired for? In my gas kiln it is difficult to see exactly what is going on inside and the usual indicators of flame color, smoke, and smell have to be sensed at a distance. It is through the senses rather than pyrometers and time charts that I gauge how best to navigate these invisible processes.

Once cooled and removed from the kiln, in opening the fired mold, we are presented with a starkly different vision than before, but one that is promising. Where the body of the lizard once was, are now the cremated remains (Fig. 11). Fine bones have calcined to a pure white matrix and, despite looking solid, they can be blown away with a breath (Fig. 12, Fig. 13). This advice is important, for any debris left inside the mold could cause a defect in the casting.

The author-practitioner includes a cautionary note on fol. 117v

Some put quicksilver inside it. But, if it is a small work or fine and delicate foliage that only has a slender exit, they make two errors: the first, that quicksilver by its heaviness can break some delicate feature inside when shaken, the other, that some grains will always linger inside that will make metals sour and hinder the perfection of the cast. It is true that if it is to empty the of some animal which is thickish and which has big conduits and passages by which the quicksilver can easily exit, like a bird or a snake, one can indeed put in it some quicksilver to break by shaking the calcined bones of the animal, because the said mercury will come out and not remain.

Thankfully, I chose to use the breath and just blow out any remains.

The mold was then reassembled and bound with fine iron wires, then returned to the kiln for a second firing, this time aiming to get the interior of the mold glowing red hot. While it was heating, a small crucible of bronze (90% copper, 10% tin) was heated in a charcoal hearth, taking half an hour to fully melt and become completely fluid. The mold was removed from the kiln, and, while still glowing inside, was placed in some sandy earth to hold it in position. Dross was removed from the top of the bronze with an iron tool, and the mirror-like bronze was poured into the mold. I noted that no smoke or smells were emitted from the mold when the metal entered, a good sign that no debris lay hidden inside the mold (Fig. 14).

The heat of both the firing and the molten metal had taken a toll on the mold (Fig. 15). When cool enough to touch, the plaster investment broke away with ease, crumbling to a fine powder. The bronze cast fell out of this mass of dusty material and revealed some degree of damage caused by the mold cracking (Fig. 16). The long outer sprue, the vent designed to carry the gases away, had suffered the most, but this was superficial as these vents would be cut away from the lizard and returned to the crucible. On the underside of the cast, small flashings of metal where the bronze had entered cracks looked dramatic but in reality either broke away or chipped off with ease (Fig. 17). Fig. 18 shows the level of detail achieved, and the shiny lines describe where the thin metal flashings have been filed away.

The author-practitioner says surprisingly little on the subject of cleaning and refining the casting (Fig. 19). Apart from the necessity of removing the sprues and gate, this silence possibly assumes that only the most successful casts would be used and the complexity of rectifying failure was best achieved by re-casting. The author-practitioner also does not discuss the need to artificially color the casting; I assume this is because he intended that the cast would simply reveal the beauty of the once living animal and its miraculous transformation into the enduring permanence of the metal (Fig. 20, Fig. 21).

Imagining the Invisible / Imaging the Invisible

Just prior to my 2015 visit to the Making and Knowing Lab, I had the chance to revisit the question I had posed early on, as to why the author-practitioner had chosen to place the gate and main feeds into the smallest elements of the animal or flower. This principle repeated throughout the recipes of Ms. Fr. 640 confounded me. I had seen so many examples of casts failing due to the misplacement (as I would judge it) of the gating system. The complications surrounding the flow and subsequent cooling and contraction often resulted in casting flaws that defied repair. However, the perseverance of the author-practitioner persuaded me that there must be value in this method. I devised an experiment to cast a perfect wax copy of the proto-lizard, as per the recipe and then analyse it using twenty-first century methods. In order to maximize the value and credibility of this experiment I needed to be able to see inside the mold as the cast happened. To do this required accessing a real-time x-ray facility designed specifically for analysing fluid dynamics of casting systems, and, as luck would have it, there is one such facility within three hours drive of my studio. The Department of Metallurgy in Birmingham University, UK, is still one of the most highly regarded departments in this field, albeit one that has changed beyond description over the last thirty years due to the development of new technologies. It was here that the following experiments took place.

Both experimental sprue systems featured the same wax lizard model as a standard form so that the variation in the sprue system (tail-fed vs. head-fed) would be the only difference. The first experimental sprue system approximately followed the author-practitioners diagram in the margins of fol. 124v with the main gate and feeds directed toward the tail of the lizard (Fig. 22). The second sprue system followed common modern art foundry practice with the main flow of liquid directed toward the bulk of the body and head (Fig. 23).

I would also like to draw attention to the gate, which is a point repeated in words and sketches throughout the sections in the manuscript on lifecasting. The author-practitioner writes “Be careful to not make your gate very wide, and do not forget to make in its conduit two or three holes and notches, and as your gate approaches the molded thing, divide it into three or four parts like fingers which are pointed and are not very deep. For the metal runs more gently without being hindered by vapors and fumes” (fol. 129v). The gate or funnel divided into three feeds was still in common use in England’s art foundries until the late twentieth century, and I had also seen evidence of it on the underside of sculptures by Degas in the Fitzwilliam Museum’s collection. Here again is a remnant of practice encoded and transmitted over centuries. Two details strike me about these repeated instructions: first, the author-practitioner understood how the evolving gases inside the mold could disrupt the flow of the metal, therefore providing alternative channels through which the metal could flow to avoid choking the mouth of the mold. Second, by notching the lower section of the gate, dross would hopefully be caught in the roughened texture, as typically most dross is deposited close to the bottom of the gate just prior to entering the cast.

Both molds were de-waxed and fired before being transported to Birmingham University. On site, both molds were placed in a kiln and fired to 450°C for two hours; the bronze used was of the same composition as previously (90% copper, 10% tin). The real-time x-ray machine is part of a heavily insulated room-sized chamber with an x-ray gun to one side and a target on the other. The mold was carefully placed between said gun and target, then an image of the mold could be viewed from an adjacent room. The viewable image showed the maximum area visible by the x-rays. The molten bronze was poured remotely on a massively over-engineered articulated arm. The complexity of setting the mold and crucible, vacating the chamber, firing the x-ray, and managing to capture the video images in an adjacent room was, if not balletic, highly impressive! The bronze, poured at a temperature of 1150°C, could clearly be seen filling the empty mold, revealing its secrets at 25 frames per second (Fig. 24). Both molds were cast within a short time of each other and under exactly the same physical conditions.

Any subsequent differences in the casts would be due to factors of flow and contraction. On opening the molds, both appeared to be of the same quality, highly detailed, and all the extremities of the lizard, as well as the entire sprue system, had worked perfectly. As I have witnessed from many other real-time x-ray experiments, this gating method also helped to reduce turbulence and capture fine elements of dross before they fully enter the mold. It also shows the ingenuity of the author-practitioner to imagine his way into the invisible, into what could not be seen directly.

Review of the two real-time x-ray videos showed that in both experiments, the flow of the metal was highly turbulent. This caused metal to splash against the internal walls of the mold until the metal reached the halfway point whereby the flow started to rectify and become calmer. This is still a far cry from the idealized gentle and controlled flow we imagine should be happening. This visual evidence was crucial in examining the realities of such practice. Witnessed in the video was the slow expulsion of air trapped inside a raised claw that was vented poorly. Here we saw that metal cast into a gas permeable mold, could under pressure push the gases directly through the wall of the mold. This event could only happen if the temperature and fluidity of the metal worked in conjunction with the temperature and permeability of the mold.

Later, the two casts were imaged again under standard x-ray conditions (120Kv with copper filter). The two casts seen side by side look very similar (Fig. 25) but on closer inspection the cast following the author-practitioner’s “tail-fed” instruction revealed hollow voids discreetly hidden within the wall of the lizard’s body (Fig. 26, Fig. 27).

Crucially, in the case of the tail-fed lizard, the x-ray describes the result of the way the metal cooled (Vid. 1). The evidence shows the following: beginning under the true right arm of the lizard, a large void formed every few millimeters with increasing size and angularity along the central core of body, until finally stopping at the mid section between the rear legs and the tail, indicating the point where the metal was last liquid just before entering a slush phase. Although this appears as a dramatic and potentially disastrous flaw, if the voids do not penetrate to the outer surface of the casting, the flaw itself becomes invisible to the viewer and the casting appears perfect. It is a gamble, to cast in this way offers greater aesthetic value to the lizard or flower because the key areas, i.e., the face or head of the flower, are not disrupted by the imposition of larger, heavy sprues. The complexity of the physics balanced against the author-practitioner’s virtuosity was the balance between success and failure.

Whirlwind in the Laboratory

The spring semester 2015 had finally arrived, but the warm summer scents of the Toulouse landscape evoked in the manuscript had been replaced by the whipping easterly winds of February in New York and sparkling ice floes on the Hudson River. The students of the Making and Knowing Project were now focusing on the lifecasting recipes in the manuscript Ms. Fr. 64 and, in the role of “Expert Maker” I would be helping them navigate.

To see this group of incredibly bright students directed by Pamela Smith was something impressive to behold. One cannot overestimate the power of the “hive mind” and there is a potent energy in bringing a diverse range of minds to the same task. However, my initial concern was that having so many students recreating the complex and sometimes confusing recipes of the manuscript could result in wide-ranging errors in the experiments. Critically, the students did not come from a practical arts background, so how precise would the reconstructed experiments be, and what would be the value of their outcomes? My concerns were soon quelled, however, as I saw the way they approached each task. The diverse range of minds and approaches coupled with the “hive mind” phenomenon overcame what could have been a highly reductive process. The students were required to engage with the complexities of lifecasting, and the author-practitioner offers a wide range of recipes, including lizards, snakes, beetles, flowers, and even a spider web. These recipes tackle the casting of many natural objects, however in practice, they can be seen more broadly as nuanced variants of the same process. By setting students onto slightly differing but essentially very similar experimental trajectories, each student could reveal and correlate details that would previously have been time consuming to gather and difficult to explain. There is also a special phenomenon that occurs when a large number of novice practitioners (students) carry out the same task. Typically, they will all find their own route, meaning that success and failure were both commonplace. More significantly, the boundaries of each process can be found very quickly which is essential for understanding the parameters of any action or material given in the recipes. What follows are some observations made during the numerous experiments carried out in those two weeks in the lab.

Two Types of Mold

The molding of flowers is repeatedly described within the text and entries appear sporadically from fol. 117r through to 155r. Many of these recipes for molding flowers fall into two principal categories.

  1. a two-part mold, designed for flatter (less three-dimensionally complex forms), including objects such as leaves, lizards, snakes, beetles, etc. For these molds, the author-practitioner suggests on fol. 116r, “One needs to mold them as soon as they are picked because they wilt and dry out. Dip them, therefore, immediately in good eau-de-vie & then put them in your tempered sand mold, like snakes.”

  2. a one-piece mold used for molding fuller, more complex objects such as roses, carnations or bouquets. This second method appears less frequently in the text, and although simpler in construction, provides more complexity: because the object to be cast is fully encased in the mold, it cannot be opened for inspection, and what happens deep inside is impossible to know. In this second method we see an increased danger of ash and charcoal lodging within the mold, potentially causing a flaw in the casting. This is perhaps why the author-practitioner does not refer to it as frequently as the two-part mold.

The students experimented with both types (Fig. 28).

Echoes of the Past

Following the two-part molding recipe for “molding a rose” on fol. 155r, the stem and small branches were removed and laid onto a bed of clay similar to the previous lizard experiment. Simple wax sprue systems were designed for each leaf and stem (Fig. 29, Fig. 30). To assist the flow of the metal, the author-practitioner suggests “And with small veins of wax adapted and joined from leaf to leaf, you can make gates.” A design for these gates can be seen in the margin, however the author-practitioner goes on to say “You can even make a vein of wax from the back of the first petal, which will join to the gate. All of this facilitates the cast” (fol. 155r). This method for creating veins or gates on the reverse of a thin casting has frequently been used throughout history, and a good example can be seen in the small bronze plaquette designed by Andrea Riccio “The Triumph of a Hero” (Fig. 31). On the reverse of the plaquette are a series of fine lines that had been created by scoring channels into the back of the mold (Fig. 32). When comparing the designs for sprueing small plants and animals in the manuscript with Riccio’s integrated sprue system on the reverse of his plaquette, we see strong correlation. Further, this integrated design is commonly in use today for both lost-wax and sand casting of small objects.

Echoes Reach into the Future

For casting delicate flowers and bouquets, the author-practitioner suggests on fols. 117r and 145v that they are cast upright in the vertical cylinder of clay. Here the design for the sprue system has been altered. The plant stem is now mounted on a wax “peg,” forming the main gate, supported by two smaller vents either side of it. Again, we see here that the instructions of the author-practitioner have echoed down through the centuries. This method of gating is so effective that it is still in use today for casting jewellery or small, complex sculptural objects, as seen in the two images from the studio of Factum Arte (Fig. 33, Fig. 34). The olive branch will join other similar castings that will eventually create the entire bronze sculpture of an olive tree. The arrangement for the sprues and gate (now molded in metal) are very similar to the author-practitioner’s recipe. Instead of a clay wall, the whole system is surrounded by a steel cylinder to create the mold, even the investment is a derivation of the plaster-sand recipe.

Improbably Fine Results

The author-practitioner also gives useful and practical advice for casting flowers that are too fine to support their own weight or that will be susceptible to movement when the “sand” (plaster) is poured into the clay mold. In the margins of fol. 117r are suggestions that “If the plants are so delicate that they rise to the top when you pour the tempered sand, one can fix and contain them with a thread passed through the circle of the mold with a needle.” As proof of the effectiveness of this technique, the permeability of the mold and the flow of the metal resulted in the improbably fine cotton thread (Fig. 35) being integrated with the casting of the rosemary (Fig. 36). To cast at such a fine level is the ultimate goal, the potential being the ability to recreate nature in all its beauty and wonder.

Making the Mold Breathe

The way the metal flows around the interior of a mold is critical to the success of the casting. As mentioned before, there are two key factors at play. First, the design of the sprue system should provide easy access for the metal to enter the finest part of the mold. Any ash from the burnt animal or plant will block the passage of the metal and must be blown out (Fig. 37, Fig. 38) before any attempt at casting is made (Fig. 39, Fig. 40).

Second, but more difficult to know directly, is the porosity of the plaster mold. The author-practitioner gives warning on fol. 146r that large bubbles of air trapped in the plaster might get stuck to the surface of the object to be cast. These bubbles would later be revealed in the casting as fine balls of metal adhered to the surface of the cast object. However, what the author-practitioner could not see is the fine porosity that makes up the matrix of the mold (Fig. 41).

We now know that this interlacing crystal structure provides plenty of space for air and casting gases to be removed from the system. Making the mold breathe is an issue that all metal founders wrestle with. The importance of this principle can be seen in the real-time x-ray videos (see Vid. 1), where the finest details seen in the claws of the lizard miraculously begin to appear and eventually fill up even though the sprues are completely choked with metal. The directions laid out in the recipes allow for this phenomenon, even though it is something that cannot be sensed directly, but only realized through the successful outcome of the casting.

Conclusion

In many ways, I find the voice of the author-practitioner deeply resonant with my own studio practice. The habit of self-correction through experimentation is something I find far more important than verbatim-learned practice. Learning through experimentation is in my opinion more flexible, particularly when applied to the wide range of possibilities that one has to contend with in the studio. Adaptability is central to the success of such experimentation, but it is only through being fully awake with our senses that we can navigate the unknown. The elemental forces that come with the changing of our seasons may be greeted as collaborators and allies. The author-practitioner is clearly highly adaptable, taking elements from previous recipes and constantly altering or challenging them until a successful outcome is reached. Confusingly for the reader, self-corrections sometimes occur over many pages with large gaps in between, making interpretation difficult. However, this also points to a real-time chronology of the text; one can sense how the author-practitioner has had to wait for an experiment, perhaps to let it dry out, or possibly because other experiments have taken precedence. I was also struck by the sense of continuity through the centuries that the manuscript Ms. Fr. 640 offers. On numerous occasions, recipes in the text appear familiar to me. The Riccio plaquette is evidence of the transmission of techniques through deep history, and Factum Arte’s Bronze Olive Tree links this lineage of ideas to state-of-the-art contemporary practice. We see in the author-practitioner’s words a continuum of makers that reaches from the long distant past, remaining unbroken for centuries, and very much alive today.

I came across the title of this essay on a bitterly cold walk to the Making and Knowing Lab one day. “In Pursuit of Magic” was spray-stencilled on the pavement in front of a rather stylish shop in Greenwich Village (Fig. 42).

These words could not better sum up the hope and aspiration that the students and I were seeking (Vid. 2). Divine providence, serendipity, or a curious signpost to the work ahead?
