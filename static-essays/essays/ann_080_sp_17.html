An Excellent Salve for Burns

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/58dr-ns42
← Back to Essays

Abstract

“Against burns, excellent” (fol. 103r) gives detailed instructions on the making and application of a salve for burns. Like some other medical recipes in the manuscript it calls for relatively few ingredients but specifies a complex making process to transform the material into a different state. The recipe is unusual among the other medical recipes in Ms. Fr. 640, however, because it possesses explicit spiritual dimensions, including pure ingredients, holy water, and the recitation of prayers. In addition, the recipe provides information about its source and trustworthiness. The reconstruction of the recipe revealed the transformation of the mixture of linseed oil and hardening wax, two hydrophobic substances, into a fluffy white ointment through constant stirring with water, and, at the same time, the purifying “ritual” brought about a visible whitening and “inspiriting” of the materials with each recited prayer and washing with holy water.

The first recipe on fol. 103r, “Against burns, excellent,” gives detailed instructions for the making and application of an “excellent” salve for burns:

Against burns, excellent

Heat linseed oil on a gentle fire without letting it boil & simmer, but once it is hot, put in a quarter as much of the newest wax you can. Once melted, let it cool, & once they begin to curdle, stir continuously with a new wooden spatula t for as long as it takes you to say one 9 paternoster, and as you say them, wash this composition with holy water, stirring all the while. Having said the first 9 paternoster, pour out the first water & put in new one, & wash & stir the composition for the time it takes you to say 8 paternoster, and the 3rd time for as long as 7, & thus you will consecutively until add new water, doing the same as above, until the last & single paternoster of nine. Then you shall have a soft & white ointment, with which you shall anoint the burn for the space of 9 days. But do not apply it any longer than this, for it would cause a your flesh to grow excessively. You b shall bandage yourself twice a day, & each time you shall wash your face with water & wine mixed together, a little tepid, not rubbing, but as if pressing with a wet linen cloth, and you shall wipe it similarly aff with a fine linen cloth, & next put the ointment, over which you can put ivy leaves. This causes hair to regrow & leaves no scar. A gunpowder maker who had almost completely burnt himself m’a & showed no sign of the burn, taught me this.

It calls for relatively few ingredients, but the creation process is rather complex. The recipe is exceptional among the manuscript’s other medical recipes because it possesses explicit religious components, including holy water and prayers. My reconstruction of the recipe focuses on the process of making the salve. I chose not to test its efficacy because possible hazards are not clear.

What is a Salve: Ingredients, Making Processes, and Application

Salves and ointments, usually oil-based, were widely employed medical preparations in early modern Europe for treating external injuries, such as wounds and burns, for external lesions such as ulcers or syphilitic sores, and in relieving swellings, sore muscles and joint pain. It is unclear what exactly was meant by the terms “salve” or “ointment” in early modern Europe, but these terms commonly appear in books of secrets, household recipe collections and cookbooks. Salves and ointments appear to have been related to “balsams;” however, the differentiation of these terms would require close comparison of different recipes and reconstruction of the substances, which is beyond the scope of this essay. Here, I specifically focus on a single salve, investigating its ingredients and reconstructing the recipe.

The recipe calls for two basic ingredients: linseed oil and wax. They were two of the most common materials used in early modern Europe for cooking, lighting, and various artisanal works. Linseed oil, however, was not a material with a well-known therapeutic value. Instead, it was widely used in the artisanal practices of oil painting and varnish making. Indeed, linseed oil is one of the most frequently appearing oils in Ms. Fr. 640, and an entry on fol. 52v about picking the seed (presumably for pressing the oil), advises picking the seeds before they are perfectly ripe. The multiple uses of materials by the author-practitioner is a noticeable feature of the manuscript, and in the case of medical recipes, as I argue in another essay, a wide range of artisanal materials are used as medicinal substances, and vice versa.1

Wax was also a versatile material, its malleable and plastic properties making it useful for a variety of artisanal work. There are 110 recipes in Ms. Fr. 640 that require wax as a molding material, casting aid, adhesive, sealant or medicine, or that mention it as a denotation of a specific consistency.2 The protean nature of wax was also valued as a material in imitative processes, as “it could readily change from one state to another, from solid to liquid, evoke the trope of transformation and mimic the material properties of metals that could be melted down and reconstituted into new objects.”3

Wax was a popular medicinal ingredient between the fifteenth and seventeenth centuries, and it was “employed in a myriad of syrups and juleps, electuaries and pills, oils and ointments, plasters and poultices, laxatives and eyewashes.”4 Its healing properties were thought to derive partly from its connections to religious symbols: its purity and its flammability were compared to the light and “humanity of Christ,” while its malleability made it a symbol for work by the hands of the Creator.5 Grades of wax were judged by purity, the best being “not very yellow” and “not too fatty.”6 A process of purification produced pure white wax: first, a practitioner crushed and cleaned the wax in mortars, removing most of the large impurities. Then the wax was simmered in an earthenware pot with seawater, alum, and saltpetre. When the cooking was finished and the wax was left to settle and cool, the impurities rose to the top of the solidified wax and were easily removed. This cooking process was repeated several times to produce the purest wax, “as white as God’s own beard.”7

This processed white wax was most likely not what the author-practitioner meant on fol. 103r by the “newest” wax. This new wax presumably had not yet undergone any purifying process and still possessed its yellow color and fatty texture. New wax was used in medical recipes and bewitching ointments.8 The process of making the burn salve that I undertook was similar to the purifying process, and, although it read like a religious ritual, it turned out to be not just a ritual, but rather a “materialization of devotional process,” brought into being through the changing state of the materials.

Holy water and prayers were believed to have healing power for a wide range of diseases in the early modern period. Like wax candles, breads, and other material objects that played a role in devotional practices (known as sacramentals), holy water could connect the temporal material world to the eternal realm of spirit. In Catholic territories, holy water was a cure all for sickness and an exorcising material that could drive away evil spirits.9

The mixing of the ingredients was timed, as was common in craft procedures, by reciting the paternoster. The paternoster was one of the best known prayers in Christianity. Its brevity made it relatively easy to memorize, and it was used in artisanal recipes as a time-keeping device:

Pater Noster, qui es in caelis,

sanctificetur nomen tuum;

adveniat regnum tuum;

fiat voluntas tua,

sicut in caelo, et in terra.

Panem nostrum supersubstantialem da nobis hodie;

et dimitte nobis debita nostra,

sicut et nos dimittimus debitoribus nostris;

et ne nos inducas in tentationem;

Sed libera nos a malo.

Amen

Making the burn salve requires nine full cycles of mixing holy water with the salve. The length of each cycle is gradually decreased by one paternoster, starting at nine paternoster in the first cycle to one in the last. Nine was seen as a significant, sometimes magical, number, connected to the Trinity, and conveying perfection.

Through this process, linseed oil and wax, both of a yellow color, were supposed to be transformed into a soft white ointment, which, according to the recipe, should be applied on the burn twice a day for nine days, no less and no more. Each time, one should wash one’s face with tepid water and wine by pressing with a moistened linen cloth and using the cloth to soothe the burns. After applying the salve, the recipe recommends applying ivy leaves to facilitate hair growth and avoid leaving a scar. Ivy was regarded as having “hairy” stems, thus its mention brings the human body and plants into homologous relation in this recipe. Perhaps this is the origin of the vernacular notion that ivy leaves have healing power, common in English medical traditions.10 Ivy could also symbolize eternal life, as it remained green through the winter.

Oil and wax were common ingredients in making salves in the early modern period. Additional materials like herbs, metals, and minerals could be added to enhance the healing power. The royal apothecary and chemist Nicolas Lemery (1645–1715) devoted a chapter to recipes for making salves and ointments. In most of these recipes, oil and wax are listed as ingredients. Wax is sometimes replaced by other similar substances possessing both viscous and solid states and a low melting point, such as turpentine or butter.11

No sense of the appearance of the salve on fol. 103r can be derived through reading or textual research of this recipe. Only reconstruction can reveal the final appearance of the “excellent” burn salve. As described below, this reconstruction reveals a surprising process of material transformation and spiritual materialization.

Reconstruction

For the reconstruction, I used cold pressed linseed oil and yellow beeswax.12 Cold pressed linseed oil contains many impurities that make it appear a darker yellow than the refined oil used for painting. Linseed oil in the early modern period was a cheaper alternative used by the poor, and I assume the linseed oil used by author-practitioner was unrefined. The wax, too, was “new,” and thus probably unrefined.

According to the recipe, the wax should be one quarter of the amount of linseed oil. I decided to measure by volume rather than weight, and I used 100 ml linseed oil and 25 ml solid beeswax in the experiment. I made the decision to use tap water instead of holy water, as “holiness” comes from the blessing of a priest, with no additional components. The recitation of the paternoster was essential, however, for it is not only a purifying prayer, but also a way to measure time. In total, the paternoster must be recited forty-five times during the process. I chose the Latin version of the prayer, as that would have been used by a literate Catholic in late sixteenth-century France. There are two ways of reciting the paternoster: singing or speaking. As I measured it, singing doubles the time of speaking. I could not determine whether the paternoster was sung or spoken at the time, therefore I arbitrarily made the decision to speak it. Furthermore, the paternoster needed to be recited by a person, rather than simply timed because the speed of the spoken prayer varies, and it is important to capture this feature of time measurement in early modern artisanal work—it was not rigorously precise, but flexible and attuned to the degree of heat and the evolving appearance of the materials.

The linseed oil was to be simmered on a light fire without boiling. The flash point of linseed oil is 222℃ and the boiling point 340℃. The oil is not to be heated to those temperatures. The proper temperature should be a little higher than the melting point of beeswax, which ranges from 62 to 65°C. In order to facilitate the full melting of the beeswax, I decided to heat to 85°C.

After melting the beeswax in the hot linseed oil, I removed the pot from the hotplate and let it cool. About ten minutes later, the mixture started to “curdle,” as called for in the recipe (Fig. 1). I then added water. Ludovic Touzé Peiffer helped by reciting the paternoster while I stirred the oil, wax, and water mixture constantly with a new wooden chopstick (as the recipe also called for a new wooden spatula).

The material transformation began in the first cycle. The substance in the pot started to increase in volume, as if water and air was being stirred into its structure. The dark yellow, viscous, oily substance gradually became whiter and creamier. The volume of the oil and wax mixture was one fifth of the pot at first; after the first cycle of washing (nine paternosters), the volume increased to one third (Fig. 2). The water I poured off from the mixture between washing cycles was much less than the amount I had added (Fig. 3). As I continued the process of adding water and stirring, the volume of the substance continued to expand, and the color became whiter and whiter. After the nine cycles of “washing,” we got a full pot of very light and fluffy, almost white, smooth, creamy, lotion-like salve with a strong smell of linseed oil (Fig. 4). The structure of the salve, however, turned out to be unstable. Water continued to be released from it, and, after a week, the salve shrank back to almost the original volume of oil and wax.

Medical Credibility

The author-practitioner claimed he learned the recipe from a Pouldrier, a gunpowder maker. Gunpowder making was dangerous work in the early modern period, and this gunpowder maker claimed to have sustained disfiguring burns. The author-practitioner noted in the recipe that he observed not a trace of these burns. A knowledge-sharing network involved multiple ways of verifying the credibility of a recipe, of which firsthand experience or observation was one. The efficacy of the recipe was proven by a claim of firsthand experience: I tried it and it worked. This is common in Ms. Fr. 640, as well as many other texts of practice at this time. The “secrets” shared among craft practitioners and within the medical marketplace largely relied upon the experience and credibility of individuals.

Conclusion

This recipe might be unremarkable were it not for its devotional aspects. The surprising aspect of reconstructing this recipe (in contrast to simply reading it) was that the process of mixing holy water while repeating paternosters caused a tangible “purification” and “inspiriting” of the raw materials, as the mixture became white and fluffier. The transformation in state of the materials in this recipe caused a devotional practice to emerge before one’s eyes in a materialized form. In modern chemical terms, this is of course an emulsion, a mixture of two or more normally immiscible liquids, and it is a commonly employed process in pharmaceutical practice to make creams, lotions, ointments, and pastes. In this recipe, however, it is an example of the kind of material transformation common to many recipes in Ms. Fr. 640, which, at the same time, brought into being a materialization of spiritual devotion. Our reconstruction was of course inauthentic: we could not capture the spiritual significance of the process—I did not even use holy water—but, even so, the material transformation converged with and made tangible the spiritual process. Perhaps that was the secret that cured the gunpowder maker’s burns.
