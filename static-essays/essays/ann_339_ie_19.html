War and Economy in Late Sixteenth-Century Languedoc: Dominique Bertin and Hélie Bachelier

By
← Back to Essays

Abstract

The erosion of royal authority and the wars of religion disrupted the kingdom of France from 1562 to 1598. However, as scholars have demonstrated recently, this troubled history did not translate into artistic decay. The professional trajectories of artisans from Toulouse, such as Dominique Bertin and Hélie Bachelier, shed light on the context in which Ms. Fr. 640 was written. It allows us to understand how these artisans put their versatile talents at the service of political power, revealing the economic and military benefits of their know-how. In the second half of the sixteenth century, several artisans stood out for their multiple skills and their interest in industry, notably Dominique Bertin (documented from 1551–1577) and Hélie Bachelier (documented from 1574–1631). Although their intellectual and professional profiles differ significantly from the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640, a study of their career trajectories can give insight into the political and economic challenges that characterize the artistic context in Languedoc.1

Versatility and Politics: Dominique Bertin

From woodwork to civil engineering, Dominique Bertin’s career was so rich in variety that in the late nineteenth century, newly discovered archives made historians wonder whether he really took part in the projects himself; they even considered there might be two masters with the same name. Recent research, however, has dispelled those doubts.2

Bertin worked for the city’s more active patrons: the municipality, the parlement, and religious brotherhoods. As a scholar who mastered Vitruvian theory, mannerist ornamental repertoire, and drawing, he quickly distinguished himself from his colleagues. By the mid-1550s, as a competent manager and a man with considerable field experience, he became a prominent figure of the Toulouse Renaissance.

From 1556 to 1558 he was commissioned by the Estates of Languedoc to work with Nicolas Bachelier on a scheme intended to straighten the river Hers and reduce the damage caused by flooding on surrounding farmland.3 Even though this engineering project was of public interest, political and social unrest at the onset of the Wars of Religion caused its demise. However, there still remains an impressive drawing of the project, made by Bertin (Fig. 1).4 His skills as a draftsman, land-surveyor, and chorographer entitled him to become an architect, and be named Engineer-General of Guyenne, as well as Commissioner of Artillery.5

On several occasions in the 1570s in the position as Commissioner of Artillery, Bertin supervised the “haulage and carriage” of guns (cannons, culverins, field guns, etc), cannonballs and barricots (kegs) of gunpowder that the city of Toulouse delivered to important local authorities, such as the seneschal, who organized targeted military campaigns in the surrounding area.6 Like Bertin, but on a lesser scale, many craftsmen, gunners, carpenters, cartwrights, blacksmiths, farriers, saddlers, wheelwrights, and carters in the Toulouse region were involved in the transportation, maintenance, and strategic use of artillery guns from the municipal Armory. The knowledge of the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 in these matters must be seen in this context of “military regionalism.”7

Bertin’s name is also linked to a renewed surge of interest in the exploitation of the kingdom’s natural resources, launched under Henri II.8 In 1554, letters patent allowed Bertin to “discover and extract from the mines, the aforesaid gold, silver, lead and other minerals, and aforementioned matters and marble” in Guyenne, Rouergue, and Languedoc. As a result, Bertin bore the title of “conducteur de marbres pour le roy” (conductor of marbles for the King) and for a decade supplied material for the main royal construction sites and some of the local sites as well (Fig. 2).9 In this activity, Bertin was supported by an important officer of the Crown, the Garde des Sceaux (Minister of Justice) and Bishop of Comminges, Jean Bertrand, a native of Toulouse.10

Bertin took advantage of King Charles IX’s entry into Toulouse in 1565 to show the sovereigns the diversity of Pyrenean marbles and minerals and to obtain the renewal of his letters patent. For the entry, he had supervised the creation of ephemeral decorations abounding with imitations of precious materials.11 From 1561 to his death, he was also “warden of the royal mines,” surrounding himself with experts “from our Kingdom, from the countries of Germany, Lorraine, and other foreign countries” to help him meet the surveying and operating requirements of his mission.12 Thanks to his early professional relations, his knowledge of ports, logging rafts, sites, and timber supplies (in the valleys of the Aure and Luchon), the former carpenter’s activities flourished from the Pyrenees to Bordeaux.13

Mining in the Pyrenees was no novelty in the sixteenth century: in the Foix area, mines had been exploited as early as the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries.14 At the end of the fifteenth century, the King authorized German miners to work in the mines of the comté of Couserans.15 Several northern masters were solicited to exploit these underground resources from the first half of the sixteenth century up to the early seventeenth century.16 In these regions ruled by the Kings of Navarre, Henri d’Albret, drawing on the Lorraine model, gave impetus to prospecting for precious metals.17 Research and assessment of mines conducted in the middle of the century systematically involved Germans, some of whom obtained, in the 1560s, the concession of silver mines from the richest comté in this regard, the comté of Foix. This industrial context may partly account for the author-practitioner’s familiarity with German metalworking know-how: on several occasions he refers to German forging (fol. 50v) and to the molders of Foix (fol. 90r, 143r).

Two sources provide insight into the stakes of this industry for the kingdom. In his Discours sur le rehaussement et diminution des monnoyes, Jean Bodin relates his visit to Bertin’s alum mining site:

As for alum, should we cut through the veins of the Pyrenean mountain, we would assuredly find lodes, not only of alum, but also of gold and silver, as was reported by several Germans and shown to me on the site by Dominique Bertin who submitted proof of all metals to King Henry with an infinite amount of copperas, alum, and marcasite.18

Quite aware of promoting his skills and his industry, and eager to improve his position, Bertin fully endorsed Bodin’s argument and even helped fuel it:

Among other things, it appeared that there is more alum than is needed for the whole of France, and yet some comes from Italy, worth over a million each year, as he [Bertin] has observed. […] and assures me that if he [Bertin] had his say, we should no longer need alum from Italy.19

Considering the links between Bertin’s area of operations, his status as a “cappitaine” and his ties to the bishopric of Comminges, historian Serge Brunet assumes that he assisted the bishop, Pierre d’Albret, who was accused of making counterfeit coin in 1566, by supplying him with imported metals.20

His knowledge of the territory provided him an opportunity to examine numerous archaeological remains and draw the attention of such ambitious personalities as the philologist from Bourbonnais, Jean Gardet. Together, they published an abridged, illustrated and annotated French edition of Vitruvius.21 Bertin made the engravings of this Epitomé by employing copperplate engraving, a technique hardly known in France and used in Toulouse for the first time.22 This procedure is referred to in a few entries in Ms. Fr. 640.

Broadly speaking, Bertin and Gardet’s method was similar to the author-practitioner’s: they drew on their experience and observations of the environment to make difficult passages of ancient treatises easier to understand. Unlike the author-practitioner, however, Bertin and Gardet sprinkle their comments with countless textual references. The comparison that best illustrates such different scholarly approaches concerns their common fascination with mirrors. The author-practitioner is amused by surprising effects and the “magical” scope of the distortions “concave mirrors” produce (fol. 5r). He also suggests different mirror positions to enhance night-time lighting (vision, reading, writing), alludes to the camera obscura and to the ability of reflected light to ignite certain materials by referring to the names of Ptolemy and Archimedes. Bertin and Gardet mention the distortions caused by “perspective mirrors” for the sole purpose of announcing their wish to publish an intelligible edition of Vitello’s thirteenth-century century treatise on optics.23

Pumps, Mines, and a Trip Hammer: Hélie Bachelier and the Attraction of Industry

In the early 1570’s, Hélie Bachelier took part in the trade of his father, Dominique Bachelier, an architect and a civil engineer.24 In 1582, Hélie followed him to Spain to repair the stone bridge in Saragossa.25 Hélie also contributed to the construction of the Pont-Neuf in Toulouse, a remarkable instance of collaboration and transmission of know-how from 1542 to 1632 (Fig. 3).26 When the “seventh pier” was built (1597–1601) he distinguished himself by designing “machines and pumps” to “remove the water and mud from the aforesaid pier.”27 Barthélémy Fraysse, a founder and gunner, supplied the necessary metal.28

His father being quite successful, Hélie Bachelier had no need to take over the family business. In fact, he embarked on a different career, and climbed the social ladder, much like the heirs of the bourgeois merchant class. A secretary in 1582, he acquired the office of contrôleur général (comptroller general) for the taillon of Languedoc, a tax levied each year to raise the pay of soldiers.29 Married in 1589 to Jeanne de Tholosani, the daughter of a juge-mage (chief magistrate), Bachelier added from then on “de” to his name.30 At the end of the century, economic opportunities were particularly attractive, and Bachelier ventured into the mining industry. In 1598 he received letters patent awarding him, for ten years, the right to:

prospect, open, and excavate all sorts of mines, gold, deposits of gold, silver, copper, tin, lead, and other metallic substances which are under the jurisdiction of the parlement of Tholose. Even those that have been formerly operated and abandoned.31

As contrôleur général du taillon of Languedoc, Hélie exploited the rich and prestigious mine of Portet d’Aspet—which had formerly been operated by Dominique Bertin—for only a few months.32 Hélie abandoned architecture and civil engineering, as well as finance, after his father’s death,33 and became involved in trade as an armorer and an ironmaster. He aimed at competing with Flanders and Milan in manufacturing arms and hoped to supply the province of Languedoc during the period of religious tension. He even had a view to taking back the town of Montauban,34 and in 1616–1617 he had a large forge and a trip-hammer built at Saint-Cyprien, on the banks of the Garonne, in order to “make arms such as muskets, harquebuses, armor, and swords, and also to beat copper.”35 In order to repay the 6,000 livres the city had lent him on this occasion, Bachelier pledged to manufacture weapons for the municipal armory. Thus, in 1618 he delivered 109 musket barrels and 645 quintals of shot to the capitouls.36 Although Toulouse and surrounding towns ordered a large quantity of arms and armor, his business never really took off, and he ended up “completely ruined” in 1627.37

Conclusion

The career paths of these two men demonstrate the increasing attraction of the metal industry from the second half of the sixteenth century on. Seen by artisans, industrial production went far beyond the scale of the town or the province of Languedoc because the economic, monetary, and political stakes of manufacture—articulated by some to gain the city’s support—possessed a kingdom-wide dimension. With a view to competing with Flanders and Milan, the aim, over and above personal achievement, was to serve the interests of the kingdom. In this respect, Bachelier seems to have been deeply impressed by Bertin’s career, and came to see the position of architect as less attractive than manufacturing arms, which had greater financial promise.
