Apprenticeship of the Painter

Abstract

“Apprenticeship of the painter” on fol. 64v identifies the oval and the cross as fundamental shapes for aspiring artists to master. The disposition of the text and its accompanying sketches suggest something of the author-practitioner’s thought process in setting down his advice. This essay situates his notion of apprenticeship in the context of model books, art treatises, and artistic training in early modern Europe. While young artists were understood to require a knowledge of geometry and anatomy, this knowledge was of a practical and embodied kind—inculcated through the pedagogical practice of copying and aided by workshop shortcuts, such as the advice about the oval and the cross offered in Ms. Fr. 640.

We know relatively little about the professional training of the anonymous author-practitioner who compiled BnF Ms. Fr. 640, although select comments he peppers across the manuscript allow us to glean a few important insights about his workshop and its organization.1 While he mentions the need for quelque garson de boutique (a shop boy) and ton garson (your boy) for the processes he describes on fols. 123v and 135v respectively, it is unclear if the author-practitioner supervised apprentices in his own workspace. Nevertheless, he clearly had opinions about the proper training of young artists. This essay focuses on the entry “Aprentissaige du paintre” (“Apprenticeship of the painter”) on fol. 64v, which contains the author-practitioner’s practical advice for the would-be painter. Unlike many other entries in the manuscript, this one was not the result of an uninterrupted flow of writing. A careful examination of the entry’s script and sketches allows a reconstruction of the author-practitioner’s thought process in putting down these recipes. This helps us attain a better understanding of how he might have conceptualized the subjects under discussion—their relevance for the painter’s craft and their connection to contemporary art treatises and instruction manuals.

The title “Apprenticeship of the painter” and the first block of text form a coherent unit that discusses the fundamentals of drawing and the importance of the oval as the basis for drawing a human face (Fig. 1). There is nothing to suggest an interruption of the author-practitioner’s flow of thought or writing. However, the style, size, and position of the script of the next block of text suggest that he added it some time after writing the first. This next section could be interpreted as a second entry in and of itself under the heading “One also presents him with these strokes & lines.” However, the size of the gap between the two paragraphs is not unlike those appearing between text blocks in other entries in the manuscript that contain multiple paragraphs, and it seems most likely that he understood this second block of text to continue the overarching subject of apprenticeship that he began with the first.

The second text block shows a much more interrupted flow of thought. The author-practitioner started by writing a capital “C,” yet he immediately changed it to an “L,” and carried on with composing his sentence: La figure de l’œuf pour visaiges (The figure of the egg for faces). Moreover, the full sentence reads, CLa figure de loeuf pour visaiges et pour les corps plies est le principal patron (The figure of the egg is the main pattern for faces and for curled-up bodies), as if it were the product of a continuous flow of thought. However, the script clearly shows that “and for curled-up bodies” was a later, interlinear addition. After the author-practitioner wrote the sentence La figure de l’œuf pour visaiges est le principal patron (The figure of the egg is the main pattern for faces), he added & la croix (& the cross), but he seems to have immediately crossed out these three words and replaced them with co{mm}e la croix est le modelle du plant droict & entier (as the cross is the model for a straight & whole figure). This change suggests that the author-practitioner did not initially associate the utility of the oval with that of the cross. He seems to have only considered the oval shape of the egg as a follow-up to the discussion of drawing the human face in the previous paragraph. It thus seems likely that the words “and for curled-up bodies” were added after he put down “as the cross is the model for a straight & whole figure,” as he was making a connection between these shapes as the two fundamental forms of the human figure. Then, perhaps, only after he finished writing all these texts, and probably also after making the drawings, he went back to the beginning and added “One also presents him with these strokes & lines,” which might be better considered an explanatory note for the both paragraphs and the drawing rather than a heading or title for an independent entry.

While the text mentions only the oval and cross as the underlying forms of the human body, there are four drawings below “Apprenticeship of the painter” (Fig. 2). Apart from a curled-up body within an oval and a standing figure on the right, there is another standing figure on the far left (not formed on the template of a cross) as well as a reclining figure stretched backwards over the arc of a partial oval.

The preceding speculative reconstruction of the author-practitioner’s flow of thought and pen suggests several points. First, the author-practitioner might not have initially planned to include drawings to illustrate “Apprenticeship of the painter” until he thought beyond the face toward the body as a whole, and, then, about the human body’s relationship to basic geometrical shapes. Second, the oval may not have been a common geometrical form associated with the entire human body at that time, and thus not as familiar to the author-practitioner as a standing figure represented by a cross. Third, although the author-practitioner only discussed the oval and the cross, he also considered it necessary to show the other two figures because “these strokes & lines”2 seemed to him foundational for training an artist: “without these strokes,” the apprentice “will never do well” (fol. 64v).

The foregoing analysis raises the following questions that this essay aims to tackle: Why did the author-practitioner make these drawings? What might the potential context for the drawings be, and from where could they have been derived? As the instructions are concerned with the apprenticeship of painters, I will address these questions in relation to the training of apprentices for the painter’s craft. Due to the schematic and spontaneous character of the sketches, it is difficult to locate precisely their source and context, thus the suggestions I offer here are neither definitive nor exhaustive.

Learning to Draw in Early Modern France

Fols. 56r–67r of Ms. Fr. 640 contain a cluster of entries related to the art of painting. A key treatise for the context of this section of the manuscript is the Livre de pourtraiture (1571) by Jean Cousin the Younger (ca.1522–1595), a didactic drawing book that aimed to teach young artists the proportions of the human body.3 The Livre de pourtraiture was a very widely circulated treatise, reprinted several times across the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries and used well into the eighteenth.4 The first folio of Ms. Fr. 640 lists a large number of names and book titles, including the name of Jean Cousin who, it is noted, “knows of the master” (fol. 1r).5 Jean Cousin the Younger’s father, Jean Cousin the Elder (1500–before 1593), published a treatise on perspective, Livre de perspective (1560), which contained mathematical and theoretical constructions of perspective. In the preface of Livre de perspective, Cousin the Elder mentioned that his son was preparing a book on drawing, which dealt with “the practice of perspective” (la practique de perspective).6 Livre de pourtraicture presents the relative proportions of various body parts as well as full male, female, and children’s figures in different postures. Cousin the Younger claimed the book intended to “describe and demonstrate” the proportions of human bodies.7 Taken together, then, the books of Jean Cousin the Elder and the Younger seem intended to form a course on art theory and practice for the aspiring painter. As will be pursued below, these two books—and their exemplification of the two poles of theory and practice—can help us understand the context of the drawings on fol. 64v of Ms. Fr. 640. I argue that the author-practitioner’s drawings, like those of Cousin the Younger, help us visualize the mode of training in an early modern painter’s workshop. A master taught by demonstrating, and an apprentice learned by copying. What the drawings show are a variety of the most basic human postures, including crouching, lying, and standing. These postures also suggest different stages of human life—a baby curled up in a womb, an adult, and a corpse—and, as the apprentice absorbed such visual templates through the practice of copying, he could put them to use later in a variety of compositions.

In his book, Cousin the Younger called himself an “outstanding geometrician” (Geometrien tres-excellent). His book also borrowed illustrations from contemporary anatomical treatises such as De humani corporis fabrica (1543) by the Brussels-born physician Andreas Vesalius (1514–1564).8 Training in geometry and anatomy were viewed in the sixteenth century as essential to a painter’s apprenticeship, and both were seen to enable a painter to create well-proportioned figures. But what did geometry and anatomy mean to a young apprentice in practice and in the (pedagogical) context of a painter’s workshop? Early modern geometry was based on the Euclidean system that assumed a small set of axioms from which many other theorems were deduced. This intellectual approach had a practical counterpart in the painter’s workshop: an artisan’s understanding of geometry was shaped through his embodied work—the skills and habits of his hand—and experience.9 Similarly, although anatomy was a systematic study of the human body within university medical faculties, it was also growing to include a practical dimension based on dissection and close observation. If we investigate the drawings on fol. 64v through the lens of daily practices of geometry and anatomy in the early modern workshop, we gain a better understanding of the origins of the author-practitioner’s intriguing conception of a painter’s apprenticeship.

Learning by Copying

According to the author-practitioner, the strokes and lines in his sketches were meant to be shown to the apprentices. What was first shown, then, is the nature of the lines themselves. The author-practitioner drew these images with a very light touch. There seems a marked difference between the pressure he put on the pen when writing the text and when making the drawings. Other images in the manuscript, such as the tools needed for tempering sand depicted on fol. 112r (Fig. 3), are made up of stronger and more calculated lines than those in these sketches accompanying “Apprenticeship of the painter” on fol. 64v. Here, he used only a few spare, spontaneous strokes to roughly define the general features of a figure. His execution of these sketches corresponds to his instructions in an entry entitled “Drawing” (“Pourtraire”) on fol. 62r. One should, he notes, first draw the outline of the subject “lightly & without any too careful work, but boldly,” so that “if you need to undo anything, you will not waste as much time as if you had elaborated it.” By drawing in this way, he claims, “you will teach yourself to be an artist.” This suggests that his drawing instructions may reflect the training given to apprentice artists. Cennino Cennini (ca. 1360–before 1427), when discussing “how drawings with the pen should be practiced” in his Il libro dell’arte, likewise advised that one should “draw lightly.”10 Similarly, the author-practitioner emphasized lightness on fol. 62r, where he advised the apprentice to draw with a light line when starting a work. Such light lines, however preliminary, were also an index of artistic ability, for, as he notes, “by a line of charcoal, masters pass judgement on their apprentices.”

Copying from model books formed an essential component of an artist’s apprenticeship.11 When the author-practitioner says on fol. 64v that “one also presents him [the apprentice] with these strokes & lines” (emphasis added), he implies that images or drawings of this sort were available in the workshop as templates and shown to apprentices for copying. Cennini devoted a chapter in Il libro dell’arte to how apprentices “should endeavor to copy and design, following a master as much as [they] can.”12 He advised them to copy the drawings of the best masters daily, and said that such practice “is no shame to [them] if the figure is good.”13 These master drawings were often compiled into model books, which were widely used in early modern painters’ workshops. These books contained figures and motifs for young artists to copy and hone their skills; they were also used to inform commissioned works, providing a repertoire of pictorial solutions for various compositional challenges. Odoardo Fialetti’s (1573–1637/38) model book Il vero modo et ordine (1608) comprised a series of progressively more complete studies of the human figure, beginning with the individual parts of the body, moving to full figures, and then proceeding to scenes with multiple people. This corresponds to the author-practitioner’s opinion on fol. 64v that an apprentice should draw “half a face, then a whole one with the ears, next the neck, then the parts of the body separately, next joined, then one figure, then two & three, finally a history.” Fialetti’s book started with a full-page plate of eyes (Fig. 4), offering a systematic progression of lines and shapes for the delineation of the eye in profile and frontal view, building complex structures from simpler starting points. A second page of eyes shows different views of each eye in a more finished state. Drawings of model eyes also appear in depictions of painter’s workshops by both Pierfrancesco Alberti (1584–1638) and Jan van der Straet or Stradanus (1523–1605). On the lower left side of Alberti’s etching (Fig. 5), a senior master is pointing to a sheet with drawings of different perspectives and types of eyes in order to instruct his apprentices. In the lower right of Stradanus’s print (Fig. 6), a young apprentice is drawing eyes of different sorts. Following the program in Fialetti’s model book, such young apprentices might move on to delineate other body parts, such as the nose, lips, and ears before moving on to full faces and finally narrative scenes with crowds of people.

Carmen Bambach makes clear that copying and drawing was a central practice in artists’ workshops.14 In The Body of the Artisan, Pamela H. Smith argues further that copying was regarded as a learned bodily habit which became a cognitive practice that could lead to knowledge and the production of effects.15 For an apprentice, then, copying was not only about training hand-eye coordination but also about imprinting images in the mind through bodily practice. For an apprentice, this would result, as Albrecht Dürer (1471–1528) expressed it, in “a measure in his eyes” (Augenmass), which would enable not just the elimination of templates but also the fertile creation of new forms.16

The Practice of Perspective

The central purpose of making a drawing before beginning to paint was to achieve the right proportions for the composition. As the author-practitioner mentioned on fol. 62r under the heading “Drawing,” after the painter finished his drawing, he should “step away from it to better judge the proportions.” The author-practitioner had much experience considering proportions. For example, in the sections of Ms. Fr. 640 dedicated to cannons and armaments, proportional relationships (between weapon parts, between caliber and charge) and conceptualizations of measure and proportion are key features of the entries. For example, on fol. 17r, the cannon-perrier is noted to have “a large ball of the King’s caliber like the others, namely of 40 lb. It bears in the breech only the width of two balls & a quarter of a ball. The front only bears the width of one ball & a third of a ball.” By comparison, small faulconets “which are under 3 quintals one gives them at least 3 balls & a twelfth part of a ball at the breech, & sometimes takes away from the front to make this addition to the breech, according to the length one gives them” (fol. 19r).

Jean Cousin the Younger’s Livre de pourtraiture, dedicated to demonstrating techniques of constructing human proportions to apprentices, represents an application of this kind of proportional calculation to human figures. Besides describing the proportions of the human figure, his book demonstrates a systematic method of foreshortening the human body in all positions by means of geometric projections. Dürer, in his pioneering work Vier Bücher von menschlicher Proportion (1528), only showed how to treat the foreshortening of the human head and foot. Cousin the Younger extended this methodology to the perspectival construction of the entire human figure, claiming his method could be extended to all postures (Fig. 7).

In his book, Cousin the Younger also included a discussion of the primary figures that underlie perspective and are thus the elements of form, including perpendicular lines, intersecting lines (which appear as a cross), angles, the straight line, and the curve (i.e., the section of a circle) (Fig. 8). Similarly, Cousin the Elder’s book had included a page dedicated to basic forms such as the line, circle, square, cross, and triangle (Fig. 9). The author-practitioner’s conceptualization of the human body as structured by a cross could have been inspired by the illustrations in Cousin the Younger’s book. It contains an image in which Cousin conceived of the human body as composed of two almost perpendicular axes, with each axis divided proportionally (Fig. 10), a depiction that bears some resemblance to the standing figure sketched on fol. 64v.

Cousin the Younger wrote that the human figure was the original basis for architectural proportions, such as in determining the height of a column or in adorning a triumphal arch.17 A tradition of mapping the proportions of human beings onto architecture had existed since at least the first century BCE, as seen in the writings of Vitruvius, which might have informed the author-practitioner’s understanding of the human figure as a cross. Vitruvius’s Ten Books on Architecture provides the ideal proportions of the human body and relates them to the ideal of symmetry in architectural form.18 The authors of early modern architectural treatises thought of the Latin cross building plan as analogous to a standing human figure. In Trattato di architettura, ingegneria e arte militare (1482), Francesco di Giorgio Martini (1439–1501) depicted such a plan, composed of circles and squares, with a human figure fit into its grid (Fig. 11).

Although invisible in Cousin the Younger’s manual, it was with the use of tools that early modern artisans constructed basic forms, perspectival constructions, and even human figures. The compass was particularly useful in this regard. Tiny pin-pricks at the center of the cannonballs illustrated on fols. 22r and 26r–27v of Ms. Fr. 640 show that even the author-practitioner used a compass in his own drawing practice. Dürer also assumed that the essence of a form was derived from the primary geometrical figures constructed through the use of drawing and measuring tools. Although images of early modern workshops do not necessarily represent reality, there is no doubt that measuring tools were used in painters’s workshops, and constructing the basic polygonal forms was an important component of apprenticeship. In the center of Pierfrancesco Alberti’s print (Fig. 5), a master is pointing with a stick toward a panel on which an apprentice is measuring the length of a line with a compass. Another master and two more young boys are looking on. By putting this crowd at the center of his etching, Alberti emphasized the importance of geometry to the training of the apprentice painter, particularly in the sense of using tools to construct geometrical forms and to proportion them correctly. Dürer’s Underweysung der Messung mit dem Zirckel und Richtscheyt (1525), often considered one of the first books north of the Alps to deal systematically with “geometry,” pays homage to Euclid in its preface. However, as the full title of the book indicates, it comprises “lessons for measuring with the compass and ruler,” which demonstrates that Dürer’s conception of geometry for artists meant the construction and accurate proportioning of polygonal forms with the common measuring tools of the workshop. For example, his instructions for constructing a regular hexagon begin:

First I shall construct a figure with six corners because it can be made without changing the opening of the compass. Take a compass and place one leg on center point a and with the other side draw a circle of the desired size, large or small. Then move the compass without changing its opening, to the periphery of the circle, and mark off this distance on it. Then continue along the periphery in the same manner.19

These written instructions rely on practical actions rather than the deduction of propositions from a set of axioms.

In Ms. Fr. 640, the author-practitioner also provides instruction in perspective (fol. 62r), not in theoretical terms but by describing the practical ways that artists use tools to construct perspective:

Perspectives in oil take a long time because of several lines that need to be done with a ruler, and for this reason one usually does them in distemper. One takes the points at one’s discretion. The main thing in this lies in having the knowledge of the point. One commonly makes two or three of them, sometimes five.

The author-practitioner concludes that “perspective is very difficult” (fol. 62v), though not in terms of its mathematical character but due to the onerous measuring practices needed to construct and master it.

Some artists constructed purpose-built instruments and tools to help them with difficult perspectives.20 An entry on fol. 62v, entitled “Perspective,” includes instruction on how to find the “point” (what we might call the “vanishing point”).21 The author-practitioner remarks that some people “lay down a ruler across the panel, then on this laid-down ruler they set the tip of another ruler, which, attached to the former by means of a screw, moves about & reaches as far as necessary without losing the point.” Another paragraph in this entry deals with how to make a plumb line.22 This entry has images accompanying the text that depict the specific tools he discusses that are used in constructing perspective (Fig. 12).

The author-practitioner’s depiction of the ruler and plumb line used for perspective alerts us to a noteworthy point: while images are not infrequent in Ms. Fr. 640, they do not appear as often in recipes concerned with painting. Of the 170 folios in Ms. Fr. 640, eighty-two have images. However, only four of the recipes with accompanying images have to do with painting. Apart from “Apprenticeship of the painter” on fol. 64v and “Perspective” on fol. 62v, another recipe on fol. 19v entitled “Mathematical figures without ruler and compass” contains an image related to the arts of drawing and painting. Its title, referring to “mathematical figures,” raises expectations for a theoretical discussion but, instead, the recipe contains a shortcut to create a symmetrical form by drawing on one side of the paper with ink and then folding the paper so that the pattern will be transferred onto the other side (Fig. 13).23 The fourth recipe is on fol. 61r. The author-practitioner uses images (Fig. 14) to illustrate the proper angle at which to hold the brush so that it casts a shadow that helps indicate the direction of the light in the image, which should not appear “too crude and too harsh.” He also specifies the angle at which a painting should be positioned in relation to the light source: not “facing the light head-on” but “half turned against it” (fol. 61r). Thus, all the recipes for the painter with accompanying images are essentially concerned with the tools and their proper handling for constructing naturalistic drawings and paintings.

A broader survey of all the images in Ms. Fr. 640 shows that they, too, are often about the appearance and function of artisanal tools. For example, fol. 112r contains a recipe related to sand casting, describing how to “temper the sand” (i.e., mix the sand with a wet binder), and the image in the margin shows the variety of tools needed for the task, such as a basin, mold, brush, pen, feather pipe, and compass (Fig. 3). Similarly, the recipes concerned with sleight of hand tricks often have images accompanying their texts; when they do, the images address the construction of special devices to achieve the deceptive sleights of hand.24 Fol. 33v, for instance, contains an entry describing how to make blood or wine issue from someone’s forehead or from a wall by means of a false- or double-bottomed vessel filled with liquid; the accompanying image shows the construction and mechanism of the device (Fig. 15).

Thus, considering the drawings on fol. 64v in the context of other images in Ms. Fr. 640 reveals that these “strokes and lines” seem to have emerged out of practical concerns and for practical ends. We begin to understand the relationship between these drawings and the hands-on mode of apprenticeship. Perspective or geometry was seen as essential to an apprentice’s training, but young painters were trained in using the tools of measurement rather than being taught perspective as a form of geometrical demonstration or of purely numerical calculation. They acquired this knowledge through practical techniques and hands-on experience.

The Practice of Anatomy

Cousin the Younger’s Livre de pourtraiture borrowed and adapted many illustrations from contemporary anatomical treatises. For example, his plate showing the proportion of a man’s torso (Fig. 16) comes from an image of one of the muscle men in Vesalius’s De humani corporis fabrica (1543) (Fig. 17). Cousin the Younger’s incorporation of anatomy and perspective in his book testifies to the fundamental importance he ascribed to both anatomy and geometry in the training of young painters.

Over the course of the sixteenth century, anatomy was emerging in medical faculties as an empirical art, one which relied on the anatomist’s use of his hands and eyes. Vesalius’s book is underpinned by the practice of dissection, and he states explicitly that the structure of the human body must be established on the basis of observation from dissections. The author-practitioner uses the word “anatomie” once in Ms. Fr. 640, by which he means an animal skeleton obtained by the process detailed on fol. 120v: “One needs to skin the animal & boil it, or else skin it & put it into good quicklime.”

If we return to the Alberti print depicting the interior of a painter’s workshop (Fig. 5), we see that even though the central group thematizes the importance of geometry—manifest in acts of measuring with tools—the activities taking place around the central group are related to the study of the human figure. Casts of fragmented body parts are displayed high on the wall. On the left, a young boy is copying after the model of a leg. To the right, an apprentice is copying after a skeleton, and a group of people are inspecting a cadaver around a dissection table. What is represented here is the view that the ability to understand and represent the human figure is to be learned by the practical skills of observing with one’s own eyes and dissecting and measuring with one’s own hands: expertise is attained by repeated copying “from life.”

By the late sixteenth century, anatomy had become an essential part of a painter’s apprenticeship. In 1589, the Antwerp engraver and print publisher Philips Galle (1537–1612) published a drawing book entitled Instruction and Fundamentals of Good Portraiture (Instruction et Fondements de Bien Pourtraire), with the subtitle “for apprenticed youth and all who feel disposed to become painters, sculptors, goldsmiths or like artisans.”25 The book contains a title page, a one-page introduction, and thirteen engravings focusing on human anatomy. The series comprises four three-print sets of a human figure seen from the front, in profile, and from behind. The four sets depict a skeleton, an écorché, a nude man, and a nude woman. A single sheet showing three nude children seen from three perspectives concludes the series.

Galle intended this book to provide a basic knowledge of anatomy for young apprentices. In his introduction, he explained his reason for publishing such a book:

for most frequently, one sees that apprentices, although long engaged in the practice of draftsmanship, nevertheless still toil in darkness, as it were, the which proceeds from an incomprehension of fundamental matters. How can one draw or paint perfectly without knowledge of the skeleton? Or how is he to understand the muscles (which can only be perceived through the skin with difficulty) if wholly unversed in anatomy?26

He observed this lack of anatomical knowledge “in many people, even in those who would take it amiss not to be ranked with the very best,” and he “therefore considered it fitting for the young to become conversant with the form and composition of the bones of the dead, and thus to sample anatomy a little.”27

Like geometry, anatomy is concerned with human proportion. The English physician and anatomist Francis Glisson (1597–1677) considered that the words “Anatome and Anatomia … signify an artificial dissection of that object in such manner as may most conduce to the perfect knowledge of the same and all its parts.”28 Anatomists therefore cut deep inside the human body in order to understand the general structure of the whole, the formation and texture of every part, and the relationship between the whole and the parts. As Andrew Cunningham shows in his study of anatomy as it was taught in the early modern university, the study of the structure of the whole includes the study of the body’s general proportion, and the study of its parts involves understanding the proportion every element holds to other parts and to the whole.29 Vesalius’s groundbreaking work, based on his empirical observation of the human body during dissection, is titled De humani corporis fabrica, namely, “the fabric of the human body.” In it, he suggests principles of proportion for the interior structure of the body. For example, he points out the proportional sizes of the first three carpal bones.30

Vesalius’s book had a significant impact on the development of medicine, and it was of great importance to doctors and surgeons. But it also greatly influenced artists. Beautifully illustrated in collaboration with Jan Steven van Calcar, the details of the body parts were meticulously represented. The Flemish anatomist himself was also very conscious about how his book might benefit artists, and he intentionally created the images so as to better serve their needs. He writes in the first paragraph of Book Two that “the membranes apparent in the face and neck of the third table and also the muscular fibers depicted, are more inclined to trouble the painter, the sculptor and the molder, to whose pursuits I wish to be of benefit.”31 He also writes that “these plates display a total view of the scheme of muscles such as only painters and sculptors are wont to consider.”32 In Book Two, Vesalius first displays the anterior of the superficial muscles of the body, and then proceeds to expose the deeper muscles layer by layer. Muscles that are intact in the previous plate are lifted away in the following plate. This certainly corresponds to the progression of the dissection, but Vesalius also bears in mind how this procedure could help the artists in a practical way. He believes that artists

should not be satisfied with an exact knowledge of the superficial muscles; they must also have a detailed acquaintance with the bones, and must make sure they are fully conversant with the function of each muscle, so that they may know when they should draw some muscle shorter, or longer, or more protuberant, or more compressed.33

In other words, the ability to gauge the length and size of human muscles, as well as their contractions, is critical for any painter who wants to realize a naturalistic human figure.

The plate Cousin the Younger used to show the proportion of a human arm is partially borrowed from Vesalius’s treatise (Fig. 18). This is Vesalius’s second plate in Book Two showing the side view of a body, with the skin, the fleshy membrane, all the superficial nerves, veins, and arteries having been cut away. The figure bears a resemblance to the leftmost striding figure in the drawings on fol. 64v. This figure has an arched back, parted legs. and outstretched arms. Vesalius’s image likely derived from a drawing made by Raphael around 1508 showing the lateral muscles of the body.34 This drawing was the basis for a plate (Fig. 19) published in the book Anatomia Carpi: isagoge breves perlucide ac uberime (1522) of the Bologna physician and anatomist Jacopo Berengario da Carpi (ca. 1465–1530), which was actually the first anatomical text augmented by anatomical illustrations not based on examples from medieval manuscripts.35

From Anatomy to Egg Shape

The arched back of the striding figure at the left of the sketches on fol. 64v is drawn by the author-practitioner as part of the lines that make up “the figure of the egg,” an oval shape. The author-practitioner shows the versatility of the oval as a basic form for constructing the human body—it defines the arched or curled back of a striding, reclining, and crouching figure. The human body was frequently conceived as a circle, and this visual analogy was commonly seen in architectural and geometrical treatises, such as the well-known drawing of Vitruvian Man by Leonardo da Vinci (1452–1519). Indeed, the curled up figure in the egg shape on fol. 64v brings to mind other drawings by Da Vinci based on his observation of a fetus cut from the cadaver of a pregnant woman. His drawing of a fetus in a breech position within an opened-up uterus bears some resemblance to the drawing on fol. 64v (Fig. 20). In the other studies, Leonardo depicts the fetus from different angles. But from whatever angle the fetus is portrayed, its body is always curled into an oval or a circle (Fig. 21), just as the author-practitioner notes that “the egg is the main pattern … for bent bodies” (fol. 64v). Leonardo’s drawings are found in a six-hundred-folio manuscript codex that has been in the possession of the British monarchs since the seventeenth century. But a textbook by Jacob Rüff (1500–1558) with the name De conceptu et generatione hominis (1554) had a much larger circulation and a wider audience.36 A woodcut from Rüff’s book also shows the coming child in a more or less naturalistic pose, curled up within an egg-shaped uterus (Fig. 22). Similarly, the egg-like curling of an infant body is twice depicted in the illustration in a 1582 pamphlet of a prodigy—the calcified fetus extracted from the womb of a ninety-year-old woman from Sens upon her death (Fig. 23). This image shows the child curled inside the uterus, still curled with parts of the uterus when it has been extracted, and extended for display. Over the course of the sixteenth century, medical practitioners took a greater interest in the anatomy of women; as a result, an increasing number of female bodies were opened up in public dissections, which were becoming more common.37 As Alberti’s print shows (Fig. 5), dissection was also considered an essential part of training in the artist’s workshop, and both men’s and women’s bodies were dissected; indeed, it is a woman being dissected and inspected the frontispiece to Vesalius’s De fabrica.38

Finally, the reclining figure in the center of the sketches on fol. 64v could be a figure in the genre of a “sleeping Venus,” but it might also be a corpse. It might be male or female, dead or alive. The figure stretches itself out from the left side of the oval, bridging the curled figure with the cross. The inclusion of this reclining figure not only adds to the variety of the drawings depicted but also makes it possible to interpret these drawings as a meaningful set. On the left is a fetus in the womb and on the right is a standing male adult. The figure in the middle can bring these figures together into a series setting forth the compositional basis for depicting different types of bodies—baby, woman, and man—or different stages of life—newborn, adult, and corpse. The author-practitioner presents the apprentice the geometric building blocks with which to compose a variety of human figures. Thus, these spontaneous and lightly-sketched foundational “strokes & lines” on fol. 64v can inform an infinite number of human shapes and poses.

Conclusion

Later editions of Jean Cousin the Younger’s book include a subtitle: la vraye science de la pourtraicture (“the true science of portraiture”). This characterization of Cousin’s demonstration of human proportions as a “science,” implies a kind of certain knowledge about the human body that links it with theoretical treatises.39 However, Cousin’s book is characterized by a tension between theory and practice. He calls himself a “painter and geometrician,” the book deals with perspective yet focuses on its “practice,” and Cousin combines mathematical constructions with illustrations from the field of anatomy, which had evolved to understand the human figure empirically. His intended training for young apprentices, combining geometry and anatomy, may seem scholarly at first glance, yet it was, in fact, deeply rooted in workshop practice and dependent upon the empirical work and publications of his contemporaries.

In Ms. Fr. 640, the author-practitioner talks about “mathematics” in terms of how to make and use tools for perspective construction. He also mentions anatomy, but here he is concerned entirely with the practical techniques of obtaining an animal skeleton. Inside an early modern artisan’s workshop, then, geometry and anatomy can be understood as practically oriented bodily activities. Fol. 64v is thus about the learning process of a young apprentice: an eye, a nose, a face, a figure, a crowd—the apprentice proceeds from copying a simple pattern to constructing a complex one, absorbing all the basic components of human figures along the way. Initially, he uses a compass and ruler to construct bodies and perspective; over time, he will “become an artist” (fol. 62v), without need of tools. These practices are the elements of geometry and anatomy that are important for apprentices. They not only develop an eye for the fundamental forms of human figure, but also form the everyday experience of apprenticeship. “These strokes & lines” that the master “also presents him,” and the apprentice’s bodily practice of copying and measuring based on these models and templates, constitute the terms in which geometry and anatomy are translated in the workshop into hands-on practices of imitation and observation.
