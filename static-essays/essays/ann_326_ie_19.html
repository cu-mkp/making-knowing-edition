Making Ms. Fr. 640

Abstract

A close physical and visual examination of Ms. Fr. 640, in terms of codicology (material structure) and paleography (handwriting), can tell us much about how it was created and handed down to its present location, and thus allows us to better understand its contents. Contrary to the first impression it might give the reader of a smoothly coherent whole written from front to back cover, it is instead the result of complex intellectual and technical movement back and forth between experimenting and writing, both at the time of its composition and later when it became embalmed as a valuable library item, right down to the present day in its most recent avatar as a digital facsimile, edition, and translation.

A close physical and visual examination of Ms. Fr. 640, in terms of codicology (material structure) and paleography (handwriting), can tell us much about how it was created and handed down to its present location, and thus allows us to better understand its contents. Contrary to the first impression it might give the reader—especially when leafing through it in digital form—of a smoothly coherent whole written from front to back cover, it is instead the result of complex intellectual and technical movement back and forth between experimenting and writing, both at the time of its composition and later when it became embalmed as a valuable library item, right down to the present day in its most recent avatar as a digital facsimile, edition, and translation. By examining it from the outside to the inside, we can attempt to turn back the clock, as in an archeological excavation, in order to understand the gradual development of the volume as we now know it. Beyond that, the object must be envisaged as the material embodiment of intentions that, as usual in any kind of research (or more widely in human experience), may have unfolded in ways that were far from linear, and therefore can be difficult to follow, but deserve to be retraced as far as possible (Vid. 1).

From Outside to Inside the Volume

The volume is currently protected by an elegant seventeenth-century red morocco binding, with gilt decoration in the style known as “Du Seuil” (framed by two sets of triple fillets, the inner frames with blank escutcheons at the corners), and all edges gilt. In the middle of the covers it bears the arms of Philippe de Béthune (1565–1649), Baron then Count of Selles and Charost: argent, a fess gules, in chief a label of three points of the second, i.e., white with a broad horizontal strip in red and, at the top, an indented horizontal motif, also in red. This is surrounded by the two collars of the royal orders (St. Michael and the Holy Spirit) (Fig. 1). The ornate gilt spine is inscribed with the monogram PP (Philippe-Pierre) in a laurel wreath, repeated in each of the seven compartments separated by raised bands, and with the rather uninformative title “Choses diverses” (Fig. 2). Inside the covers, the pastedowns are of marbled paper, and two entirely blank endleaves in seventeenth-century paper are found at both ends of the volume.

Philippe de Béthune was the youngest brother of the famous minister of King Henry IV, Maximilien de Béthune, better known, from 1606, as the Duke of Sully. Philippe led a prestigious military career in the Wars of Religion, under Henry III and Henry IV. This was followed by administrative and diplomatic functions: appointed a member of the Council of State in 1599, he was sent the next day on an embassy to James VI of Scotland and then, more importantly, to Pope Clement VIII (1601–1605). During his years in Rome, he developed a special taste for Italian painting, with a particular interest in Caravaggio (whom he apparently helped out of prison in 1603), and for book collecting. Considered a highly accomplished and well-educated gentleman, Philippe was later chosen by Henry IV to be the governor of his newborn second son, the duke of Orléans (1607–1611). Philippe’s last mission (1624–1630) was once again to Rome. During his lifetime he gathered an impressive collection of art, books, and manuscripts, which he passed on to his son Hippolyte. Hippolyte (1603–1665), born in Rome and the godson of Pope Clement VIII, likewise a warrior and a diplomat, chose to offer the collection, which had grown to some 2,000 volumes in the meantime, to King Louis XIV in December 1662, after turning down an enormous offer of 300,000 livres tournois from that queen of collectors, Christina of Sweden. The “Collection de Béthune” is one of the great collections in the Manuscripts Department of the Bibliothèque nationale de France. It consists in large part of original letters and manuscripts of a historical and political nature, but also comprises a number of technical works.1

How and when the volume Ms. Fr. 640 came into the hands of Philippe de Béthune remains unknown, similarly to the rest of his collection:2 Habent sua fata libelli, and such an avid collector of manuscripts would have relied on a wide network. At least the binding can be dated with some precision. The paper used for the endleaves is watermarked with the arms and monogram of Anne of Austria, queen of France from 1615 to 1643. Philippe de Béthune was made a knight of the royal orders in 1619. The “tinctures” (colors) of his arms, as specified above and well known from other sources, do not follow the conventions that came into use in the late 1630s, when a coherent hatching system was introduced for heraldic engravings. Instead “gules” is represented in two different ways, with the “fess” cross-hatched and the “label” solid. So the binding was made after 1619, and presumably (at any rate the engraved stamp for the arms) before circa 1640.

Inside the cover, the manuscript shows a treatment no less typical of Béthune bindings (see Vid. 1). The original gatherings were cut up into separate leaves, which were then re-margined by gluing separate paper strips along all edges, before sewing them back together into quires.3 This technique served at least two purposes: different volumes could be made the same size and thus neatly aligned on the collector’s bookshelves. More importantly, the binder could confidently trim the pages without damaging any writing, especially marginal notes that extended too far towards the edges (top, sides, and bottom). The only difficulty was that in some cases, the paper strips needed to cover part of the writing, just a few letters here and there; but this could be mostly avoided provided there were a few millimeters of blank space on at least one side of each edge.4

Separately mounting the original pages also destroyed any evidence of the original codicological structure. The current gatherings are made up of ternions (3 bifolia = 6 leaves = 12 pages), except for the last two, which are binions (2 bifolia = 4 leaves = 8 pages). In each bifolium, both leaves probably share the paper strip at the back, which was then folded and used for stitching the quire together, but that is difficult to ascertain without breaking the binding.

Not knowing whether the current structure might have disturbed the original sequence could be especially problematic for Ms. Fr. 640, considering its highly complex textual stratification (as we will see). There are no signs of quire signatures or catchwords, such as those commonly used to preserve page and quire sequence in manuscripts and printed books. What we do find is consistent foliation, applied twice. The earlier foliation starts on what is currently the third leaf, numbered 1 (not taking into account the two preliminary blank leaves). Throughout the volume these numbers are often covered, partly or entirely, by the paper strips, but it is impossible to decide whether they existed before the Béthune binding was made, or if they belong to an early phase of that same operation. At any rate, they are in a hand not found elsewhere in the manuscript and in a style common in the seventeenth century. The second foliation, written on top of the paper strips, starts on the first folio, so that it is constantly two leaves ahead of the earlier numbers. In this edition, reference is always made to the latter foliation (Fig. 3). The different foliation and the quite different content of fols. 1r–2v and 170v in comparison to the rest of the manuscript also raises the question of whether the lists of titles, authors, and jotted medical recipes on these four leaves were originally conceptualized as belonging to the unvarying series of recipes and processes that make up fols. 3r–170r. Perhaps the binder had originally thought of using what are essentially scribbled lists as endleaves, rather than part of the body of the work.

In order to assess the original structure of the manuscript, the main evidence lies in the paper itself, thanks to the presence of watermarks. The whole manuscript consists of paper bearing a single watermark, that of Nicolas Lebé: a large letter B (standing for “Le Bé”) on a crowned escutcheon, with the name inscribed on a scroll underneath (Fig. 4, Fig. 5). Variants of this watermark are known during the period 1561–1598.5 The Lebé were a prominent paper-making family from Troyes in Champagne, whose production could be found all over France, though more sporadically in the Midi.

Is it possible to tell whether the binding preserved the original page sequence? The best that can be done is to ascertain if the distribution of watermarks follows a regular pattern. Within the paper mold, watermarks are usually placed at a distance of ¼ of the total width of the sheet, so that in a regular folio volume such as Ms. Fr. 640—where a full sheet is folded over just once to make a bifolium—they are entirely visible in the middle of one leaf in two.6

In order to gain a detailed overview of the whole volume, we first transformed the sequence of pages into a string of numbers, differentiated according to the presence or absence of a watermark. The string could then be tentatively broken up into equal segments, according to whether the quires would have contained 4, 5, or 6 bifolia (the most usual numbers), and shifting the boundaries of these segments until every watermarked leaf could be joined to an unmarked leaf: that is, joined symmetrically across a central line representing the central fold of each quire. This was done as a practical experiment with the participants of the first Making and Knowing Text Workshop in 2014, and produced an interesting result: the original quires were certainly quinions (5 bifolia = 10 leaves = 20 pages), but with gaps representing 22 leaves in total (of which possibly 4 joined = 2 bifolia) that had been removed from the volume at an undetermined stage. Additionally, the first and last quires had only three bifolia instead of five (Fig. 6).

Thus, the binding does not appear to have caused any permutations, at least within single quires (displacing entire quires is another accident that can occur in binding). But one cannot tell when and why the missing leaves were torn or cut out. There are at least two possibilities that come to mind: either they were removed by the author, maybe because of mistakes, or the binder left them out after cutting up the original quires, perhaps because they were blank.

Some missing leaves could be placed in more than one position within a given quire. Regardless, it is possible to reach a reconstruction where all would have been located between self-contained pages (i.e., with no text carried over from one page to the next), so that complete entries of the text could theoretically have been left out without leaving any noticeable traces. This is just a cautionary observation, since there is no further evidence to suggest any loss of text (but for one exception, see below). A potentially more intriguing hypothesis is that the pages were blank because of a non-sequential writing process: Ms. Fr. 640 is now a relatively solid block of writing, but still with occasional blanks, and it is far from impossible that it contained a number of totally blank leaves before the Béthune binding.

The Text

That leads us to the process of writing the text, and to the hands who wrote it: the first and last leaves, as often, are among the most heterogeneous elements of the volume. But once their relationship to the body of the work has been put into focus, they offer precious evidence of the circumstances in which the whole manuscript was produced. The contents can be briefly summarized here, just to provide a general sense of the textual structure.

Fol. 1r contains a short list of three names and addresses; these are followed by a longer list of authors and books (ancient and modern), written in two columns and in several batches, mainly in small French (gothic) cursive, although the last two lines but one stand out in a large italic hand. The rest of fols. 1 and 2 is generally blank, with isolated notes, mainly medical and bibliographical, at the top of each page.

The bulk of the text, from fol. 3r, is the collection of recipes, observations, and experiments that kept the Making and Knowing team busy for six years.

The last page (fol. 170v), curiously, appears to be upside down. At the bottom, which becomes the top when flipped, we find three short notes on the management of lands belonging to the Ouvrier family in 1578–1579, namely at St. Frajou near Toulouse; the Ouvrier, as is discussed in the essay by Colin Debuiche and Sarah Munoz, were a prominent family of merchants in Toulouse.7 These notes are followed by medical and bibliographical notes not too different from those on the opening pages, and in similar scripts.

In material terms, the last page tells us that the volume was originally destined for another use: the author-practitioner recycled a partially or almost entirely blank volume used by the Ouvrier family (or one of their agents), which suggests he might have been connected with them. The bibliographical notes in a larger script at the bottom of the page, after two paragraphs of practical recipes, are similar to those at the bottom of fol. 1r. This suggests a consistent chronological sequence: the author-practitioner used the last page first, then flipped the whole volume (which must already have been at least stitched together if not properly bound), and started compiling his recipes and experiments on fol. 3r, where he inscribed a cross to indicate the beginning, as was common practice. Roughly at the same time he must have added a couple of largely written bibliographical notes at the bottom of the last leaf. Later on, this page was gradually filled in using a smaller script.

The Ouvrier notes also offer an essential chronological endpoint: the author-practitioner began to use the volume not before 1579. Further clues to dating the composition and writing of the text may be provided by the date of 2 September 1581 on fol. 9v in the entry entitled “Waste Book” (Brouillart) (which provides an example of a dated entry in a merchant’s waste book). The publication date of some of the book titles listed on fols. 1r and 2r can be identified, and these largely cluster before or in the year 1581, indicating perhaps that at least this list was compiled in 1581.8

Scripts and Hands

The variety of scripts apparent on these first and last pages raises the question of the different hands active in the manuscript. The main writer (Hand A) is clearly the author-practitioner, rather than a scribe in his service, considering the whole process of composing the text, which includes laying out headings, writing in recipes, processes, experiments, observations, all frequently accompanied by innumerable corrections and additions. He uses two distinct scripts: the body text is written in a short round cursive rooted in French tradition, with essentially gothic letterforms; headings and Latin words are in a larger italic script. Many writers commonly alternated both scripts in the same manner in the late sixteenth century. The author-practitioner’s hand is fully competent, fast and regular, but not that of a professional scribe. Fols. 2v, 157v, and 161r contain three passages in highly cursive handwriting that defies identification (and in part interpretation) at first sight. But content and context suggest this is actually the author-practitioner himself (i.e., Hand A), albeit in speed-writing mode—and possibly at a later date than the rest of the manuscript (Hand A’ ) (Fig. 7).

Four other hands appear in different parts of the manuscript (Fig. 8):

Hand B: on fol. 9v, an entry showing how information should be entered in merchants’ account books is in a reasonably elegant italic hand, perhaps (also based on content and language) that of an unidentified Toulouse merchant;

Hand C: on fol. 20r, the recipe for making millas, a Toulouse delicacy, is in a different French hand, similar in quality to that of the author-practitioner, although more elaborate in style; maybe the hand of an obliging acquaintance who knew the recipe (Hand C);

Hand D: on fols. 57r–58r and 73r–79v, a series of techniques for coloring, varnishing, and gilding wood, bone, horn, and metals is in a rather more sophisticated hand, yet contains a number of uncertainties about terminology, as well as gaps in the script, apparently left blank to be filled in later, for example on fol. 58r, where a gap is left after “Les carna{ti}ons et [gap]....” This individual left gaps for words presumably to be inserted on fols. 57v, 58r, 73r, 75v, and 79r (with one term inserted in a different hand on fol. 73r). On fols. 77v, 78r, and 79v, the writer made faulty attempts to understand what in context is clearly meant to be the resin sandarac. He made a couple of tries, writing Sang de Rai and Sang da Rag which was corrected in a different hand (probably that of the author-practitioner) to Sang Da Rac (and Sangdarac on fol. 79v). On fol. 78r, Hand D also wrote trois (three) for bois (wood). These various irregularities suggest that the author-practitioner had these entries copied, perhaps from a text that was difficult to read, maybe by a professional scribe, or he dictated them to someone who was not familiar with the materials or was working in a noisy environment.

Hand E: fol. 170v, notes on the management of lands belonging to the Ouvrier family in 1578–1579.

Composition of the Manuscript

Leafing through Ms. Fr. 640, even before reading the contents, makes it materially obvious that this is not, and was not planned to be, a continuous, sequential work, written from the first page to the last. By combining material observations and content analysis, it is possible to gain some insight into the overall genesis of the manuscript, which largely would seem to reflect the development of the author-practitioner’s activity in the workshop. Nevertheless, the pristine state of the manuscript, with only a few small stains or possible traces of dirt, indicates it was hardly kept open and updated in the midst of molten metals and smoky furnaces. Rather, the author-practitioner seems to have moved back and forth between workshop and study: even when documenting his own experiments, he must have relied on his immediate memory and maybe on rough notes to write his entries.

The pages are almost entirely filled with text: mainly in the form of uninterrupted entries dealing with specific topics, under explicit headings; but also, in many cases, with marginal additions also in the author-practitioner’s hand, albeit smaller and often more cursive in execution. Nevertheless, short blank spaces can occur between entries, mostly at the bottom of a page, but occasionally also at the top or in the middle.9

Within single entries, there are frequent changes in handwriting (not in the hand): differences in the color of the ink, the cut of the quill, or the size and formality of writing. Usually occurring between sentences or at least consistent parts of sentences, such variations are typical of writing in successive stints, returning to the same entry with additional information, experiments, and afterthoughts, such as on fol. 10r or fol. 125r. The process becomes particularly clear when the end of an entry collides with, or even wraps around, the following heading. More subtle variations occur in the layout, including in justification, alignment, and spacing of lines, or distances between headings and body text.

The marginal texts, which at first sight could be considered notes to the main text, are in fact not functionally distinct from such addenda, and should rather be considered a continuation of the same process after the author-practitioner had run out of space at the end of an entry. Only in very few instances did he write in the margins when there was still blank space left at the end of the entry.

On the whole, the standard writing process seems to have been based on headings followed by a paragraph plus a varying amount of blank space based on a rough estimate of potential addenda. The author-practitioner returning to the same topic in separate entries with similar headings represents further steps in the same workflow, on a different scale, and often distant from each other.

A difficult question is whether variations in ink and handwriting can only signal different strata of writing within a page, or if they could be plotted onto a more general timeline, as clues to the relative dating of entries and pages across the volume. It is tempting to ascribe the neater pages to the earliest stage, and the few extremely cursive notes to the author-practitioner’s latest afterthoughts, with everything else in between much more difficult to disentangle.

This leads to another problematic question, one we may never answer, about the scale, or the granularity, according to which the volume was originally planned. A degree of randomness is not inconsistent with the style of books of secrets, even in published form. Still, the largely chaotic composition of Ms. Fr. 640 seems to reflect a non-sequential workflow on the part of the author-practitioner, exploring many subjects at the same time. He may arguably have begun to discuss different topics on separate pages, with what at first were possibly blank pages in between to be filled in gradually and more or less consistently, by trade and/or type of material: paints, varnishes, and the art of the painter; molding and foundry techniques; metal processes; arms and artillery (their production and use); animals and plants; and medicine. Those perhaps initial entries, as argued in the previous paragraph, seem to correspond to some longer entries, or sequences of entries, which are frequently written in a neatly rounded hand in medium brown ink, especially at the beginning of the manuscript and in many places where a new entry begins at the top of a page. On the contrary, the end of an entry at the bottom of a page is often cramped, suggesting that the top of the following page might have been taken up by pre-existing text.

Just as gradual additions to single entries led to subsequent overflow, the larger divisions would have inevitably grown out of control, as the author-practitioner continued compiling and experimenting in various directions, and inserting entries on different topics in all available blank spaces, to the extent that the initial order, if any, became largely blurred. Pages containing short entries on diverse topics sometimes show variations in handwriting typical of cumulative additions over time; others, conversely, can be just as diverse in content but homogeneous in form, suggesting the information was acquired simultaneously and possibly from the same source (or just copied out at the same time from rough notes).

Finally, variations in overall density may suggest shifts in the author-practitioner’s interests over time: an ever-increasing interest in molding and casting in particular, leading him down every rabbit hole in search of better “sand” and more exciting objects to mold or cast from life, would explain the accumulation of material in the latter part of the volume, where the margins are largely saturated with additions, and single entries seem to have been continued in leapfrog mode, by moving to the next available blank whenever coming up against a pre-existing entry on a different topic.10 Exploiting the last pages to such an extent also confirms the volume was ready-stitched and presumably bound, otherwise, it would have been simple enough to add further quires to accommodate the additions.

How the author-practitioner could find his way about his book is somewhat mysterious. In a few instances, impelled by lack of space to skip several pages while writing on a given topic, he resorted to rough cross-referencing, just by indicating the number of pages the reader should turn to find the next entry—the longest such leap being 15 pages—or even by an approximate point in the volume, such as “towards the last third of this book.”11 This also confirms that he never used foliation, and therefore could not have built an index, but must have relied entirely on his memory of the book to locate any previous entries.

Publication as a Goal of the Text?

How close was Ms. Fr. 640 to being transformed into an actual “book” for a wider audience rather than for the use or enjoyment of a single writer/reader? A few pages toward the end of the volume, together with the first and last pages, shed some light on the question.

Fol. 169r contains a summary of subject headings, some of which are annotated with “mis” (put) and “veu” (seen). This looks like a to-do list, with the annotations perhaps indicating whether the process of the subject heading was “put” into a text or “seen.” This list might have been placed here at any stage, even before the previous pages were written upon. In fact, it might have been deliberately placed at what was to be the end of the manuscript, followed by two potential endleaves (fols. 169r–170v), but these also, like many others, were finally filled up by the overflow of never-ending additions on metalwork. The list of headings could refer either to adding entries on various topics to Ms. Fr. 640, or to lifting those entries from the manuscript and transferring them to another copy, in preparation of a definitive compilation. The one argument in favor of the second hypothesis lies in the presence, at short distance, of two more passages that likely point to the intention of making a proper book.

Two lengthy entries, on fols. 162r and 166v–167r bear the intriguing heading: Pour la Boutique (“For the workshop”). The first is dedicated to excerpts from Herodotus (pages 1–5 of the Latin translation published by Henri Estienne) on Greek trade and metalwork, followed by a metaphorical paragraph in which the author-practitioner expresses hopes of trading knowledge from his “boutique” in exchange for the “secrets” of his “benevolent readers”:

As small peddlers lay open small wares in order to buy richer ones & to profit more and more, so I, from a desire to learn, am exposing what little is in my workshop to have in receive, through a common commerce of letters, much rarer secrets from my benevolent readers (fol. 162r).

This passage sheds crucial light on the second entry (166v–167r), an otherwise mysterious collection of largely self-justificatory expressions, which make sense as the author-practitioner’s advance response to anyone reproaching him for disclosing valuable technical and professional “secrets.” Thus the heading “Pour la Boutique” most plausibly points to elements gathered for use in what was to become the front matter of a book. The reference to the “benevolent reader” in particular is typical of a prefatory context.

Moreover, the first line on fol. 166v, “Si quelque chose est obmis par les moissonneurs…” constitutes a direct link to fol. 1r, where a different phrasing of the same expression is also found. This, together with the excerpts from Herodotus, strongly suggest that the vast bibliography jotted down on the endleaves was a reading list, a list of potential sources of literary material that could be exploited to enhance the literary status of a publication in the eyes of the benevolent reader. How far the author-practitioner went down that road will remain unknown, but his notes on Herodotus, which reveal an insecure command of Latin and proceed no further than page 5, raises the suspicion that he had carved out for himself more than he could manage.

As to the use of La Boutique, representing a place where goods are both produced and marketed, it perhaps was the title the author-practitioner had in mind for his prospective book. He even uses it in direct juxtaposition with another famous title, by favorably comparing his Boutique, which deals with practical activities of all sorts, to the Tabula Cebetis, a classic work on contemplative life from the 1st or 2nd century B.C., mentioned offhand as an apology for idlers: “Tablet of Cebes, idle, but the workshop represents all things active.”12 And a fitting and original title La Boutique would have been for a book of secrets, albeit echoing other titles of the period that made metaphorical and humorous use of archetypical places—it brings to mind the successful works published in the 1580s by Tommaso Garzoni (1549–1589): Il Theatro…, L’Hospidale…, La Sinagoga…, and of course La piazza universale di tutte le professioni del mondo.13 Whether the author-practitioner gave up on his book, lacking the literary skills (and connections?), or died before he could bring his project to fruition, there is no trace of a book ever published under that title.
