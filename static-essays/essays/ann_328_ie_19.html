The Physical Construction of Ms. Fr. 640

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/ar0p-h544
← Back to Essays

Abstract

Important clues about manuscripts can be inferred through analysis of their bindings. This essay not only examines Ms. Fr. 640’s current binding, but also attempts to determine its original construction and appearance by comparing it with other French manuscripts available in the collections of Columbia University Libraries, and by reconstructing a model of its binding and gatherings using a textblock made of “watermarked” paper following Marc Smith’s collation.

The text of BnF Ms. Fr. 640 was written some time in the late sixteenth or early seventeenth century in a blank book, and its leaves have been preserved in a tooled leather-bound volume dating from the first half of the seventeenth century. The manuscript was bound in this form after the binder cut its spine folds and remargined each leaf, creating new gatherings in place of the original bifolia. In her introductory essay, Pamela H. Smith describes the material form of the volume and the textual and material evidence for the author-practitioner’s intention in creating the manuscript, likely compiling existing notes and recipes into a continuous text in this codex form.1 In studying the manuscript as a physical object, a modern reader is several steps removed from the actions of this creator in putting pen to paper: leaves excised from the original binding, formed into new dimensions through the extension of the margins, bound into a new volume with different characteristics, and, for many of its readers, studied only through digital images. What can we predict about the form of the original binding, and how might those predictions contribute to our understanding of the writing process, and through it, this text?

Evidence Provided by the Paper

Although the leaves from the book’s earlier binding have been separated from one another, evidence remains of the manuscript’s former state. Two studies of the physical book provide substantial information about the provenance of this work and the form it held when the text of Ms. Fr. 640 was written in its pages. The watermark study conducted by Marc Smith has elucidated the relationship of the conjoint leaves. This masterful study of the book’s collation, using only the evidence of the relative placement of the watermarks, achieves the recreation of the connections between the formerly conjoint leaves, and thus the makeup of the quires.2 The evidence of the last folio, fol. 170v, shows that the paper leaves originated from a reused bound blank book,3 and through this knowledge we can get closer to understanding the form of the book at the time the manuscript was written. Colin Debuiche has explained the context of the manuscript within the collection of Philippe de Béthune, now held in the Bibliothèque nationale de France.4 The conclusions reached by Colin Debuiche and Marc Smith identify the leaves of Ms. Fr. 640 as having once been part of a bound account book, blank except for a single page, now fol. 170v, that was later rebound into a gold-tooled leather binding fit for a nobleman’s library and decorated with his arms.

The creation of the original account book began with its paper. The leaves of Ms. Fr. 640 consistently bear the watermark of Nicolas Lebé (1550–1605),5 papermaker in Troyes.6 The wide distribution of the products of this paper mill, particularly for use in stationery bindings, that is, “the bindings found on books made to be written in,”7 are amply documented. Lebé’s watermark has been recorded in other bound manuscripts such as the Elizabethan English manuscript programs for a collection of allegorical paintings;8 a manuscript by La Boétie dated before 1585,9 and Bodleian MS Mus. Sch. E423, an English music partbook dated to the late sixteenth century;10 though also in printed books.11 The appearance of Lebé’s paper stock in English books and manuscripts is not surprising because most white paper used in England at this time was imported from France,12 but it serves as a reminder that the use of a paper was not limited to the area of its production.

When Ms. Fr. 640 was rebound for Philippe de Béthune, the spine folds were trimmed, and the individual leaves were guarded and remargined with new paper (Vid. 1). As Marc Smith points out, the added margins would allow the book to be trimmed after re-sewing and would also provide the means for making a volume the same height as others on a shelf.13 Trimming, or ploughing, a book’s edges after sewing was an expected step in any finished inboard binding during the hand press period and would have been a necessary prelude to gilding the textblock’s edges. Books began to be shelved upright in libraries during the sixteenth century, and the preference of some book collectors for books of regular height after this transition has been documented: Samuel Pepys recorded in his diary in January 1665 that he had directed for the spines of the books in his library to be gold-tooled in a uniform manner; one year later he installed bookcases for his library that incorporated wooden blocks serving to elevate the smaller books on the shelves to a uniform height with the taller ones, and he further had these blocks gilded to match the spines of the books on the shelves.14 In addition to preserving the edges of the original manuscript leaves when the rebound volume was ploughed, and potentially unifying the dimensions of a bound work within a shelved collection, remargining was a common practice that could be used to address damage to text leaves, to provide space for marginal annotations, and to enlarge page dimensions when, in the later seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, “large paper” became synonymous with luxury.

Stationery Bindings

In spite of the actions on the part of the manuscript’s seventeenth-century binder that have eradicated evidence of the previous binding, simply knowing that the leaves were contained in a stationery binding provides important information. In the world of book production in the hand press period, the binding of blank books to be used for writing formed a separate line of the trade from bindings of printed books.15 In France, as elsewhere in Europe at this time, printed books were frequently sold in unbound sheets, and they would be bound by relieurs-doreurs de livres (binders and gilders of books) through a series of steps. These included folding and beating the unbound printed sheets with a hammer to reduce type impressions; supplying endsheets; sewing; rounding and backing (Fig. 1); ploughing to trim the textblock edges, which might then be colored or decorated; working endbands; often–though not always–preparing and lacing on boards of wood or paper board; covering; and possibly finishing with tooling and other decorative techniques.

In contrast, stationery bindings, which are formed of bound quires of blank paper and prepared for use as account books, music manuscripts, and notebooks, could be made by marchands-papetiers (stationers), who also sold materials such as inks, writing implements, prepared parchment, and blank paper in sheets. Stocks of blank paper sheets would be formed into quires by gathering a specific number (four, five, or six were common gatherings), folding them in half and sewing through them. Alternatively, an individual handmade sheet of paper could be folded multiple times to create a four- or eight-leaf quire, as the outer folded edges would be trimmed off in later stages of binding. A twentieth-century textbook on stationery binding instructs that the weight of the paper itself must be a determining factor in how many leaves are used to form a gathering, with fewer sheets per gathering when the paper is thickest.16 This sensible guideline balances the need for robust sections, so that paper will resist tearing against the sewing thread, with the need for fluid motion and flat opening that thicker sections might not afford to the binding. Though textual evidence of stationers’ methods of practice in earlier periods is lacking, such considerations are likely to have similarly influenced their choice of how many bifolia would be used to form a section. Statutes of 1599 and 1660 for the papetiers in Paris established that these merchants, though they were not master bookbinders, were permitted to bind books if they did not round them (Fig. 2), but left them with unrounded spines (dos quarré) (Fig. 3).17 In spite of the distinctions in roles implied by these statutes, individual practitioners might in fact be simultaneously booksellers, bookbinders, parchment makers, or practitioners of many other trades with less connection to book production.18

Knowledge of the characteristics of a stationery binding may support certain assumptions about the original binding of Ms. Fr. 640. When a book is destined to be used for writing or drawing upon, it is desirable for the binding to manifest certain qualities. The spine will ideally open easily, because restraint in this area will cause the leaves to flex more, making it difficult to write evenly, and will reduce access to the full page in the area of the gutter. Spines that are not heavily rounded therefore usually have an advantage in being able to open easily and fully. A durable binding that will withstand frequent use would also be desirable, so that parchment, an animal skin that could be prepared with a tough surface, was often chosen to cover ordinary blank books. A style that became closely associated with stationery bindings employed a parchment or leather cover that had leather bands affixed across the spine via decorative lacing (Fig. 4, Fig. 5). It utilized tackets to attach the sewn textblock to the cover (Fig. 6). This style, often referred to as a “ledger binding,” was in use by the early fifteenth century and continued through the mid-nineteenth century.19

Many other stationery binding styles utilized parchment as a cover (Fig. 7) that could be laced or tacketed onto a textblock and could also be internally reinforced using cartonnage or boards. In its simplest form, less time is required to bind a book with a limp parchment cover, because it can be easily folded to fit the textblock and laced on using the slips of the sewing supports. For reasons of strength and economy, parchment was frequently used to cover stationery bindings of all kinds—to the extent that the stationery binders of London came to be known as “Vellum Binders.”20 Laced-case limp parchment bindings came into use in France and Italy after the 1470’s and were in widespread use throughout Europe in the sixteenth century.21 Additional advantages of a laced-case limp parchment structure are that it renders the book lighter than a leather-covered inboard binding, and that it is easy to protect the leaves by attaching ties and/or extending the cover’s fore-edges with folds or a flap that will enclose the textblock’s edge when the book is not in use, thus offering more protection if it is carried about in the course of use and writing. The works of a contemporary artist, Claude Vignon (1593–1670), notably his depictions of St. Jerome in his Study (ca.1620–1630) and St. Ambrose (Fig. 8), suggest the ubiquity of these books in late sixteenth and early seventeenth century France, as they display these laced-case limp parchment bindings in abundance.

There are certainly many surviving leather-bound and decoratively-tooled stationery bindings that are structurally similar to bindings found on printed books of the same date (Fig. 9). Without any physical evidence, it is impossible to say that Ms. Fr. 640 was not originally bound in a leather-covered binding such as might have been produced for sale by a French stationer in the sixteenth century. For example, the inventory in the 1557 will of Jehan Le Charron, a marchand-papetier of Paris, included about 500 blank books bound in parchment and an unspecified but apparently smaller number of blank books “reliés en bazenne verte et rouge” (bound in green and red basil, a thin tanned sheepskin).22 However, the images of Ms. Fr. 640 show that none of the edges of the inlaid manuscript leaves at the beginning and end of the textblock bear any signs of the staining that often offsets from the turn-ins of leather on the inside of a book’s cover.23 For comparison, some staining from the turn-ins of the current binding is clearly visible in Ms. Fr. 640’s front and rear flyleaves and even continues to be visible far enough into the textblock to be seen on the lower added-paper margin of fol. 170r–v. If similar staining were present on the original manuscript leaves, it would indicate that a previous binding was of leather, but its absence leaves us less certain, as staining from the turn-ins does not always occur. Furthermore, there may have been blank endleaves that were not retained in the new binding and these could have protected the surviving text leaves from staining. The fact that a choice was made to rebind the manuscript might help to support an assumption that the earlier binding was undecorated and ordinary, since a sumptuous gold-tooled leather binding would be less likely to be discarded when the book changed hands than a plain parchment one. However, in the case of an owner such as Philippe de Béthune, the opportunity to rebind it in the style of the other books in his library and with his arms might lead to the replacement of even a rather highly-decorated original binding.

Determining the Style of the First Binding

Thus, the style of the earlier binding of Ms. Fr. 640 cannot be known with any certainty, and there are many possibilities. Manuscripts from France dating from this period in the collections of the Columbia University Libraries serve to illustrate stationery bindings and their opening and handling characteristics. The laced-case limp parchment binding of Smith Western Add. Ms. 9 (Fig. 10), is a compelling example that is relevant to Ms. Fr. 640 for several reasons. Like Ms. Fr. 640, it is a stationery binding containing a manuscript compilation of entries, in this case of mathematical and astronomical texts and figures, and it dates from roughly the same presumed period, 1586–87.24 Tantalizingly, the leaves of SW Add. Ms. 9 also bear a watermark from the paper mill of Nicholas Lebé, a variant of the example seen in the Ms. Fr. 640 leaves. Like Ms. Fr. 640, these leaves are also in folio format and the handmade paper sheets were gathered in sets of five or six sheets to create the sewn sections. On one of the leaves, the writer of SW Add. Ms. 9 has used the technique of folding the leaf several times as a means of creating straight lines without using a ruler in the manner described by the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 on fol. 19v, “Mathematical figures without ruler or compass” (Fig. 11).25

An assessment of SW Add. Ms. 9 as a potential model for the original binding of Ms. Fr. 640 is supported by several features of its current binding that are consistent with a date and place of origin in France in the later sixteenth century. SW Add. Ms. 9 was bound by sewing on four single alum-tawed thongs. While double supports are most common in bindings of the fifteenth and early sixteenth century, the occurrence of single supports on French limp parchment bindings grows in frequency through the sixteenth century.26 The number of sewing supports used to sew a book is influenced by many factors: geography, the date of the binding, level of economy, and format. In Nicholas Pickwoad’s survey of sixteenth-century French printed books in the Ramey collection, of the books larger than 200 mm, eighty percent were sewn on four supports rather than three.27 The textblock of SW Add. Ms. 9 was sewn “all-along,” that is, not abbreviated by the binder in ways that had emerged earlier in the sixteenth century as economical shortcuts, to either bypass sewing stations (points where the sewing thread emerges from the spine fold to wrap around the support), or to sew multiple sections using one length of thread by switching between sections at the point when the thread emerges from the spine. These techniques were normal by the late sixteenth century on French printed books: results from the Ramey survey showed that all-along sewing was extremely rare among books printed in the 1590’s and that sewn textblocks in wrappers—those not bound in boards—were more commonly sewn using abbreviated techniques.28 Because comparable studies have not been undertaken of French stationery bindings of the sixteenth century, it is not possible to determine the rate at which all-along sewing might have been more frequently used than in printed books, but more robust structures remained common in stationery bindings. Dudin’s eighteenth-century French treatise on bookbinding attests to this need, as well as the requirement for ease of opening, specifying that leaves of the ledger bindings, or reliures de Lyon, must be “attached very securely, yet in such a manner that the Book opens very easily and far into the gutter, and to achieve this the side where it is sewn must be flat….”29

The endbands and cover features of SW Add. Ms. 9 are similarly appropriate to a stationery binding from France in this period. A binder forms endbands at the head and tail of the book’s spine using a needle to take thread through the centers of some or all of the folds of the leaves, linking from one to the next, usually while wrapping around a core material. In medieval European bookbinding, the slips or extensions of the endband core reinforced the attachment of the boards by lacing through them in various ways. By the early sixteenth century, binders had begun cutting off endband slips so they did not extend beyond the spine onto the boards, but during this period they were still commonly used to attach parchment covers.30 The endbands of SW Add. Ms. 9 are formed in a typical style for limp parchment bindings: the sewing threads are worked over a core of alum-tawed skin, and the slips of the core are used to lace through the book’s cover. The parchment used to cover SW Ms. Add. 9 is plain skin, presumably from a sheep. Extensions of the covers folded at the fore-edge would once have covered about one third to one half of the textblock edge when the book was closed. Now their presence is easily overlooked because the parchment has shrunk significantly, and the cover with its extensions unfolded and flat just manages to cover the textblock. The cover was fitted with a pair of fore-edge ties made of alum-tawed skin. Attachment techniques used to affix ties are quite varied, and this example matches the type designated in the Ramey survey as “Type 2” (Fig. 12) in its variation for narrower turn-ins, a style that appeared most frequently on Paris imprints, but was not limited to that location.31

Physical Form of the Binding and the Researcher’s Experience

Exploring the question of Ms. Fr. 640’s format at the time of writing may enhance understanding of the author-practitioner’s project in unpredictable ways. Consideration of the manuscript’s origins in what has been identified as a reused account book highlights the familiarity with accounting present in the manuscript itself. The entries for “Merchant” (fols. 9r, 9v, and 38v) discuss bookkeeping and the various books used to keep accounts. In this context it may be relevant that the folding technique explained in 19v is also frequently seen in account books, where it has been employed to create vertical columns, in addition to the use for ruling in mathematical figures discussed by the author-practitioner. Debuiche and Muñoz have traced the connections between Ms. Fr. 640 and the Ouvrier family of merchants in Toulouse.32 The author-practitioner’s access to an account book and choice to document accounting practices provoke curiosity, particularly in light of Ms. Fr. 640’s omission of recipes for making writing ink and many other mundane aspects of the physical process of creating texts.

What is lacking when one reads a text divorced from its original binding? The sensory experience of handling a book contributes to an understanding of the text in its historic and social context. Christopher Clarkson wrote of the “multiple and separate components” of the codex book:

Combined these form numerous subtleties of historical interest and theoretical evidences, indicating period fashion and provenance; divided, they lose much of their meaning and power to conjure human thought…What needs most to be preserved of the physical aspect of an historic object is the inexplicable, the spontaneous and the unconscious qualities inherent in the particular age…33

When a reader handles Ms. Fr. 640, the aural, visual, and tactile qualities of the original paper and ink may still be discernible, but the motion of the leaves and flexibility of the cover will be altered by its later binding (which in itself has a place in the manuscript’s history, about which it conveys its own messages). In using digital images for access to the text, the actions of navigating the text and the original direct relationships of the page images to one another no longer exist in the ways they did in the earlier bound volume. The visual language of the original cover materials and endsheets, which could inform a reader about the place this volume held in an economic hierarchy of books, is lost. Importantly, the evidence the binding might have provided to link the book with specific owners or bookbinders, possibly helping to elucidate the author-practitioner’s identity, is not available. Appreciation of the role of the book’s physical format in influencing the arrangement and confluence of entries is less immediate.

Though it could not address every aspect of these challenges, the Columbia University Libraries Conservation Laboratory produced a model of Smith Western Add. Ms. 9’s binding using a textblock made of “watermarked” paper following Marc Smith’s collation, for Making and Knowing Project participants to consider together with the evolving components of the Digital Critical Edition of Ms. Fr. 640 (Fig. 13, Fig. 14, Fig. 15, Fig. 16). Any attempt to recreate the lost structure of Ms. Fr. 640 by relating it to the binding of SW Add. Ms. 9 could only ever attain the status of an educated guess. Nevertheless, the exercise of seeking to understand the book’s earlier binding demands engagement with the social and economic context of blank books in sixteenth- and seventeenth-century France, and thus offers the opportunity to appreciate an additional aspect of the author-practitioner’s intentions, actions, and experience in creating the manuscript by choosing to write it within a bound volume.
