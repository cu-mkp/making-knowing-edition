Gout or Gonorrhea? The Riddle of G.

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/pc0c-te91
← Back to Essays

Abstract

Ms. Fr. 640, fol. 7v, contains a curious recipe titled “To relieve the pain of G.” This essay seeks to uncover the meaning of “G.” in this entry by comparing the recipe to three other similar entries in the manuscript: “Against gonorrhea,” also on fol. 7v, “Against Go.,” on fol. 16v, and “Against cold gouts,” on fol. 46r. Contemporary sources, including medical treatises and reference works, offer clues about the purpose of this recipe, mentioning similar ingredients and methods of preparation and application. Indeed, the medicinal properties of the recipe’s ingredients—marcasite, storax, and urine—serve specific purposes that can be associated with early modern conceptions of gout. A reconstruction of the recipe considers whether the process of distilling these ingredients was feasible.

Introduction

Fol. 7v in Ms. Fr. 640 contains a recipe “To relieve the pain of G.,” which is followed by an entry on the same page titled “Against gonorrhea.” Fol. 16v presents another recipe with an abbreviated title, “Against Go.” A later entry on fol. 46r is titled “Against cold gouts.” This essay compares these four recipes in order to determine the relationship among them and the subject of the puzzling “g.” on fol. 7v. Interestingly, while the two recipes on fol. 7v are written in the same hand, the first entry, “To relieve the pain of G.,” is in French while “Against gonorrhea” below it is in Latin. Could this suggest that they were copied from, or transmitted by, different sources? The answer, however, does not help us to disambiguate the meaning of “G.” Contemporary definitions of both gonorrhea and gout, a reconstruction of the recipe “To relieve the pain of G.,” and an analysis of the medicinal virtues of its ingredients help to answer this puzzling question.

Gonorrhea and Gout

The comparison among the form of these recipes yields more questions than answers. To begin with, it is unclear what, exactly, “G.” and “Go.” abbreviate—do they refer to gonorrhea? Or perhaps gout? Or another ailment altogether? Moreover, this appears to be a heterogeneous group of recipes in terms of the materials and procedures described by the author-practitioner. For example, the first entry on fol. 7v uses a distillation of “a pound of finely powdered golden and yellow marcasite, half an ounce of storax, 4 lb. of urine.” The subsequent entry on this page provides instructions on how to make an “injection” that contains “old smith’s water,” armenian bole “reduced in the finest powder,” and “common honey.” Fol. 16v is a very short entry about another injectable, this time made of a quince-seed infused water; fol. 46r is equally brief and instructs us to combine guaiac oil with oil of sulfur, “and apply.” Viewed as a whole, the first entry on fol. 7v and that on fol. 46r deal with how to make a topical remedy, while the other two entries describe injectables. Based on textual analysis and material considerations of the information provided in the two entries, “Against gonorrhea” and “Against Go.,” it is likely that the latter is indeed in reference to gonorrhea and not gout. However, none of the four recipes address the alleviation of pain apart from the oblique reference in the puzzling title on fol. 7v, “To relieve the pain of G.”

How relevant was pain to contemporary definitions of gonorrhea and/or gout? Randle Cotgrave’s definition for gonorrhea in his 1611 Dictionarie of the French and English Tongues provides useful insight: “Gonorrhée” is described as “the running of the reines;” using the French word, reins, for kidney, it characterizes gonorrhea as an excretion or a discharge originating in the kidneys.1 An earlier source, Bernard de Gordon (c.1450–1500), provides these definitions for gonorrhea: “c’est emission de sperme sans volupté” (“the emission of sperm without sensuous pleasure”) and “Item note que toute froidure n'est pas gonorree, mais froidure intense paralitiquant” (“be aware that not all cold is gonorrhea, but intense paralytic cold [is]”).2 These sources impart a general understanding of contemporary medical notions of gonorrhea, in which various kinds of genital discharge and the general loss of semen could be diagnosed as gonorrhea.3 The author-practitioner’s recipes for making injections on fols. 7v (“Against gonorrhea”) and 16v (“Against Go.”) may relate to such localized symptoms. The former is clearly an entry about gonorrhea treatment, and we might assume the same for the latter, as they both describe preparations for injectables—the question remains, injections where? While neither manuscript entry explicitly addresses symptoms or the patient’s pain caused by this disease, we can imagine the discomfort in the application of this particular remedy.

By contrast, the entry “Against cold gouts” on fol. 46r and that to relieve “the pain of G.” on fol. 7v do clearly point to physical discomfort and can be understood as a general topical palliative for a set of related humoral afflictions. While modern medicine understands gout as a form of arthritis occurring in the joints, early modern sources suggest a rather different understanding. According to historian of medicine Michael Stolberg, gout was a type of “flux,” an accumulation of fluid morbid matter identified by a “local and/or painful symptom.”4

Pre-modern definitions of gout present a wide spectrum of complaints. In La grande chirurgie de Guy de Chauliac, a medieval French surgical guidebook, gout appears to be understood as synonymous with arthritis: “Arthretique ou goutte est douleur de joinctures engendree de la fluxion de humeurs aux joinctures” (Arthretique or gout is joint pain produced by the flux of humors of the joints).5 A fifteenth-century treatise on bodily hygiene titled, Le Regime tresutile et tresproufitable pour conserver et garder la santé du corps humain, understands gout within a framework of humoral theory; here, gout is related to a paralyzing moisture in the body and not explicitly linked to joint pain.6 The Dictionnaire du Moyen Français identifies nine different types of gout, including Goutte colique (colic gout),7 Goutte culaine (posterior gout), Goutte paralytique (paralytic gout, which refers not to the body, but to a kind of marital ennui) and Goutte volante (flying gout).8 Cotgrave’s entries for gout also emphasize localized pain as a characteristic of this ailment. He distinguishes six variations of gout, of which the following four reference specific kinds of pain: Goute crampe ou, crappe (“The Crampe; or, a Convulsion”), Goutte nouée (“the Gowt in any of the joints”), Goutte serene (“Blindness, or extreame dimness of sight, caused by obturation of the Optick Sinews”) and Goutte Sciatique (“the Sciatica; a pain, ache, or stitch in the hippes”). Collectively, these definitions map out a migratory path of pain around the gout sufferer’s body.

Gout was also understood as a catchall for a variety of bodily pains. Stolberg affirms that gout, like other “fluxes,” was not limited to the joints, but “could also appear in very different body parts and could move from one place to another.”9 With regard to the gout entry on fol. 7v, the author-practitioner does not specify for which part of the body this treatment is intended; he only suggests that the medicine-soaked cloth be applied “on the pain.” How, then, did this distillation recipe on fol. 7v work to alleviate gout pain?

Marcasite and Storax

Related to contemporary medical notions of the treatment of gout is the entry on fol. 7v:

To relieve the pain of G.

Take half a pound of finely powdered golden and yellow marcasite, half an ounce of storax, 4 lb. of urine, incorporate well together, little by little, in a mortar, then boil all together quite strongly. But the pot needs to be well covered so the smoke does not escape. Then distill the imbibed urine, separated by inclination, in an alembic, well luted and covered with a copper head. And soak a cloth with the said water and apply it lukewarm on the pain.

Essentially this recipe is a distillation based on the combination of three main ingredients: the powdered golden and yellow marcasite, storax, and urine. Its application to the affected area is straightforward: a warm, wet cloth soaked in this distillate is directly laid upon the skin to provide pain relief. While it is not possible to locate the exact provenance of this recipe, Guy de Chauliac’s fourteenth-century surgical treatise suggests that such a recipe was a long-established remedy. Under the section “Of hand gout” (De la chiragre), Chauliac instructs to perfume the affected area “avec la fumée qui s’esleue du vinaigre, ietté sur la marcasite…. Finalement ils l’emplastrent avec du galban, et ammoniac, et semblables” (“with the vapor that rises off the vinegar, thrown on the marcasite... in the end, they plaster it with galbanum, ammoniacum, and the like”).10 Though this is only one step of a longer recipe, it closely resembles the author-practitioner’s distillation entry in that it contains similar ingredients: an acid (vinegar), marcasite, and an aromatic gum resin (galban). Moreover, the physician affirms that the recipe is topical: he prefers to apply the remedy to the body with drenched sponges—“les esponges trempées.”11 It is possible then that the author-practitioner’s distillation is an iteration of an established recipe: a concoction of an acidic liquid, marcasite and a gum resin. This instruction is also attributed to Chauliac’s master from Toulouse (mon mestre de Tholose), which leads me to wonder if this entry in the manuscript is a remnant or iteration of a long-established Toulousain gout recipe.

Though the composition of this possible gout recipe is not particularly strange, we do not know what medicinal properties were attributed to its ingredients. According to the early modern barber-surgeon Ambroise Paré (1510–1590), gout medicines “which are made to mollifie” should have a “moderately heating, and humecting faculty” to dissolve the gouty compacted matter.12 Does our recipe follow this same logic? Which elements of the recipe provide heat and which are humectant?

In his discussion on gout, Paré suggests the use of a “repellant” medicine combined with a “resolving” agent. For Paré, marcasite falls under the category of a “repelling or repercussive medicine,” which is used to “force backe the humour which flowes from any other place into the part” and to “mitigate the heat of such inflammation as that defluxion of humors hath caused, yea oft times to asswage and helpe paine.”13 Paré describes the composition of these “repercussives” as mixtures and vinegar solutions of a mélange of plants, oils, herbs and minerals.14 The medium by which they are applied—linen cloth, bandages and compresses—is also, according to Paré, “repellant.”15 Storax is a medicinal simple used in “resolving medicine,” which dissipates the build-up of morbid matter by way of its intense heat and moisture. And so, according to Paré, the resin acts as the “humectifying” agent in this curative analgesic. The properties of storax will be further addressed later in the essay. First, it is important to identify what the author-practitioner is referring to when he calls for “golden and yellow marcasite.”

The author-practitioner’s use of marcasite in his gout recipe complicates this picture because it is difficult to be certain which mineral he is referring to; marcasite and pyrite are often conflated as they are both made up of iron disulfide, although they are two different minerals with distinct crystal formations.16 Cotgrave defines “gold and yellow marcasite” as being a “fire-stone; a mineral that smells like brimstone; and is of two kinds; the yellow, shining as gold; and the white (the purer, and better of the two) like silver.”17 Cotgrave’s shining particles—mimicking gold—relate to the author-practitioner’s description of marcasite; his reference to the smell of brimstone relates to its mineral composition. Further ambiguities are reflected in the sources. For example, though Paré describes marcasite as cold and dry,18 Guy de Chauliac views the mineral as being hot in the second degree and dry in the third.19 (Could it be that Chauliac associates the mineral with heat because of its sulphuric qualities?) And so, it is not possible to know quite what medical properties the author-practitioner attributes to the marcasite. Is it cold and “repellant”? Or hot and “resolving”? An examination of the recipe’s other two ingredients—storax and urine—suggests some tentative answers to these questions.

Cotgrave describes storax as a “sweet Gumme” and differentiates between two types: storax calamite (“the best kind of storax brought from Aleppo, and kept in canes of the leaves of reedes”) and storax liquide (“liquid storax”), a gum he defines as being synonymous with Myrrhe stacte, which he describes as “the best kind of Myrrhe … [it] is indeed the iuyce, or gummie liquor of the fattest Myrre.”20 Both storax descriptions remark on the high quality of the gum but fail to provide a physical description, save for the implication that storax liquide, given its name, is an essential oil. A similar distinction is made in a 1555 Spanish translation of Dioscorides’s De materia medica by the physician Andrés de Laguna. Here, storax is defined as:

liquor de un arbol que se parece al membrillo; y aquel se tiene por mas excellente, que es roxo, grasso, resinoso, blanquezino en sus granos, qye preservera muy lungo tiempo oloroso y quando se ablanda, da de si un humor semejante al miel. [a liquor from a tree that is similar to quince; the most excellent one is red, thick, resinous, and whitish grain, it preserves its smell for a long time, and when it softens, it gives off a humor similar to honey].21

Dioscorides makes mention of both the liquor and the resin: a red-white, odiferous substance. It is this solid form of storax that Paré mentions in his list of diaphoreticke simples, substances that by intense heat dissolve impacted morbid matter. His list includes styrax, calamita and benioinium, which are all solids.22 If the storax/styrax is meant to impart intense heat in order to dissolve the impaction of humoral matter, then it is possible that the marcasite is, in fact, there to further block the buildup of said humors, creating a sort of early modern icy-hot compress. However, would this recipe—which calls for four pounds of urine—produce enough distillate to soak a cloth that is then to be applied to an afflicted body part? My reconstruction will address this question, as well as the feasibility of the distillation recipe itself.

Reconstruction

For the purposes of our reconstruction, I chose to use iron disulfide, commonly referred to as pyrite.23 It both matches Cotgrave’s description of its gold and yellow color and it contains a high percentage of sulfur, giving it a sulphuric smell. Following Dioscorides’s description and Paré’s listing of the resin as a diaphoreticke, I decided it would be best to use the tree resin rather than the liquor, or essential oil. For this reconstruction, the Making and Knowing laboratory purchased Styrax benzoin resin. The first step of the reconstruction was to powder the pieces of pyrite and storax. In questioning the feasibility of this recipe, my first concern was whether I would be able to easily powder the pyrite with a mortar and pestle. Current gemmological standards rank pyrite between 6 to 6.5 on the Mohs hardness scale, meaning that this mineral can be scratched by an iron file.24 But, does its scratchability mean that it is also easily powdered?

For the powdering portion of the reconstruction, I began with the harder of the two substances—the pyrite. The author-practitioner does not give instructions on how to do this, which suggests that it was such common practice that it did not merit further elaboration. Powdering and grinding were an integral part of distilling; the title plate engraving of Jan van der Straet’s Nova Reperta series, depicting the discoveries—or inventions—of the New World, includes a mortar and pestle in its illustration of distillation paraphernalia (Fig. 1). It is possible then that the phrase “powdered marcasite” in the distillation entry provides enough detail for a practitioner well-versed in medical distillations.

Powdering the pyrite did not take long and proved to be quite easy. After pounding the pyrite for thirty seconds, the pebble-sized pieces broke down into smaller, granular ones. I ground it down further with the pestle until it was a combination of powdered and granular bits. Next, I chose to powder the storax. Though the author-practitioner does not does specify in what state the storax should be used, his later instruction to “incorporate [all the ingredients] well together, little by little,” suggested that the storax should also be powdered. Being a much softer substance than the pyrite, the storax crushed into a finer powder. It was sticky and oily, and smelled strongly of vanilla and cinnamon. Overall, I deduced that anyone with a mortar and pestle would be able to successfully grind down pieces of pyrite and storax to a fine powder (Fig. 2, Fig. 3, Fig. 4).

The last main step was to carry out the distillation itself. For this, I measured out the urine, pyrite and storax, and I set up my “alembic” (Fig. 5, Fig. 6). Following the author-practitioner’s instructions, I slowly incorporated the storax and then the pyrite into the urine. As neither is water soluble, they did not dissolve. The mixture did, however, become cloudy with the storax particles, while the pyrite turned everything gray (Fig. 7). There is no mention in the entry of what this mixture should look like. A main concern of the author-practitioner is that the ingredients be well incorporated, and so I took this change of color to be a sign of the ingredients blending together. The author-practitioner then instructs the reader first to boil all the ingredients together and, second, to “distill the imbibed urine, separated by inclination.”25 That is, after the urine has been infused with the pyrite and storax, it should be decanted. Only the sediment-free urine should then be distilled. For my distillation, I combined the boiling with the distilling process.26

First, the urine mixture was placed inside a pot. A pyrex bowl was set inside as well, in order to catch the distillate. The pot’s lid, which I covered in a sheet of copper to mimic the copper-headed alembic referred to by the author-practitioner, was placed on top, upside down (Fig. 8). When the mixture began to boil, we filled the copper-covered lid with ice and allowed it to continue boil until almost all the liquid had evaporated (Fig. 9). The result was a yellow-hued distillate that smelled of all three of its ingredients: urine, pyrite and storax (Fig. 10). The distillation yielded just under 20 ml and had gone from a ph of 6, before the distillation, to a ph of 7 to 8 after, which means that it was slightly more alkaline than before and almost close to the ph of water. My reconstruction managed to produce a substantial amount of distillate considering that I used a fraction of the amount of urine called for in the entry on fol. 7v. This led me to conclude that if this distillation were to be done with the recommended four pounds of urine it would certainly produce enough distillate to soak a cloth and apply it to the gouty area.

Conclusions

Overall, the reconstruction proved that this recipe would have been accessible to a practitioner with the proper distillation equipment. It was not difficult to put together, and it produced enough distillate to be usable as a topical remedy. An examination of the contemporary understanding of the recipe’s ingredients, especially the marcasite and storax, elucidates the fact that this remedy was thought to break up and dissolve an accumulation of morbid matter. Such an illness is what Michael Stolberg identifies as a “flux,” a general medical term under which gout falls. Given these considerations, this recipe “to relieve the pain of G.” is most likely for gout, which we can define as a painful ailment derived from a flow of morbid matter, including but not limited to pain in the joints.
