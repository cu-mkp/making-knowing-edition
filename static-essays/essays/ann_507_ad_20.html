For Making Very Beautiful Color of Gold

By , DOI: https://doi.org/10.7916/s784-zk74
← Back to Essays

Abstract

Among the many recipes in Ms. Fr. 640 for making gold color, an unusual one entitled “For making very beautiful color of gold & of little expense,” on fol. 76v calls for mixing ground orange peel with sulfur. The reconstruction of this recipe did not result in “a very beautiful color,” but more experimentation is necessary.

Gold Colors

Ms. Fr. 640 contains many entries for creating the color of gold. Some of these involve no gold at all, such as “Color of gold without gold on silver” (fol. 29v), “For making bronze in gold color” (fols. 73r and 78v), “Gold color” (fol. 100r), and “Good mixture to color gold” (fol. 104r). Some contain no gold, but are intended to enhance the effects of applied gold, such as “Seat for gold” (fol. 29v), which involves mixing ground yellow pigments with glue to make a mordant onto which gold leaf is to be applied. Other recipes involve applying gold leaf—elemental gold that has been hammered into thin sheets—or making elemental gold into pigment for writing or painting in gold. The author-practitioner discusses gilding with gold leaf at many points in the manuscript, and he provides two different processes to prepare gold for painting and writing, both of which he calls “or moulu” (fol. 8v and fol. 75r).1 These processes for gold colors, techniques for applying gold leaf and for making paintable gold can be found in other contemporary recipe texts,2 or can be found on early modern objects still extant today. Fol. 76v contains a seemingly implausible process for making gold color:

For making very beautiful color of gold & of little expense

First take very yellow orange peel, & clean them well of the white that is inside, & pulverize them very well and in a very clean mortar. And take as much of sulfur, & grind all together, and put everything in a glass vial, & keep them in the cellar or other damp place for the space of eight or ten days. Then when you want to use it, one needs to warm it and use it where you want, & you will see a very beautiful color.

This recipe is included within a section of Ms. Fr. 640 written by a scribe (fols. 73r–76v), which contains many short recipes for coloring diverse materials. Unusually for Ms. Fr. 640, the recipe appears to have been copied almost verbatim from the pseudonymous Alessio Piemontese’s book of secrets. A comparison between this recipe and a 1559 French edition of Piemontese shows marked similarity:

While there are only tantalizing hints of the author-practitioner copying so closely other recipe texts,4 those passages written by a scribe (fols. 57r–58r, 73r–76v, 77r–79v) deserve further attention. Leaving aside such an investigation for the time being, this essay offers an account of our reconstruction, which should also be viewed as provisional because many more trials are necessary in order to understand how this recipe could make a “very beautiful color of gold.”

Reconstruction

We selected three varieties of orange citrus fruits—tangerine, navel orange, and Valencia orange (Fig. 1).5 Modern American Valencia oranges are a hybridized version of an orange imported from Spain in the last half of the sixteenth century.6 Valencia oranges are probably not Spanish in origin, but, instead, may have been developed in Portugal.7 While we do not know if this type of orange approximates what the author-practitioner would have used, the Valencia orange may be more historically authentic than the navel and tangerine, which were hybridized much more recently.

Reconstruction I

We undertook two reconstructions of the experiment. The first step was to “take very yellow orange peel, & clean them well of the white that is inside, & pulverize them very well and in a very clean mortar.” We washed the oranges and removed the zest with a knife (Fig. 2). We ground half the peels from each type of orange individually in a “very clean mortar” while they were still wet (Fig. 3), and the other halves were placed aside to be dried. We ground the peels for about ten minutes to a paste and then, as called for in the recipe, we added an equal amount of sulfur. The mixtures were then ground for an additional ten minutes until the peel and sulfur were fully combined.

We then followed the instructions to “put everything in a glass vial, & keep them in the cellar or other damp place for the space of eight or ten days” by placing the three mixtures in individual glass beakers. We put these unsealed beakers into a closed container with about an inch of water standing at the bottom (Fig. 4, Fig. 5). We stored the beakers in the lab refrigerator at approximately 5°C, for eight days. On removing the mixtures, we noted that they had absorbed some of the water. The pulverized mixture was moist to the touch and compacted more easily than before being left to sit. We observed no change in color.

Following the directive that “when you want to use it, one needs to warm it,” we heated the mixtures to various temperatures. We heated the mixture of Valencia and sulfur to a temperature at which to melt the sulfur (115.2°C). This mixture ignited and burned off (Fig. 6, Fig. 7, Fig. 8). We then slowly heated the navel orange and sulfur mixture for ten minutes with a target temperature just over the melting point of sulfur. The substance browned and the mixture blackened even before we reached 110°C (Fig. 9). The mixture was then heated with a target temperature of 100°C, and again the mixture turned brown, although this time, it retained a yellowish cast (Fig. 10).

The results of the heating trials were then individually ground on a marble slab. Three types of binders were added to both the heated and unheated mixtures. The binders were Venice turpentine, linseed oil, and a 1:1 mixture of Venice turpentine and linseed oil.8 The resulting paints were strained through linen cloth and applied to gessoed stucco, made according to the recipe for “Stucco for molding” on fol. 29r.9

The results could not be called “a very beautiful color.” In the unheated mixture, the mixture clumped together (Fig. 11), and the heated pigments resembled brownish mustard (see Fig. 14, under the label “1st attempt”).

We decided to make an additional trial of a recipe for a gold color with an organic material as its base. This recipe from Phillip’s A Booke of Secrets is supposed to produce a “good gold colour” using aloe: “A good gold colour. Take linseed oile, put into it a little Aloe Epaticum, and alum, let them seeth well in a leaded pot.”10 We combined linseed oil with alum (potassium aluminum sulfate) and aloe epacticum (dried and ground juice of the aloe plant)11 and brought the mixture to a boil. We then cooled the mixture, ground it in a mortar and pestle, and applied it to the stucco models. It too appeared brown with a slight yellow cast (Fig. 12).

Reconstruction II

In our second reconstruction, the procedure remained almost the same, except that we dried the orange peels for approximately two weeks and then ground them into a fine powder, an operation that took the aid of several others in the lab (Fig. 13). This powder was combined with an equal amount of sulfur, and half the mixture was placed in a glass flask in a sealed container with an inch of water at the bottom, and kept in the refrigerator at about 5°C. We combined the other half with linseed oil and painted out the result onto the stucco models (Fig. 14). The result might possibly be called “a very beautiful color,” although it still contained particles and had a coarse consistency (somewhat less visible than in the previous trial).

Conclusion

Lack of time ended our trials with this test of the dried orange peel and sulfur mixture. We realized that in our first reconstruction, our expectation that the sulfur was the important coloring agent led us to heat the mixture to a point where the sulfur would melt. As noted, this caused the mixture to turn brown. Out of this failure, we realized it would be fruitful to experiment with warming (chauffer) the mixture rather than heating (cuire or recuire) it. We did not, however, have time to carry out this test, and we leave it to our curious readers to try it themselves.
