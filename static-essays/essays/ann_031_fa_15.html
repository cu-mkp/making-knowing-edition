Damasked Cloth

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/vgj0-yh50
← Back to Essays

Abstract

An unusual recipe on fol. 15r involves resist-dye techniques to create a pattern on cloth that imitates damask textile. This essay reconstructs the dyes, and follows the entry’s instructions to reconstruct the process of resist-dyeing. It concludes by situating this process within other entries in Ms. Fr. 640 that aim to produce imitations of more expensive materials and to create the impermanent artworks and decorations of ephemeral court and civic festivals.

Introduction

The recipe “Damasked cloth” (Drap damasse) on fol. 15r describes how to make imitations of damask cloth and embroidery by creating patterns with dyeing techniques.

You can damask a cloth with two different colors and imitate embroidery without adding anything to it, in this way. Once it is dyed yellow, pounce onto it such a pattern as will please you. Then you will baste some string or a thicker cord onto the pouncing and thrust it into a dye of guesde woad or pastel woad & it will become green, except that which is beneath the string, which will remain yellow because the green dye will not have penetrated there. And you can do thus with other colors, and instead of cord or string, add some pieces of paltry cloth, cut into moresque shapes, on top of the first color. In that manner, you will have cheap embroidery (fol. 15r).

This recipe is unusual in that it is one of the few entries with instructions for dyeing textiles in Ms. Fr. 640. It explains how to create patterns by dyeing fabrics yellow first, then overdyeing them with a blue dye, with instructions on how to block out areas where patterns are to be created. The recipe, however, neither mentions materials for obtaining yellow colors nor discusses processes of making dyes. What does the missing information on dyeing—necessary for the execution of the recipe—tell us about the context of the authorship? Did the author-practitioner have any knowledge of dyeing? If not, how was he able to write the recipe? Was it simply the result of his collecting recipes in circulation, or was it essential to his practice? This essay aims to answer these questions by examining relevant contemporaneous recipes and conducting hands-on experiments.

Despite being unusual in the manuscript in terms of technique, the recipe is not separate from other pursuits in the manuscript: the author-practitioner’s interest in imitation is clear throughout Ms. Fr. 640, and the recipe “Damasked cloth” should be considered in this context. Whereas the author-practitioner’s ultimate goal in the numerous recipes for casting flowers and imitating gems seems to be to imitate the shapes and colors of nature, his concern in this recipe is to imitate artifacts. His interest in making fancy fabrics inexpensively is similarly found on fol. 67r, where he mentions techniques to make cheap banners with ephemeral materials.

The ephemeral art of civic and courtly celebrations, which flourished in early modern Europe, provides an important perspective for understanding the end use of damasked cloth and thus the author-practitioner’s intention in writing the recipe. “Ephemeral art” is a generic term for a variety of festival decorations and monuments, produced for public events such as civic processions, royal entries, weddings, court funerals and coronations.1 As these decorations would usually be demolished once the event was over, they were made of impermanent materials and thus were not meant to last long.2 Partly because of this, the value of ephemeral art for the work of early modern artists and craftsmen has been underestimated in modern scholarship.3 By situating the recipe “Damasked cloth” in this historical context, the annotation attempts to illuminate the larger social setting where the author-practitioner practiced and wrote the recipe.

What is “Damasked cloth”?

We initially translated Drap damasse as “Damask cloth,” but, as with many other reconstructions of manuscript entries, the process of preparing or carrying out a reconstruction leads to a closer reading of the text. This revealed that damasse (actually damassé) derives from the verb damasser and can thus better be translated as “Damasked cloth.”4 Randle Cotgrave’s Dictionarie of the French and English Tongues defines “drap” as “cloth,” “woolen cloth,” and “broad cloth,”5 but it has no entry for the adjective “damasse” (i.e., damassé). The Dictionnaire du Moyen Francais (1330-1500) defines “damassé” as a “pattern in the fashion of damask,” and Frédérique Godefroy’s Complément du dictionaire de l’ancienne langue française et des tous ses dialectes du IX siècle au XVe siècle says that it is “cloth from Damascus, made or fabricated in the fashion of damask.”6 What is “damask” (damas)? In the same dictionaries, “damas” is defined as a fabric or silk-like cloth originally from the city of Damascus, with the Dictionnaire du Moyen Francais describing it as a cloth “whose design stands out on matt satin or conversely.”7

The definition of “damask” in the early modern period thus generally matches more recent definitions. The Dictionary of Textiles, published in 1915, states that “the true or double or reversible damask is woven both the ground and the large floral Jacquard patterns in eight-leaf satin and the single damask in five-leaf satin weave.… Usually made of cotton or linen and used on the table.”8 Fairchild’s Dictionary of Textiles, published in 1959, provides further information on its history and use:

Originally a rich silk fabric with woven floral designs made in China and introduced into Europe through Damascus, from which it derived its name… Damask has been made for centuries and is one of the oldest and most popular staple cloths. Used for upholstery and draperies, table cloths, napkins, bedspreads, face towels, evening wear, etc.9

The Victoria and Albert Museum defines “damask” as “a woven material whose effect depends on the differing play of light on its pattern surfaces, which alternate between the smooth face and the contrasting reverse of satin weave.”10 This definition makes it clear that the patterns of “damask” are created using identical colors of threads for warp and weft. In other words, its patterns are created by difference in luster rather than color, as we can see from a damask cloth made in Italy between 1600 and 1650 (Fig. 1).

To sum up, in the early modern period, “damask” referred to “patterned woven textiles,” often used for “interior decoration.” The author-practitioner’s goal in the recipe “Damasked cloth” is to imitate “damask” not by weaving but by dyeing. His plan is to first dye a cloth yellow, then block out desired areas with string or cloth pieces, and, finally, overdye the fabric with a blue dye. In this way, while the ground would turn green, the blocked areas would remain yellow and thus create patterns. His instructions on how to block areas on the cloth with string, cord and cloth pieces is very detailed, suggesting a strong possibility that he designed and carried out the recipe himself.11

However, over the course of preparing for the reconstruction, we began to doubt the feasibility of the procedure. We initially translated tu faulfileras as “you will sew loosely.” But given dye’s tendency to run through the fibers of cloth, it seemed unlikely that the desired dye-resist effect would be obtained by sewing strings “loosely” to block out the targeted area. To achieve dye-resist effects, the area has to be blocked out very tightly (something I learned from my hands-on experience of tie-dye at an indigo-dye specialist’s workshop in northern Thailand).12 Moreover, the Centre National de Ressources Textuelles et Lexicales (CNRTL) defines “faufiler” as “attaching big ornaments provisionally onto a piece of cloth,” thus we realized that a better translation of tu faulfileras would be “you will baste.”13 With tight basting, the blue dye might be kept from penetrating the side where the string was sewn, but it seemed to us that the blue dye would ultimately penetrate the cloth from the other side, where string was not sewn. Similarly, adding “some pieces of paltry cloth, cut into moresque shapes, on top of the first color” did not seem as if it would be effective, as when the whole cloth was soaked in the woad dye, the blue dye would easily penetrate the cloth pieces that were supposed to resist it, and reach the areas beneath that were designed to remain yellow. In this way, it seemed the blue dye would color the entire cloth evenly green.

Dyeing Yellow: Weld

The recipe “Damasked cloth” does not specify which materials can provide yellow dyes, however, Ms. Fr. 640 includes the following ingredients for yellow dyes and pigments: saffron (safran, fols. 20r, 29v, 38r, and 41v),14 weld (gaulde, fols. 10r and 56r), lapathium acutum maius (fol. 40r), marigold (soucy fol. 38r),15 and turmeric (terra merita, fols. 76v, 98r, and 148r).16 None of these recipes, however, describe how to process each material for dyeing textiles.

Cennino Cennini’s Il Libro dell’Arte includes a brief recipe for dye made with saffron,17 and a Dutch recipe book, translated into English in 1583, contains a fairly detailed recipe using weld.18 Secondary sources confirm that both weld and saffron were widely used for dyeing fabrics in Europe.19 Given that the key procedures in both recipes—“use of lye (or ash)” and “adding heat”—are often found in other contemporaneous dyeing recipes,20 it is likely that a similar practice had been developed in France by this time. The 1583 recipe using weld reads:

To make this dye, put too foure pounde of woollen yarne, or cloth, two pounde of woad. Put this woad in a Kettle, and cast therein of fayre water, then cast agayne halfe a tin dishe full of ashes out of the fyre, and as soone as it seethes, put your woollen yarne therein, then let it seeth eyght or nyne pater noster whiles long, then putte your yarne forth, and put a little water in, out of the kettle, and then a few ashes out of the harth, and put your woollen yarne agayne therein: and so let it seeth a good while, if it be a broune blue, it shall be a darke greene, and if it be white woollen yearne, so it shal be a yealow colour.21

The recipe on fol. 15r does not provide information about quantities. We sourced a small metal plate that matched the size of the beaker in the same approximate ratio as the kettle and the tin dish in the 1583 recipe (Fig. 2). As for the quantity of fabric, we measured out a volume for each fabric that would make the beaker neither too cramped nor too empty. We recited the Pater Noster22 in English nine times, which took three minutes and forty-five seconds.23

What differentiates this recipe from most modern dye recipes is not only the lack of precise quantities but also the use of ash. Wood ash, generally made into lye, was used to help extract dyes from plants when making them into pigments,24 and from this, we can presume that the ash was used to help extract yellow dye from the weld. Most modern recipes, however, do not include ash or lye. Instead, they define weld as a plant requiring mordants such as alum and copper.25 When combined with an alum mordant, for instance, weld dye produces an intense yellow.26 The use of alum is observed in other recipes in the 1583 recipe book for making green colors, in particular, but it is rarely found in the recipes for yellow colors including this one.

The liquid mixture of water, weld, and ash—made as instructed in the recipe—was murky throughout the experiment (Fig. 3). However, after washing off all the residues, we obtained good yellow colors of varying shades: a light yellow on the white silk (Fig. 4), a relatively dark yellow on the unbleached cotton (Fig. 5), and a greenish yellow on the unbleached linen (Fig. 6). It is hard to tell precisely what caused the shades to vary, because both the properties of the fibers and the ground colors of the fabrics before dyeing have an effect. For instance, even though cotton is harder to dye than other fabrics, we achieved a fairly deep yellow on the cotton fabric. That was in part because we used unbleached cotton, whose ivory color was already close to yellow.

With potash alum, a typical mordant used in modern recipes for weld dyeing, we might have obtained a far stronger yellow color on all the fabrics. But it is uncertain if such an intense color is what the recipe intended. Although alum was available in the late sixteenth century, the recipe does not specify its use. More importantly, our reconstruction proves that this weld solution can yield good yellow colors. The relatively simple procedure also suggests that yellow dyes were easy to obtain, in contrast to blue dye made from woad.

Dyeing Blue: Woad

For the blue dye, unlike the yellow, the recipe “Damasked cloth” suggests specific materials for achieving the color: woad, or as the author-practitioner called it, guesde ou pastel.27 Such mention was probably due to the limited choice of materials for blue. Whereas Europeans could obtain yellow dyes from a wide variety of plants, for blue dyes, they depended on woad as their major source, at least before the indigo plant proliferated over the course of the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries.28 Woad was an important cash crop, widely cultivated around Toulouse in southern France, Thuringia in Germany, and the Marche in Italy.29 In Toulouse, in particular, woad production played a crucial role in the local economy, not only supporting farmers and dyers, but also creating numerous wealthy merchants.30 The large-scale trade of woad is confirmed in Ms. Fr. 640 as well. On fol. 38v, the author-practitioner states that while those who trade in velvet and other materials in small quantities do not keep double-entry books, those who sell in bulk and deal in woad have double-entry books because it is important for them to keep detailed records.

The sole recipe in Ms. Fr. 640 directly relevant to the production of woad on fol. 39r specifies when to sow and harvest the plant, how to discern good-quality leaves, what soil conditions and places for cultivation are best, and what the average yields should be.31 But it does not provide practical instruction on how to process the leaves, which suggests that the author-practitioner did not practice woad-dyeing himself. This becomes even more apparent in the line that reads: “One assays it in the dyers’ vat, and to fill a vat with it, one needs six balls of it (fol. 39r). Referring to the dyers in the third person implies that the author-practitioner was not the dyer, however, just before this, the author-practitioner writes, “The goodness of the pastel woad is known when, put in the mouth, it gives a taste as of vinegar, or when crumbling & breaking it, it has some mold-like veins which are as if golden or silver.” These vivid descriptions would seem to have come from first-hand accounts or even from his own experience of the woad dye.

The author-practitioner’s attention to plants was not limited to woad. A recipe on fol. 44r for extracting dyes from flowers contains similar information, although no details about the process of making dyes. Both these recipes prove his interest in the uses of flowers and where they can be found—rather than the process of dyeing per se—and explain the meager description of the dyeing process in the recipe “Damasked cloth.” His interest in these plants perhaps also connects to the numerous recipes for making life cast flowers in Ms. Fr. 640. Over the course of sourcing flowers for casting, he may have become familiar with their habitats and characteristics, including their dyeing properties.

Although the author-practitioner apparently did not practice woad dyeing, by being based in Toulouse, which at that time was the center of woad production and trade, he would not have had much difficulty sourcing woad dyes from one of the local dyers. The early modern method of making woad dyes was laborious and complicated. After harvesting, the leaves were crushed and reduced to a paste, which then was molded into balls and dried. The dried woad balls could be kept for a long time and were easily transported. To be used for dyeing, these balls were broken up, mixed with water, and fermented.32 The historian of dyes, Dominque Cardon, notes that in Thuringia, “this was a job for experts and was reserved for members of the Waidgilde (the Woad Guild), who took an oath never to reveal the secrets of their craft.”33 In England, the smell of the fermentation was so strong that Queen Elizabeth I prohibited it within an eight-mile radius of her residences.34

After fermentation, the paste was formed into small lumps known as couched woad and was ready for dyeing.35 The couched woad was ground and placed in a vat, which was filled with hot water. Then lye was added to alkalize the solution, and when the liquid turned yellowish green, the fabrics were placed in the vat.36 The couched woad was used alone in medieval times, but beginning in the seventeenth century cakes of indigo were added to intensify the dye. The proportion of indigo cakes to couched woad gradually increased, and, ultimately, woad was replaced by indigo.37

Due to the unsuitability of the Making and Knowing Laboratory for setting up a fermenting woad vat, it was not feasible to reconstruct the early modern dye.38 We instead opted for a chemical dye39 with woad extracts.40 We prepared the textiles as instructed in the recipe (Fig. 7, Fig. 8) and soaked them in the chemical woad solution.41 On the silk, the area that had been dyed yellow turned into green as described in the recipe. But the blue dye reached the areas beneath the string and cloth piece and dyed those areas green also. As a result, no pattern remained (Fig. 9, Fig. 10). On the cotton, by contrast, a faint pattern of greenish-yellow color was created (Fig. 11, Fig. 12).

The contrasting results on the cotton and on the silk can be explained in two ways. One is that cotton is generally harder to dye than silk. Another explanation is that the cotton fabric used in the experiment was thicker than the silk. It would have taken longer for the blue dye to penetrate the cotton cloth. If we had used thicker and coarser fabrics, we could have obtained much better results. This suggests that, if the author-practitioner intended thick and coarse fabrics in the recipe, his procedure makes sense, and he may actually have experimented himself with this technique. With his skill and the good quality woad dyes made in Toulouse, he might well have produced much better “damasked cloth” with clearer patterns.

Conclusion

The reconstruction of the recipe “Damasked cloth” suggests that the author-practitioner may have practiced the technique himself. Our initial doubt about the recipe’s feasibility—on the basis of the scanty explanation of the dyeing process and the seemingly infeasible procedure provided—was partially laid to rest over the course of reconstruction. The author-practitioner does not seem to have been a specialist in dyeing. Even if he made yellow dyes himself, the procedure for which is relatively simple, he does not seem to have made woad dye. Instead, he probably sourced those dyes from elsewhere. Our reconstruction also reveals that his instructions were not as implausible as they first appeared. The patterns created on the cotton cloth confirm that the desired effects suggested in the recipe could be obtained, and thus the text could have been based on the author-practitioner’s own practice. As noted throughout this essay, preparing and carrying out a reconstruction prompted us to engage with the manuscript more actively, critically, and carefully, and thus can be viewed as a powerful method of close, critical reading.

The “Damasked cloth” would never have matched the elegant luster of authentic damask but the imitation could have had its own uses. By examining the recipe “Damasked cloth” in the historical context of the ephemeral art in early modern Europe, we can surmise why the author-practitioner might have collected and carried out this recipe. Given that damask was often used for “interior decoration,” there was probably high demand for cheaper imitations for decorative purposes at festivals and public events. As many craftsmen and artists were employed in preparing such events,42 it is possible that the author-practitioner was involved in such work and that the recipe was written for such temporary ends.

Versatility was an important virtue of the artist and the craftsman in the early modern period.43 Even court artists were often employed to produce practical and decorative works, thus it is difficult to draw a line between “applied or decorative art” and “high art.”44 Such versatility is well observed in the author-practitioner’s work, which encompasses a wide variety of techniques, mediums and materials, and demonstrates applications of known techniques to different materials. The recipe “Damasked cloth” is evidence of this versatility as well as the probable significance of ephemeral art in the author-practitioner’s life. Scholarly research on ephemeral art has relied largely on chronicles and texts written by court historians,45 thus Ms. Fr. 640 helps broaden our source base and allows us insight into the production of ephemeral works of art and daily life from the perspectives of materials, techniques, and artisanal production.

Woad and the indigo plant have one of these glycosides in common, which is known as indican; woad has additional precursor glycosides called isatan A and isatan B. When the leaves are crushed, the indican reacts to the oxygen in the air, forming insoluble blue indigotin, which is then processed into indigo. In order to dye textiles, the indigotin needs to be rendered soluble again in an alkaline solution so that it can impregnate the fibers. Once the fibers are exposed to the air, the indigotin re-forms. The indigo plant was imported into Europe even before medieval times but used mostly as a pigment. Its use as a dye was prohibited to protect the woad industry until the sixteenth or seventeenth century. The major difference between woad and the indigo plant is their efficiency in dyeing. When used in the same amount, the indigo plant yields a larger quantity of indigo dye than woad, and this is why the indigo plant ultimately came to replace woad in Europe. See C.P. de Lasteyrie, A treatise on the Culture, Preparation, History, and Analysis of Pastel, or Woad: The Different Methods of Extracting the Coloring Matter, and the Manner of Using It, and Indigo, in Dyeing, translated from the French by H.A.S. Dearborn (Boston: Rowe & Hooper, 1816), 126-38.↩︎
