Abstract

BnF Ms. Fr. 640 offers a wealth of information on gunpowder weapons. During the wars of religion, gun technology was of the utmost importance, not only for those who produced these instruments of war, but also for those who made a profession of arms and had to ensure that these guns were safe to use. This essay examines the entries in Ms. Fr. 640 relating to weapons against the backdrop of this turbulent period in and around Toulouse, and compares them to other sources that treat gunpowder weaponry in sixteenth-century Europe.

Wars of Religion

The text of BnF Ms. Fr. 640, set down in Toulouse after 1579 by an anonymous author-practitioner, offers a wealth of information to readers interested in all sorts of weapons. The wars of religion are an important context for Ms. Fr. 640, as Toulouse was much involved in the warfare, and, from 1562 on, Catholics and Protestants fought fiercely in the area.1 In 1562 violence erupted when the local people, mainly Catholics, confronted the Protestant urban elites who had seized the town hall. The city took up arms to defend the Catholic cause and many Protestants were killed. The Catholic League, extending its power in Toulouse, expended extensive funds upon military equipment and the payment of soldiers. In 1563, Toulouse’s treasurers partially financed the siege of Montauban and paid for provisions and artillery ordnance. In 1570, Gaspard de Coligny, Seigneur de Châtillon, Protestant Admiral of France, advanced with his army upon the city, justifying its military preparations. In 1572, after the St. Bartholomew’s Day massacre in Paris, the Capitouls and the Parlement gave the order to arrest the remaining Protestants in the city. In 1586, Toulouse supported the governor of Languedoc, Marshall de Joyeuse, with ordnance and ammunition.2

In 1589, Bishop Urbain de Saint Gelais, closely related to the Guise family, became governor of the city.3 Some time afterward, however, Duke Guillaume de Joyeuse reentered the city, and Toulouse remained under the control of his family, all the while supplying his army in Languedoc with guns and gunpowder. In 1590–92, there were still two Protestant strongholds in the villages of Montastruc and Miremont, and Toulouse sent a large quantity of weapons4 to the army of Joyeuse entrenched before these towns. This war continued on through 1595 in the neighborhood of Tournefeuille, Portet, St. Sulpice, Lézat, and Vitarelles de Montech.5

During these turbulent decades, weapons technology was thus of utmost importance, and it is not surprising that the author-practitioner took an interest in it, perhaps even meeting with the principal cannon founders in the Royal arsenal of Toulouse. The knowledge he gathered on weapons was of great importance to nobles and the social elite leading the troops, but also to various kinds of practitioners, lower officers, and gunners, for whom it might eventually pave the way for social mobility. The intended audience for the gunnery sections of Ms. Fr. 640 was not professional founders and other metalworkers making the weapons, but rather people who used the guns and needed to judge the quality of the weaponry (for example, by scratching the gun to see the alloy, noted on fol. 17r), which was very important for several reasons—not least for determining whether the gun was likely to explode.

Sources on Weaponry in Sixteenth-Century France

Ms. Fr. 640 contains remarkably detailed information on guns and gunnery. Where did this information come from? I examine first the passages about gunnery (fols. 17r–19v). The Rezeptliteratur (recipe literature) about gunnery goes back to the early fifteenth century when the first Feuerwerkbücher (gunpowder books) were written in Germany.6 These collections of practical recipes for gunners were widely disseminated very early on. A French version of a Feuerwerkbuch is the Livre du secret de l’art de canonnerie, which continued to circulate through the sixteenth century.7 The type of questions that fill these texts suggest that they might have been used for interviewing apprentices during gunner exams.8 Fifteenth- and sixteenth-century French manuscripts produced by local gunners concerning artillery are relatively rare.9 Fortunately, however, higher officers in the craft also wrote a number of texts apparently containing similar information.10 Printed sources on gunnery in this period are rather meager, but Jean Errard’s La Fortification réduite en art (1595), and Jean Hanzelet’s Pyrotechnie (1630) are relevant texts that help us to understand the anonymous author-practitioner. Contemporary texts also existed in English in the period,11 and recent work by Brice Cossart has illuminated sixteenth-century Spanish schools of gunners.12

Even compared to these texts, the sections on artillery in Ms. Fr. 640 provide a wealth of information about the state of the art at the end of the sixteenth century. Their form suggests that the author was not a gun founder himself but rather he collected knowledge in discussions with practitioners.

A first section in Ms. Fr. 640 concerns entries about grenades and fireworks (fol. 8r), a second one deals with the diversity of a gunner’s activities, including firing a cannon at night, unspiking a cannon, defending a town, bringing a cannon over land, among others, as well as with different kinds of shots and different kinds of pieces (fols. 21r–28r). A third section deals with portable pieces like arquebuses (fols. 43v–45r), and a fourth section, spread over two parts, details what a petard is (fol. 90r and fol. 167r).

This essay offers a threefold overview of these entries: The first part deals with the different kinds of cannons with which the author-practitioner was acquainted in Toulouse and the gunnery techniques he observed there. The second part treats gun casting and boring techniques. The third part discusses the techniques used for defending or attacking a place.

“First, to know one’s peece”: The Art of the Gunner

According to English author Richard Wright, the knowledge required of a gunner was “to know his peece, to know the powder and how to make saltpeter, to know the despart of his metall, to lay his peece, to know the carriages, to use the quadrant, to shoot at poynt blanke and at diverse angles of random, to make laddles and cartridges, to make fireworks and gabions.”13 All these subjects are present in Ms. Fr. 640.

Calibers

“To know his peece” meant primarily to know the different types of cannons and their calibers, which takes up fols. 21r–28r in Ms. Fr. 640. There were four families of cannons: culverins, cannons, falcons, and mortars. Each weapon had its proper use: cannons to open a breach during a siege, culverins for breaches too, but from afar with more powder, mortars for parabolic shots over walls, potentially with incendiary projectiles, and lighter and mobile falcons and falconets to be used on the field (campaign artillery) alongside infantry and cavalry, or to destroy besieged gunners.

Gun size had been stabilized under Henri II when his commissaries (among whom was Jean d’Estrées) defined the 6 calibers of France, but no real standardization was actually achieved for a long while. Bolognese engineer and military author Francesco De Marchi left a detailed description of the French reform in a report to the Duke of Parma dated 1553.14 The six types of caliber in this report were the cannon and the demi-cannon (counting for one), the culverin, the bastard-culverin, the sacre, the falcon, and the falconet. Rationalizing the calibers was a means to save money because it standardized ammunition. Around 1580, Abra Raconis described precisely the six categories for the Paris arsenal, near the Celestins convent.15 In the early seventeenth century, in Benedit Vassalieu’s Receuil du règlement général de l’ordre et conduite de l’artillerie (1613), the sacre disappears but the other categories remain (Fig. 1).

Standardization was in fact a Europe-wide phenomenon, with the Italians and Spaniards imitating the French. The English had already defined ten calibers in 1543 under Henry VIII, and again in the 1590s, Cyprian Lucar reduced them to 9 categories (double cannon, cannon, half cannon, quarter-cannon, culverin, sacre, minion, falcon, falconet). The proportions and figures given by sixteenth-century English treatises can be compared with the data provided by a French manuscript by Abra Raconis on French calibers.16 Comparing the tables given in English sources to those of Raconis on French calibers reveals numerous discrepancies due both to their chronology and to the fact that, although different realms sought standardization internally, it did not extend beyond their borders.

Culverins

Culverins such as those described by the author-practitioner are characterized by their proportions, and especially by the ratio between their caliber and their length (volley): more or less 1:30. The long volley was used to guide the projectiles and to compress the gas of the explosion within the chamber during a longer period of time. Their length oscillated between 4 and 5 meters (18 pans) and their weight around 4 tons. They could shoot bullets weighing between 30 and 40 lbs. up to 4500 paces. Culverins were probably the costliest and most sophisticated weapons of their day (Fig. 2). At the end of the sixteenth century, they were much safer than previously, and, according to the Italian Girolamo Cataneo, they could fire up to 45 times a day.17 A demi-culverin (35 quintals, approx. 3 meters long) was less powerful and could only shoot bullets of 20 lbs. A bastard culverin of 30 quintals (shot: 15 lbs.) was generally used to destroy smaller pieces of fortification like gabions. The author-practitioner names these guns and their shot, but tends to use local units of measurement.18

It appears that gunners were especially aware of the particular use of their pieces. As a result, a siege was like a chess game in which pieces had characteristics that were powerful in combination. The abilities of culverins varied considerably. When one compares pieces kept in museums with the characteristics given by Ms. Fr. 640 and with the figures provided by Abra Raconis or De Marchi, it seems impossible to establish a standard rule. In theory, however, a bastard could shoot bullets of 18 lbs. and a demi-culverin, bullets of 12 lbs. The main inconvenience of the culverin was its cost. In the late sixteenth century, after a long debate involving, among others, Niccolò Tartaglia, the first great theoretician of ballistics, the efficiency of oversized volleys was challenged by specialists.19

Cannons

The second family of cannons were preferred for breaching shots (that is, shots capable of destroying a fortified wall) at short distance by means of batteries.20 Compared to culverins, cannons are a sort of compromise because, although they are shorter, they are also less cumbersome, more robust, and more mobile. The ratio between their caliber and their length is approximately 1:18 or 1:20. Their calibers were usually larger than those of culverins (10 to 40 lbs. for the double cannon) (Fig. 3, Fig. 4).

A cannon could fire about 80 times a day.21 According to Blaise de Vigenère, a French author and diplomat of the period also known for his interests in cryptography and for his Traicté du feu et du sel, there were two kinds of cannons circa 1560: simple cannons weighing 3000 lbs. and reinforced cannons weighing 5000 lbs. Each royal regulation for ordinary cannons (calibre de roi) defined the different sorts of cannons, and, within the family, distinguished between double cannons (used for the siege of Pignerol circa 1570), courtauds (a term coined in Burgundy), serpentines, and perriers (the name suggests they initially shot stone bullets).22 Since their range was quite limited (80 paces according to the author-practitioner), canons perriers were used to aim at ditch casemates.

The cannon fires at point-blank from five to six hundred paces. But not for battering, in which it can only perform well from two hundred paces, or three hundred paces at the most, and it should not be any farther. Its ball commonly weighs 40 lb of the King’s caliber. There is a cannon-perrier, which weighs xxv quintals, which are small, short cannons. It is for fighting the ditch casemates & for battering at close range. It bears a large ball of the King’s caliber like the others, namely of 40 lb (fol. 17r).

Mortars

Another category of pieces in the family of cannons was mortars. They were adapted to ballistic shots and are mentioned in inventories (in Amiens, for instance) and appear in contemporary representations, for example of the siege of Boulogne by Henry VIII, and the siege of Alesia by Melchior Feselen (Fig. 5).23 These mortars are not to be mistaken for the mortiers described on fols. 21v and 23v by the author-practitioner, which are named “pétard” in other parts of France (for instance in Cahors in 1580) and are explosive devices rather than cannons.

Other Guns

The “pièce de campagne,” or field piece, is altogether different:

The field piece weighs ten or twelve quintals & is ten pans long or 12. Its ball weighs vi or vii lb & its charge is 4 lb of powder […] Their caliber is also small, which makes the breech 3 balls thick. They are used for following a camp promptly & for the defense of cities & houses, putting them on the walls or on a tower (fol. 18v).

This piece of artillery figured as an intermediary between the cannon and the falcon, weighing 10 to 12 quintals, measuring about 2.2 meters and shooting bullets of 6 to 7 lbs. It also relates to the passe-volant which is just a bit smaller but shoots bullets of 2 lbs. The main characteristic of these pieces, which could be drawn by only two horses, was their extreme mobility. In the municipal archives of Toulouse, these pieces are frequently mentioned from 1562 to 1605.24 The author-practitioner describes them on fol. 18v:

The passe-volant weighs vi quintals, is eight or nine pans long. Its ball weighs two lb, &, for its charge, a lb & a half of powder. At the breech it has three balls & two in the front. Two horses can move it, for a single horse does not begin to move a piece. It is used for the defense of houses, or for taking among the infantry to break a rank of cavalry.

Some archaic pieces also survived in late sixteenth-century Toulouse, like iron “fowlers,” which the author-practitioner mentions as canons a chambre on fol. 24v. Fowlers are those which have reinforcements on the interior in the entire space which contains the powder. Fowlers, forged rather than cast, with their removable chambers, belong to a tradition going back to the previous century and were fairly cheap to produce.

Falcons

Another family of guns is that of falcons. The animal denominations, with pieces named after snakes and birds of prey, is anthropologically meaningful. The esmeril, the falcon, the falconet, the sacre, were birds of prey used by aristocrats for hunting. As artillery pieces, they were easy to move, could approach close to the walls, and could be used to back up infantry or cavalry units. They could also defend fortresses, as Raconis mentions: “Falconets, arquebus à croc, muskets and other small pieces, are of such calibers that are used for defending a place or for a war campaign.”25 This accords with the mode of use described by the author-practitioner, who added that they were useful for the defense of fortified houses (maisons fortes):

The falconet weighs 4 quintals, is x pans long. Its ball weighs one lb & a quarter, its charge is half a lb of powder. At the breech three balls, at the front, two. To move it, two horses, although such pieces are hardly moved insofar as they only serve to defend houses. The ones which are carted around either for battery or for siege combat, are cannons, culverins, medium-sized bastardes & field pieces (fol. 18v).

The author-practitioner ends his description with arquebus à croc (wall guns) (Fig. 6), double muskets, and muskets, that is, pieces served by two or even only one person for the defense of places (not to be mistaken for the personal arquebus). Their weight (from 1 to 2 quintals) forbids use on the field.

Ms. Fr. 640 also mentions artillery organs (fol. 22v):

Then one mounts them on a medium thick board close to one g another & one makes notches on the board this way & through these notches one passes the hook of the arquebus which has a hole through which one passes iron pegs from under the board. Then, when one wants to aim it, one mounts the middle one & that on both sides. If you want to make a double row, or triple or quadruple or more, you need only place a similar row of boards like the first, one on the other. For the other This kind of orgues penetrates & is quite stable. For the other ones which are made of one piece like a solid square have cannons only three four one pan & a half long. Also, they only serve for making a salvo at an entry or for defending a door.

These curious devices, assembling diverse tubes of arquebuses, were especially useful for the defense of gates. Leonardo da Vinci’s drawings of artillery organs are particularly famous, but such pieces, called ribaudequins in France and Burgundy, were in use in Northern Europe decades before Leonardo drew them. Recently, in the Musée de l’Armée, Paris, a ribaudequin from the 1550s was reconstructed out of surviving tubes, which provides a sense of what the author-practitioner might have seen in Toulouse (Fig. 7).

Elements of the “peece”

A gunner also had to be knowledgeable about the diverse elements of a piece, such as breech, volley, mouth or muzzle, trunnions, excess metal, bore or barrel, and the author-practitioner rehearses all these:

There are three reinforcement rings on a cannon, one at the breech, one in the middle, one at the mouth. When founders want to work on their craft or render the cannon or other piece lighter & easier to cart around, they make it from the ring at the breech to the ring in the middle of the thickness & proportion of the balls, as is said above. But from the ring in the middle to the ring of the mouth & they diminish it one line, or more or less, on each side, always taking their measurements with the compass from the straight line which they make in the middle of the model of paper or otherwise (fol. 25v).

Interestingly, the author-practitioner refers here to the transmission of this knowledge through written expertise and the use of the compass.

Gun Casting, Boring Techniques, and Shot

Each piece had so many singular characteristics that a gunner had to become accustomed to its individuality in order to serve it: not only to learn its balance but also to understand the quality of the chamber, that is, whether the piece was well bored, with a regular windage (the space between the bullet and the chamber). For that operation, the gunner could use special instruments: parallel staffs, or a metal instrument to grip the piece in every place to know the thickness of the metal (fol. 25v). A too-large volley could induce a loss of pressure and diminish the speed of the bullet. An irregular volley could block the bullet and make the piece explode. Irregularities within the volley would also jeopardize the life of the gunner because hollows or protrusions could fix a bit of grit that would ignite the gunpowder at the wrong time. Controlling the windage was thus essential.
Wadding and ramming in charging were the other conditions of a good shot. This explains why each piece of artillery was supplemented by pieces of equipment used by gunners and mates: powder ladles, sponges, rammers, and pieces for the carriage. The ladles were especially important as a means to unburden the gunners from measuring the necessary loads:

One charges with the ladle all pieces from the cannon to the piece of four quintals de char , and one gives them two ladles of cannon powder or one & a half of arquebus powder. To smaller ones, one only gives one ladle, & those of one quintal are charged with a small charge (fol. 18v).

A lanterne or ladle was a sort of semi-cylindrical shovel open at the top, attached to a staff the length of the barrel channel. It constituted an early form of gauging. The gunner had to load the ladle to the end of the gun bore and then turn the ladle upside down so that the black-powder would stay within the barrel. The gesture had to be precise enough not to spread any grains of powder along the chamber. Ladles were also used to measure the volume of powder, thus each piece had its own ladle. Luis Collado’s Italian treatise on gunnery offers schemes of ladles shown in proportion to a certain number of bullets, which is exactly what Ms. Fr. 640 also describes on fol. 18v.26

Cartridges (cartouches or gargousses) went a step further, although at the cost of a necessary preparation of paper or canvas: “Some have invented to load the cannons with cartridges” (fol. 25r).

Bullets are also described in Ms. Fr. 640 (fol. 26r and 27v) and even drawn to scale for each type of piece, although instruments called gunner’s calipers existed that could have spared the gunner that drawing exercise.27 They were like a pair of compasses with curved branches to estimate the size of bullets.

Casting Shot and Guns

Sections of Ms. Fr. 640 are devoted to the making of pig iron with a sophisticated furnace.28 The pig iron could be cast on a flat floor within molds shaped as half-bullets. Elements of furrows similar to the one described by Ms. Fr. 640 have survived at Panningridge (Sussex), where bullets were made. Half-molds were found in other excavations at Batsford and at Brightling and Tickeridge.29 Alternatively, the bullet could be shaped by hammering into shape and filing with soft iron. It seems that some bullets were also cast in bronze, although it was more costly:

Some make a mold of the caliber of the piece, & temper it, then beat a soft iron piece cut to size, & when this piece is very red, they beat it into the mold & round it off with a large file, considering these balls to be stronger than the melted ones which, being brittle, are more subject to breaking (fol. 16r).

Ms. Fr. 640 also alludes to gun casting through considerations about the proportions of metals used (fol. 17r). Most cannons in the sixteenth century were made of bronze, although from the 1540s there were some marginal experiments in Normandy (Breteuil) on less expensive guns made of cast-iron (naval artillery). Some archaic forged guns also survived in some arsenals belonging to local aristocrats. However, cannons made of bronze were more robust and could withstand the forces created by the explosion of huge loads of powder and by the percussion of iron bullets within the volley. A new alloy was necessary that contained a reduced portion of tin in comparison to the alloy previously used for bells (80/20). Ms. Fr. 640 is especially valuable because those proportions are detailed for each kind of piece.

The pieces were made of copper, tin, and rosette copper (reddish copper). Tin plus rosette copper produced an alloy called metal. A falconet belonging to the town of Saint-Lys that was made in Toulouse, shown at the Musée des Augustins during the exhibition “Toulouse Renaissance,” shows the reddish color of the metal (Fig. 8).30

Strangely, although the author was probably himself an expert in small casting techniques, the text does not describe the process of casting cannons. It provides no clues about the molds, nor about the furnaces, nor the casting pits, although in an entry on “Foundry casting” (fol. 15r) he does mention that “one casts large works such as artillery, bells and similar.” There were significant differences between the casting of large and small objects, and, where the author-practitioner experiments constantly with the casting of small objects, his observations about gun casting are less experimental and more documentary, a tone that suggests he only had second-hand knowledge of gun founding.

To gain more information about the casting and construction of guns, we must turn to Abra Raconis’s treatise which described a process very similar to that given by Leonardo da Vinci in Milan in the 1490s, with clay molds and models strengthened with horse hair and bands of iron.31 The author of Ms. Fr. 640 concentrates rather on the recognition of good alloys, for instance in the following passage:

One recognizes the good alloy of a piece by seeing it. For if, with the greenness which they expel on the surface, they redden, that is to say that they are composed of a sufficient quantity of rosette. If not, they are only of metal, which shows up whitish. If they themselves do not make this demonstration, scratch & you will see.

The good alloy for pieces is of three parts of rosette & one of the fine metal of la large bells, where there is more rosette than in the metal of small bells. The metal commonly costs xv lb & fine rosette xv or xvi (fol. 17r).

Gun Boring

If casting techniques used in the arsenals of Paris and Malines are not described by the author-practitioner, one passage of Ms. Fr. 640 deals precisely with gun-boring techniques:

They are very difficult to bore when they are of metal because the substance is brittle & frangible. But one z ought not to push the borer with too great a force nor continue all in one push, but pull it out often. In that case, one makes the touch-hole muzzle of the cannon bigger than the ball in order to avoid the necessity of boring, because the substance is cast & expands & pushes the mold. If the borer, which should be neither like a swallow tail nor a point but be cut in the round like a wimble, if it breaks, one ought not to leave it there to rust (fol. 99v)

Such techniques were described in the 1540s by Vannoccio Biringuccio in his Pirotechnia, although the boring process was horizontal.32 In German treatises, by contrast, it was vertical. No details are given in Ms. Fr. 640 on these issues; however, the author-practitioner provides a very detailed description of the drilling tool at the end of the boring bar: sixteen cutters fixed on a cylindrical shape acting together on the bore, while Biringuccio only mentions four cutters (Fig. 9). It perhaps indicates something new to the author-practitioner.

Laying siege

Artillery and undermining techniques used to take a city or a fortress is another important theme of Ms. Fr. 640. Most vivid here is the description of a battery and its platform. Thomas Digges, in Stratioticos, stated that “the inequalitie of the ground or platforme, and wheeles of the carriadge reculing” was a great cause of problems for a gunner, as much as irregular boring or the tempering of the piece hot or cold.33 The author-practitioner was well aware of such problems when he wrote:

Once one is close to the place one wants to batter, the gunner goes at night to reconnoiter the most convenient place to mount the battery & if he does not see well, one gives a false alarm in order that those on the inside throw torches. Very often, one batters where it is strongest because the assailed, disdaining the battery, do not make repairs there. Commonly, there are not many garrets & other defenses in that place. And when one has made a breach at the strongest part & the cannon removed, it will surprise the assailed. One approaches fortified towns at night, but to poorly fortified towns, pioneers bring gabions and once these are placed, one puts some boards or planks behind the gabions to keep the pioneers safe, while behind, they fill the gabions with some earth by means of their shovels. And while one does this, one raises some false alarm on another side. And before, one has fired a volley at the defenses. [The gabions] The pioneers carry the boards to make a level ground or a platform of four fingers or half a foot thick, and one sets them into the earth. The platform is five or six cano feet larger than the wheels of the cannon on each side m if there is only one cannon. But if there are more, the platforms touch. The distance which is between the pieces is the width of two gabions (fol. 24v).

This text can be better understood by comparing it to an illustration inserted by Benedict Vassalieu in his manuscript Treatise offered to Duke Gaston d’Orléans in the 1620s (Fig. 10). First of all, a good battery needed a leveled ground and a regular platform made of strong planks. These planks, brought by pioneers, had to be lightly inclined in the direction of the target so that it would be less tiring to put the cannons back in place after their recoil. To protect the pieces, a gunner had to build gabions, which were cylindrical wicker baskets filled with earth or stone. Gabions were made of willow or nut-tree branches. Trenches were also sometimes needed to set up or to visit a battery. Engineers’ manuals of the time explain that these trenches had to zig-zag. In Vassalieu’s manuscript, the bullet-proof plank covering the gun-port, described by the author-practitioner, is visible. Buried powder magazines, well behind the pieces, are also visible, as well as the traditional gunners’ tools: rammers to push down the powder, as well as hay, ladles, sponges, and even buffers, which were thin cylindrical pieces of wood inserted between the ball and the black powder. Buffers were sometimes required for cannon because the shortness of the volley could not optimize the compression of the gas by the bullet alone.

Most batteries shoot at fortifications from a short distance (about 200 paces). The description of the diverse pieces already made this point. This means that the cannon, for instance, generally shot at “poynt blanke” with the barrel, however, bigger pieces like culverins or double cannon could shoot from a second line, and, in that case, aiming at the targets with a certain degree of precision was highly necessary.34

Then a gunner loads it with a ladle, & with the rammer which is at len the other end of the ladle, two men ram the powder as much as they can, in nine or ten pushes, until they find the powder to be hard & well-compacted. Next, another puts hay or straw and the others ram a little. Another puts the ball and the hay-bearer puts in as much of it as before & one rams a little. Some put wooden bungs but this runs the risk of making the piece burst. And where one has to fire many cannon shots, one would need many bungs, which would cost much to carry. Once the cannon is loaded, xx or xxv pioneers push it forward with bars, keeping to one side behind the gabions without taking off the board until the cannon reaches it. Once the cannon is in front, the gunner mounts it with the wedges which are at the back, taking the sight on each side and then in the middle. Then another who is nearby gives fire. Each gunner cannon needs 3 gunners: the loader, the one who aims, the one who gives fire. Sometimes, against a camp or into a breach, one shoots cartridges, made as you have elsewhere, or else one uses a basan full of flint stones, cart nails & similar (fol. 25r).

Generally, for aiming, gunners needed no instruments apart from the cannon itself and a sight mounted at the breech end. However, to shoot at night (with no vision of the target), the author-practitioner admits that a quadrant was useful (fol. 21r). The idea is to control the required elevation of the piece during the day or briefly at night, using fireworks, and then reproducing the shot even with no vision, thanks to a quadrant. The quadrant is a very basic instrument that had been described by Niccolò Tartaglia in the 1530s (Fig. 11).

It is a set square made of two rulers assembled with a graduated quarter-circle plate and a plummet. William Bourne, an English master gunner and contemporary of the author-practitioner, explained how to use it:

Take a quadrant, with a rule fast thereunto, and put the rule into the mouth of the peece, and coyne the breech of the peece up and downe, untill the plummet hang at the corner of the Quadrant, and then shall the Concavitie of the peece, lye right with the Horyzon, neyther higher nor lower: then shoote off the peece against the butte: if the shotte bee under the marke, it is more then the right line, then you must bringe the peece neerer unto the butte, banke or marke: but if it be the just height of the marke, then remoove the peece farther off from the marke, and so removing the peece forwardes and backwardes, you shal finde the true right line of the peece.35

If one knows that a target can be reached with a piece for a given elevation, one can then construct a table of ranges and elevations, and can precisely adjust the elevation of the piece, reading it on the quarter of a circle of the quadrant thanks to the plummet line.

Often, battery fire was not sufficient to destroy a gate or a bastion, thus elite soldiers had to practice what they called an “escalade” and use explosives. The Toulouse text describes two kinds of these: the petard (curiously called mortier by the author-practitioner), and the buckram sausage. Both devices were more or less described by Hanzelet in his Pyrotechnie. Petards were commonly used to destroy doors or gates. Hanzelet explained that petards had to be made of red copper (rosette) with 1/10 of yellow copper but they could also be made of an alloy of lead and tin. Petards were filled with gunpowder and closed by a wooden buffer (in the manuscript, the shape of the buffer is that of a half-bullet). According to Hanzelet, petards were fixed on doors with some kind of fork (Fig. 12).

According to the author-practitioner, the structure of petards is somewhat different: their shape was cylindrical and their length was about 40 cm, with a content of 25 lbs. of gunpowder. The alloy proportion was also different: 4 quintals copper for 1 quintal of the kind of metal used for bells. To form a buffer, a half-bullet was covered with an iron cross. There was no fork to fix this device, but instead four drilling screws passing through four handles:

Such a petard is made which weighs three quintals, carries a cannonball flat on the side on which it must exit & round on the inside of the cannon, as if it were a cannonball cut in half. It is two pans long. It is charged with xxv lb of powder for at it needs to be full up to the mouth & it needs to burst. It does not have a different thickness at the breech than at the mouth & is all of one piece. Its substance needs to be better than that of pieces, & for 4 quintals of fine copper there ought to be only one quintal of metal in order that it holds the blast, &, bursting with more force, has a greater effect. It is for putting against a door with a large iron cross in front of the ball, and s once loaded, it needs to be covered with a well-sewn thick canvas which should be anointed c all over with turpentine. It needs to have four handles, made while it is founded, for it is thus easier to place. The iron cross is joined to the mouth with the canvas with which it is covered. To place it, it The handles must be kept at the edge of its muzzle, as you see. To place it, one needs three or four iron pegs one pan long & as thick as a finger which should have their point like a wimble gimlet, & the entire leg as a screw, like an auger, & a ring on the other end to turn them with a short stick which has play within the ring. And the pegs are placed in the door but not straight but for they would not have any strength, but at an angle as if you wanted to fix them toward the middle of the petard, and to do this, the hole of the handles needs to be quite large. In this way, the firing petard pushes the pegs along & across into the door & makes more of a breach (fol. 23r).

“Sausages” made of fabric were used in complement with petards, probably to act as a kind of fuse to cross a ditch. They were sacks of linen moisted with turpentine oil, vinegar, and aqua vitae. In Hanzelet’s book, sausages are also used to fill up a casemate’s gun ports. The anonymous author-practitioner provides more details about the sausage’s making and its use:36

take eight or nine canes of buckram strip or more if the ditch is larger, and let the strip be four or five fingers wide. Have it well sewn in such a way that it is like a gut through lwhich a stick as thick as a finger can pass. Fill it completely with good powder. When the sausage is full, dip it well in lightly, to wet the canvas & not the powder, in two parts of vinegar & of one of eau-de-vie. Next, let the said canvas dry well, and then one needs to join to the said sausage a long string or cord which should be two times longer than the sausage, & one ought not to tighten it to the sausage but only to bind it to it at both ends & to sew it on the sausage in a few places in the middle. Next one needs to anoint the entire sausage with very good turpentine, and at whichever end of it you wish to join to the touch-hole of the petard for giving fire, you can sew a or tightly attach a large strip of canvas one empan wide which should also be well soaked in turpentine; & this strip must be split in the middle in order to wrap & tie it around the petard, and one needs to attach it in such a way that the touch-hole should be close to u mthe the tip of the sausage, which should be open, should be joined to the touch-hole of the petard (fol. 23r–v) [Fig. 13].

Defending a Place

Other sections of Ms. Fr. 640 consider ways to defend a place. Artillery organs could be useful to defend a gate, but grenades and mortars as well as mutton skins filled with stones or sharp metal pieces were also used in defense:

There is nothing that has a greater effect for the defense of a breach than petards placed at the entryway at the time of the assault. They carry a lot of ammunition. be One fills them with cart nails, large steel dice, bits of chain & similar things. And when one senses that the enemies are close, one sets fire to all. Grenades, too, well-made and aptly thrown, cause great damage. And to make them worse, one puts coarsely pestling pestled glass either in the molds or in the crucible, when one wants to cast them; this worsens the wounds. The grenades ought not to be too brittle. But it is good that they be somewhat alloyed with the substance of the pieces in order that, holding the blast a little rather than breaking, they have more violence (Fol. 21v).

The concept of shrapnel with all sorts of sharp objects, even pieces of broken glass, was, it seems, well understood here. Hanzelet, and many English or Italian authors, devote many pages to conceptions for similar grenades and fireworks. Some sixteenth-century grenades can be seen at the Army Museum in Brussels.

Artillery Logistics

Every piece of artillery depended upon extensive and complex logistics. First, there were the pieces necessary for fixing mounts and carriages. The wheels of cannons were incredibly complex pieces of technology, using different metallic elements.

P A ch cannon must not move unless it has two harnesses, be they of wood or iron. And to furnish the wheels, powder box, and other furniture of the cannon, one needs seven quintals and a half of iron (fol. 23r).

Second, there were the horses (up to 25 for double cannons in Ms. Fr. 640, or 8 for a falcon). Thanks to Vassalieu’s treatise, we understand how horses were harnessed to the carriage. Some American film footage of the 1930s showing a mechanical harvester in a field gives us other clues.37 The drawing power of horses also served to draw carts and wagons carrying bullets, cartridges, powder barrels, individual weapons, and instruments. An idea of the size of ammunition trains can be gained from the fact that a double cannon could fire about thirty to forty times a day, and a falcon one hundred times.

Each piece was served by at least one gunner and two gunner’s mates, and dozens of wheelwrights, carpenters, coopers, smiths, fletchers, and wagonwrights were also attached to the service of an artillery train (see Fig. 10 showing an artillery battery). Around a gun, there was commonly a master gunner, equipped with a lintstock to light the fuse, different mates using all sorts of tools (ladle, sponge, rammer, worm, etc.) to load the powder and the shot and to clean up the barrel, and some other mates to displace the piece with levers.

Sappers were needed to enlarge narrow passages, to clear the pathways and to dig up trenches (see Fig. 10). According to the author-practitioner, 100 pionniers (or “sappers”) were required for a single large cannon (fol. 24v). This is confirmed by the numbers of sappers sent to the entrenched camps around Toulouse and salaried by the city.38

Period account books also show that camps often possessed spare wheels because, despite their robustness, they constantly broke.39 The large number of horses can be seen in the account books of Toulouse’s “state of artillery” from 1562 to 1615 (Archives municipales de Toulouse, CC and EE series), where the amount of money paid for horses and carts is truly bewildering: 7 livres 10 sols by day and by horse. For instance, to provide ammunition for the Camp of Cornusson in 1577, 83 horses were needed, the cost of which was 622 livres (EE51).

Conclusion

The author-practitioner offers rare testimony about the materiality of artillery at the end of the sixteenth century that is comparable to Leonardo da Vinci’s testimony about Milan’s arsenal at the end of the fifteenth century. Although the author-practitioner was not himself a gun founder, he was obviously very familiar with the community. His detailed account raises the question of whether he would have been able to enter the space of the Arsenal, which was ordinarily subject to entry restrictions. He must have been acquainted with important people in the city government and perhaps with the “commissaire à l’artillerie” who in 1595 was Jehan de Gout. The author-practitioner may not have had authorisation to enter the Arsenal, which could explain the absence of precise information on the actual founding of guns. Although his knowledge about the casting pits seems to be meager, he provides detailed information on alloys and drilling tools.

On fols. 17r and 49r, we also learn that the author-practitioner was in touch with master founders in the Toulouse Arsenal, the Poncet family. Nicolas, Jean, and Étienne were at that time founders in Toulouse, and Étienne had the title of gunner.40 Along with many other artisans, the Poncet lived near the river, in the capitoulat “la Daurade,” close to the grounds of an abbey (Fig. 14).41 In the range of his knowledge and his interest in casting and weaponry, the author-practitioner resembles an artisan like Barthélémy Fraysse who was a cannon founder and a gunner, and participated in siege warfare.42 Besides contact with founders, it would appear that the author-practitioner was on rather friendly terms with the military. The interest in weaponry may even suggest that the author-practitioner was involved in the political life of Toulouse, perhaps a Catholic concerned with the fight against the heretics.
