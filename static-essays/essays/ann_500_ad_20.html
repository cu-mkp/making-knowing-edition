Mr Toad’s Wild Ride: From Bestiary to Shop Manual

By DOI: https://doi.org/10.7916/3dck-4657
← Back to Essays

Abstract

In sixteenth-century Europe, animals often served as allegorical embodiments of moral principles, while toads and vipers, in particular, were valued primarily for their putative medicinal value. In a marked departure from these conventions, the author-practitioner records his thoughts on capturing, restraining, maintaining, and killing these animals. Although not a natural historian by vocation, in the course of casting amphibians and reptiles from life into objects of art, he amassed considerable knowledge of the habits and characteristics of this often spurned class of animals, especially of vipers. The few people who dealt with these animals—including other artisans, viper catchers, and apothecaries—did not record their observations. In this essay, I survey the author-practitioner’s forays into herpetological husbandry, capture techniques, and methods of euthanasia. I compare these observations with the few other accounts from the time, which do not contain the detail or accuracy found in Ms. Fr. 640.

Introduction

Some years ago I came across the Making and Knowing Project’s edition-in-development of BnF Ms. Fr. 640 as part of my research into the role of vipers in Renaissance Florence.1 As a former reptile keeper at the Smithsonian’s National Zoo, and now a docent at the National Gallery of Art in Washington DC, it was refreshing to find a text that, for its time, treated reptiles in a straightforward manner. With few exceptions, the herpetological observations in Ms. Fr. 640 are non-judgmental and offered solely as practical advice on obtaining, maintaining, euthanizing, and ultimately casting these animals into objects of art. In this essay, I explore the author-practitioner’s approach to reptiles and amphibians and compare his views not only to the prevailing schools of thought in sixteenth-century Europe but to later opinions as well—up to those of the present day.

Scientific writing in Europe began dealing with nature as a legitimate topic of discourse in about the mid-sixteenth century. Prior to that time, responses to nature were often recorded only in the course of (1) an investigation of another subject, such as the Christian moralizing of medieval bestiaries;2 (2) concocting remedies for maladies; or (3) repelling or trapping various animals.3 Herpetological references were scarce and fleeting.

This should not come as a revelation; reptiles and amphibians in Europe were never valued as food—apart from their medicinal value, there was little impetus to study them.4 For instance, the Hortus Sanitatis, the compendium of healing that appeared in the late fifteenth century, described not only plants but the animal species of the natural world, focusing on their medicinal value.5

As the publication of herbals proliferated in Europe, the study of animals struggled to establish a firm footing in the universities and medical curricula of the period—it was often a poor cousin to botany.6 By 1560, however, natural history as a discipline had crystallized.7 The appearance of Conrad Gesner’s (1516–1565) monumental Historiae Animalium (1551–58) laid the groundwork for the study of animals that would become known as scientific zoology. Considered from that perspective to be the first modern zoological work, its aim was to survey the entire animal kingdom. Over 500 pages, however, were devoted to the history and care of horses, cows, dogs, goats, and chickens.

Even so, very few natural history texts went into multiple printings. One notable exception was Pietro Andrea Mattioli’s (1501–1577) Commentaries on the materia medica of Dioscorides, first published in 1544. Within a quarter-century, 30,000 copies of this herbal had already been sold.8 Given its immense popularity, it comes as no surprise that Ms. Fr. 640 contains a reference to Mattioli (fol. 35v) vouching for the safety of two bitter herbs (one of which is, ironically, toxic). The Commentaries contained approximately three dozen herpetological references—usually noting which plants either counteracted poisoning from snakes or toads or repelled these bothersome creatures.

Toadstones and Toads

One interesting comment in Ms. Fr. 640 (fol. 13v) seems to reflect the pharmacological wisdom of the age. It describes the Toadstone as

The kind which comes from the head of the toad & has the figure of the toad painted naturally [….] It is held that if one puts the powder of it on a brazier in the chamber of some persons at night, they will be neither able to move, nor speak, nor thwart thieves.

The belief in a magical stone found in the head of a toad was widespread in Europe. We see it illustrated in a woodcut (Fig. 1) from the Hortus Sanitatis. This legendary stone was held to be a remedy against plague and other ailments. The notion had such traction that the eighteenth-century French chemist, Nicholas Lémery (1645–1715), felt compelled to set the record straight and affirm that the stones were, in actuality, the molar teeth from a West Indian species of Marine fish.9 Toadstones, as well as other variations of prepared toads, remained popular in the use of amulets to ward off pestilential diseases. These remedies eventually lost favor in the eighteenth century, perhaps owing to the disappearance of bubonic plague from western Europe.10

Except for their questionable therapeutic value, toads have long been held in contempt.11 The author-practitioner’s brusque comment “If by chance you want to mold this vile animal…” (fol. 109v) reflects the strong antipathy toward toads in Europe.

From the second century, bestiaries like the Physiologus employed toads and other animals in their allegories to promote Christian values. The third-century author Aelian (ca. 170–ca. 235 CE) claimed that if a man gazed at a toad, it would stare back, breathe on him, and sicken him.12 Even Leonardo da Vinci (1452–1519), borrowing from the fourteenth-century best seller the Fior di virtù, writes this of avarice: “The toad feeds on earth and always remains lean; because it never eats enough—it is so afraid lest it should want for earth.”13

The toad’s reputation did not improve over time. Carl Linnaeus (1707–1778), father of binomial nomenclature, demonstrated his aversion to snakes and toads in several instances, including this description from his 1758 Systema Naturae:

…most amphibians are rough with a cold body, ghastly color, foul skin, fierce face […], foul odor, harsh call, squalid habitat and terrible venom. Their Author [God] has not, therefore done much boasting on their account.14

Well into the following century a French naturalist felt the need to enlighten us to the fact that “Toads are squat and disagreeable in shape […] it is difficult to comprehend why nature has stamped [it] with so repulsive a form.”15 It would appear, then, that the author-practitioner is in good company. Beyond aesthetics, however, a curious allegation on fol. 109v of Ms. Fr. 640 asserts that

if [the toad] were to piss in your hand, it would burn your hand & itch, as if you had handled nettles. But the most powerful remedy for this is to put your hand into fresh earth & to cover it as if you wanted to bury it.

This treatment apparently works for snakes as well, since we are advised on fol. 110r that upon being bitten or fouled by a snake one should “bury his hand in fresh & damp earth [….] [which] pulls out all the venom and nothing else. It is the same with toad’s venom.” It is difficult to divine the origins of this fallacy, since toad urine is not caustic, and if it were, hand washing would be a more effective recourse than dirt. Nevertheless, this notion persisted in folk wisdom and was perpetuated in one form or another even by learned scholars. Linnaeus remarked in his diary that when bitten by a snake “The peasants […] submerge the injured limb in the soil.”16 To his credit, Linnaeus also observed that “it is possible […] if one seizes the [toad] with forceps, then she pisses from each wart a white juice, which damages and poisons.”17 It is true that, while toad urine is benign, the glandular secretions are toxic. What is puzzling is that the author-practitioner, who had surely handled many toads in the course of his practice, would have failed to make that distinction.

Making the Catch

Aside from several technical entries on the molding of toads and frogs, the author-practitioner has little else to say on these animals. That there is no discussion of capturing or maintaining toads signifies that this endeavor is so unremarkable that its details just go without saying—toads are relatively easy to find and probably one of the easiest animals to catch. This is assuredly not the case with vipers and lizards. Consequently, Ms. Fr. 640 contains a trove of observations regarding characteristics, habits, and capture techniques of these reptiles.

Old tropes, such as “the only good snake is a dead snake,” die hard. A print from Mattioli’s Commentaries shows a farmer in the background preparing to kill a snake (Fig. 2). Unlike this farmer, the author-practitioner does not have the option of maiming the snake—the specimen must be in pristine condition—intact and suitable for molding. This being the overriding goal, considerable attention is given to non-damaging capture techniques, none of which involve beating the animal to death with a branch or scythe.

The author-practitioner takes pains to reassure us that, unless they are large, snakes seldom bite when taken (fol. 110r); “water snakes hardly bite” (fol. 112r); and that, according to the crayfishers, “they [vipers] hardly ever bite in water” (fol. 107r). I find these statements perplexing, given that European vipers are terrestrial and water snakes feed on fish—which they will bite and eat while submerged. From personal experience, I can affirm that water snakes will bite underwater.

On more solid ground, the author-practitioner prompts the reader to use a thick woolen cloth to capture snakes since the teeth are too small to penetrate the fabric (fol. 107r). He offers additional hints (fol. 110r), such as placing one’s foot on the snake, close to the neck, and grabbing it close to the head because the snake

cannot bite if it does not have the means to stretch its neck & make a sinuous & oblique turn, & it cannot harm with a direct attack, since he cannot move forward in a direct line, but writhes in the shape of an S.

This is sound advice. Another technique I have used, particularly with larger snakes, is to grab the tail (which is often the last thing you see before the animal scoots off), gently sling the snake between the your legs (clamping the legs together), and slowly pull the snake forward until you can restrain the head. I should add, this is not advised for venomous snakes, and it would be a good idea to wear heavy jeans.18

The snakes caught by the author-practitioner were destined for lifecasting. Objects with lifecast flora and fauna were very much sought after in the sixteenth century, and they graced many a Kunstkammer (chamber of art) collection in European courts and cities.19 Many such objects are ornamented with small grass snakes (Natrix natrix), often all about the same size. These were probably caught by snake catchers in brood nests of these snakes that appear every spring. We might speculate that the experience of catching snakes in such a nest is captured and fixed in metal in the mass of writhing snakes at the base of the Nautilus Ewer pictured in Fig. 3, Fig. 4, Fig. 5, Fig. 6.20

On fol. 107r the author-practitioner acknowledges that “The lizard is more tedious to catch by hand than the snake & bites without letting go & grips like pincers.” Therefore, he recommends noosing lizards with a string fashioned into a slip-knot while having some assistants distract the lizard by whistling. This 400-year-old technique is remarkably similar to current practice, although herpetologists are continually refining the procedure.21

There is considerable detail on fol. 111v on restraining lizards after capture, such as, “one takes them with two fingers from the sides of the head […] makes them open their mouth […] takes the end of its lower mandible with the end of the fingernail… [and so forth].” He seems to be concerned that one not get bitten because “[the lizard] clenches very strongly. And if he were to bite living flesh it would not let go, & there would be no better remedy than to promptly cut off his head.” Clearly, that would defeat the objective. It is true that large lizards have a strong bite and can be extremely reluctant to release their grip; however, there are less draconian ways to persuade them to disengage, such as immersion in water or pouring spirits into their mouth. At the National Zoo we used a commercially available mouthwash which, when sprayed into the mouth, effected a quick release.

Hunting Vipers

Prior to the modern era, reptile capture had never been treated with the level of detail seen in Ms. Fr. 640. This is in spite of the fact that vipers were an important commodity to the apothecaries of Europe. Vipers were an essential ingredient in theriac, a medicinal preparation dating back to Galen (129–ca. 200 CE), which remained the most sought-after antidote in Europe for the plague.22

There are numerous references in the literature to viper catchers, who hailed from a profession that tended to be hereditary and was not in the habit of publishing its methods. Their trade secrets and, more importantly, the location of hunting grounds were rarely divulged.23 Although modern herpetologists freely share information that furthers the profession, they may also be reluctant to publish details detrimental to their field work. A colleague of mine, for instance, has documented over 500 Timber Rattlesnake dens along the Appalachian Mountains. Whenever we went into the field together, I had to swear never to reveal the location of any dens we visited. There was a legitimate concern that visitors or unscrupulous snake collectors would disrupt the balance of those sensitive snake populations.

The dearth of information in the early literature on snake hunting makes two sixteenth-century illustrations even more remarkable. The first (Fig. 7) is of a viper catcher, which appeared in multiple editions of Mattioli’s Commentaries. Given the author-practitioner’s reference to Mattioli (fol. 35v), he may even have seen this image. The viper catcher we see in this rather idealized depiction is clearly not concerned about snakebite and is not using any tools, other than his hands. He is more comfortable than I would have been.

The second image (Fig. 8) is an engraving that pictures snake hunters in Italy using dogs, forked sticks, and wooden tongs to capture their quarry.

With the exception of the dogs and of the vipers feeding on toads, this tableau mirrors a description of viper catchers from The History of Drugs, a seventeenth-century tract compiled by Pierre Pomet (1658–1699), pharmacist to Louis XIV:

[vipers] are to be taken either by the End of the Tail, or else with a Pair of Tongs; because this Animal, when he finds himself squeezed, bites whatever he meets with […] the Reason why the Takers of them ought to be very cautious. The Keepers of them ought to be so likewise, lest by Mischance they should escape and get into any House, which would be very dangerous, especially where there are Children.24

Pomet also goes on to record that “Vipers breed in wild stony places in Dauphiny and Poitou” and that the peasants “take the Vipers in Spring Time or Autumn, because they are then fatter, and more active than in any other Season.” This accords with what we know of theriac production throughout the early modern period in Italy. Most sizable cities conducted a ceremony beginning in May, when vipers were captured and killed for that therapeutic potion.25

Other than the recognition that reptiles shelter underground in the winter (fols. 116r, 143r), Ms. Fr. 640 is silent on when and where to find vipers. The greatest likelihood of seeing vipers is in spring, when they are emerging from their hibernacula. On the other hand, they would be fatter in the fall after feeding all summer. Perhaps the author-practitioner, as many generations of vipers catchers had done before, felt this was a “secret” better passed on orally.

Observing Nature

The author-practitioner’s appreciation for the viper is palpable when he praises the animal in such terms as “It is the most beautiful snake that can be molded, because it has very beautiful scales…” (fol. 129r). In another entry he goes on to describe the viper further:

I molded a viper, which, for casting is more appropriate than any other snake because it has scales beautiful & very visible on all the body & principally on the head and under the throat. It has a flat head, the snout reddish […] & snubbed like the horned asp […] big jowls, the eyes very close to the snout, and the mouth wide open, where it has double canine teeth on each side, all coming out of a strip of flesh that covers and clothes them.… (fol. 125v).

This description suggests that he is familiar with not only the Asp Viper (Vipera aspis) found in Toulouse, but with other horned vipers, such as the Lataste’s Viper (V. latasti), which is found in Spain.26 The accuracy of this observation stands in contrast with another statement on fol. 107r that reads, “The dangerous [snakes] are recognized by their blue & azure eyes.” Note that Fig. 9 clearly shows the red eyes and vertically elliptical pupils found in all European vipers, which Linnaeus correctly describes as “The iris colored as fire, the pupil vertical and black.” It is not clear what the author-practitioner was referring to—perhaps by “blue” he had in mind the eyes of a snake about to shed its skin. Since all snakes do that, however, this is not a helpful diagnostic trait.27

Another discordant note in the margin of fol. 124v is, “The principal thing is that the ears of the snake come out well-molded.” Surely the author-practitioner must have known that snakes lack external ears. The author-practitioner is on firmer ground in noting that vipers have fangs and that “Other snakes have a double row of teeth” (fol. 125v). He goes on to accurately describe the tongue sheath on the lower jaw as a “tube of flesh, made like a dog’s penis, from which comes its stinger.” I do not recall this particular comparison having ever been made.

Also, in perhaps the first written description of the male viper’s paired sexual organ, the hemipenis, the author-practitioner notes that “The male ejects from its nature, which is at the bottom, when it is firmly pressed, a little mass like half an arquebus ball made in the genitals and full of very venomous spurs” (fol. 129r). Other than misreading the true nature of the spurs (which aid in attaching the hemipenis to the female’s cloaca), this is a spot-on observation—quite remarkable for its time.

Herpetological Husbandry

The body of work regarding herpetological husbandry prior to the modern era is quite meager and references that do exist are tantalizingly lacking in detail. The art historian Giorgio Vasari (1511–1574) tells us that the Florentine painter Pesello (1367–1446)

took so much pleasure in counterfeiting animals, of which he kept some of all sorts alive in his house, and made them so lifelike and vivacious, that there was no one in his time who equaled him in this branch of his profession.28

Then, there was one, Gisbert Horstius, who in the mid-sixteenth century kept serpents in what was evidently a rock-walled enclosure in his garden in Rome, apparently for the benefit of artists and naturalists whom he would occasionally entertain. He had a reputation for being able to handle snakes fearlessly without being bitten.29

In both these cases there was a professional or monetary benefit in maintaining captive specimens. Likewise, there was a compelling motivation for the author-practitioner to do the same. The arrival of a fresh specimen might not always coincide with the shop’s timetable. Several entries in Ms. Fr. 640 underscore the importance of using a freshly killed animal “before it becomes thin. One ought to put it to death only a quarter of an hour before you want to mold” (fol. 110r). He reiterates the point that keeping an animal too long before killing would result in an emaciated specimen that will not cast well (fols. 106r, 110r, 112r, 115r). Hence, several entries deal with housing and feeding specimens that could not be molded promptly. Husbandry, however short-term, was a necessary means to an end.

The author-practitioner suggests that snakes be kept in barrels full of bran or earth in a cool place, sometimes covered with manure (fols. 109r, 112r, 116v).30 This seems to have been the customary method for holding snakes.31 Nicholas Lémery also observed that apothecaries kept vipers in tubs with bran or moss, although he believed that these snakes never ate after capture but could still live up to a year without food.32 Those utilizing vipers for theriac production had, of course, a different agenda from that of the author-practitioner, who enjoins the keeper to “give [snakes] any live frog or other little live animal, for they do not eat anything dead” (fol. 109r). There must have been instances where the snake regurgitated its meal, especially if handled (fol. 110v), which apparently led to the observation that a snake “can keep a frog engulfed for two to three hours & renders it up completely alive” (fol. 109v). Johann Bechstein, an eighteenth-century naturalist, also noted that “Often I have cut open a snake which had swallowed a frog, and the frog jumped out again.”33

In the course of maintaining his snakes, the author-practitioner had occasion to observe their behavior, leading to a most remarkable passage revealing that, however pragmatic his husbandry practices may have been, the underlying Christian ethic was never too far beneath the surface. He has “noticed that when they [snakes] want to bite or eat something, they do not bite with a direct attack but with sinuous turns & from the side, as do Satan & his disciples” (fol. 109r). He goes on to describe the snake’s mouth in metaphorical terms: “It has a small head but a very long body. The entryway to sin seems small & inconsequential, but the consequences of it are very great.” This evokes the medieval bestiary tradition of using animals as allegorical devices to elucidate moral principles.

Killing and Preserving

The enterprise of capturing, housing, and feeding specimens is entirely in the service of one objective—obtaining a pristine model for casting. Unfortunately, there is no avoiding the final step—killing the animal. In The Book of Art, Cennino Cennini (ca. 1360–before 1427) notes that

You may similarly cast any member separately, an arm, a hand, a foot, a leg, a bird, a beast or any kind of animal or fish. But the animals must be dead, because they have neither the sense or firmness to stand still.34

Another Italian Renaissance artist, Pisanello (1395–1455), was known to have arranged dead specimens so that they appeared to be in motion.35 This drawing (Fig. 10) from one of his model books is perhaps an opportunistic rendering of what appears to be a recently pithed green lizard (Lacerta viridis). A depiction of a lizard in a fresco in Verona executed by Pisanello suggests that he may have referred back to this sketch for salient features, such as the scales under the throat and tail.36

Naturalists, who often accompanied ships in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries to catalogue and collect new life forms, also needed to preserve animals for voucher specimens. Because glass containers were expensive, difficult to seal, and fragile, herpetological specimens (prior to 1800) were usually preserved in wooden casks with various combinations of ethyl alcohol.37 Specimens were even stored in casks of rum or other drinkable substances if necessary.38

Today, herpetological specimens are generally euthanized using injectable barbiturate drugs. In the field, this is not always feasible, so chloroform, ether, or alcohol are often used.39 Post-mortem specimens are preserved in alcohol, formalin, and, occasionally, brine. Except for the availability of syringes and modern fixatives, the euthanasia and preservation of animals detailed in Ms. Fr. 640 are quite similar to current practices.

As an indication of how important it was to secure an intact specimen for molding, the author-practitioner devotes considerable attention to methods of both killing and preserving these animals. There are no fewer than six entries (fols. 109v, 112r, 114v, 115, 143, 165v) describing various combinations of brandy, spirits, vinegar, and even urine—all designed to kill the animal quickly, with minimal damage or contortion. He is aware that, unlike amphibians, snakes take longer to die and advises putting a drop of something stronger, like “aquafortis in the mouth, which stuns them well” (fol. 109v), and “if you are in a hurry to cast, make him take through the mouth some good eau-de-vie or else mix a little with the vinegar. And soon it will be dead, without any lesion which deforms it” (fol. 112v).40

Conclusion

As I read through Ms. Fr. 640, I am struck by the clear, evidence-based observations that are recorded purely in the interests of advancing the author-practitioner’s profession. Based on his frame of reference, some of his more conjectural remarks are completely defensible; others are somewhat less so—that is, until one realizes that a century before Ms. Fr. 640 was penned, another astute observer of nature, Leonardo da Vinci, was delving into fanciful tales and reflections on animals from iconographic material that he plumbed from the Medici library.41 In his old age, he amused himself by compiling these various passages into a bestiary of his own that, like the rest of his some 5,000 pages of manuscript, was never published.42

Likewise, the parenthetical interjections of the symbolic view of animals into what is essentially a down-to-earth shop manual might also be a reflection of an inquisitive and playful intellect. In any case, the manuscript presents us with a remarkable set of observations that provide insight into a sixteenth-century practitioner’s engagement with the natural world.
