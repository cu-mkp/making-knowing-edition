Circulation of Knowledge in Europe

Abstract

Several comments in Ms. Fr. 640, including on fol. 128v, show that the author-practitioner held techniques of metalworking practiced by Germans in high esteem, and he provided detailed information about practices and materials in the German lands. This essay investigates how such knowledge could have reached the author-practitioner.

The historiography of early modern metalworking technology has focused much on the innovations of the Italian peninsula, celebrating Donatello’s early experiments with life-casting,1 Antico’s first adoption of hollow indirect casting,2 the so-called “birth” of small collectors’ bronzes in northern Italy,3 and Cellini’s massive sculptures he claimed to have cast in a single pour.4 Certainly, many more writings by Italian metalworkers were printed and survive today than from any other geographic region, which might help to explain the sustained scholarly interest in this area.5 But how important were Italian ideas and technologies for the formation of artisans and craftspeople outside the peninsula in the sixteenth century? If we use Ms. Fr. 640 as a window onto the exchange of ideas in early modern France, a different story might emerge. The entry “Whitening of cast silver” on fol. 128v, which includes the statement, “I have seen an excellent German working thus,” indicates that the author-practitioner observed German metalworkers. He included no equivalent observation about metalworking craftsmen from any other geographic region; indeed, in remarks about techniques used in specific locales, he only mentions Germany in connection with metalworking techniques worthy of emulating.

Metalworking in the German Lands

While scholarly interest has favored Italy as the source of Renaissance innovation in casting technology in the early modern era, contemporary sixteenth-century chroniclers made clear that the German lands, and especially the Imperial Free Cities of the Holy Roman Empire, enjoyed international reputations as centers of innovation and invention. Nuremberg and Augsburg were especially highly regarded for metalwork. Intellectuals and philosophers such as the astronomer Johann Regiomontanus chose to settle in Nuremberg in the late fifteenth century “because I can easily procure here all necessary instruments, particularly those which are indispensable for the study of astronomy, and also because I can easily keep up a connection with scholars of all countries from here, for this city, on account of its concourse of merchants, may be considered the central point of Europe.”6 The importance of Nuremberg as a hub of commerce and wealthy merchants created a highly cosmopolitan city that included both scholars and craftsmen known for their work not only in the fine arts but also in creating precision instruments for timekeeping and astronomical observation. The Parisian philosopher Petrus Ramus spent four days in 1568 visiting the artisan workshops of Nuremberg and was thoroughly impressed by the way these artisans unified theory and practice in their work.7 In the same period, the master goldsmith Wenzel Jamnitzer (1508–1585) was producing complex lifecast objects destined for imperial Kunstkammern from his “large and prosperous”8 workshop in Nuremberg. The author-practitioner’s interest in casting from life, in portrait medals, and in other objects that would have formed part of a Kunstkammer collection, makes it possible he was familiar with Jamnitzer’s work, an interest that could have drawn him to a study of German craft.9 Following the textual clues left us by the author-practitioner, we can begin to understand the ready exchange of knowledge across geographic borders that characterized the experiences of the early modern craftsperson.

Evidence of German Metalworkers in Ms. Fr. 640

Fol. 128v provides an interesting glimpse of the author-practitioner’s esteem for German skill. He documents in detail the techniques of the “excellent” German metalworker whom he observed clean a silver lizard that had come out of its mold with some sort of crust. He makes a point of noting that the processes he records were observed in person: the lizard was cast “en ma presence” (“in my presence”). A number of other entries indicate that the author-practitioner had actually observed German craftsmen at work and spoken to them at length about their techniques and traditions. On fol. 125r, in a recipe discussing the methods for soaking plaster, the author provides a striking ethnographic detail: “With this plaster, thus reheated as powder, one can cast medals that do not fear the rain, especially if they are varnished. One can find these in Germany, on the houses. But take heed that the water be very hot, & if it is boiling, there is no danger. All plaster molds, alone or mixed, release in it.” This observation indicates familiarity with German houses that could have come from travel or from conversations with German metalworkers.

The author-practitioner records a number of metalworking techniques that he ascribes specifically to the Germans, for example, on fol. 32v for casting very light candlesticks; fol. 63r on German casters’ preference for lead from Flanders; and fol. 72v about German techniques for casting lead very thinly. Fol. 149r includes two descriptions of German methods, one related to goldsmiths, and the other, entitled “Various arts from Germany,” about German metalsmiths’ use of mechanized hammers powered by water mills, and the specifically German method for drawing iron wire. On fol. 159v, a type of waterproof plaster used for statues in German fountains is discussed in some detail. Finally, in a note on fol. 120r, the author-practitioner remarks that certain mixtures to increase the fluidity of molten gold have only been widespread in Germany for less than forty years. Such specific knowledge about the history of a technique would likely have required conversation with a German craftsman. In addition, the author-practitioner seems to know a good deal about where to source German materials such as the material “spalt”—outside the town of Augsburg (fols. 106r, 108r, 119r, and 119v), and provides detailed information about the best lead, which is used in Nuremberg: “The best, fattest & softest is brought from Germany and is not in saulmon, but in lattes of around an arm’s length & three fingers’ width. It is transported to Lyon, and from this, the leaf is made for small mirrors in Nuremberg” (fol. 30r). It is also significant that the author-practitioner appears to have employed German words, such as “spalt,” and it may be telling that he shows some confusion in rendering this term, alternately using “laspalte,” “spalt” or “spat” (see fols. 106r, 108r, 119v, 138r). He also uses the term “stuf” (118r), apparently loaned from German.

No other geographic region is given such attention by name, indicating a notable interest in German technique in particular. It seems the author-practitioner must have observed many German metalworkers to accumulate so much knowledge of their work.

The Movement of Craftspeople in Europe

It was not uncommon for craftsmen to travel across regional borders, as many journeymen apprentices looking to learn from master craftsmen in other communities would spend a portion of their training on the road. In the German lands, this travel was known as the Wanderjahr,10 and was compulsory for many journeymen craftsmen looking eventually to establish themselves as masters in their own right. In 1449, seven percent of Nuremberg’s total population was made up of such journeymen, who worked temporarily for local craftsmen.11 In France, this voyage was called the Tour de France, and, while generally opposed by the monarchy,12 remained a popular way for journeymen to escape some of the rather strict laws of apprenticeship in France.13 While both systems of travel seem to have focused mainly on the knowledge that could be gained in the lands of one’s own native tongue, there are plenty of documented journeymen who traveled internationally, choosing a region or city with the best reputation in their craft.14 Significantly, for the context of Ms. Fr. 640, German miners and metalworkers had been invited to the mining regions—including the Pyrenees—of the Kingdom of France since the fifteenth century. It is possible that the author-practitioner gained his knowledge about German metalworking without leaving Toulouse and its surroundings.15

Conclusion

Despite being relatively equidistant geographically from both the Italian peninsula and the German territories, the author-practitioner found metalworking techniques from German lands noteworthy, perhaps indicating that their reputation for excellence was greater than that of Italy at the time. Indeed, the only time he mentions Italy in relation to metalworking comes on fol. 109r, when he describes the Italians’ custom to mix their wax with tallow, a less useful technique than mixing it with turpentine or butter. Otherwise, Italy and Italian artistic centers such as Florence, Venice, Milan, or Rome are mentioned only in relation to the materials to be obtained from them,16 including Venice turpentine, paper from Florence, and red lake pigment from both cities. Italian technique is only specifically noted in reference to painting.17 Other countries with important artistic reputations, such as Flanders,18 are also seen as a source of painting techniques rather than metalworking. From these comments in Ms. Fr. 640, we can conclude that, for the author-practitioner, German metalworkers and their techniques were most worthy of documentation and emulation, providing evidence that Germany might be considered a center of casting innovation equal to or greater than Italy at the time.
