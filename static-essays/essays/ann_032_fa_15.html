Color of Gold without Gold on Silver

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/jz70-zv73
← Back to Essays

Abstract

Ms. Fr. 640 contains several recipes detailing techniques to imitate the optical properties of gold using less precious materials. Of these, two recipes (fols. 29v and 57r) detail the making of a “color of gold without gold on silver” by applying a golden colored varnish over silver leaf. The varnish derives its tint from terra merita, which the author-practitioner identifies as “turmeric root” (racine de cucurme) on fol. 148r. Yet much remains mysterious about terra merita, its preparation, and its use in the history of craft practice. This essay reconstructs the turmeric-based “gold without gold on silver,” and it investigates the occurrence of terra merita in artisanal texts and the use of yellow varnishes to color metals.

Creating “gold without gold” has been a concern of alchemists, artisans, and artists for centuries. In fact, it is one of the primary concerns of Physika kai Mystika, a recipe book for dyeing and coloring thought to date back to the first century CE and often considered one of the earliest alchemical writings.1 It is thus unsurprising that the author-practitioner of BnF Ms. Fr. 640 includes not one but at least ten recipes that involve creating a golden color without the use of elemental gold.2 Of these, the ones found on fols. 29v and 57r, “Color of gold without gold on silver” and an untitled section within a larger entry called “Painter” respectively, are recipes for applying a gold color to silver leaf. One aspect sets them apart from other similar recipes found in other manuscripts and later texts: the instructions to use terre emerita or terra emerita.3

Though a quick internet search reveals that terra merita is another name for curcuma longa, commonly known in English as “turmeric,” the actual identity and nature of terra merita is still, perhaps, the biggest mystery of these two recipes.4 First, reconstruction quickly showed that the average turmeric found in a pantry today could not be used as a pigment. Moreover, references in historical sources about the use of turmeric as a pigment are few and far between prior to the eighteenth century. Finally, the intended use of the author-practitioner’s “gold without gold” remains an open question. This essay explores both the use of turmeric and the subject of gold-colored varnish.

Materials and Techniques

Though similar in aim and ingredients, the two recipes differ slightly. On fol. 29v, in order to obtain a color more beautiful than fine gold, the author-practitioner instructs us to color silver leaf with some terre emerita and, once it is dry, to apply a coat of “spike lavender oil varnish and of sandarac.” The recipe is short and gives few indications of quantities or surface to be covered. It assumes the existence of a silver-gilded surface on which the terra merita is to be applied. The spike lavender oil varnish called for may be the one detailed on fol. 4r. The first mystery comes in the form of the terra merita, which must be dry before applying the varnish, implying that it was previously wet. It had thus been mixed with something else in order to make it liquid.

On fol. 57r, in a continuation of an entry entitled “Painter,” begun on fol. 56v, the author-practitioner instructs to take terra emerita—this time powdered—and boil it together with varnish, either of spike lavender oil or turpentine. As we shall see, this slight modification has a significant impact on the final result of the reconstruction of these recipes for gold without gold. Another interesting aspect of this recipe is its preoccupation with how long the varnish takes to dry: “a quarter of an hour, in winter as well as summer,” so long as no aloe is added. Apart from its use of terra merita, this recipe closely resembles recipes found in other manuscripts, notably the auripetrum recipe of Petrus de S. Audemar.5

Reconstruction

The first step in reconstructing these recipes was to create the gilded silver leaf surface on which the terra merita and varnishes would be applied. Gilding is a very delicate task, and would have required years of training. In Tournai, during the Renaissance, goldsmith guild regulations required apprentices to train in gilding for two extra years on top of the basic two-year training period because of the delicate and costly nature of the task.6 It is unsurprising, then, that my attempts at gilding after an afternoon of training were time consuming and far from perfect, despite the smooth surface provided by my gesso panel and burnished bole (Fig. 1, Fig. 2).7 Once the gilded silver was burnished, the groundwork had been laid for the testing of the recipes.

In experimenting with the turmeric for the recipe on fol. 29r, it quickly became apparent that the turmeric could not be used as a pigment in its store-bought powdered form. Attempts to mix it with water or oil to form a paint revealed that some fibers could not be ground down to create a smooth paint (Fig. 3), and a continued examination of the traces applied to the gesso showed that the color obtained was not fast on exposure to light and faded quickly (Fig. 4, Fig. 5). A pigment would have to be extracted from the turmeric in order to create a more stable and particle-free color.

According to modern sources, the best option for extracting the pigment is a solvent extraction using ethyl acetate.8 This technique is also mentioned in an 1874 treatise on the manufacture of color.9 The primary extraction experiment used grain alcohol with a 95% alcohol (ethanol) content as a solvent. The pigment obtained from this extraction was not a powder but a thick paste. Because it was supposed to dissolve in oil, I attempted to make a paint with linseed oil, using a muller to grind the two together. This created a rich orange paint (Fig. 6).10 I applied this paint to a plain white gesso square (Fig. 7) and to two of my silver leaf squares (Fig. 8) to serve as the layer of terra merita required by the recipe on fol. 29v. Though it could not be mistaken for gold, the paint was translucent enough to allow some of the metallic sheen of the silver leaf to shine through.

The varnishes were made following recipes in Ms. Fr. 640. For the turpentine varnish, I used the recipe found on fol. 3r and for the spike lavender oil varnish, the recipe found on fol. 4r.11 After making the turpentine varnish, I poured some of it into a new beaker and added to it the terra merita obtained previously. I then repeated the process with the spike lavender oil varnish. Because of the color of the Venice turpentine used to make it, the turpentine varnish possessed an amber hue, while the spike lavender oil varnish was only very lightly colored. After adding the terra merita (i.e., the turmeric extraction), the turpentine varnish became a deeper golden yellow, while the spike lavender oil varnish took on a brighter shade of yellow. Once all the varnish was applied, both the plain varnishes to the turmeric-painted silver and the two different types of terra merita colored varnish (hereafter golden varnish) directly to the silver (Fig. 9, Fig. 10),12 I was able to draw some conclusions, although I was also left with questions.13

Conclusions and Questions Following the Reconstruction

The optical properties of the golden varnish-covered silver, which was made following the instructions on fol. 57r, were much closer to those of gold (Vid. 1, Vid. 2).

Of the two golden varnish varieties, the turpentine was the most gold-like in color, as the initial color of the gold-colored spike lavender oil varnish was too bright to pass as gold. After two weeks, the color of the squares using the spike lavender oil varnish had faded significantly, while those with the turpentine varnish held their color much better (Fig. 11).

The square with the golden spike lavender oil varnish had faded to a color very close to genuine gold leaf, but the rapidity with which it faded puts into question the durability of the color (Vid. 3). Significantly, the author-practitioner warns on fol. 4r against using spike lavender oil varnish with color because it will “eat away at the color” (mange les couleurs). The results show that this was indeed the case, which raises questions about why he would suggest the use of spike lavender oil varnish later on in the manuscript. Since the note on fol. 4r describing the effects of spike lavender oil on color is written in a slightly sketchier and hasiter hand, it is likely that this note was added at a later date, after the author-practitioner had attempted to use the varnish and noticed its effects. This provides further evidence that the author-practitioner was more than simply a collector of recipes—he was trying these recipes himself.

Moreover, though the result of my reconstruction was successful in producing a gold-like effect, it is still distinguishable from real gold. In The Materials and Techniques of Medieval Painting, Daniel V. Thompson, on the subject of substitutes for gold in the Middle Ages, suggests that “medieval eyes were very easily deceived, for most of these substitutes are very weakly like real gold. If they were yellow and shiny, and particularly if they had any sort of metallic sparkle, they were acceptable.”14 This raises questions about the author-practitioner’s intention: was his primary concern to produce the color of gold, or to reproduce as faithfully as possible other properties of gold, such as its luster and shine?

Finally, I was left with questions about the varnish. It had been very viscous and nearly impossible to spread, it did not dry in one quarter of an hour, and an impression was still left on the varnish when touched with the tip of the finger after a half hour. Was my process in making the varnish faulty? Is this related to my interpretation of terra merita and the turmeric extract I used? This brings me to a last series of questions: how did the author-practitioner work with his terra merita, especially for its use in the recipe on fol. 29v? It seems, based on my experiment, that it would be nearly impossible to create a smooth and bright varnish without performing some kind of extraction on turmeric. If that is the case, and, since there are no indications to do so in the recipe, it might be assumed that such an extraction was a fairly well-known process. How extensively, then, was turmeric used in Europe at the time? And how common was “gold without gold”?

Historical context

Gold Without Gold

Techniques for reproducing the color and effects of gold have been known for centuries and appear in the written record in various forms. One technique, described by Cennino Cennini in Il libro dell’arte around the turn of the fifteenth century and common in all of Europe by the Late Middle Ages, was the use of mosaic gold, also known as aurum musivum.15 This was used to illuminate manuscripts, to paint, and to write on both parchment and panels, and it was made with a mix of sal ammoniac, tin, sulfur, and quicksilver.16

If, however, we concentrate on the techniques in Ms. Fr. 640, namely, the application of a gold-colored varnish over silver leaf, there are several versions predating by far those found in Ms. Fr. 640. Assumed to be written in the second half of the first century CE by its most recent translator Matteo Martelli, the second half of pseudo-Democritus’s On the Making of Purple and Gold contains multiple instructions for the use of liquid washes or paints for coloring metals. Similar recipes can be found in the Leiden and Stockholm papyri (early fourth century CE).17 One such recipe tells us to “take Cilician saffron,” which Pliny says is the “most esteemed” kind,18 “dissolve the saffron flowers with the above prescribed grape juice [Aminean wine]; make a wash as is customary. Dip in silver leaves until you like their color.”19 Pseudo-Democritus ends his instructions, as he does many others, with “nature conquers nature.”20 Was this feeling of conquering nature through successful imitation one that our author-practitioner also experienced?

In his twelfth-century treatise On Diverse Arts, Theophilus uses a similar technique on tin. Like pseudo-Democritus he does not apply his coloring matter onto the metal but calls for the immersion of the metal leaf into a hot mixture of wine- or beer-colored gold with alder buckthorn and saffron. Varnish is then applied over the tin sheet and left to dry. Once dry, Theophilus tells us that “you have leaves of tin which you may lay on your works as you wish with hide glue.”21 This two-phase process of separating the coloring from the varnishing phase has parallels with the first recipe for “gold without gold” found on fol. 29v of Ms. Fr. 640, although the wording of the recipe seems to indicate that the silver leaf has already been applied to a ground prior to the application of the terra merita for coloring.

Cennino Cennini provides a set of instructions for coloring metal to imitate gold. He, like Theophilus, also uses tin and gives some insights into how and why this technique might be used. Cennini tells us that “gold tinted tin is made in the following way: Take up a completely smooth board three or four braccia long and have it greased with fat or with tallow. You put some of the plain tin on it, then you overlay the tin with a solution called gold size.”22 Though Cennini does not provide a recipe for this gold size and seems to confuse his instructions for gilding on tin with those on the making of gold-tinted tin, it can be assumed that this gold size is used in a fashion similar to our author-practitioner’s varnish. Though he describes the process of making gold-tinted tin, Cennini discourages its use stating that “most people habitually decorate walls with gold-tinted tin because it is less expensive, but I urge you to take this advice: force yourself always to do your decorations with gold leaf and with good pigments, especially on the figure of Our Lady.” His justification is that, while more costly, the use of gold leaf will help the artist to acquire a better reputation that might eventually lead to commissions.23

In her collection of medieval and Renaissance treatises, Mary P. Merrifield provides a description of a gold-colored varnish that the manuscripts she transcribes call auripetrum. Both Petrus S. Audemar and Eraclius list recipes for it. In these recipes, Spanish saffron and aloes are used to give the varnish its gold hue.24 Merrifield, perhaps influenced by Cennini’s reference to the price of gold leaf, states that auripetrum was used when patrons were unwilling to pay for gold because the price of gold was “beyond the reach of all but the rich.”25 Based on a document relating to the expenses of paintings executed in the chapel of S. Jacopo di Pistoia, Merrifield claims that this type of varnish was used in “mural pictures” though what she means by this term is unclear.26

Although the source Merrifield provides for this information has proven difficult to find, I have identified at least two examples of the use of this process on objects that predate Ms. Fr. 640. One example of this technique is a thirteenth-century panel painting of S. Francesco d’Assisi, where testing has shown that the now blackened tin background was once covered with a golden varnish.27 The other is the frame of the Tresfjord antependium from Tresfjord, Norway (Fig. 12), which was once gilded with silver and made to look like gold by applying a varnish.28 Though objects using this technique are difficult to identify due to the fading of the pigmented varnish and the eventual tarnishing of the silver, both of these applications of a golden varnish might still have been in use when our author-practitioner was writing.29

While my research has sought parallels for the application of golden varnish onto a silver substrate, an intriguing document from early seventeenth-century Paris shows that less precious metals were being painted with terra merita, likely in imitation of gold—an important piece of context for the author-practitioner’s “gold without gold” recipes. The document, dated December 29, 1617, registers a commercial partnership between two peddlers who, between them, had various pieces of latten colored with a [cold] enamel of terra merita (en couleur de terramerita esmaillee). These small works of imitation gold functioned as bridal adornments for young women living in the countryside (servant a atours d’espousees pour filles de villaige). This suggests that the two peddlers were supplying the businesses of atourneresses, that is, women who rented bridal clothing and jewelry in early modern France.30 While it is impossible to know what was the binding medium for the terramerita esmaillee applied to the latten, the term esmail could refer both to vitreous enamels as well as to pigments suspended in oil or resin (i.e., cold enamels), that could be painted onto various substrates, including metals, glass, canvas, and panel.31 Given that the author-practitioner was also collecting recipes for cold enameling as well as for recreating the optical effects of gold in less costly materials, this archival document offers us a rare glimpse into the cultural applications of the artisanal processes compiled in Ms. Fr. 640.

In addition to their application on various metals, varnishes that mimicked the effects of gold were also painted over other materials, notably leather. Published in 1589, a French translation of the pseudonymous Alessio Piemontese’s Book of Secrets contains a recipe titled “To make gold layer that is used with leather and will seem like silver and by applying it to silver or glass will look like gold”32 that instructs in using a golden varnish to gild leather. According to John Waterer’s Spanish Leather, leather hangings were at the height of their popularity in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries and could be found in most of Europe. Despite their popularity, few of these survive today in their original state both because the leatherwork itself has been damaged or has not been conserved and because the “gilt,” often silver covered with varnish, has tarnished.33 Although these gilt leather hangings originated in Spain, they were traded in France as early as the thirteenth century. Waterer indicates that “in 1241 we hear of ‘le corduan de Limoges et de Toulouse,’” corduan being the French name for the Spanish guadameci leather because of the importance of Cordoba in leather production.34 This is especially relevant as the author-practitioner was active in the Toulouse region. In the sixteenth century, the demand for leather increased, and the techniques for its processing developed significantly.35

By the seventeenth century, Holland, especially Amsterdam, had become famous for its gilt-leather hangings. According to the Victoria and Albert Museum, “the most important manufacturer, the Compagnie van Goudleermaken (Company of Gilt Leathermakers), was established in 1641 and continued till about 1700.”36 Some examples of these Dutch leather hangings can be found in their collections (Fig. 13, Fig. 14).

A 1766 book based on the writings of Roger de Piles, who was active in the late seventeenth and early eighteenth century, briefly mentions the process of gilding leather using silver leaf and varnish.37 The French naturalist Georges-Louis Leclerc Buffon also refers to this technique in the eighteenth century, explaining that a yellow varnish he calls or de couleur is often used to color gilt leather and the copper garnishings on furniture, among other things. In his opinion, this “gives an air of opulence to the poorest of materials” yet is quite easily distinguished from real gold.38 Once again, it seems that this process was intended as an imitation of a costly material, namely real gold.

The making of this silver-gilt leather coated with varnish is described in Diderot’s Encyclopedie. In the article “Dorure,” M. Papillon indicates that linseed oil is mixed with saffron, poix greque, and aloe, is strained, and is then applied to silver or tin leaf in order to gild leather or parchment.39 This process is illustrated on a plate from the Suite du recueil de planches published in 1777 (Fig. 15).40

The technique of using gold varnish over silver leaf also appears to have been used on furniture in southern Italy, especially in Naples and Sicily, where it was called doratura a mecca in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries. An example of this can be seen on a mid-eighteenth century side table in the J. Paul Getty Museum (Fig. 16).

The practice of using this type of colored varnish was in fact so widespread that the 1741 Dictionnaire universel du commerce explains that

the ease in deceiving the public with this false gold leaf has prompted the Regulation of 1721 by which it is forbidden, under threat of confiscation and a one hundred pound fine, to all painters and gilders, to make any work in varnished silver, unless they declare it with the bureau de la Communauté, have had an plaque brought by the Juries with this inscription, “Varnished silver without gold,” and for greater security, have put someplace visible on the work a silver leaf so that one might know it is not made with gold.41

The above research demonstrates that the use of gold-colored varnish was a common practice, especially in the making of gilt leather and Italian furniture, however, in the accounts of this practice, terra merita is rarely mentioned. How common an ingredient was it at the time? In order to answer this question, I now turn to an investigation of the main ingredient of the two recipes in Ms. Fr. 640: the terra merita.

The Search for Terra Merita

The use of terra merita in the recipes on fols. 29v and 57r, as opposed to more commonly used yellow coloring agents, such as saffron, is what most differentiates Ms. Fr. 640 from the rest of the literature relating to gold-colored varnish.42 Although occurrences of the terms “terra merita” or “curcuma” are increasingly common from the eighteenth century on, sixteenth-century sources about the use of turmeric in the craftsman’s workshop are more elusive.

Searching for the use of terra merita or turmeric as a pigment in modern publications at first yielded few results. In their book on the trade in artists’ materials, Jo Kirby and her co-authors include only a short note stating that it was “found in India and other parts of Asia, used medicinally. It was used for dyeing in its home territory, but only to a very limited extent in Europe in the eighteenth century.”43 In Natural Dyes, Dominique Cardon repeats the seemingly established notion that “in Europe, turmeric, known as terra merita in the eighteenth century, was only used to dye wool and even then on a limited scale.”44 Ralph Mayer’s The Artist’s Handbook is even more confusing in defining terra merita as an obsolete “fugitive yellow lake made from saffron or curcuma root.”45 This is an obvious misunderstanding of the term. Ms. Fr. 640 thus appears to provide new information about the use of turmeric as coloring agent as early as the sixteenth century.

The Pigment Compendium by Nicholas Eastaugh et al. offers a more helpful description, referring to sources such as Pierre François Tingry’s 1804 treatise on varnishes and Robert Dossie’s 1758 The Handmaid to the Arts.46 In instructions for making a bright yellow ground, Dossie says that “the effect may be still more heightened by dissolving powdered turmeric root in the spirit of wine of which the upper or polishing coat is made; which spirit of wine must be strained from off the dregs, before the seed-lac be added to it to form the varnish.”47 Tingry affirms this process in his statement that “terra mérita is employed in varnishing only under the form of a tincture.”48 Although this is not the powder the author-practitioner uses, it lends credence to my previous attempt to extract turmeric pigment by using grain alcohol.49 Tingry also states that “it enters into the mixture of those coloring parts which contribute the most to give metals the color of gold,” the very purpose it is used for in Ms. Fr. 640, meaning that terra merita’s use for this purpose was already well established by the early eighteenth century.50

The publications contemporary with Ms. Fr. 640—which we estimate to be late sixteenth century—that most frequently make mention of curcuma (turmeric) are unsurprisingly the herbals, many of these relying on earlier sources such as Dioscorides’s De materia medica, written in the first century CE. Dioscorides never saw the plant in question, and he did not use any of its common names, but he does describe the ginger-like root with the color of saffron fairly accurately, even mentioning its depilatory characteristics, something for which it is still employed to this day.51 Pietro Andrea Matthioli (1501–1577), a source perhaps known to our author-practitioner as he mentions a “Mathiol” on fol. 35v, came to the conclusion that Dioscorides was indeed referring to curcuma.52 In his work Historia generalis plantarum, published in 1586, Jacques Dalechamps also mentions the many medical properties of the curcuma root, although he makes note that “today it is more commonly used by Painters and Alchemists than by Doctors.”53 Pierre Pena and Matthias de L’Obel make a very similar remark in their herbal: “[Terra merita is] today more frequently [used] by painters and chymists than in medicaments.”54

Despite the herbal authors’ statement that curcuma was commonly used by painters, contemporary confirmation from practitioners beyond the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 has proved elusive. Even the 1617 Parisian document, discussed above, which notes a cold enamel (i.e., paint) of terra merita applied to latten jewelry in imitation of gold, tells us relatively little about the processes of its making beyond what we can extrapolate from the phrase terramerita esmaillee. The most relevant reference is in Benvenuto Cellini’s Treatises on Goldsmithing and Sculpture, where Cellini calls for the use of “cuccuma,” which we can suppose to be a misspelling of the word curcuma, in a “Recipe for Making Colours and Colouring the Gilded Parts.” His process is quite different from the author-practitioner’s varnishing of silver leaf, instead requiring the material to be dyed to be dipped into the heated mixture that includes the turmeric. He also notes that this coloring matter is weak.55 Two other sources contemporary to Ms. Fr. 640 make use of turmeric, in the context of dyeing fabric. Gioanventura Rosetti’s Plictho, first published in Venice in 1540, provides instructions for dyeing various types of fabric and describes dyeing leather yellow using curcuma and “roche alum” as a mordant, showing that turmeric was used as a dye in Venice as early as 1548.56 Blaise de Vigenère (1523–1596), in a supposed translation of a text by the Greek Philostratus (190–ca. 230 CE), instructs that terra merita may be added to a dye intended to dye wool purple, although only certain people do so.57

This use of turmeric as a dye appears to have been quite common later on, not only in France where it was used, among other things, to dye hair blond,58 but also as far away as Japan, where it was used to dye cloth. It was also commonly used in India.59 Despite the existence of contemporary references to its use as a dye, the eighteenth-century Encyclopédie méthodique: Arts et métiers mécaniques lists the painting of hardwood floors as its only use.60

It is not until the mid-eighteenth century, in Secrets concernans les arts et métiers, that I found the first mention outside of Ms. Fr. 640 and Cellini’s treatise of terra merita being used as an ingredient for giving a gold color to metal, in this case to tin or steel. It is not, however, the main ingredient, giving precedence to orpiment.61 As noted above, Tingry also describes the use of terra merita in varnish in the early nineteenth century.

One hypothesis to explain the lack of early references to the use of turmeric might lie in the fact that one of its common names in sixteenth-century herbals was safran d’Inde or safran d’Indie, which could indicate that it was listed as saffron in recipe books, or simply substituted for it, though I have found no source that would prove this confusion of the two.62

Conclusion

Clearly, much about the use of turmeric remains to be researched, not the least of which was its place in the spice trade and the routes by which turmeric was brought into Europe. Its appearance in Ms. Fr. 640 and other early modern texts, however, shows that terra merita, which this essay has understood to be turmeric in some form, was known earlier and used more extensively than some modern scholars believe.

Quoting Maxine Berg, Ulinka Rublack suggests that “the material world can only properly be understood through an engagement with ‘actual products’: ‘how they were designed and made’ and ‘what they were used for.’”63 But how can we investigate these questions when the object of our study is of an ephemeral nature? Examples of the technique of using silver to imitate gold, though few in number, exist to this day in museum collections, but as we do not know in many of these cases what pigment was used to create the golden color, can we be sure that the author-practitioner’s recipe produced the same results? The reconstruction of the recipes for “gold without gold” allowed the reproduction of one of these “actual products,” which provided a useful starting point for historical research on both the technique and the ingredients. Although our author-practitioner does not indicate how this “gold without gold” was to be used, seeing the results of the reconstruction and comparing it to similar techniques used throughout history in Europe, we can begin to imagine how the technique might have been applied.
