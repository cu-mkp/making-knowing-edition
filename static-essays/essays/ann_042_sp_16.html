Shadows Beneath the Skin: How to Paint Faces in Distemper

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/a6bs-0765
← Back to Essays

Abstract

In an entry on fol. 93v titled “Face in distemper,” the author-practitioner describes a method for depicting a face in paint on canvas. This essay analyzes that entry, contextualizing it in relation to other painting recipes in the manuscript and to other early modern artisanal treatises, before turning to laboratory reconstruction. Ms. Fr. 640 points to the intertwined relationship between representing skin and the body’s shadows, thereby reflecting broader concerns of early modern artistic practice. The author-practitioner also advises altering the hue of flesh shading by gender, which may owe something to humoral theory. Among the pigments he suggests using for male shadows is jet, a substance rarely mentioned in other painting treatises. The results of the reconstructions suggest that the specific paint medium intended by the term “distemper” may have been glue, but this is not conclusive and further experimentation is necessary.

This essay explores the relationship between shadows and flesh in Ms. Fr. 640 through a textual and practical investigation of the entry “Face in Distemper” (“Visaige a destrempe”) on fol. 93v, a set of instructions for depicting a face in paint.1 The process begins with painting shadows onto a canvas support with a small brush, followed by applying flesh tones in a translucent veil, before returning with another brush to lay in a final set of shading in cross-hatching. In this brief entry the author-practitioner uses a word for shadows (ombres) six times, while his term for skin tones (carnation) appears only twice. Reading this account alongside other related entries in the manuscript raises the following questions: why are shadows such a fundamental part of painting skin for the author-practitioner? What was painting a destrempe in material terms, and how does painting skin manifest particularly in that medium? Does his advice for painting a face in this way actually work in practice? And why did the author-practitioner suggest coloring the shadows of skin according to gender?

To examine these issues, this essay begins by considering the author-practitioner’s references to shadows and the shading of skin in relation to wider artistic practice. It then turns to discuss the author-practitioner’s gendering of shadows, and a possible connection to humoral theory. It concludes with an investigation of the process of painting a destrempe through recipe reconstruction, which has offered a deeper insight into this technique than was possible with reading alone.

Shadows and Skin

Shadows (ombres) are a recurring preoccupation in Ms. Fr. 640. The author-practitioner uses different spellings of the term apparently interchangeably, so ombre, umbre, and variations on these are found throughout. These terms occur on sixteen folios, demonstrating a range of interrelated meanings from the painterly to the environmental, with entries on eleven of those folios relating to painting techniques in particular. One can variously translate ombre and umbre into English as shadow, shade, shadowing, and shading. In Cotgrave’s French-English dictionary of 1611, ombre is translated as “a shadow; also, a shade, or covert; also, a color, cloake, vale, pretext, or pretence for; also as Umbre.”2 The term is rich and multivalent, with connections to a particular pigment (umbre). An English translation as “shadows” preserves some of the ambiguity and range of the term, in preference to making a sharp distinction between “shading” as an artistic method and the depiction of a “shadow.” The author-practitioner invariably uses ombre or umbre as a noun in the entry on fol. 93v.

The author-practitioner does not appear to have been primarily a painter, and the manuscript is characterized by its variety of content relating to a wide range of artisanal practices. It includes recipe entries and working notes for diverse processes ranging from casting procedures to means for creating imitation gemstones to taxidermy methods. However, the author-practitioner does include a significant number of entries about painting methods in the manuscript, and it was clearly an area of interest to him. According to an analysis undertaken by the Making and Knowing team, about 14% of the manuscript entries relate to painting, paintings, or painters.3 This is the second largest category of entries, superseded only by the number of entries on casting (ca. 34%). Entries pertaining to painting techniques cluster between fols. 56r and 67r, and it is among these entries that the terms ombre or umbre occur most frequently. In Ms. Fr. 640, shading and shadows appear as concerns proper to the painter.

It would be surprising if any treatment of painting technique in late sixteenth-century France failed to put an emphasis on shadows. Shadows emerged as vital to various aims of art and art theory during the Renaissance, such as depicting volumetrically convincing objects, creating a sense of space and depth, confirming presence, conveying naturalism in its many variants, and constituting an essential element of the painter’s identity and brief. At the very opening of the Quattrocento, Cennino Cennini (ca. 1360–before 1427) advised readers of his Libro dell’Arte to depict draperies and persons through attentive modeling from pure hue to bright light.4 Gian Battista Alberti (1404–1472) emphasized the critical role of light and shade for painters, altering the Cennino system to encourage the addition of black for the deepest shadows, with the goal of achieving the greatest impression of relief.5 The radical research of Leonardo da Vinci (1452–1519) into the effects of light and shadow at different moments and in different environmental conditions constituted a watershed, and his writings on shadows were to become immensely influential, shaping the development of the theory that they were not totally dark but contained some colored reflections.6 Giorgio Vasari (1511–1574) drew on Pliny’s account to offer the tracing of a man’s shadow on the wall as an origin myth for the foundation of painting itself.7 By the end of the sixteenth century, Caravaggio (1571–1610) and the tenebrists had demonstrated how powerfully shadows could contribute to moving the emotions of the viewer, with works that drew much of their force from a dramatic opposition between deep shadows and bright lights. Victor Stoichita’s A Short History of the Shadow gives the fullest account of the Renaissance history of the shadow, a history which lies beyond the scope of this essay but within which the author-practitioner’s interest in the use of shadows and shading in painting belongs.8

Shading Flesh

The author-practitioner often mentions ombres in recipes for painting skin tones. In the entry “Shadows” (“Ombres”) on fol. 58v, he writes, “when you are making a painting with several persons, just as the flesh colors have to be different, so do the shadows [ombres].” In “Work of the Flemish” (“Labeur des flamens”) on fols. 60v–61r, he notes that once the imprimatura is well evened and dry, the Flemish, “draw their portrait & layer their natural flesh color, leaving the space for shadow [ombres]” (fol. 61r). And in “Shadows” (“Umbres”) on fol. 63r, he observes, “German painters make their shadows [ombres] on flesh color of men with ground jet, stil de grain yellow & ocher.”

Other historical painting treatises relate flesh and shadows in ways that can illuminate the connection between flesh and shadows in Ms. Fr. 640. From the Medieval period to the seventeenth century, when writers discussed methods for depicting faces and bodies they habitually included instructions for how to shade them. One of the earliest known treatises in the European tradition, De diversis artibus (On Divers Arts) by Theophilus (fl. ca. 1070–1125), opens with a chapter dedicated to “The mixture of pigments for nude bodies,” and in section three offers “the first shadow pigment,” for “the eyebrows and eyes, the nostrils and mouth, the chin, the hollows round the nostrils…and all the distinctive limbs of the nude body.”9 The 1598 English translation by Richard Haydock (1569/70–ca. 1642) of the 1584 Trattato dell’Arte della Pittura, Scoltura et Architettura by Giovan Paolo Lomazzo (1538–1592), a near-contemporary text to Ms. Fr. 640, makes the ability to depict light and shadow on flesh a key expression of artistic skill: 

In the work of Raphael, Leonardo, Coreggio, Titian, they handled the lights and darks so well to make their pictures seem natural rather than artificial. Where amonsgt other thinges you shall finde certaine Spottes in the carnation; the reason whereof the vulgar eie cannot conceave; not withstanding these conceipted workemaisters expressed their cheifest art therein; considering themselves that the light falling upon the flesh, cause these and such like effects: in which kinde Titian excelled the rest… Againe Titian to make knowne his arte in lights and shaddowes, when he would expresse the lightest part of the bodie, used to adde a little too much white.10

Observations about the techniques of figure painting and the shading of skin also appear together in the De Mayerne manuscript, compiled by Swiss physician Sir Théodore Turquet de Mayerne (1573–1655) from his conversations with practicing artists. De Mayerne notes, “Faces and Flesh Parts White lead, yellow, ocher, brown red. For shading: white lead, yellow ocher, ivory black,” and, in a discussion of lamp black, he adds, “shadows for flesh parts are excellently made with earth black.”11 In The Art of Limning, written circa 1627, Edward Norgate (1581–1650) describes how to paint portraits in miniature, goes into detail on the variety of flesh tones and shadows possible, connecting the two closely, and finishes with the following summary:

But to prescribe an absolute and general Rule is both impossible, and a little ridiculous, Nature herselfe soe infinitely full of variety in the shadowes and colors of faces, and all differing from one another that, when all is said that can be, your owne observations, practice and discretion, must bee your best Director.12

In the entry for “Face in distemper” on fol. 93v, the author-practitioner describes a method for painting a face that begins with an underlayer of painted shadows, builds up flesh tones on top, and finishes with a delicately hatched shadow layer on the very surface. Les ombres thus provide both the foundation and the final step of a layered painting process for depicting a face. A long tradition supports the author-practitioner’s instructions, as medieval and early modern painters often put down a darker first layer beneath flesh tones when depicting skin. The use of a verdaccio layer, a greenish-brown layer made of earth pigments, as a base for skin tones beneath egg tempera and fresco paintings, is described by Cennino Cennini in his Libro dell’Arte, and artists widely used it for thirteenth- and fourteenth-century Italian paintings. Yet a dark base was also used by painters elsewhere in Europe, as Ann-Sophie Lehmann and Marjolijn Bol have explained:

To depict skin accurately… intricate, yet surprisingly similar paint systems evolved in Italian, French, German, and Netherlandish painting. These entailed a complex layer build up on a dark, often greenish under layer for a turbid medium effect, and very fine strokes or dots on the surface to suggest fluent transitions.13

Paintings in protein and water-based media often have dark undermodelling layers, although they are by no means exclusive to them. Joseph Interpreting the Dreams of Pharaoh, an unfinished painting attributed to German artist Jörg Breu the Younger (ca. 1510–1547), gives insight into the application of a grey undermodelling layer for a painting in distemper on linen. This layer is visible on the dogs in the foreground and on the figures clustered around the Pharaoh on his throne (Fig. 1). While not especially dark today (it may have been subject to fading), the undermodelling nevertheless effectively indicates where the artist intended to place the shadowed areas in a completed version of the work.

We can therefore see that the interwoven relationship between the painting of skin and shadows conveyed by the author-practitioner fits well into a broad panorama of painting practice in Europe from the late Middle Ages through the seventeenth century. The specific technique that he indicates—putting down a base layer of shadows before applying the flesh tones—has its roots in traditional processes for painting in water-based media dating back to at least the thirteenth century.

Gendered Shadows

The author-practitioner’s system for painting flesh by beginning and ending with shadows bears further scrutiny. In a set of related recipes on fols. 65r and 66r, the author-practitioner instructs the painter to use a distinct set of pigments for the shadows of women and for men. His gendering of shadows suggests that they play a fundamental and more complex role than might be apparent at first pass.

The author-practitioner spells out this distinction in the recipe on fol. 65r entitled “Shadows.” Here, he states that men’s shadows are yellowish (jaulnastre), while “blacks which make a greenish black are appropriate for women’s shadows.” On fol. 66r, in another entry entitled “Shadows,” he again genders the pigment choice, writing, “for women, asphaltum, umber, & a little lake.” Although the author-practitioner does not specify that the face in the recipe on fol. 93v is female, the pigments he suggests for the shadows (sap green, bistre, and ocre de ru) are similar to those he indicates on fol. 65r as suitable for producing “a perfect shadow [umbre] for women in distemper”: sap green (verd de vessie), bistre (bistre), and a black (noir). Following the word noir, the author-practitioner left a space as if he intended to specify which black to use, but this gap was never filled in. In the recipe on fol. 93v, black is omitted, and ocre de ru is included instead. A combination of sap green, bistre, and black would result in a greenish cast to a dark color.14 It therefore seems likely that the face that the author-practitioner intended on fol. 93v was a female countenance. But why did he consider “greenish black” particularly appropriate for women’s shadows as opposed to men’s shadows? More broadly, why does the author-practitioner distinguish in this way between the colors of the shadows of male and female faces?

The author-practitioner’s prescriptions for gendered shadows belong within a tradition of painterly practice evinced in text and image. It is common to find different hues in male and female skin in early modern paintings, where women are often portrayed as being lighter skinned than men. Depictions of Adam and Eve make this difference very clear, as seen in depictions as diverse as Cornelis Cornelisz. van Haarlem’s 1592 Fall of Man (Fig. 2) and an early sixteenth-century French manuscript illumination of the same subject (Fig. 3). Moreover, in the catalogue for the exhibition Hot Dry Men, Cold Wet Women: The Theory of Humors in Western European Art, Zirka Filipczak has argued that Rubens used skin coloration to indicate that the figure who appeared to be Diana was really Jupiter in disguise in his 1613 Callisto and Jupiter (Fig. 4).

This gendering of flesh tone was not a strict convention; rather, in the words of Filipczak, it was more a “common tendency to show women’s bodies as whiter than those of men.”15 Artists expressed a widely held interest in variations among skin tones, not only between the genders but between the old and young and between different ethnicities—Andrea Mantegna’s Adoration of the Magi, for instance, revels in the possibilities for experimentation in flesh tones offered by that biblical story (Fig. 5). Vasari also makes this general interest among painters clear: “nor can one believe how much grace and beauty is given to a work by varying the colors of the flesh, making the complexion of the young fresher than that of the old, giving to the middle-aged a tint between a brick-color and a greenish yellow.”16

Early modern painting treatises frequently suggested using different colors for male and female skin. A note from the De Mayerne manuscript, under the heading “Methods for doing the nude and faces,” exemplifies this concern: “carnation is usually made with white lead, or ceruse with vermillion or lead minimum, or lake, or yellow ocher, as one will have it or as the work requires for the masculine, as well as the feminine, flesh tones.”17 According to Ann-Sophie Lehmann, one of the earliest recipes to distinguish male and female flesh colors occurs in a Dutch manuscript compiled before 1500. Here, lead white, vermilion, and rose make a good color for a woman’s body, while Parisian red and lead white are suitable for female faces; for the body color of men, yellow ocher should be included, a combination of pigments that would lead to a more yellow hued flesh tone.18

It seems that early modern painting treatises usually instructed the gendering of skin color through the carnation layer, and that therefore the author-practitioner is somewhat unusual in choosing to vary the skin tones of men and women primarily through tinting their shadows differently. However, he was not unique in gendering the painting of shadows. In book two of his De’ Veri Precetti della Pittura (1586), Giovanni Battista Armenini (1530–1609) explains that “there are those who add some pure terra verde to these darks; others add the terra verde burned and baked in the way described for yellow. There are others yet who add terra di campana, especially when they wish to imitate the delicate shadows appearing in the flesh of young women.”19

The Humors

Humoral theory can help account for the artistic interest in the subtleties of skin. In the early modern humoral worldview, women were considered colder and wetter than men, as seventeenth-century French physician Marin Cureau de la Chambre expressed: “the Temperament of Man is hot and dry, and that of the woman cold and moist.”20 Conventional medical theory held that bodies were composed of four humors (blood, phlegm, black bile, and yellow bile), which combined to form a particular “temperament” or “complexion” that differed according to sex, age, and other factors. Specific elements constituted each humor (water, fire, earth, and air), and contemporaries associated each with certain qualities. According to Cureau de la Chambre, “reading the signs inscribed on the bodily exterior” could reveal the inner, fundamental aspects of a person. The color of a person’s complexion could indicate what “complexion” of humors he or she possessed. For instance, a man with a ruddy face had a predominance of the sanguine humor (blood) in his temperament. Humoral constitutions thus provide a lens through which to view differences between male and female skin colors. A cold and wet temperament would tend towards the phlegmatic, and towards a pale, cool tone of the skin.21

Lomazzo’s 1584 Trattato demonstrates that some early modern artists varied the colors used to depict skin according to the humoral composition of the person being represented. Lomazzo’s treatise explains for its readers the theory of the humors and exhorts the painter to pay close attention to the way that the temperaments of men express themselves outwardly, in actions, gestures, features, and the color of the skin, advising painters to “make the skin of sanguine figures rosier, melancholics swarthier, cholerics yellower, and phlegmatics paler.”22 In book six, chapter nine, Lomazzo describes the colors that correspond to the expression of the four humors in the flesh of human bodies, and in chapter ten, entitled “Come l’ombre debbono seguire il colore delle carni” (“How the shadows must follow the colors of the flesh”), he explains how the shadows must follow the colors of that humorally tinted skin, specifying relevant pigment combinations.23 

It seems that for the author-practitioner, as for Lomazzo, shadows and flesh were so closely interwoven that variation in one necessarily implied variation in the other. The entry “Shadows” (“Ombres”) on fol. 58v notes, “when you are making a painting with several persons, just as the flesh colors have to be different, so do the shadows.” Yet unlike Lomazzo, the author-practitioner’s material specifications for depicting gender begin and end with shadows. The “Face in distemper” entry on fol. 93v describes how to apply shadows as the first layer of the depiction, embedding the distinction between representations of males and females at the foundation of the work. There are no references in Ms. Fr. 640 to changing the carnation colors for subsequent paint layers to distinguish males from females—this difference is specified only for the painting of shadows.

Artists varied the “complexions” of men and women for many reasons that did not necessarily owe anything directly to humoral theory. For instance, elite women often kept indoors, and female standards of beauty promoted pale skin. Yet, in the early modern worldview, the colors of the flesh were fundamentally related to both the inner constitution of the person covered by that skin and increasingly to its external appearance. The very word “complexion,” used today to refer to the skin of the face, referred previously to the combination of humors in the body.24 According to Kimberly Poitevin, by “1601, the word had also come to mean ‘a coloring preparation applied… to “give a complexion” to the face.’”25 The surface of the skin revealed the inner—humoral—self on the outside, through the “complexion,” but by the end of the sixteenth century this exterior might be altered through the application of paint to skin.

Painting Skin with Skin

Human skin is translucent, laminar, and the appearance of its surface owes a great deal to the layers beneath. The parallel between the author-practitioner’s system for painting a face using layers of distemper and the layered composition of human flesh is provocative. Studying real skin through its surface, one can see cool greenish or bluish tones below, in the veins, particularly if the skin is pale, as was fashionable for women in sixteenth-century France. Women in early modern England sometimes painted blue veins on their skin, which points to the contemporary sensitivity to the play of veining beneath female flesh, and by extension to the impact of the inside of the body on the outside. Does the author-practitioner gender the base-tone from the very start to mimic the way that human skin, translucent and layered, reveals the inner self, constituted of the humors, lying beneath?

The use of distemper draws a particularly close connection between the depiction of flesh and flesh itself. “Distemper colors” (couleurs a destrempe) could be made of glue, which was normally made either from animal hide or parchment clippings (which also ultimately originated from animal skins). Although this recipe does not specify what the author-practitioner intends by “distemper colors” (couleurs a destrempe), on balance it seems to indicate the use of pigments bound in animal glue.26 In the process of painting a face in distemper, then, the early modern practitioner might be creating skin using skin. It is difficult to know how much thought was given to this connection, but certainly early modern artisans would have been far more aware of the origins of the materials they used than most people are today. How differently might the act of “mimicking” skin be considered if the process were carried out using skin itself? The very language used to describe the painting of skin in this way seems to draw some connection with humoral theory. Destrempe is connected etymologically to the Latin temperare, that is to proportion or mingle properly, which is also the root of the term “temperament,” i.e., the blend and balance of an individual’s humoral makeup that was also manifest in the “complexion.” The material synchronicity between the substances being used to paint and the material being depicted is intriguing here.

In the entry for “Face in distemper” there are moments when the language that describes the process for painting a face seems to converge with the language that would be used to describe a living person, particularly when the author writes, “and again with a more lively flesh color [carnation plus vive], one touches the more prominent areas” (fol. 93v). The French seems to press for a more symbolically rich reading because of the evocation of “incarnation” present in the term carnation, particularly when connected with the notion of something being plus vive, that is, “more lively” or “more alive.” A dense web of meaning thus appears to connect the author-practitioner’s instructions for shadowed flesh in this recipe with the internal humoral composition of the body that was such a fundamental part of early modern understandings of the body’s inner workings.

Reconstructions

I carried out reconstructions with three principle aims: to understand what medium was intended by the term destrempe, to understand the difference between colors indicated for the shadows of male and female skin, and to establish whether the author-practitioner’s layered painting system is effective for imitating skin.27 My hands-on experimentation focused primarily on the entry on fol. 93v, supplemented by related recipes across the manuscript. In addition, I experimented with the use of jet as a pigment for men’s shadows. The translated instructions of the entry on fol. 93v read:

Face in distemper

Because colors [painted] in distemper [a destrempe] dry promptly & one would not have leisure to perfect the shadows & touches on the face, one wets the reverse of the face which is on canvas with a wet sponge. Then, with a small paintbrush, one makes the shadows, which do not come undone. Next, one lays the flesh color, which does not prevent the already painted shadows from appearing [i.e., showing through the paint]. And again with a more lively flesh color, one touches the more prominent areas. And with another paintbrush, one does the shadows again by hatching. The shadow strokes do not come undone because they are made of bistre, which stains the canvas like rust. The said bistre is good with for making shadows in distemper mix, for in oil it has no body & would not dry but with great difficulty. One mixes in the said bistre, for shadow, ocre de ru & a little sap green. The best bistre is the greasy & shiny kind from the fireplaces of large kitchens. It is difficult to grind & screeches on the marble.

Sizing

In the recipe, the author-practitioner makes no mention of preparing the canvas using a size or ground layer, which initially seemed strange. Available literature on the materials and techniques of early modern distemper paintings suggest that they usually had a size preparation layer. Some works executed with glue on canvas by Dieric Bouts have a thin glue size preparation, while Mantegna’s Sampson and Delilah has a more substantial one.28 On the other hand, technical study at the National Gallery London carried out on glue distemper paintings by Quentin Massys and Dieric Bouts were not able to prove whether they ever had a size layer.29

In the study of early modern artisanal craft treatises, the issue of tacit knowledge is pervasive. Writer-practitioners would often have been able to rely on their readers to fill apparent gaps in their texts with such knowledge.30 To investigate whether tacit knowledge of the habitual practice of sizing canvases for painting was supposed to undergird this recipe, I prepared two canvases differently.

I stretched two linen canvases onto wooden auxiliary supports, and left the first one raw, that is, unsized. I prepared the second canvas by sizing it with two layers of 10% rabbit skin glue with a small addition of champagne chalk.31 

I then proceeded to experiment by first wetting the back of the canvas, as instructed, and used a small brush to paint in the shadows of a face according to the recipe from fol. 93v. Once I began the process of painting, some of my questions about canvas preparation were swiftly answered. The sized canvas did not allow the moisture to penetrate from the back to the front of the canvas and therefore rendered the instruction to do so pointless (Fig. 6, Fig. 7).32 Therefore, it seems probable that the author-practitioner intended an unsized canvas as the support for painting in this case and that its absence from the entry was not an oversight or a call to use tacit knowledge. This proved a lesson in careful reading of the text and following its advice before rushing to draw conclusions from prior, secondary reading.

Paint Medium

Identification of the medium for “Face in distemper” is complicated by the fact that the term destrempe, or words closely related to it, is used around ninety times across the manuscript in a variety of senses, including in recipes for casting as well as a binder for pigments. This ambiguity is related to the larger issue of polysemy surrounding the English word “distemper” (and its cognates in other languages). As a type of paint, it is always defined as water-based, but there is no agreement in current scholarship as to its specific meaning. Andrea Rothe seems to equate it with an animal-glue binder, remarking that although Mantegna’s painting technique for his Adoration of the Magi “was generally described as painted in tempera or oil, recent analysis carried out by the Getty Conservation Institute has identified the medium as distemper (animal glue).”33 More recently, Sophie Scully and Christine Seidel, in an article on a tüchlein by Justus van Ghent at The Metropolitan Museum of Art, describe distemper as a “catch-all term used to refer to pigments bound in a water-based medium, sometimes gum or egg-white, but most often animal glue.”34 More broadly still, the Oxford English dictionary defines distemper painting as, 

A method of painting, in which the colors are mixed with some glutinous substance soluble in water, as yolk of egg mixed with water, etc., usually executed upon a ground of chalk or plaster mixed with gum (distemper-ground): mostly used in scene painting, and in the internal decoration of walls.

The confusion around this term and related terms is not only an issue of dictionary definitions, it is also connected to the methods and limitations we have for discerning the paint media used in historical paintings. As Rothe explains, there is some “difficulty in analytically distinguishing egg from glue, as both are complex proteins. The analytical results can also be unreliable because infusions of glues from later relinings and consolidants make it difficult to determine whether they are part of the original medium.”35

Practitioners were concerned primarily with the results that they could achieve and the handling properties of their materials—not their identification at a chemical level. Sometimes they may have used egg white, glue, or gum arabic interchangeably, as suggested by a recipe in the De Mayerne manuscript which is very similar to that on fol. 93v.36

I experimented with painting the shadow layer of the face using the three most likely paint media: glue, egg white, and gum arabic. For the trial using glue as the medium, I used rabbit skin glue at a concentration of approximately 10%. Working on a damp canvas proved to be very important for this medium, as the humidity of the support improved the paint flow and slowed the drying time, avoiding the rapid coagulation that occurred when animal glue paint was used on the sized canvas. This was extremely important, because the fast drying time of glue seems to be one of its main limitations in comparison with other paint media. Using animal glue as the medium and following the instructions of the text to use an unsized canvas dampened from the reverse was quite successful (Fig. 8).

Using egg white as the medium on unsized and dampened canvas also produced a successful result, and the medium was similar in handling properties and final result to the animal glue. By contrast in the trial with a distemper made from gum arabic on unsized canvas, the penetration of the canvas by moisture flooded the paint, causing it to flow excessively beyond the bounds of the underdrawing. It seems challenging to use gum arabic with a very damp canvas, though there are many variables that could have contributed to my lack of success with this medium in this particular trial.

Pigments for a Woman’s Shadows

When painting the shadow layer, I used the pigments suggested by the author-practitioner: bistre, ocre de ru, and a little sap green. Bistre (or bister) is a black-brown pigment that conservation scientists Maria L. Roldan et al. note is normally made from wood soot collected near to a flame.37 In the recipe on fol. 93v, the author-practitioner specifies that “the best bistre is the greasy & shiny kind from the fireplaces of large kitchens.” He also notes that such bistre “is difficult to grind & screeches on the marble,” and indeed, when I ground bistre in water in my reconstruction, discussed below, it did make a particularly grating sound. According to Rosamond D. Harley, bistre is mainly used with water-based binders, which corroborates the author-practitioner’s instruction that it is “good for” distemper but not for working in oil.38 Wood soot may have been used as a pigment since very early times due to its ease of preparation, low cost, and tinting power, but the seventeenth century is normally cited as the period in which the term “bistre” began to be used. The use of the term in this manuscript, thought to date from the late sixteenth century, may be a significant early example.

Sap green was a pigment made from ripe buckthorn berries, and the French term vert de vessie (lit., “bladder green”) has been thought to originate in the fact that it was often stored in a bladder.39 Ocre de ru was a form of ocher, therefore one of a group of “yellow or brownish-yellow iron oxide pigments, deriving their color from goethite (α-FeOOH) or a similar mineral.”40 It is thought to have been somewhat brown in color and was far more expensive than ordinary ocher. When combined and ground on the slab with water in the Making and Knowing Laboratory, these pigments came together to make a brownish grey with a hint of green, essentially just enough to “cool” the brown and prevent it from tending to red (Fig. 9).

Pigments for a Man’s Shadows

Having experimented with the combination of pigments suggested for the depiction of shadows in a face on fol. 93v, and established that they were in all probability considered appropriate for representing a woman, I was keen to understand the difference in hue between those and the shadows that the author-practitioner advised using for male figures. In the entry on fol. 65r in which the author-practitioner specified the use of pigments to arrive at a “greenish black” for women’s figures, he instructed the painter to use “very strongly ground jet” mixed with ocher and white lead for the “yellowish” shadows of men. He repeated the direction to use jet (geiete) as a pigment for male shadows in an entry entitled “Shadows” (“Umbres”) on fol. 63r: “German painters make their shadows [ombres] on flesh color of men with ground jet, stil de grain yellow & ocher.” Jet is a variety of lignite (fossilized wood) that is characteristically black or dark brown. There is a long history of using jet in jewelry; it can be polished to a high shine, is relatively soft and thus easy to work, and is relatively lightweight. Whitby in the British Isles is a particularly well-known source of jet, and the jet bought for this experiment was acquired from there, but the material was also mined in France.  

Jet seems to be absent from the secondary literature as a material for the production of black pigment.41 I was therefore very interested in evaluating whether it could be used as a pigment.

When I cut the jet, it was dark blue inside, and when it was first crushed, the particles had a blue, glass-like appearance, likely due to the state of dessication. Further pounding in the pestle and mortar produced a yellowish tint, and the color gradually changed towards a dark blue-green. When ground with water the color remained a dark blue-green. I experimented with grinding it in linseed oil to assess the effect of medium variation on color and the hue shifted to a green-gray when ground further with the oil (Fig. 10).

Carnation Layers

I proceeded to follow the rest of the recipe using animal skin glue as a medium. This allowed me to investigate the handling properties of this rather unusual medium in more depth. The next layer to be applied after the shadow was the first “carnation” layer: “Next, one lays the flesh color [carnation], which does not prevent the already painted shadows from appearing” (fol. 93v). No specific instructions were provided in this entry for the combination of pigments to be used for the “carnation,” so I drew upon entries elsewhere in the manuscript to guide this aspect of my reconstruction. I used madder lake and yellow ocher in combination with champagne chalk to create a pink skin tone.42 Chalk was selected over lead white for this layer, because it functions as an opaque pigment when bound in glue, whereas in oil it becomes transparent. Chalk was used as a white pigment in paintings produced using glue on linen, for instance those by Dieric Bouts.43 It was thus of interest to try using chalk as an opaque medium in this method, and it did indeed function successfully to lighten the combination of yellow ocher and madder to achieve a bright flesh tone (Fig. 11). The application of this flesh colored layer over the shadowed layer beneath was successful; as suggested by the entry, the carnation veil remained translucent and allowed the shadows beneath to penetrate to the surface (Fig. 12).

Next I followed the indications that “again with a more lively flesh color, one touches the more prominent areas” (fol. 93v). For this stage, I used lead white in combination with the madder lake and yellow ocher to achieve a brighter white and to see what contrasts could be achieved using both lead white and chalk white in a painting in glue on linen (Fig. 13). Although using lead white increased the opacity of this layer somewhat in comparison with the chalk white, it remained sufficiently translucent so as to allow the subtle penetration of both the shadow and first carnation layer through to the final surface. Areas that began with the dark shadow as the base and were then covered by two further layers of carnation, such as the center-right of the forehead, looked notably cool and pale in the final result (Fig. 14).

Paint Handling

Entries elsewhere in the manuscript proved useful as additional guides to the process of painting—how to handle the paint, what ductus to adopt. In an entry on fol. 65r entitled “Flesh color” (“Carnation”), the author-practitioner emphasizes the importance of “softening”:

You must know well the mixing of colors & the appropriate shadows, but above all make sure to soften them well. And note & observe that a thing which appears flat needs to be softened lengthwise, as if going from right to left like [illustration of diagonal hatching], and whatever ought to appear round must be softened in circles.

By soften (adoulcir), the author-practitioner seems to mean blending, especially between the darks and the lights, which creates a more even surface and crucially allows one to achieve an impression of relief on a flat surface:

if you want to model relief well, soften round things by rounding them with the point of the paintbrush & the rest with the flat part if it is flat, & thus for the others according to their nature, & lightly with the point of the dry & flattened paintbrush & with patience.

The need to soften between lights and darks in the production of flesh tones raised the question of how achievable this would be with a glue-based medium that dries quickly. The author-practitioner addresses this problem in a marginal note to the entry “Painter” on fol. 56v:

In distemper do not mix your different colors together for this makes them die, but use each separately. And in order that they do not dry & that you have time to soften, moisten the back of the canvas.

Following the procedure for “Face in distemper,” I found that it was possible to achieve some blending of colors thanks to the moisture provided by wetting the back of the canvas with a damp sponge (Fig. 15). This advantage was limited, however, as wetting the canvas too much from the reverse slightly re-dissolved the layer beneath and risked causing the medium to reticulate. Thus, while further wetting might have made increased softening possible, it might have done more harm than good to the overall effect.

Final Shading

It was not possible for me to achieve a blending effect in the final shadow layer applied at the end of the painting process. This was because the number of glue paint layers had built up sufficiently on the surface to prevent the moisture applied from the back of the canvas from coming through to the surface. To further saturate the canvas with water would have caused too great a reticulation of the glue painted layers already applied (some reticulation was already observed before I stopped wetting the back). This may explain why the author-practitioner insists on cross-hatching as the technique to be used for the final shadows on the face:

And with another paintbrush, one does the shadows again by hatching. The shadow strokes do not come undone because they are made of bistre, which stains the canvas like rust.

Much as I was, the author-practitioner seems to have been concerned with the risk that the shadows might come “undone.” Hatching allows a visual gradation between two paint layers when either the medium is opaque or the layering of a fast drying paint renders wet-on-wet blending impossible. The application of the final hatching shadow layer was quite successful in the experiment, particularly where hatching was done over multiple layers of the skin tone (Fig. 16, Fig. 17).

Reconstruction Insights

The reconstruction process suggests that the most likely medium intended by distemper in this case is animal skin glue, although considerable further experimentation is needed to reach firm conclusions. I found both egg white and glue were successful media for this painting method in my experiments, but textual clues indicate that the author-practitioner did not mean egg white when he used the term “distemper.” In a recipe on fol. 66r, the author-practitioner writes that because it is unpleasant to paint in distemper on wood, some temper or thin their colors with egg white and apply it with a sponge. Here, he is clearly distinguishing between distemper and egg white, which, though it can be used as a substitute binding medium, is not quite synonymous.

The main caveat to this conclusion in favor of animal skin glue distemper is the fact that the author-practitioner does not advise keeping the glue warm while painting. In the reconstructions, it was necessary to keep the glue medium warm at all times in order to paint with it. Other painting treatises do mention this, for instance De coloribus diversis modis tractatur in sequentibus, compiled by Johannes Alcherius (fl. 1382–1411), is very insistent on the subject, saying: “lastly, let the size, or sized water be warm; I say warm; lest it may be conglutinated… when it is cold it will be congealed like jelly.”44 Attempts to use animal glue as a binding medium without heating were unsuccessful. Here again, one faces the question of tacit knowledge—did the author-practitioner assume that any painter would know to keep the glue warm? Or did some property of his glue, perhaps its concentration or derivation from a particular animal, for instance, allow him to use such a medium without heat? We do not know whether the author-practitioner ever tried out the instructions he gives here, so it is also possible that information is missing because he is reporting it secondhand.

The combination of pigments indicated by the author-practitioner for the shadows on fol. 93v creates a brownish grey with a hint of green. His indication to combine pigments to form a greenish tint suggests that, according to his system of gendering the color of shadows, the face that he had in mind here was a woman’s.

My experiment with jet was not particularly successful or conclusive, although it did determine that jet can be crushed with relative ease into a powder that could potentially serve as a pigment. I found the pigment to be a dark blue in water and a greenish-grey in linseed oil. Many variables could have contributed to the unexpected color of the final pigment I produced. These results surprised me because I expected a pigment derived from jet would be black, not least because coal, similar in chemical composition to jet, has sometimes been used as a black pigment. It is also possible that the author-practitioner may have made a mistake in suggesting “jet” (geiete) here, as there are two strike-throughs in the recipe on fol. 65r, and at one point he appears unable to decide on the appropriate term for a tool and replaces it with a small drawing of a pentagon. Towards the end of the same recipe, he writes “black of” (noir de) and then leaves a space, perhaps as though intending to return and fill in a qualifier later. This could indicate that the author-practitioner had obtained his information secondhand, or that perhaps he felt uncertain about the materials and process he was recording in this recipe. 

I was able to achieve a fairly successful depiction of a face using glue as the medium and drawing on advice about techniques for painting in distemper and skin elsewhere in the manuscript to support the instructions on fol. 93v (Fig. 18). The translucency of the medium permits the shadows to show through to the surface of the image effectively, and a combination of softening and cross-hatching allows the painter to create a suggestion of relief. The superimposition of translucent colors affords an imitation of the layered, translucent quality of human flesh. The achievable results would undoubtedly improve with repeated practice.

The author-practitioner was very interested in the different techniques required for working in distemper as opposed to in an oil medium and he is sufficiently attentive that his directions for the methods required for handling distemper paint were effective in practice.

In a series of marginal notes on fol. 56v under the heading “Painter,” he discusses the need for training in the specific media and makes an observation about regional variation:

The one who makes a profession of working in oil will hardly work well in distemper if he has not been trained well from adolescence. For the way of working is different, because when washing the paintbrush for distemper, one always leaves it moist. And on the contrary, when one cleans the paintbrush in oil to soften, one wipes the paintbrush well. Otherwise, the work would run & would soon be all disfigured.

***

The Italians soften by hatching with a large flattened paintbrush which makes serrations.

***

And they do not lay their shadow all at once like the Flemish, but make them hatching lightest toward the light then a little darker next & finally a little blacker to better make project & come out.

***

In distemper do not mix your different colors together for this makes them die, but use each separately. And in order that they do not dry & that you have time to soften, moisten the back of the canvas.

The author-practitioner here associates Italian practice with distemper, as evidenced by the requirement for hatching which corresponds to the manner of shading he prescribes for the final shadows in the distemper painting of a face. We may assume that the Flemish technique he discusses is oil painting. During the sixteenth century a majority of Italian painters turned to oil painting, which had long been the dominant technique in northern Europe. It is therefore interesting to note that the author-practitioner, at the end of the sixteenth century, still associates protein and water-based media with Italian painting practice.

Conclusion

The process for painting a face in distemper described by the author-practitioner has the potential for success. The favorable outcome of my reconstructions depended, to a certain extent, on my own prior experience with painting. Someone without any paint handling experience would probably find different results. This was my first effort at using a glue medium, and over the short course of this reconstruction, I was able to significantly improve my handling of the paint so that between the application of the first shadow layer and that of the first carnation layer, my movements began to feel more instinctive, and the experience of engaging with the materials brought about less frustration. During the application of the second carnation layer, I experienced relative confidence, control, and some fluidity. Such progress indicates the method would become more effective and successful over time as one’s skills improved.

The author-practitioner’s instructions fit into a European tradition of painting in protein- and glue-based media that stretched from the Medieval period through to at least the seventeenth century. The system of painting described in “Face in distemper,” and corroborated by my textual research and hands-on work in the laboratory, is very similar to the process of painting in glue described by Jill Dunkerton in 1991.45 It seems very possible that the medium intended by the author-practitioner in this entry was glue and that the pigments he suggests for the shadow layer would achieve a green toned shadow of the kind he considered appropriate for shading women’s skin. However, a great deal of further experimentation and research would be necessary to draw firm conclusions.
