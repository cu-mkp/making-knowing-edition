Lean Sands and Fat Binders

Abstract

The entry for “Eau magistra” on fol. 84r provides instructions to produce a liquid binder to be mixed with a dry powder for the making of sand molds. Recipes for “Eau magistra” and recipes that mention magistra in BnF Ms. Fr. 640 contain many different ingredients, including elm root, vinegar, salt water, glair of egg, and “good, pure wine.” It is not clear why some binder recipes in Ms. Fr. 640 are titled magistra while other binders, seemingly identical to them in function, are not denoted as such. The dry material, or “sand,” is sometimes described as “dry and lean,” thus requiring a wet or sometimes “fatty” substance to bind it together. This essay reconstructs the entry on fol. 84r, and discusses the framework within which these qualitative binary categories of fat and lean can be understood.

Magistra

The entry for “Eau magistra” on fol. 84r provides instructions to produce a liquid binder to be mixed with a dry powder for the making of sand molds. Recipes for “Eau magistra” (fols. 84r, 84v, 87v) and recipes that mention a magistra (fols. 68r, 69r, 69v) in BnF Ms. Fr. 640 contain many different ingredients, including elm root, wine, vinegar (fol. 87v), salt water (fol. 84v), glair of egg (fol. 69r), and “good, pure wine” (fol. 69v). The entry on fol. 84r calls for finely ground and dissolved sandiver or rock salt, moistened with tartar oil:

Eau magistra

Dissolve rock salt or salt finely pulverized sandiver & put on marble in a cellar, & it will dry out by reheating the mold, & will give it a bond to withstand several castings. Try to moisten it with tartar oil.

It is not clear why some binder recipes in Ms. Fr. 640 are titled magistra while other binders, seemingly identical to them in function, are not denoted as such. For example, on fol. 85v the author-practitioner notes that founders use egg whites beaten together with earth to make molding sand for bell copes, as this “makes it come out d neatly, & lays down & settles the substance.” Nonetheless, the recipes for “magistra” and other binders in Ms. Fr. 640 share some general similarities: they help bind the dry mold material in such a way that it allows a crisp impression of the pattern, releases the pattern, enables the sand to endure more than one casting, and allows easy release of the cast object.

Fat and Lean

The dry material, or “sand,” is sometimes described as “dry and lean,” thus requiring a wet or sometimes “fatty” substance to bind it together. For example, in describing sand made from ox foot bones in fol. 84v, the author-practitioner writes that “It molds on its own very neatly. But because on its own it is very arid & lean, it wants to be well moistened & dampened with wine boiled with elm root,” and in the margin, he writes, “A fatty metal needs lean sand.” On fol. 85r he recounts a successful mold material:

once again lightly anointed it with clear walnut oil & gently passed a linen cloth over it so that it does not remain too anointed, and I noted that, by this means, it would come out better in release, because the ceruse, once moistened with water of glair of egg, would not attach to the oil. This worked very well and I molded a medal of high relief very neatly, without any sticking, which a lot of good sands, such as felt, burnt bone & iron dross, had failed to do on the first try. I reheated it & my mold became hard like marble, and by this I knew that sands for molding high relief should be well moistened with some water, which gives them body & compactness, such as glair, gummed water, ea wine boiled with elm root, &c; and lightly oil the medal, it withstands as many castings as you wish, for it is as hard as glass.

However, he then appears to reconsider, and goes on to say,

Since then I have realized that this sand, even though it is excellent & endures many castings & molds very neatly, nevertheless is fatty and makes things porous. Thus, soft lead and the does not come out so well. But try to mix it with a lean sand, such as pumice, scales & similar things to give them body & so that they release better. For lean sands barely release well and yet they receive metal well.

A search of Ms. Fr. 640 for gras (“fat,” “fatty”) and maigre (“lean”) reveals many instances. Our reconstruction of the recipe on fol. 84r, in conjunction with these other instances in Ms. Fr. 640 of fat binders and lean sands, helped to illuminate the meaning of the categories “fat” and “lean” that scholars have identified in other early modern writings as an important aspect of the understanding of materials in early modern Europe.1 Fat and lean were part of the system of qualities, similar to the Aristotelian categories of wet, dry, hot, and cold. They formed part of a “material imaginary” shared by the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 and other metalworkers. Our reconstruction of the “Eau magistra” recipe on fol. 84r showed that both dry and wet were used to support the terms lean and fatty. Moreover, our investigation revealed an unexpected sensory aspect to the property of “fattiness.”

Reconstruction

In order to test the “Eau magistra” recipe on folio 84r, we first sought out suin de verre, or sandiver (also commonly referred to as glass gall), as the recipe stipulates.2 Randle Cotgrave defines suin de verre as “sandever,” a “fatty substance floating on glass when it is red-hot in the furnace, and which being cold is as hard as stone, yet brittle, and easily broken.”3 A nineteenth-century source defines sandiver as composed of “all those salts contained in common alkalis that readily melt at somewhat less than a glass-making heat, and are either naturally considerably volatile, or have little if any affinity for silex, and do not unite in the composition of glass, but, being superficially lighter, rise to the top.” Apparently this byproduct of glassmaking was “generally skimmed off with iron ladles, and sold to metal refiners as a powerful flux.”4

In the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, sandiver was used as an ingredient in crocus metallorum in addition to antimony and saltpeter for an alchemical recipe, as a pesticide for garden walks, where it “destroys both weeds and vermin,” and seems even to have been used in France for culinary purposes, “to powder their meat, and to eat, instead of common salt.”5 In general, sandiver had multiple uses, as De Blancourt writes:

this salt which the French call (Suin de Verre) Sandever is useful for several purposes, and in several Chymical Operations. It has besides some other Uses and Virtues that are not known, even to very few of the Learned; I could tell some very surprising and wonderful uses of it. But this may serve to whet the Industry of, and excite the Curious to further Enquiries.6

Our search for sandiver as “glass gall” revealed this to be an obsolete material, impossible to obtain in modern glass making due to standardization and quality control of ingredients which ensure that byproducts such as sandiver do not result from production. A toxic product called “sandiver” is still produced in glass recycling. However it is now defined as the contaminants—such as asbestos—that result from the recycling of waste glass and which must be separated from the glass mass in the furnace in order to produce usable glass.7

Thus, with no feasible way to obtain sandiver for the magistra experiment, we decided instead to follow the recipe’s suggestion to use rock salt in place of sandiver. Sandiver and rock salt seem to have been seen as interchangeable or at least related in some processes—in “Eau magistra” on fol. 84r, as well as for an eighteenth-century author who suggested in Van Nostrand’s Engineering Magazine that for “pig or sow iron, videlicet, the ashes of wood and other vegetables, all kinds of glass and sandiver, common salt and rock salt” be mixed into the iron mass to purify it.8

Molds to Withstand Many Castings

sandiver and rock salt are suggested as dry binding materials in more than one recipe in Ms. Fr. 640, where the author-practitioner comments on the strength of the molds they produce. For example, the recipe titled “Sand from a mine in Thoulouse” on fol. 84r lists sandiver as an ingredient in the sand which will make it withstand several castings:

The sand from Thoulouse, which is taken, from the depths of the earth, from the small hill of Puy David, is excellent in itself, but to make it withstand several castings, I mix it with pulverized sandiver & moistened, which hardens it, & takes body & bond with it, & makes it withstand more than five as many castings as you wish. Try to moisten the pestled glass and other e sands with the waters of the aforesaid salts. It wants to be, like all natural sand, well reheated before putting it to use.

The “Eau magistra” recipe, also on 84r, likewise specifies that the resulting mold should be strong enough to withstand several castings. Similarly, the recipe on fol. 89r, which calls for mixing together pulverized ox bone and rock salt then moistening them in a damp place, is said to have withstood many castings.

To reconstruct the process on fol. 84r, we ground rock salt, mixed it with oil of tartar,9 then mixed it with a dry sand composed of reground molds from previous casting (made from pulverized brick and plaster). When mixed with the eau magistra of fol. 84r, this recipe produced a two-piece mold that, despite some crumbling and partial disintegration, remained intact enough for a second clean casting (Fig. 1, Fig. 2, Fig. 3, Fig. 4). We cannot decisively conclude from this single trial10 that the magistra of fol. 84r was responsible for the success of this mold, however, our investigation of the ox bone and rock salt recipe on fol. 89r also produced an unexpectedly hard mold that was firmly bound together and remained intact enough to be cast more than once.11

These two recipes specify rock salt or sandiver mixed with the dry sand material. Thus, the salt forms a “dry binder” for the sand, rather than a wet binder such as elm root infusions and egg whites.12 The binding action occurs when the salt mixture is moistened. The “fatty” property of rock salt or of sandiver remedies the “lean” properties of the dry mold. The author-practitioner makes this explicit when he writes in a recipe for sand made from sal ammoniac and alabaster powder that “ammoniac is fatty and a va is nevertheless sandy, which makes it release well. There is no better bond than salts appropriate for metals, for once mixed in powder, they get moistened together & dry & reheat together” (fol. 89v).

The Sensory Experience of Fat and Lean

How can salt be understood as “fatty”? In our reconstructions of the recipes on fols. 84r and 89r, we struggled with the concepts of fat and lean, as they did not make sense within a modern understanding of salts, much less of “fat.” We began to understand the meaning of fat and lean, however, when we ground rock salt for both the “Eau magistra” of fol. 84r and ox bone-rock salt sand of fol. 89r (Fig.5, Fig. 6). After grinding the salt in granite mortars as finely as possible and storing it in an airtight jar, we returned to the lab a few days later and found that the texture of the salt was very “sticky.” The salt had transformed from a fine, dry powder that poured freely from an outstretched hand to a sticky substance, in which the particles clearly adhered to each other, creating an unexpected sensation of resistance when running one’s fingers along the surface. The familiar material of salt unexpectedly possessed a completely different set of properties, and we felt the “fattiness” of the rock salt, which also afforded a new workability for our molds. This allowed us to apprehend the place of “fatty” in the “material imaginary” of the author-practitioner,13 and it allowed us a rather different perspective than is given by our modern classification of salt as sodium chloride.

Conclusion

We concluded that paying attention to the framework of qualitative binaries such as fat and lean can help us understand the early modern experience of materials in relation to the practitioner’s body: the material properties of hot, dry, wet, cold, fatty, and lean also described the characteristics and temperaments of human bodies, and early modern people thought about their bodies and their selves as needing to be tempered and balanced by ingesting materials and foods of opposing qualities. Materials had their own characters, with likes and dislikes, and they, too, needed to be balanced and tempered in similar ways (and sometimes with the same types of remedies). Moreover, as the primary tool in the workshop, the human body worked in collaboration with and in reaction to the materials. Materials were often tasted, smelled, listened to, and handled to determine their wants and needs.14 In addition, the fluids of the body often became materials themselves—urine, phlegm, even the “white thick saliva that is found on the teeth” (fol. 134r)—were used in the work. And, materials, such as the fumes from metals, were known to penetrate the bodies of those who worked on them. These analogies and intersections between human body and natural materials fostered the view that the microcosm of the human body functioned as a part of and in concert with the macrocosm of nature. Sensory apprehension, in this view, could consist in a resonance between the body and the materials it worked. Observation thus meant not only paying attention to the materials, but also being alert to the sensations of one’s own body.
