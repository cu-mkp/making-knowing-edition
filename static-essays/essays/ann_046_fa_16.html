Pain, Ostie, Rostie: Bread in Early Modern Europe

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/vmhr-4038
← Back to Essays

Abstract

What is bread in the context of Ms. Fr. 640? This category of food appears in the manuscript under three different names: bread, host (37r), and toast (79v, 123v). The meaning and use of these terms are considered as well as the production of bread and Hosts in the early modern period and the pathways by which bread could have entered the author-practitioner’s workshop. Flour was the key factor in bread production, often selected by availability, preference, and beliefs about particular cereals. The baking practices required for different grains influenced the breads one could bake. Some, such as oatbread, could be baked on the hearth at home; others, such as fine wheat bread, required the high and constant heat of a specialized oven and were chiefly baked professionally. The varieties of flours, breads, and processes show the meaning of “bread” in the period of the manuscript’s compilation cannot be taken for granted.

Table 1 enables us to see the three principal terms used to refer to bread: pain, rostie, and ostie. While the first is simply the generic noun “bread,” the other two imply a specific kind of bread, with rostie referring to toast and ostie to the Eucharist of Christian Communion. Table 1 also allows us to see the use of bread in utilitarian and creative contexts, such as feeding birds and training horses, or cleaning printing plates and erasing graphite and ink. Looking closely at bread’s presence in the manuscript also results in a surprising outcome. Short references to specific flours and consistencies begin to complicate the generic term “bread” and raise the deceptively simple question: what is bread? By examining a variety of agricultural, medical, and culinary sources, this essay seeks to answer this question and to explore what role bread played in an artisan’s workshop in late sixteenth-century Toulouse.

Bread is a baked mixture of flour and water, often with yeast and salt added. Already, this rudimentary definition points out the main variable in the recipe: the flour. Far from being solely dictated by availability, the choice of flour depended as much on personal preference—often defined by class norms—as on beliefs and characteristics attributed to different varieties of cereal.3

Bread from Antiquity through the Early Modern Period

In the ancient West, bread constituted the primary source of nourishment.4 Indeed, the Greek word sitos and the Hebrew word lehem both carry the general meaning “food” as well as a specific designation for “bread” or “grain.”5 Although the primacy of bread as sustenance was unchallenged, prominent thinkers such as Homer, Aristotle, Pliny, and Galen of Pergamon held strong beliefs regarding the hierarchy of cereals in terms of nourishment. While Homer declared barley meal and wheat flour to be the “marrow of men,” Pliny and Aristotle claimed that although it was the oldest cereal, barley was to be “mostly fed to animals,” with Aristotle professing that wheat is “more nourishing than barley.”6

Galen held strong opinions regarding the hierarchy of grains. His extensive study of cereals in On the Powers of Food articulated the principles of a healthy diet, which were adhered to up through the early modern period.7 In order to understand his logic in placing wheat flour above all others, we must suspend our contemporary understanding of the nutritional values of fiber. Indeed, for Galen, the “slowest breads to pass” held the best health benefits.8 He noted the useful purgative and cooling qualities of barley in treating fevers, and he held the nourishment from barley bread to be “considerable,” though far less than wheat bread.9 Millet bread to Galen was the “most laxative,” while oats were only to be consumed by humans in times of famine, when they could be made into bread.10 The best bread for Galen, then, was made from wheat flours, which “possess great power, provided they are digested, and… strongly nourish the body, thereby affording remarkable strength to those who eat them,” but even wheat was subject to a hierarchy.11 Indeed, the “most nourishing wheats” were hulled and appeared yellower than “spongier wheats” that contained a lot of bran and were consequently more “purgative.”12

Additionally, Galen specified the process by which the best bread was to be made, writing that “superior breads from compact and heavy wheats” contained more yeast,13 had been kneaded the most, and had been left to rest between rising and baking.14 The best bread was baked in a moderately hot oven, while the easiest to digest was cooked on an earthenware hearth tile on a moderate fire.15 Such superior breads were “genuinely white.”16 Baking over a grill or hot ashes, on the other hand, led to a “bad” and “uneven” bread.17 Further, Galen forbade using additives such as beans, lentils and peas to make bread, a practice often carried out by the poor in times of scarcity.18 Indeed, this practice continued well into the early modern period. The sixteenth-century author and recipe collector Hugh Plat was interested in foods consumed in times of dearth and recorded various recipes containing additives such as “peas, beans, beech mast, buckwheat, chestnuts, acorns, vetches, arrowroot, turnips, pumpkins, and parsnips.”19 Among Plat’s recipes is one that uses wheat straw to make bread obtained from Bernardino de Mendoza, the Spanish Ambassador to France.20 This practice of adding various herbs and pulses to bread was common, although also thought to be dangerous. For example, Modenese chronicler Giovan Battista Spaccini reported in 1596 at Ferrara that “it is believed that the cause of many illnesses, of which numerous people even die, is the bad breads which the people eat, namely that of beans, cabbage, and plain oil.”21 This potentially noxious practice was forbidden to bakers, as emerges from a 1592 account from Modena relating how bakers and the judge of provisions were arrested “for having had forty sacks of bay leaf ground to be put in the wheat flour to make bread for the square, where it caused the poverty to those who bought it to worsen, so that for two days there were many people sick enough to go crazy, and during this time they could not work or help their families.”22

Although the mentions of bread in Ms. Fr. 640 are rather brief, they show an appreciation of the characteristics of different flours. Fol. 29r features a detailed recipe for stucco which specifies that rye flour rather than wheat flour is to be used, as the latter is rougher and less moist when combined into a paste.23 This reference to the comparative moistness of different flours is already found in Galen, who extols bread’s “moistening” quality for health, but this characteristic was clearly just as relevant to some artisanal practices.24

Bread in the Urban Realm

Let us turn our attention to the production of bread in early modern Europe. From the multitude of bread varieties available, it is clear that social status played an important role in bread production and consumption, since specialty ovens and the fuel required to heat them made some breads less accessible. For example, manchet, a bread made of the finest white flour and favored by the wealthy in England between 1550 and 1600, required a purpose-built oven that ensured the bricks were evenly heated at a consistent, high temperature.25 This was true of leavened wheat bread in general.26 The necessity of an oven excluded a large segment of the population from baking such bread at home, restricting them instead to barley and oat bread, which could be baked in pots in front of the hearth.27 Alternatively, dough could be prepared at home and then taken either to the local baker or to a communal oven to be baked for a fee.28

But even for the lower social classes, bread was not only a matter of cost but also of preference. In Juan Luis Vives’s Dialogues, a set of Latin translation exercises for Vives’s students in Bruges dating to before 1540, a conversation between a group of men highlights different tastes for varying amounts of yeast and water and degrees of sponginess and kneading.29 A preference in grain and baking technique is also evident.30 Often, preferences for certain cereals arose regionally. Oats remained consistently popular in northern England, for example, even as the status and popularity of wheat grew.31 Preference was to some degree dictated by the availability of grain and flour. In France, the three-field system predominated, in which one field was left fallow to replenish itself, one was planted with a winter grain (wheat or rye), and the third with a spring crop (oats, barley, peas, or beans).32 This ensured a constant yield of grains.33 This variety is visible in the manuscript, which mentions rye, millet, and also a finer flour that is likely to be wheat. In protoindustrial rural France, merchants hired peasants to process the harvested grains before importing them to urban artisans, who turned them into finished goods.34

Toulouse, being a large city, had access to a wide variety of grains coming from the surrounding region, which was one of the most fertile in France.35 From the twelfth to the nineteenth century, Toulouse also featured a halle aux grains, an impressive warehouse for grain, pointing to an abundance of this important staple.36 In 1599, the Swiss medical student Thomas Platter noted that Toulouse’s mills were “the most remarkable one could hope to see in all Christendom” and that they could “provide more than enough flour each day for a hundred thousand persons.”37 An official from Toulouse in the early modern period noted that “the people here know no other food than wheat; it would not be possible to make them adopt another.”38 This statement is probably hyperbolic, but it points to the predominance of wheat in Toulouse in this period. Such a preference is also observed in a more general sense by the eighteenth-century French agronomist Henri-Louis Duhamel du Monceau, who observed that “the inhabitants of the cities use a wheaten bread almost exclusively.”39

Bakers had the undeniable monopoly on bread production in early modern Europe, a claim supported by the dearth of recipes for bread in cookery books. As early as the thirteenth century, in the Ménagier de Paris, a vernacular French recipe book, the author advises the host that when receiving dinner guests, his wife should purchase ten dozen loaves from the baker, some “day-old, flat, one-penny bread, and three dozen four-day-old brown trencher breads.”40 It is notable that in this earliest of French cookery sources, no recipe for bread is mentioned; instead, the text refers the reader to the baker to obtain bread.

Bakers’ guilds exerted considerable power in France until guilds were abolished in 1791.41 In 1640 in Dijon, the bakers, unhappy with their situation, refused to continue working.42 The magistrates menaced them with fines and even threatened to destroy their ovens, but ultimately they agreed to a new bread tariff that afforded the bakers a greater profit, a clear indication of the guild’s power.43 This is not to say that bakers had unbridled authority in this period. Certainly, the importance of bread as a base staple also made the bakers’ guild one of the most closely regulated craftsmen’s associations in the early modern city.44 Various tactics were used to keep fluctuations in the price of grains from affecting the price of bread. In England, the Assize of Bread, a set of laws regulating the size and quality of loaves, dictated that the price per loaf stay constant, adjusting instead the size of the loaf to reflect market cereal prices.45 This ensured that poorer citizens could always afford a loaf, even in times when grain supply was low and prices skyrocketed.46 In Augsburg, a different approach was used: fine, white wheaten bread was sold at a high price and rye bread at a low one, such that the wealthy subsidized the cost of bread for the poor.47 The cost of bread was determined by municipal authorities, unlike other commodities whose prices were set by the artisans themselves.48 Consequently, relations between bakers and their local governments were often tense.49

A poem written by a woman about the city’s various craftsmen in sixteenth-century Toulouse indicates through its sardonic tone a similarly strained relationship between the populace and the bakers:

Item les gentils Bolengiers,

Qui font leurs pains petiz et legiers,

Affin ne nous facent dangiers,

Comme chose poisante et utille:

Sont fort loyals en ceste Ville.

***

The kind bakers,

Who make their breads small and light,

In order that they not cause us danger,

As do heavy and useful things:

Are most loyal in this city.50

Despite these tensions, bakers’ guilds formed an essential presence in the early modern French city.

The structure of these guilds resembled that of other guilds, with a master baker, apprentices, and journeymen constituting the workforce. While many cities did not permit women to be apprentices, in some cities women did work in bakeries, as substantiated in a report of a female baker in the household of Rudolf Dietzinger of Wadenswil (1648–1696/99).51 In the late sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, guilds required journeymen to travel for a minimum amount of time to work in different cities, a practice called “tramping.”52 Bakers, millers, brewers, and butchers usually employed journeymen from rural areas of the surrounding region.53 Within bakers’ guilds, the journeymen were known as compagnons or garçons.54

The master garçon in charge of the bakeroom, a dark and stuffy room located either behind the bakery or in the cellar, was known as the geindre, so-called for the cries he emitted during the intensely physical activity of kneading.55 Indeed, kneading bread was a deeply corporeal process, with garçons often employing both hands and feet to work the large amounts of dough in their kneading troughs.56 In fact, many believed that the sweat of the baker improved the taste of the bread.57

The physical labor of baking caused an exceedingly high rate of attrition among workers.58 In addition to having to work all night in an insalubrious environment, garçons were responsible for a large number of tasks, including cutting and preparing wood for the oven, collecting charcoal, lighting and tending the fire, drawing water, winnowing and bolting the grain, conditioning the flour, preparing the leaven, weighing and making loaves, baking them, and cleaning the oven.59 The rhythm of work was determined by the baking of the loaves; it was so sustained that garçons often slept on top of the ovens and urinated in buckets used to carry water, a practice that was still observed in the twentieth century.60 And yet, the necessity of bread ensured the survival of and need for this guild, despite the economic stress of the industry and the emotional and physical pressures endured by artisans.

Although bakeries were crucial to production, baking bread in the home remained a widespread practice. In France, street vendors sold “bread-water,” probably yeast or sourdough, for those who wished to make bread at home.61 Brewers in the Middle Ages, most of whom were female, often sold barm for such a use.62 English housewives who brewed beer kept a little yeast to pass on to their neighbors because this product could be expensive.63

Bread in the Religious Realm

On fol. 37r, a medical recipe to warm the stomach and unstop the liver mentions ostie dipped in wine in combination with wormwood powder and capilli veneris. This medicine is said by the author-practitioner to “[clear] the phlegm and gas.” The use of the word ostie, which would normally be translated as “Host” or “Eucharist,” is intriguing in such a quotidian remedy.64 Looking at medieval and early modern Eucharist manufacturing practices, it seems improbable that an ostie would be used in such a recipe.

A Protestant print mocking the Catholic production of the Eucharist sheds light on what such a practice might have entailed (Fig. 1).65 The print depicts the entire process of preparing the Host, from picking the grain to inspecting the final product. The various activities are numbered, and a key at the bottom briefly describes each: “here he is sown, / here he is harvested, / here he is beaten, / here he is winnowed, / here he is milled, / here he is cooked, / here he is, / here he is tested to see if the workmanship is good.”66 This print pokes fun at the fastidiousness demanded by Carolingian legislative documents known as capitula, which firmly established the manufacture of the Host as a clerical activity, taking it out of the domestic realm where laypeople had previously participated in its production.67 Early in the Middle Ages, it was specified that the finest, most costly quality of wheat should be used.68 By the ninth century, it was widely accepted that the Host should be made of unleavened bread, in accordance with the bread Christ was believed to have served at the Last Supper, which would have had to be unleavened in order to satisfy Jewish Law.69 By the eleventh century, specific instructions were laid down for manufacturing the Host.70 For instance, two abbots of Cluny, Bernard (between 1060 and 1075) and Ulrich (in 1080), specified in their customaries that, before breakfast, the best grains of wheat should be chosen by hand and washed.71 In France, the Church likely obtained its wheat for Host production through the tithes peasants owed, which could constitute eight to fourteen percent of the crop yield.72 This so-called prélèvement was usually made directly from the field.73 A trusted servant was to carry the grain to the mill, and after washing the millstones and donning a white cloak to cover his entire body save for the eyes, was to grind and sift the flour.74 After Lauds, three deacons or priests were to combine the flour with water from a vessel used during Mass on a clean table, making sure not to let breath or saliva contaminate the Host.75 It was also in the eleventh century that a special tool was devised to differentiate Hosts from other unleavened bread disks (Fig. 2).76 This tool, called a Host press, comprised two long handles, at the end of which were two plates. One was incised with roundels featuring designs, including figural scenes, crosses, rosettes, and stars, while the other plate was left bare.77 The mixture of flour and water was put in the roundels and the press was placed over the fire in a chimney to cook the wafers.78 The entire process, either overseen or carried out by the priest, was characterized by solemnity and cleanliness; as Alfonso X’s Las Siete Partidas from 1256 to 1265 emphasizes, “the priest must make it very cleanly.”79 Moreover, faces and hands were to be washed before undertaking this holy labor.80 No contact was to be had between bodily fluids of any sort and the flour and water. This process differs from the corporeal early modern experience of baking bread for secular consumption.

Though the production of Hosts was tightly controlled, there are surviving accounts of them being stolen and used outside the realm of the Church. The possibility of “contraband” Hosts making their way into secular spaces such as an artisan’s workshop cannot be excluded and could explain the use of the word ostie in the manuscript. However, an incident that occurred in Normandy in 1627, in which a young shepherd was executed after having kept the consecrated Eucharist in his mouth rather than swallowing it, thereby taking it out of the church, makes clear that the Host and its whereabouts were not taken lightly.81 This incident was far from isolated, as occult practices frequently utilized Hosts.82 The existence of a Confrérie du Corpus Christi in sixteenth- and seventeenth-century Toulouse, which acted as a regulatory body for the production and dissemination of Hosts, makes the use of an ostie in the recipe to warm the stomach and unstop the liver unlikely.83

Even if the medical recipe on fol. 37r was unlikely to have required an actual Host, we might still ponder whether the term ostie carried religious significance, especially as it appears with wine. This recipe can be compared with another medical recipe on fol. 103r, in which reciting the paternoster nine times serves as an indication of the time required to first stir the mixture and may also have had spiritual significance.84 Nevertheless, we should not exclude the possibility that ostie is used not to designate a Host per se but an unleavened bread.85 Given its medical context, the use of the term ostie might allude to the fact that the carrier for the wormwood and capilli veneris should be a simple mixture of flour and water, a mixture that the word Host conveys. Knowing the religious affinity of the author-practitioner would be of use in speculating about the likelihood that he would use a word designating the all-important Catholic sacrament to colloquially refer to a sort of cracker.

The orthographic similarity of ostie and rostie and the fact that each only appears in the manuscript once (in preventive and curative functions) raises the question of whether they might refer to the same thing, namely a sort of cracker or toast. Is the “r” in rostie a slip of the pen, or, conversely, is ostie missing its first letter? Jean Nicot’s Thresor de la Langue Française from 1606 defines rostie simply as “panis tostus,” or toasted bread.86 The use of rostie in a preventive medical context on fol. 79v and 123v, where it is prescribed to be eaten with butter to prevent the entry of the smoke of metals into the body, is akin to the use of ostie in the curative recipe on fol. 37r. The shared medical context perhaps supports thinking of the latter as a misspelling of the former. A cursory search for “ostie” on Gallica failed to identify other instances of the word being used within the domestic and medical realm. Further research on the use of the word ostie in secular contexts would therefore be useful.

Bread in the Workshop

By way of conclusion, and with this rich historical web of bread in mind, we can now turn to the question of how bread entered the workshop of the author-practitioner. The prominence of bakers’ guilds in France in the period makes the bakery a likely source for the bread used in the manuscript’s recipes. However, as has been argued above, we must not dismiss domestic baking practices, tasks that predominantly fell to the wife of the household. The common practice of dispensing bread as payment in kind to workshop employees might also support a domestic provenance.87 The physical proximity of the workshop to the kitchen might have blurred functional distinctions between these two spaces in the early modern period.88 Specifically in Toulouse, the capitouls, the city’s chief magistrates, gave bread to artisans, which may also be a source for the bread in the manuscript.89 Another possibility, which some of the language in the manuscript corroborates, is that bread was baked in the workshop itself. Indeed, references on fols. 84r, 129v and 130r to carrying out tasks in the mouth of the oven after bread has been taken out, an indication of the temperature desired for these tasks, suggests the possibility of baking bread in the workshop. Additionally, the resemblance in visual sources between bakers’ ovens (Fig. 3) and metalworkers’ ovens (Fig. 4) supports this possibility.90 The recipe for “Preparing sand for box molds” on fol. 161r explicitly mentions a “bread oven.” This reference may suggest that a metalworker’s oven could also serve as a bread oven. Indeed, many sources describe a domed oven as being best for baking bread.91 Clearly, bread was ubiquitous, and our task in determining its source is made difficult not so much by the lack of textual evidence but by the abundance of contexts in which bread was baked during this period.

What this preliminary study of the world of baking in early modern Toulouse has demonstrated is the pervasive and diverse presence of bread.92 In the manuscript, concise and scattered allusions to bread support an understanding of this material as something both ubiquitous and highly particular. The category of “bread” is characterized by diversity, from fine wheaten loaves baked in specialized ovens and rustic oat breads cooked before the fire to amalgams of various pulses and unrefined cereal flours consolidated into a dense dough. Although the author-practitioner does not elaborate on his particular understanding of bread, occasional references to specific flours demonstrate his awareness of their different properties. This merits further research to elucidate the context of bread in Toulouse. Nevertheless, this introductory study helps us imagine the context for the author-practitioner’s use of bread in his workshop.
