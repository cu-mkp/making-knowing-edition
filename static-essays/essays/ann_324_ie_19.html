Ingenious Materials: Salts as Material Metaphor

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/4vf4-2867
← Back to Essays

Abstract

Like blood and gold, salt was a material that brimmed with meaning in late medieval and early modern Europe. As a material metaphor, salt became a way to think about the innate generativity and fertility of nature for authors such as Bernard Palissy or Joseph Du Chesne. Yet I show that the analogy between human wit and nature’s creative power remained absent from Ms. Fr. 640. Here then is the puzzle. If any material in this manuscript book of recipes were a material metaphor, surely it would be salts. Yet ingenuity does not overtly define the manifold roles of salts in Ms. Fr. 640. This striking difference with other books of secrets gives us an analytical thread to pull. How do we get from the experience of salts, as it surfaces in many recipes of the manuscript, to the elaborate ingenuity of salt in contemporary authors such as Palissy or Du Chesne? By contrasting Ms. Fr. 640 with books of secrets that make more of salts, we can probe more finely into how the experience of materials relates to textual tradition in shaping this genre.

Salts in BnF Ms. Fr. 640 pose a puzzle for the history of craft knowledge. In early modern Europe, salts included our common table salt (NaCl), as well as a family of substances from sal ammoniac (NH4 Cl) to the various nitrates involved in producing saltpeter, sharing properties such as volatility, sharp taste, or surprising crystallizations. Practitioners of the processes described in Ms. Fr. 640, such as the Huguenot potter Bernard Palissy and the chymical entrepreneur Johann Rudolf Glauber, ascribed enormous powers to salts.1 Palissy, for one, held an unspecified “salt” to be the generative principle of all things.2 By finding in salt nature’s power to generate new substances and objects, even new life, thinkers such as Palissy put salts and human ingenuity in parallel. To use the hoary trope, man’s making imitated Nature’s creating. If Nature’s power to create lay in the earthy residues of salt, then salt directly corresponded to the human inborn faculty of making knowledge, the ingenium.3 Thus practitioners played on old proverbs about salt as wit, just as Benvenuto Cellini, hoping to impress King Francis I, embodied his goldsmithing ingegno in a saltcellar: his fertile craft paralleled Neptune’s salty power to impregnate earthy Venus.4 Books of secrets, as we shall see, often expanded on salt’s generative powers.

Here then is the puzzle. If any material were a material metaphor, surely it would be salts. Yet ingenuity does not overtly define the manifold roles of salts in Ms. Fr. 640. This striking difference with other books of secrets gives us an analytical thread to pull. How do we get from the experience of salts, as it surfaces in many recipes of the manuscript, to the elaborate ingenuity of salt in Palissy? By contrasting Ms. Fr. 640 with books of secrets that make more of salts, we can probe more finely into how the experience of materials relates to textual tradition in shaping this genre.5

I. Ingenious Salt

Palissy and Cellini are only the most visible peaks in a whole continent of writers interested in salt and its meanings. In a range of sources, practitioners of distillation, metallurgy, molding, casting, and similar skills transformed salt into a central metaphor of nature’s power.

Matter’s powers to conceive, generate, nurture, and birth complex things made it ingenious, in the early modern vocabulary of invention. Perhaps the most influential alchemical treatise of the later middle ages was the Testamentum (1332), later ascribed to Ramon Llull, constructed around the conceit of a dialogue between Nature and an alchemical Master.6 The work of the philosophers’ stone could only be accomplished through their cooperation, “through the innate ingenuity of nature and the will of the master.”7 Before the Romantic restriction of genius to individual talent, the language of ingenuity described inborn (ingenitum) powers of generation, in a conceptual architecture that can be traced across vocabularies of wit, esprit, gheest, and so on.8 The extension of wit to matter and things was not unusual. While lettered men prioritized Cicero’s use of ingenium for rhetorical invention, they also knew that ancient Romans talked of the ingenium soli (ingenuity of the earth) and described big machines and legal ruses as ingenia.9 The idea that human abilities mirror those of the soil and environment in which they live was a commonplace for Renaissance historians, captured in the phrase genie du lieu.10 The analogy between the fertile minds of makers and nature’s own generative power could be especially tight, however, in alchemical treatises, which taught the reader-practitioner to discipline their own ingenuity to partner that of nature.11

The step from an ingenious power of generation to salt was small, in such works. Salt was already a mysterious product of earth and ocean exhalations, Aristotle had taught in the Meteorologia 2.3, a residue much like the sediment left by food digested in the body. Medieval Islamic authors such as Al-Razi, whose classifications of salts became cornerstones of Latin matter theory, encouraged reflection on salts within recipes of distillation and transmutation, including within the category of salts several different substances: sal ammoniac, vitriol, alkali salts, common salt, and others.12 The underlying capacity for crystallizing or condensing substances out of a liquid chaos, or liquefying solids, already made “salt” a fundamental principle of matter well before Paracelsus crowned it as one of his tria prima, along with mercury and sulphur.13 By the late sixteenth century, therefore, a diverse family of materials belonged to a grouping we still identify as salts.

Around the time our author-practitioner was gathering his recipes, ingenuity was intensifying as a problem for explaining nature. By the end of the sixteenth century, many followers of Paracelsus looked to salt in particular to explain nature’s generative powers. It was widely assumed that nature possessed a kind of ingenuity. Telesio’s De rerum natura (1570) went beyond the commonplace that as nature reproduced materials, plants, and animals, it worked on analogy to a craftsman; he argued that nature in fact displayed intelligence in producing things.14 This view can be traced through even supposed Aristotelians, such as William Harvey, who explained the reproductive abilities of the vegetative soul as evidence for an “inborn nature” (connatum ingenium).15 Thinkers of all kinds sought to isolate these animating intelligences, by the late sixteenth century, whether as seeds, vital juices, spirits—or as salts. In fact, the idea that seeds organize the coagulation of bodies into precious stones or metals—an idea with Platonic and Stoic pedigree—overlapped with intuitions about how salts acted in dissolving and crystallizing.16 By the 1580s, one of the most thoroughgoing of such theorists, Blaise de Vigenère, joined Palissy and Cellini in presenting salt as the generative principle of all things.17 He, and then Joseph Du Chesne and Michael Sendivogius in the early 1600s, argued that the Paracelsian tria prima was purely saline: sal ammoniac, sal nitre, and common salt.18

The differences between these writers’ views are enormous, but they also shared a key substratum of sensory experience, as Rémi Franckowiak has argued. All of these were reader-practitioners engaged with furnace and distillery, who believed that the way to understanding spirited matter is through those techniques that require salts to melt metals, that reduce organisms to ashen salts, or prompt new crystals to form. We may wonder, then, to what extent are their laboratory experiences the source of their distinctive ideas about salts as ingenious materials? Ms. Fr. 640 offers an intriguing trial.

II. Uses and Sources in Ms. Fr. 640

Salt permeates Ms. Fr. 640, an index of the diversity of recipes it contains. Salt is used for preparing food, to preserve codfish or brined artichokes (fols. 7r, 98v). Salts, mixed with a little alum (itself a salt), scrub clean a blackened tooth (fol. 47r). The salt from cabbage ashes is a “remedy against pain” (fol. 2r). With vinegar, salt helps verdigris turn wood green (fol. 78r). Salt works as a sand. Heated, washed, then ground with lead, it becomes a fine, spherical powder for an hourglass (fol. 10r). Salt is an abrasive (fol. 37v). It can be mixed with other rock minerals or burnt bones of oxen and, shaken through a sieve, salt is the sand that receives the imprint of a model—becoming the matrix for casting (fols. 68r, 84r, 84v, 88v, 89r, 89v). Salt is the key ingredient in the eau magistra that makes casting sand “fatty” and clump to receive a form, and then release it (fols. 83r, 84r, 84v, 89v, 99r).19 Salt is a binder, holding together pigments, cement, or plaster.20 With “red greasy earth” and pigeon dung, salt makes a water for tempering hot metal (fols. 56r, 67v). Salt corrodes and putrefies, so that mixed with vinegar it loosens rust, helping to clean metals (fols. 116v, 128v, 163v), or whiten gemstones (fols. 38r, 100v). Salt is an alternative to a dying lizard for coloring tin golden (fol. 98r). Heated, it serves as a component of glass (fols. 20r, 36v, 99r, 100v, 100r). When brazing, soldering, or melting copper, gold, or silver, salts are the fluxes that help the molten metal run (fols. 4r, 39r, 81v, 101r, 102r, 104r, 106r, 116v, 120r, 120v, 123r, 166v).

The author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 distinguishes carefully between kinds of salts: common salt; saltpeter; burnt salts; rock salt; sal ammoniac. These terms link the author-practitioner to the sources of salt in late sixteenth-century France. “Common salt”—of deep nutritional and cultural value in premodern European culture—had since the thirteenth century been subject to the gabelle or salt tax.21 Most common salt would have come, as it had for centuries, from one of the evaporation fields along the Atlantic coast south of La Rochelle, near Saintonges, or Brittany.22 Our author recognizes the variability of “common” salt: it can be rough, or highly refined and white (e.g. fols. 10r, 102r, 104r, 159v). He also uses rarer varieties of mined salts: rock salt, or what he sometimes calls mineral salt. At one point the author-practitioner describes “the salt called de Langlois de armonic” which he compares to “that of Cardona and Montserrat,” famous sites in Catalonia of mined salt deposits (fol. 38r).

The earth and sea were the readiest sources of our artisan-practitioner’s salts. Other salts required time and labor to be made ready for consumption. He describes the use of saltwort, a plant grown in marshes along the Atlantic coast of France—burning it yielded a salty ash alkali strong enough to use in making glass (fols. 36v, 68r). Saltpeter, a common ingredient, could be found in two varieties, natural (scraped from precipitates on cave walls) and artificial. Since the author of Ms. Fr. 640 never specifies, he likely would have intended the more common artificial saltpeter, widely known in early modern Europe as an expensive constituent of gunpowder, expensively and laboriously refined from heaps of earth containing urine and excrement.23 Tartar was a commonly found byproduct of winemaking, scraped from the insides of used wine barrels; in turn, it was calcined and refined into salt of tartar. Likewise sal ammoniac, which occurs in forty-one recipes of Ms. Fr. 640, came in natural and artificial varieties; it was widely advertised as the product of far off places, dug from under the sands of Africa, but our author is more likely to have found it as an import from “the East,” produced by sublimating the soot of burnt camel dung.24 Indeed, this old recipe may explain why the author-practitioner advised adding a little rose honey, clove oil, and cinnamon to improve the “bad odor” of sal ammoniac applied to the teeth (fol. 47r).25

The genre of recipes reinforces the variety and singularity of these salts in the manuscript. Most of the time, their uses do not overlap and—apart the semantic field of sel—the author-practitioner gives no overarching theory to bind them together. The fact that salt turns sand “greasy,” “fatty,” or acts as a mater or “magistra” in structuring molds does suggest a generative power, perhaps. After all, in these cases salt becomes the special something that makes everything else hold together or take on form. But the recipes rely on these powers without thematizing them in a way that allows us to think the artisan-practitioner intended to foreground those meanings. In fact, a recipe might specify mixing specific amounts of common salt, sal ammoniac, and saltpeter, a specificity of use that suggests the author-practitioner easily recognizes the difference between these substances. Only at one place does the author-practitioner substitute one salt for another: “And on all of this, put in a gros pour ℥ of sel de verre; & some & the common people put in e sandiver that they find at the glassmakers' or apothecaries'. But alkali salt, as you know, is better. ” (fol. 100v). We would recognize sel de verre as a version of alkali salt; the author-practitioner, however, seems to distinguish them on the basis of origin; what was called alkali salt was usually purified from ash, such as from burnt saltwort. Even when he asserts a near-equivalence of substances, the emphasis is on subtle distinctions of quality—“alkali salt, as you know, is better.” These examples show the author-practitioner keen to distinguish salts by both source and use, not running them together in the wondrous web we find in the theoretical literature of his contemporaries.

III. Recipe Books

Can Ms. Fr. 640 help us see the web of meanings spun around salt? Several essays in this Edition sketch the shape of our uncertainty about what the author intended with the recipes.26 It is hard to decide definitively whether it is a workshop record, a personal documentation of trials, notes towards a published book of secrets, or something entirely different. It seems fairly certain, however, that the manuscript is not fully dressed up to go out in public. It does not perform all the meanings, therefore, embedded within it.

The well-known case of Benvenuto Cellini helps us begin to tease apart the layers of genre and meanings, seeing how recipes might gain these meanings. Salt features in his book of recipes, and is thematized in the saltcellar he famously presented to Francis I. In his own recipes, published as the Due trattati, Cellini uses salt, sal ammoniac, tartar, and salpeter as fluxes for soldering, in metal and glass polishes and varnishes, in pigment binders, and for making aqua fortis (nitric acid) to dissolve gold.27 But in Cellini’s recipes a theory of salt is never at the surface. Instead, it is the saltcellar that unlocks our view of how Cellini saw salt, as the material analogue of his own fertile wit. In his careful reading of the object, Michael Cole has shown how acutely sensitive Cellini was to the broader resonance of salt as a metaphor of the goldsmith’s ingenuity.28 Cellini’s reclining Land and Sea personified the crafty struggle implicit in Aristotelian saline chemistry, in which earth and water artfully produce a composite of burnt and wet earth; his poetry and the object itself represent salt as an emblem of human power to decompose and recombine gold, glass, and color. Cellini made Francis I the saltcellar for many reasons; but all of them are bound up around the conceit of the artisan’s ingegno for creating con sale.

A restrained praxeology is just what we might expect from recipes. In fact, Cellini’s Trattati and Ms. Fr. 640 treated salts in almost identical terms as those found in medieval collections of recipes. The De diversis artibus of Theophilus Presbyter deploys salts in corroding copper to make “salt green,” or in water for tempering steel, or—most frequently—as a flux in molten silver and gold.29 The enormously successful book of secrets by Alessio Piemontese concentrated its references to salts in its sixth book, on precisely the topics of metalwork in which salts appear in Ms. Fr. 640.30 In such earlier segreti, salts were omnipresent but not verbose.

Nevertheless, sixteenth-century printed books of secrets developed into much more than simple protocols of technique. The unique selling point of Cellini’s Trattati was not particularly subtle: his recipes often lapsed into self-aggrandizing tales of how he had delighted the French king and other noble patrons, which illuminate little of how he thought about salt. To find a larger audience, many such books offered entertaining observations or the wonder of curious explanations.31

Other editorial strategies supplied salt with additional meanings. Giovanni Battista della Porta mentioned salts hundreds of times, often with the same uses we would expect from Ms. Fr. 640: food preservation, tempering steel, and counterfeiting precious stones. These uses in fact require different salts, but the text runs together different uses. A group of novel uses connect salts with fertility. Certain plants, ancient writers had already reported, thrive when watered with salt water—coleworts, sweet betony, and even date-trees. In fact, salt directly aids fertility, since “keepers of sheep, and goats, and Mares, do besmear their hands with salt and nitre, and then rub the generative parts of them in the time of their coition, for their more lustful and eager performance of that action.”32 Offering aids for human conception, he noted that “Salt also helpeth Generation: for it doth not only heighten the Pleasures of Venus, but also causeth Fruitfulness. The Egyptians, when their Dogs are backward in Copulation, make them more eager by giving them Salt-meats. It is an Argument also of it, That Ships in the Sea, as Plutarch witnesseth, are alwayes full of an innumerable company of Mice. And some affirm, That Female-Mice will conceive without a Male, onely by licking Salt. And Fish-wives are insatiably leacherous, and alwayes full of Children.”33 The lopsided ancient and modern anecdotes available to a curious Neapolitan nobleman supported the idea that salt fertilized.

When pressed, writers of books of secrets adopted explanatory frameworks to connect salt’s myriad uses. Della Porta gives over fifty references to salts in Book 10, “Of Distillation.” Here those he calls “the Paracelsians” are a starting point, because of their interest in the extraction of essences, which della Porta stresses is “a very difficult work; for they may be either Oyl, or Salt, or Water.”34 A comparable framework is found in Vannoccio Biringuccio’s metallurgical manual, Pirotechnia (1540), which took its starting point from Pliny’s long natural history of salt. After surveying the usual methods of extracting common salt by evaporation, Biringuccio presented some sources of saltpeter, sal ammoniac, rock salt, and sal alkali, some “natural” and most “artificial”—none of his descriptions are detailed enough to reproduce, but they nevertheless struggle to enunciate a common origin of all these salts. He summed up the chapter with a theoretical claim about the qualities shared by this diverse range of salts: “I am sure that salt can be extracted from anything that has a biting sharpness and that is converted into ashes by fire.”35 Such accounts reasoned from analogies between artisanal processes for producing salts, culled partly from contacts, rumors, and ancient sources such as Pliny, which were then filtered through a fundamentally Aristotelian chemistry of salt as a product of water, earth, and fire. Thus, after della Porta and Birunguccio, printed books of secrets increasingly presented the idea that not only did salts serve generative and transformational process, but were themselves transformed out of water and earth by fire.

Within this new wave of printed books of secrets, one of the most elaborate accounts of salt is found in Hugh Plat’s Jewell House of Art and Nature (1594).36 The book shares more with the works of Della Porta and Alexis Piedmontese (whoever he was) than with Biringuccio; it collects household recipes for food and health, and devotes books to distillation, molding, and casting metals. The most unusual addition was its entire second book, titled “Diverse new sorts of Soyle not yet brought into any publique use, for manuring both of pasture and arable ground, with sundrie concepted practices belonging therunto.” This new “sort of soyle” was lime, called marne (or marl). Plat chiefly drew on Bernard Palissy, who had explained salt as the fifth element, reasoning from the common experience of how salt crystallized within the processes common in distillation, molding, and casting: salts come from evaporated seawater, putrefied matter, the lees of wine, and the burning of plants grown in water.37 Palissy had also added an entire book on a certain earth which was whitish, salty, and had the power to regenerate sterile soil. In The Jewell House, Plat did more than translate Palissy’s ideas. He connected these two parts of Palissy: he explained the source of marl’s generative power through its saline chemistry. Even though salt often served to decompose matter, it could also regenerate. The prophet Elisha had restored a bad well and unfruitful land with a bowl of salt (2 Kings 2).38 “Neither is there any place in the whole world,” Plat pointed out, “where that generative vertue doth more abound, or where there is infinit generation and multiplication of creatures, then in the wide Ocean. And I doo verely beleeve yt the spirit of God which in the beginning did spread itself upon the waters (which I doe hold to be certen fire) did make them to be of that nature, that is to say, thicke and salt, and by that reason they are much more apt for the generation of all creatures, then any fresh waters whatsoever.”39

Plat identified Palissy as his source of the idea that is the common agent behind all the varieties of salt—but he also found Palissy too bookish. Palissy “telleth us of the exceeding fertillity, that is found in salt waters, by that infinite generation and multiplication of fishes, yea of Venus her selfe, that Primum mobile, in the procreation of children, and the other of those pleasant grapes, growing in the salt Marishes of Xantoigne [Saintonges].”40 Yet this was not sufficiently down to earth for Plat. He finds Palissy too hesitant in distinguishing this salty principle (“vegetative salt”) from the diversity of real, physical minerals. Instead, he lumps Palissy in with the philosophers, claiming his own knowledge is much more practical, gleaned from experience itself. He reported one who “of his owne mother witte sowed a bushell of salte long since upon a smal patche of barren grounde at Clapham which to this daye remayneth more fresh and green.” This salty success confirmed his own experience:

“And for my part, I must here acknowledge that the best naturall phylosophie that ever I coulde learne in this point, was neither out of Aristotles phisicks, nor Velcuries naturall philosophy, nor Garsceus meteors, nor out of any of the old philosophicall Fathers, that writ so many hundred yeares past; but that little which I have, I gathered it on the backside of Moore fieldes, where by sundrie undoubted argumentes, I did heare it maintained, that all those elements, doo onely differ in attenuation, and condensation.”41

As a result, Plat proceeded to advise spreading one’s soil not only with marl, but also with baysalt and brine.

Such advice may well be the product of Plat’s own experience. To be sure, too much common salt will render most soil toxic for most plants. Yet in smaller quantities, common salt was known as a fertilizer. Theophrastus, Aristotle’s student, had noted this use, and natural philosophers bent on schemes of improvement studied it too.42 Nevertheless, the case of Plat reveals how generalizations could grow within the burgeoning genre of secrets.43 Plat claimed that his knowledge of agricultural salt came “of his own mother witte,” as he said of the Claphamite, and his own experience. But what he described as experience was already an amalgam of cultural touchstones. Beyond more ornamental references to Ovid and other poets, Plat synthesized different parts of Palissy’s own oeuvre, biblical references, the Wittenberg botanist Valerius Cordus, and other books of secrets. His vernacular epistemology of salt was hybrid all the way down, a cornucopia of the tangled sources driving the genre of secrets.

Conclusions

Like blood and gold, salt was a material that brimmed with meaning in late medieval and early modern Europe.44 A rich example of where the generativity of salt might lead from earthy experience to heavenly insight is an unpublished early treatise of the young shoemaker Jakob Boehme. The Aurora offered a heady mixture of alchemy, natural philosophy, and apocalyptic theology.45 Boehme distinguished two forms of a primordial salt principle, Salitter, in which one form is dark, smelly, and earthy and the other form is pure, clear, and filled with heavenly power. This corresponded to the experience of saltniter, first a product of manure and then refined as a flash-filled crystal.46 The prime responsibility of salitter is celestial generation.47 For Boehme, a kind of divine materialist, salitter was literally the stuff of ingenuity. Salitter provided the divine matter, the sharpness, heat, vitality, and indeed the intelligence with which every human, indeed every natural body, is made. As a material, salt could become a way to think about the innate generativity and fertility of nature.

Yet I have suggested that the analogy between human wit and nature’s creative power remained absent from Ms. Fr. 640. This illustrates one of the most striking features of this manuscript for the modern historian: that despite the books it lists, and despite the richness of its collection, it aims mostly at practice. This is not news. Perhaps more surprising is what comparing Ms. Fr. 640 with the tradition of printed books of secrets says about the genre as a whole. The laconic nature of recipes has often encouraged historians to oppose them to books of natural philosophy. Historians of recipes have often pointed out that even printed collections codify practice, not theory.48 Books of secrets therefore seem to represent for some historians a backlash against the bookish traditions, a revolt of the crafts against elite scientia. This appears true if we compare the restrained case of Ms. Fr. 640 only with the more highly worked philosophical works by Joseph Du Chesne or Blaise de Vigenère, where salty metaphors of ingenuity crystallize. But, comparing these against a range of recipe books, a study of salt suggests these are only the poles of a continuum. As Elaine Leong has observed, recipe books in manuscript were valued precisely because they were manageable, while in print they accrued new agendas that proliferated out of control.49 Printed recipes were more swiftly laced with explanations. With Hugh Plat, we can see such explanations redeploying the arguments of other recipe books. Rather than finding recipes opposed to bookish theories, the case of salt shows how arguments for practice, once they left the workshop, themselves quickly became intertextual. On the first folio, recto, of Ms. Fr. 640 there is a long list of authors, from ancients such as Strabo and Alexander of Aphrodisias to moderns such as Polydore Vergil and Bernard Palissy. If the author-practitioner planned to use this reading list to work up his recipes for publication, perhaps—we are left to speculate—at that stage he might have assembled his particular salts into a universal salty principle. At any rate, this was what his peers did.
