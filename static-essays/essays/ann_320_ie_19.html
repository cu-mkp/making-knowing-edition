Everything depends upon the trial ("Le tout gist à l’essay"): Four Manuscripts between the Recipe and the Experimental Essay

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/vj69-8h20
← Back to Essays

Abstract

A comparison between four manuscripts demonstrates an evolution from the genre of the recipe to the experimental essay. As a notion of general experience slowly transformed into the practice of experimentation at a particular time and place, how-to accounts became increasingly historicized and personalized. In contrast to widespread narratives depicting modern science as a thinning of previously thick, embodied knowledge, this evolution demonstrates knowledge thickening, becoming more personalized and explicit both about material experience and symbolic interpretation. This is apparent in the case of Ms. Fr. 640, in the frequent use of the first-person voice, especially at points in which the author-practitioner was most actively engaged in speculating about and experimenting with new techniques.This long-term evolution of the experimental essay situates the epistemology of making and knowing in relation to previous long-term accounts concerning the scientific method. Previously, the experimental essay has been associated with Restoration experimental philosophy, as it developed out of the works of Sir Francis Bacon. In such accounts, the experimental essay serves as the ideal expression of a particular epistemology. This essay backdates the experimental essay and demonstrates its slow emergence from the genre of the recipe. Showing the experimental essay’s connection with prior genres also suggests experimental philosophy’s connection with previous epistemologies. Neither the experimental essay nor experimental philosophy emerged fully formed in the works of Bacon and his followers. On the slow course of development that produced them, we can situate Ms. Fr. 640 at an important stage of the personalization and historicization of experiment.

Personhood and Thickness in the Experimental Essay

This essay1 situates BnF Ms. Fr. 640 on a spectrum of emerging literary techniques that produced the experimental essay, the key genre associated with English experimental philosophers and often heralded as the ancestor to the scientific journal article.2 The author-practitioner was writing at a historical moment of methodological diversification that opened up a range of different ways to inscribe knowledge, and to transmit it along a spectrum between artisanal and scholarly audiences and practices. In this case, the method that was diversifying was that of recipe writing, which offered a long-established convention for how to inscribe and transmit practices of making. Writers departed from recipe writing to greater or lesser degrees, even as the genre of recipes persisted robustly, as it does still today. In the author-practitioner’s case, I argue, his manuscript fell between a standard recipe collection and a collection of experimental essays. Here, I compare it to three other French manuscripts spanning the late sixteenth to mid-seventeenth centuries, Bordeaux Ms. 1213, Sloane Ms. 3426, and Sloane Ms. 3423.

The experimental essay typically has been put in the context of lengthier philosophical genres, such as the dialogue and the treatise, rather than the recipe.3 Michelle DiMeo, to my knowledge, has thus far been the only scholar to contrast the recipe and the experimental essay. She does so in the case of the genre conventions Robert Boyle deployed in the experimental essay versus in his recipes in the late seventeenth century.4 This essay explores an earlier moment, before the conventions of the experimental essay had been established and while it was still emerging as a genre.

This analysis of a slow development from recipe to essay calls into question a powerful narrative concerning the nature of modern scientific rhetoric. As Ted Porter has noted, at the heart of many accounts of early modernity, from Michel Foucault’s Order of Things to accounts of the invention of the scientific method, we find modern science presented as the “thinning” of knowledge. According to such accounts, a superficial, universally valid, and highly technical database of information, produced by following the rote steps of a depersonalized, and thus objective, scientific method, replaced an embodied, local, subjective, world of allusion and symbolism entangled with materials themselves. As Porter has argued, thickness bears within itself tradition and cultural meanings attached to local practices and materials, whereas thinness presents itself as universally portable and replicable because it is freed from cultural baggage; “Science, allied in practice with industry and bureaucracy, stood more than ever for a standardized world, for uniform rules that presume no need for the nurturing of traditions or the cultivation of judgment.”5 This dichotomy is illusory, Porter argues, because actual scientific labor is also thick, material, and embodied and practiced as a localized craft learned from a master.

Through this analysis of the emergence of the experimental essay, we might add a further twist to Porter’s revisionist account of the modernist transition from thickness to thinness. The ancient genre of the recipe is, in fact, extremely thin, at least in its explicit textuality. As William Eamon has discussed, late antique encyclopedists efficiently packaged knowledge for transmission and translation, shearing off many particulars from their original contexts and omitting features, such as images, that might have accompanied original texts.6 It is my argument here that the long-lived genre of the recipes was thinned in late antiquity, and then thickened in early modernity, evolving into the new genre of the experimental essay.

What made an experimental essay? In the case of the genre in late seventeenth-century England, Maurizio Gotti has pointed to brevity, richness of detail, a “lack of assertiveness,” a limited use of rhetorical devices, clarity, simplicity of form, and objectivity (which Gotti sees in an eagerness to differentiate “degrees of certainty”).7 The idea of objectivity is a complex and polemicized one in the period;8 putting objectivity aside, I will look for Gotti’s other criteria, in particular the use of the first person, explicit self-reflexivity, and the prominence of the idea of “essays” or trials themselves.

Some literary critics have differentiated between literary essays and experimental ones along the axis of thinness and thickness Porter identified. They have assumed that even though experimental essays are self-reflexive and appeared in first-person narration, this was a depersonalized narration, unlike the first-person narration of literary figures such as Montaigne. George Levine wrote in his introductory note to James Paradis’s essay, “Montaigne, Boyle, and the Essay of Experience,” that in the very “literary form through which Michel de Montaigne sought to embody the self,” the self was also expelled, “experience was depersonalized,” and thus “it was made available to a community of scientific experts.”9 Likewise, Paradis argued that Boyle’s “I” was different from Montaigne’s since Boyle’s “experimental essay has a core of experimental fact that, by convention, is at a remove from the idiosyncratic self.”10 Both Levine and Paradis’ interpretation, although appearing in a volume entitled One Culture, rest upon assumptions about what divides science from the rest—that is, depersonalization, expertise, and facts. They anticipate the rhetoric of the contemporary scientific periodical that Paradis, like many others, traces back to Boyle’s use of the experimental essay.

The work of Barbara Shapiro, however, has since shown that the seventeenth-century English culture of fact was not depersonalized, but heavily socially constituted.11 What we see textually in comparing texts that span a spectrum between recipes and experimental essays is a much greater personalization of knowledge. There is no reason to assume that it is any less the case in experimental essays than in other essays. As experiment slowly transitioned from a general experience to a notion of a contrived experience in a particular time and place and a particular individual, accounts of process were increasingly personalized and historicized.12

Both recipes and experimental essays were products of large-scale historical processes. The ancient traditions of recipes and visions, honed for transmission through centuries and across languages and cultures, travelled lightly. Such terse formats may have left implicit practices of critical self-reflexivity, that in the greater expansiveness afforded by cheaper material supports for texts, such as paper, and more conducive conditions for knowledge production in general, could be explicitly dilated upon.

Essays were inflected by the skepticism and intersocial doubt that reigned in the early modern period for several reasons, including the wars of religion that racked France from the 1560s, the general untrustworthiness of the printed word, and the emergence of new forms of probabilistic reasoning, such as the “reason of state.”13 Essays presented themselves as an individual viewpoint, open to doubting and being doubted. Recipes do not present themselves in this manner. This is not to say that recipes were used uncritically. As Elaine Leong has shown, users were well-versed in an array of tools for querying and testing recipes, including copying, marginal annotations, assurances through attribution, amending, and crossing out.14 These practices show, as Michelle DiMeo noted, that “a recipe was not considered fixed even as it entered print.”15 Through widespread practices of textual emendation and marginal annotation, readers treated recipes as open to doubt, but they did so against the grain of the text of the recipe itself, which often claimed efficacy and universal validity. Essays, by contrast, make practices of self-criticism explicit and open up their infrastructure to a deeper level of querying by others.

Essays offered probable knowledge in lieu of the certainty promised by philosophical systems. As Dana Jalobeanu has written, “It is true that for Montaigne, as for Bacon, the human mind can never be fully at rest; seemingly ‘fixed’ opinions are merely illusory marks which do not steady its course.”16 As Steven Shapin wrote concerning Boyle’s adoption of the genre, “the essay (that is, the piece-meal reporting of experimental trials) was explicitly contrasted to the natural philosophical system.”17

The essay never replaced the recipe; recipes outlasted the experimental essay by centuries. Still, we might note change over time in how the early moderns built bridges between these genres. While recipes still abound today, they are generally not accepted as a source of valid knowledge within academia or other centers of elite learning. This was not the case in the early modern period, when long-separated streams of knowledge were purposefully brought together. The development of the experimental essay out of the recipe offers one means to offer a nuanced account about a spectrum spanning formal scientific rhetoric and textual practices of the craft world.

To survey what the spectrum might have looked like when it came to literary technologies for engaging experimentation, I will compare the rhetorical features and mise-en-page of four French manuscripts on paper: Bordeaux Ms. 1213, Ms. Fr. 640, Sloane Ms. 3426, and Sloane Ms. 3423, the latter two from the collection of Sir Theodore de Mayerne (1573–1655). Viewing these manuscripts as falling along a spectrum presupposes that there is no dichotomy between implicit and explicit, embodied and textual knowledge. Rather, there are forms of texts that are more heavily connected to embodied practice than others are, and that leave more knowledge tacit.18 Likewise, there are also material practices and experimental gestures that rely more heavily on textual inscriptions than do others.

Bordeaux Ms. 1213

The Bordeaux Ms. 1213, a collection of alchemical, pharmaceutical, culinary and cosmetic recipes, represents standard, international recipe practice. This manuscript, which I was only able to consult as a digital surrogate, offers a good comparison to Ms. Fr. 640.19 It is 141 folios long, 210 x 150 mm, and, like Ms. Fr. 640, contains more than one hand. One recipe’s attribution to “Mr. Malus ma[istre] dela monnoye de bourdeaux,” that is, Jean Malus, master of the mint at Bordeaux from 1587–1594, suggests an approximate date, although the manuscript was written over a period of time. The manuscript thus comes from a similar time as Ms. Fr. 640, and not far from Toulouse, where Ms. Fr. 640 was likely composed.

The manuscript begins with a practical alchemical treatise divided into short chapters, beginning with the noblest metal, gold (magistre d’or). It then continues with recipes in disordered fashion.20 These are often medical, but some are for cosmetics or perfumes,21 six are for making fake gemstones,22 and one offers direction for making various shapes out of coral.23 The medical recipes include a fair amount of gynecological material.24 The manuscript includes an “alphabet des caracteres,” or a guide to chymical symbols.25

The owner or owners interacted with the recipes by marking many with a cross, occasionally commenting negatively (“elle est fausse”),26 making emendations,27 (rarely) crossing out entire recipes,28 and occasionally approving with a “good” (“bon”). In one case they did so even more enthusiastically in Latin; for the use of oil of boxwood as pain relief for teeth asserted as taking effect so quickly that it “is as if by enchantment”—“Note this because it is true and most certain.”29

One recipe for “Pills against the plague” is approved of as “good.”30 The next recipe, “A well proven recipe against the plague sent from the king of Spain to the queen of France” is also marked “good.”31 The latter points to the standard way of offering assurance for a receipt by invoking a distinguished provenance. Both this recipe and the “pills” relate to Estienne Ydeley’s 1581 Sovereign Secrets and True Remedies against the Plague (Secrets Souverains et Vray Remedes contre la Peste), where the two recipes are also found next to each other in reversed order.32

What is not found in this manuscript is usage of the first person, much historical detail, or provenance information for the manuscript itself. This is in contrast to Ydeley’s published work, in which Ydeley, in order to garner approval for his cures, notes how he used a particular medicament himself at a certain breakout of the plague, giving us a place and a year.33 Ydeley’s first-person claim to having successfully used this recipe can be put in the context of frequent sixteenth-century learned medical first-person allusions to having “made a trial,” although Ydeley goes a bit further in recording a time and a place.34 Ydeley does so not because it was his standard practice to record the time and place of his interventions, but in order to offer the context of a particular breakout of the plague as evidence of his medicine’s efficacy.

Ms. Fr. 640

I consulted this manuscript through the Making and Knowing Project’s digital edition. Ms. Fr. 640 includes some recipes indistinguishable in form from Bordeaux Ms. 1213, such as fol. 44v, “Excellent water against the plague of Monsieur de Montorsin” or fol. 170v, “Against plague.” Both are short paragraphs of a series of commands, with limited detail and no first-person narration about what was tried. As in any recipe collection, these point to a certain amount of repetition and haphazard order; the two medicines against the plague are separated from each other, and there is no comparison between the two or speculation about what might make them efficacious. As in the case of Bordeaux Ms. 1213, the author-practitioner does not note the time or location of processes. Marginal censures appear, although infrequently, and in one case in Latin with a “non bona” (fol. 13r).

Such standard recipe presentation in this manuscript is especially apparent for the medical recipes, which appear throughout the manuscript. Notably, almost no medical recipes in this manuscript relate to sicknesses of women or children, compared to three for dog’s mange, suggesting that the author-practitioner was not collecting them for the purposes of household management.35

The author-practitioner explicitly reached to knowledge domains other than his own in his frequent categorization of craft knowledge under the headings of various professions, lending at times the nature of a common-place book organization to the manuscript.36 Collecting professions was a widespread period interest, evidenced in Tommaso Garzoni’s 1585 showcase, Piazza universale di tutte le professioni del mondo, and the author-practitioner’s list of a variety of processes and the collection of craft knowledge under different professional headings, which adumbrates later efforts in what was called the history of trades, such as John Evelyn’s catalog of arts and commonplace book of trades.37

Among the forms of knowledge of different trades that the author-practitioner was interested in documenting were practices of apprenticeship and learning as well as literary technologies, not only in the obvious case of the trade of the “scribe,” but also in the array of different books kept by merchants. He described how merchants have four books, one a reference work for apprentices, one the rough daily account or the “waste book,” the “sales book” in which the latter is put into a “clean copy” and finally, the perfected “account book,” so authoritative that it “is taken as evidence in court” (fol. 9r). He went on to offer particular examples of what entries in a waste book might look like.38 The mercantile waste book became a common motif and practice among figures such as Guido Pancirolli and Francis Bacon who were interested in the waste book as a mechanism for quickly noting doubtful knowledge in a repository that could be further tested over time. At a later stage, knowledge of a higher level of reliability might be extracted from it and inscribed in a more authoritative volume.39 Elaine Leong has also noted a practice of keeping multiple recipe books, copying only those recipes which had passed through a trial.40

In some parts of the manuscript, the author-practitioner’s approach differs substantially from standard recipe presentation. Making trials is a central feature of this manuscript. A frequent term we find in this manuscript is essaye. It appears in some form 40 times, compared to 10 uses for experiment or experience, and 33 for secret.41 First-person narration also distances the manuscript from a standard collection of recipes. The first person occurs (in variations of je, j’ay and j’en), very conservatively, 130 times (not counting occasions when the pronoun was inferred from context, first-person possessive pronouns, etc).42 The first-person plural occurs more rarely; twice it refers to general categories, such as humans or Frenchmen, but two times it refers to a particular practice of a specific plural “we.”43

The author-practitioner’s use of the first-person contributes greatly to the manuscript’s sense of immediacy, allowing us to feel as though we can follow along in the course of his trials. At the same time, these first-person accounts are not only of what he did or observed, but also of what he speculated, giving us clues to the author-practitioner’s practices of abstraction from sense experience. The phrase “I believe” (je croy) appears 15 times, “I don’t know if” (je ne scay si) 4 times, and “I think” (je pense) once. The phrase “I find that” (je trouve que) also appears four times; this phrase, to my ear, occurs at a more intermediate stage between sensual apprehension and more abstracted intellectual speculation, illustrating the range between forms of knowing more closely and more distantly entangled with bodily engagement. As Tillmann Taape discusses in his essay, “I find that” (je trouve que), as in the entry at fols. 85v–86r, offers a relatively firm conclusion that the author-practitioner has drawn from repeated experience.44 He reports his findings, but does not speculate further about them until he adds, “I believe that [je croy que] the secret to cast well lies in finding a sand that receives the metal well, one for lead, the other for another, for each one has its particular one” (fol. 86r).

On the first occasion at which the first-person appears, “Painting esmail d’azur in oil” on (fol. 11r), we find a progression loosely repeated in some, but by no means all, other entries. First, different views of others are collected (in this case, three different techniques). Then the author-practitioner offered his first person account of a trial of one of these techniques. It was good, but it had a drawback (“It loses a little of its vividness”). He then offered another technique that he had also tried to remedy this defect. At the very end of the entry, he reiterated, this time in the typical impersonal command voice of recipes, both the standard practice and his improved version. The author-practitioner has thus made explicit the process through which he generated a new recipe out of older ones, and he did so by recounting the sequence of his trials and retrials in the first person.

Although not formalized, similar stages repeat elsewhere. One of the clearest examples of this progression appears on the second occasion of the use of the first person. “For whitening [cornaline]” (fol. 12v–13r) begins with considering the views of others, and continues to a speculation of a method (apparently not attempted), the detailed account of an attempt and its failure, and a successful retrial. It concludes with speculation concerning two variables in the process that it might be better to change in the future.

One of the most speculative cases is that of the “work done in Algiers” (fol. 52r), since this is for a phenomenon that the author-practitioner evidently did not succeed ultimately in producing, and perhaps had not yet even attempted, but about which he nevertheless recorded his speculations. These include speculation about what the original intent of a command in the recipe was and how it might be adapted to local conditions through effectuating the same intent through other means. Thus, the author-practitioner speculates how he might adapt the process for growing an alchemical silkworm in Algiers to a cooler climate by attempting to heighten the heat of the process, through the use of hot food or drink.

I do not know if it would be good to water it [the horse] occasionally with water of sulfurous baths, & to sometimes give it fenugreek or other hot foods, for the intention of the worker is to it to use the heat of its dung, & the climate here is cooler than that of Algiers.

Readers familiar with the eight modes of Bacon’s literate experience (experientia literata) will recognize some of those tactics here such as variation (variatio), or “varying the experimental setting through a number of variables,” as well as transfer (translatio) “from one kind of art or practice to another.”45

The use of the first person is not consistent throughout the manuscript. The processes for casting are especially replete with first-person narration and self-reflexivity about success or failure, repeated trials, and speculation about alternative methods. This bears out Pamela Smith and Tonny Beentjes’ point concerning the particularly high level of experimentation and repeated trials in the sections on casting.46 Only in the sections on casting do we hear anything about the author-practitioner’s own teachers.47 It is also concerning casting that the author-practitioner specifies, “as we have taught you,” in one of the very few uses of the first-person plural in the manuscript (fol. 117r).

Over the course of several pages, the author-practitioner describes a series of different trials of sands for casting.48 In one such description, he documents his stages of trial, failure, mental processes, and concludes with a general causal explanation for his eventual success (“For lean sands barely release well and yet they receive metal well,” fol. 85r). In marginal notations, he notes ideas for future trials.49

In a single entry on fols 118v–119r, “Casting in frames,” we find an explosion of 21 first-person pronouns (and far more if we include inferred pronouns), followed by a self-reflexive “Advice [advertissement] about the above.” The remarks in the advertissement suggest that there were times when our author-practitioner did not succeed as well as one might initially have supposed from the very coherent and detailed account of a series of complicated processes he had just given. He mentions the possibility of the sand shrinking in the frame, and warns that the cast must be thin, and that the sand cannot be reused for “molding en noyau.” These warnings suggest that such poor outcomes had occurred at various points to the author-practitioner as well, especially since this is only one of two advertissements that appear in the entire manuscript, the other one being also on the subject of casting (on fol. 115r). We might gather from this that his account represented his perfected process following a series of trials and errors.

Through his first-person usage, the author-practitioner takes access to hidden realms of knowledge into his own hands. As William Eamon has discussed, an air of mystery attached to even the most seemingly practical, printed, and widely available “how-to” collections of secrets. The impersonal commands of the recipe carried with it the history and authority of oracular pronouncements and mystery-cult initiations. Initiation into learning from a master remained alive and well both within the alchemical tradition and in guild rules and ceremonials. The author-practitioner himself makes reference to the relationship between books of secrets and Eleusinian mystery cults, defending his efforts to publicize his secrets against the charge that he profaned the Eleusinian goddesses.50

The author-practitioner’s immediate engagement with religious ideas and experiences are both implicit in material process and explicit in the author-practitioner’s own symbolic interpretations of nature. For instance, the making of a linseed oil ointment against burns transforms dramatically along with the recitation of a number of paternosters specified in the recipe, as discovered by the Making and Knowing Project in reconstruction.51

At one point, the author-practitioner ruminated textually on what his direct confrontation with nature taught him about principles pertaining to moral and spiritual life. This passage is particularly significant because it occurs in the context of his trials concerning life-casting from snakes, the arena in which he was most self-reflexive. He notes how snakes, “when they want to bite or eat something, they do not bite with a direct attack, but with sinuous turns & from the side, as do Satan & his disciples.” Likewise, he continued in this symbolic vein to observe about the snake, “It has a small head, but a very long body. The entryway to sin seems small & inconsequential, but the consequences of it are very great” (fol. 109v). The intertwining of symbolic interpretation at the moment of the author’s greatest degree of self-reflexive, process-oriented literary technologies questions one popular Foucauldian account of the “thinning” of the modern world, that is, William Ashworth’s argument concerning the decline of emblematic views of nature.52 The thickening of recipe practice through increased first-person narration and self-reflexivity about process also contributed to increased explicit symbolic interpretation of the book of nature.

Mayerne and the Author-Practitioner

The next two manuscripts I will place on this spectrum come from the papers of Sir Theodore de Mayerne. The Huguenot royal physician at the early Stuart court, Mayerne might seem rather distant in time and place from the author-practitioner. However, Mayerne belonged to a dynasty committed to bridging artisanal and scholarly worlds in ways that intersected with the author-practitioner’s context. He is also being compared to the author-practitioner in other introductory essays by Jenny Boulbollé and Maartje Stols-Witlox and by Jo Kirby and Marika Spring.

Mayerne’s grandfather, Etienne, had helped establish the silk industry in Lyon in 1537.53 His father, Louis, in a French translation of a section of Vasari, defended the mathematical and mechanical arts at length in a dedication to François Roaldès (1519–1589), Regent of the University of Toulouse.54 Roaldès was known for having spent his youth “curiously contemplating the works of nature” before moving on to legal studies.55 He had visited Italy in 1579 and joined the circle of Pinelli, and he later published in the vernacular on ancient and modern war machines (1586) and composed a vernacular treatise on viticulture, De la vigne (published in 1886).56

In his letter to Roaldès, Louis Turquet championed mathematics as an aid to both the higher sciences and the mechanical arts. He reflected contemporary political theory that placed an emphasis not just on agriculture and trade, but on the patronage of domestic manufactures. Due to the neglect of mathematics, the higher sciences have become very uncertain and fallen into general disrepute (“en detestation generale”). But the mechanical arts have also suffered, so that the once honorable title of “mechanic” has become “a vile and abject epithet.”57 Turquet proves critical concerning “experience,” in the absence of other teachers, which according to the vulgar is the “mistress of the arts.” He argues that there is a shorter path, which is “the study and research of the sciences and arts and disciplines, and above all those of the mathematics. . . without which it is impossible to rise to the contemplation of spiritual and intelligible things nor to descend to those material and sensible.”58 Only the mathematical disciplines, he argued, can “reform the world.”59 It seems that Louis Turquet is urging Roaldès to patronize a reform of learning in a way that would create new bridges between speculative and mechanical arts, rendering both more certain. In his published political writings, Louis Turquet offered many practical suggestions for how to institutionalize forms of practical expertise for political benefit, including the patronage of manufactures.60

Phillipe de Béthune, the first known owner of the author-practitioner’s manuscript, likewise reflected period political consensus when he advocated in his Conseilleur d’Estat that manufactures, in addition to trade and agriculture, formed the basis for the riches of a state.61 A large and excellent population of craftspeople produced both wealth (abondance) and reputation (credit); one had to actively seek out (rechercher) good craftspeople and increase their number (multiplier).62 He suggested founding public houses (maisons publiques) for manual arts as a way to attract good craftspeople to a state.63 Some were of the opinion that the practice of manual arts should be reintroduced to monasteries.64 Later, Béthune expanded on his idea of a public house of manufactures, suggesting that to the public houses for manual arts (Maisons publiques pour les Arts manuels) could be added religious seminaries, ecclesiastical sciences and other learned professions, as well as noble academies.65 Béthune thus made the selection, growth and improvement of artisans a basis not just for wealth in the state. He also listed artisanal education first in a range of all further forms of education. A growing population of excellent craftspeople would confer further political benefits. The establishment of manufactures new to a place would increase the population of cities and thus increase the state’s wealth and power.66 One technique for pacifying a conquered population was encouraging its people to engage in all sorts of mechanical arts, which typically tend to divert their spirit away from trouble; “ordinarily those who are raised in the workshop (la boutique) do not desire trouble, nor war.” 67

Mayerne’s background thus reflects the intersections of artisanal and learned cultures at the highest levels in the author-practitioner’s context. Roaldès and Béthune reflect, respectively, interest from the academic and political quarters of “reforming the world” by elevating the status of the mechanical arts and bringing them into the circuit of elite praxis. Mayerne himself turned to alchemy, rather than to mathematics as did his father, to build this bridge and reform the world.

Sloane 3426

Sloane 3426 is a volume belonging to Mayerne and containing copies from the papers of others. Here I examine the first section, entitled, “The Following were excerpted from the miscellaneous papers of Briot in London, November, 1626” (Sequentia ex Miscellaneis D. Briot excerpta fuerunt Londini Mense Novembr. 1626). It includes material drawn from Mayerne’s shared circle of Huguenot Paracelsians that formed in the early 1600’s in Paris and in which several members of the Briot family had participated.68 Recipes are attributed to, among others, Nicholas Briot (1579–1646) and his son Philippe Briot, [Jean Cusin] Mutilet,69 M. [Étienne] de Clave,70 the personal physician of Philippe de Béthune,71 [Guillaume de] Trougny,72 [David] Ramsey,73 and Mayerne himself.74 Thus, through this manuscript, we have the odd experience of reading Mayerne’s copy of another’s copy of his own recipes. This perspective furnishes a unique platform for self-reflection on Mayerne’s part.

This manuscript begins with an account of creation, entitled “Doctrine Generale,” revealing the operations of principles of mercury and sulfur throughout the cosmos. It offered a defense of the “art of alchemy,” an art that was under attack during Mayerne’s time in Paris, and which he defended in print.75 The Doctrine Generale was followed by a collection of recipes, many of them copied from other attributed sources, starting with iron (“Preparation de Mars”). In subject, these are chrysopoetic, medical, veterinary, culinary, horticultural, oeconomic (house-keeping), and craft-related, including recipes for making colors, varnishes, waxes, cements, and many recipes for gilding. There is also one practical joke: “To make it so that a cup sticks to the lips of one who drinks.”76 This manuscript can thus be found simultaneously at two ends of a spectrum between the practical and the theoretical, with the highly specific recipes at one end (closer to Bordeaux 1213 than to Ms. Fr. Ms. 640), and the explicitly generalizing theory of the Doctrine Generale at the other.

It is also equivocal in other ways. The first person appears, but far less frequently than in Ms. Fr. 640. In one case, an (unattributed) recipe made use of the first person. “For softening gold” includes the claim, “I made use of this composition very successfully.”77 In another recipe attributed to Guillaume Trougny, “Injection for fetid gonorrheas,” Trougny writes, “I cured with this in 24 hours.”78 This is about the same frequency of the first person that one might find in a contemporary printed book of secrets.79 Furthermore, the use of the first person here functions as it normally does in recipe collections in order to assert efficacy. Speculative uses of the first person, such as “I don’t know if,” or “I think,” do not occur.

The usage of the term essay is rare. It appears in one recipe for ink by Nicholas Briot, where one is instructed to “constantly test [essayant tousiours]” oak galls slowly boiling in wine, until the point when the ink is red when it dries. Mayerne, who made several corrections to this recipe throughout, noted at the bottom margin, “This ink never fades. Mr Briot senior. He thought a great deal of it.” Mayerne then offered his own version adjusting a few of its ingredients.80

The main text of the recipes themselves includes little self-reflexivity. Several recipes draw attention with an attributed note. A recipe for a “huile de Vitriol” includes a “Nota” signed by “R.” An “Oil of the Tincture of Antimony” has a note signed “Mutilet.”81 One recipe, “Pour la Rage,” for use on humans or animals suffering from rabies, is followed by an “Observation.” This records that “This receipt was practiced frequently by the Herberts of Orleans and by du Menil, the maistre d’hostel of the King, and it was also experimented frequently by Damain, on rational as well as irrational animals.”82 There are other, vaguer attestations. One “R” who appears throughout, claims that his “Attractive that must be applied on the top of the head … was proven by more than a thousand people, it works well for ophthalmology and recently two girls with the help of God recovered their sight which they had almost lost by using it.”83

In addition to the occasional note, observation, and Mayerne’s marginal annotations, the recipes sometimes include general principles or causal explanations. For instance, “R.” made the general statement, set off in a separate paragraph and larger letters as though it were the title of a recipe, “Every metal sublimed by the salt of mixts resolves later through cooling into an oil and finally through the fire coagulates into a stone.”84 Sometimes explanations for effects are offered in the course of a recipe; for instance, Mutillet’s method for distilling spirit of wine produced more quantity and of better quality than the ordinary method, he claimed, “because the salt and the tartar retain the phlegm and release the spirit, which they also fortify and improve.”85

Mayerne’s recipes, and his alone, include references to other named written texts. For instance, within his “Syrup purifying the blood,” Mayerne disagreed on dosage with “what Theophraste said in his treatise on Hellebore.”86 In “For the complicated hernia,” he referred to the method of [Jeremiah] Barth “in his commentaries on the Tyrocinium Chymicum of Beguin page 141.”87

In Mayerne’s marginal notes, we also see evidence of some of his innovative practices of self-reflexivity, as well as the possessiveness of his personal intellectual property, marked often with a personal monogram. “Syrup purifying the blood” affords an especially interesting instance of self-reflexivity, since, after Mayerne copied his own recipe from Briot’s miscellany, he added a marginal historical note, identified with his monogram: “I once prescribed this syrup for Johann Martin, a Rosicrucian, the nephew of Mr. Briot. . . he took it for fifteen days. . .having been cured, he departed for Muscovy where he died.”88 This note suggests how for the physician Mayerne, some characteristics of the experimental essay, such as richness of detail, first-person accounts, and historical detail, were informed by the medical genre of the observation (observatio) and its use in the individual case report.89

Further first-person notes are found around Mutillet’s “Excellent balm for wounds,” where Mayerne notes above it, “I had this balm. . . from Mr. Du Teil a physician of Geneva. It is very good. I made it.”90 A second note at the bottom of the page is marked with Mayerne’s monogram and offers his suggested changes to the recipe, “I would prefer to impregnate the oil separately.”91 At other times, Mayerne uses the margin to remind himself to try a recipe with a different ingredient. Instead of one recipe for an extract of amber, involving several days of digestion within a strong spirit of wine, he reminds himself, “See if the amber, being also a liquid, couldn’t be dissolved in the oil of potiole or that of spike lavender to make a transparent and very drying varnish.”92 In his notes throughout Sloane 3426, Mayerne is slightly more speculative, self-reflexive, and historical than his contemporaries; he also relies explicitly more on printed texts than do his alchemical collaborators.

Sloane 3423

This manuscript is entitled “Experiments & operations on matters of dyeing done by me M[ayerne]” (“Experiments & operations en matiere de teincture faittes par moy M [monogram]”). It recounts Mayerne’s experimental labors over an eleven-year period (1639–1650). This work is much more luxuriously produced, with a great deal of glorious use of many different colors of ink, and formal mise-en-page, than the other three manuscripts. It obviously was not the notebook that Mayerne must have used to record his practice initially but was retrospectively written, likely over the course of many years. Despite its high finish, it does not appear to be intended for print.93 It is primarily in French with occasional use of Latin, Italian, and the odd English word.

Sloane 3423 expands upon and formalizes many of the ways that Ms. Fr. 640 broke away from the recipe mold. In this manuscript, Mayerne departs from recipes, and also invokes learned practices, more than the author-practitioner did. However, in other manuscripts, Mayerne records secrets in classic fashion, illustrating how he could employ or modify the genre of recipes as it suited him.94 In this manuscript, Mayerne appears quite intentional in the ways he distances his practice from a standard recipe collection. He personalizes and historicizes the accounts of his experiments; at the same time, he does so within regular and semi-formalized methodical stages.

Beginning with the title he gave to the work as a whole, Mayerne utilizes both his possessive monogram and the first person throughout the manuscript. He also consistently includes the place, which is mostly at Chelsea, but sometimes also in London (he had houses in both locations).95 He notes the date of trials without any obvious reason for giving us these (as had been the case for Ydeley, whose dates referred to notorious outbreaks of plague). Mayerne also records his collaborators for individual experiments. He regularly applies formal, self-critical categories to each “Essay,” including “censures” of his previous attempts, and “speculation to try” (“speculation à essayer”) for future ones. He offers reminders, or nota benes, to himself. He cross-references to printed works, such as the standard dyeing manual, Plichto, or collections of secrets (“Item les secrets des Birelli & autres”).96

The vast majority of this manuscript is in the first person, beginning with the first sentence, “I prepared a pair of wool stockings;” Mayerne also specified that he did this on 11 September 1639 in the presence of Fletcher, a professional dyer.97 There are a few passages where Mayerne copied recipes in the third-person voice of a more standard recipe. Thus, at fol. 2r, Mayerne copies down in Latin a recipe for dyeing thread red with brazilwood. The entry concludes with “It is good” (“Bona est”) and “I made it” (“feci”), and a further note about how thread absorbs color in the case of cochineal. He does not mark these comments with his monogram.

The next recipe, also in Latin, was for dyeing on silk using cochineal and gamboge. This was in part a failure, which offered an opportunity for intensive self-reflexivity. Mayerne entitled this entry, “A chance dye, almond flower-colored. On taffeta. bizarre but beautiful, notable for its production of mixtures: not able to be imitated because of the work. It is futile to do more, etc.”98 He also noted in the margin at the very beginning, “A poorly founded speculation.” He concluded in French, “This was a speculation that did not succeed that well.” He follows this with a number of reasons, in French, for this failure:

NB. The lemon juice ruined the cochineal and the gamboge also did not accommodate itself to it.

The non-alumed silk produced a dirty color.

The alumed did better, and while it was still wet it had an unpleasant purplish redness, but once dried, it became the color of almond flowers, or the color of flesh that is a bit brown. A somewhat dark Pink color.99

Although this seemed a failure to him and a “bizarre” color, his assistant, the professional dyer Fletcher, thought a lot of it and said that merchants would value it highly. This represents an otherwise unattested use of the term “pink” as a pale reddish color in the period, as ordinarily pink in the seventeenth century denoted a yellow.100 Mayerne was, it seems, operating at the edge of fashion and in the company of a world of professional dyers whose color lexicon left no other textual remains from the period.

Mayerne continued, in a section labelled “Way to Try” to offer new ways to try the same process. He gave himself the memorandum, “Try this operation again this way.” Noting how he had to be flexible in the proportions he added and always adapt them to circumstances as they arose, he noted “Everything depends upon the trial.”101 Here we see the “cycle of trial, failure, replication, and a responsive, adaptive approach to unexpected outcomes” that is the central epistemology of making and knowing.102

Alas, the phrase “I failed” recurs repeatedly in subsequent pages.103 However Mayerne’s failure was productive, both for him in terms of further speculation, and for us, in terms of the ways his discussion of failure situated his account as the record of a historical experiment in a particular time and place. Whereas his successful following of the recipe using brazilwood ends abruptly with the—typical within recipe literature—taciturn note, “I did it” and “it is good,” his second, failed recipe engendered much speculation, further trial, and a recorded interchange with a professional dyer that opens up an otherwise lost historical context of changing fashions and color terminologies in textile dyes of the period.104

Mayerne methodically tries out various processes by varying a single factor. For example, on fol. 14v, we find “Chelsea the 23rd of April 1640 Different trials made on wool stockings dyed in yellow with weld.”105 He first tried dying these wool stockings in a vessel of “new tinplate, very clean and well scoured.”106 The results were “pretty enough. Could suffice (Mediocrement beau. Peult passer).”107 Next, he tried a different kind of tin. This was a failure, as Mayerne noted, “Wrong. Lacks spirit (Faultif. Manque d’esprit).”108 After two more trials, he concluded, “Of these four trials, the first and most of all the last are the best. The other are not rejectables. The second is to be noted, but not imitated.”109 He thus considered even failed trials noteworthy.

As in the author-practitioner’s list of books to obtain (fol. 2r) and a list of various artisanal processes perhaps for trying (fol. 169r), Mayerne composed many lists. He cataloged different liquids, metals, and vessels, used in current practices.110 He collected speculative new dyestuffs to try, such as a list of woods.111 He speculated about the identity of substances he had heard about that were unknown to him, such as one ingredient he had been informed was used in Southeast Asian calicos, a salt called sunchura. In his “Speculations and conjectures on the salt sunchura” he drew up a lengthy list of all sorts of salts to consider.112

He frequently exhorted himself, especially following a failed attempt, with commands and commonplaces. For instance, at one point he notes, “Cook, cook, cook, and do not weary.” Versions of this were a command common in many alchemical classics as a constant exhortation to keep working.113 In addition to this standard alchemical admonition, Mayerne sprinkled throughout his manuscript quite a few proverbs in various languages. These included forms of encouragement, intellectual advice about how to go about speculating, and injunctions to secrecy. For instance, in an account of one faulty trial, he cited from Petrarch in Italian, “He who is too subtle breaks his neck.” On the same page, he recorded another Italian proverb, “he who leaves the old path for the new often finds himself misled.”114 Elsewhere, he reprimanded himself in Latin, “The hasty dog gives birth to blind pups,”115 “Experience is the master of affairs,” “Through various difficulties, experience made art, example having shown the way,”116 “May a better fortune follow a weak beginning,” and the formula known as Ockham’s razor, “It is futile to do with more that which can be done with fewer.” He cautioned himself not to reveal his processes with a “He has lived well who has lived in obscurity.”117

While constantly and clearly identifying his authorship and the time and place of each essay, Mayerne also ventriloquized, as an exhortation to himself, the sonorous admonitions of the ancients. Such quotations, in the first case, stemmed from alchemical literature. They also recall Montaigne’s weaving together of quotations in his Essays, ironically using commonplaces as a vehicle in which to locate the self.

Mayerne personalized his work still further than did the author-practitioner, particularly through his possessive monogram. His unusual use of commonplaces to exhort himself towards the proper mindset for experimentation personalized and interiorized experimental labor is a form of “ego document.”118 At the same time, we also see in his papers more systematic collection, cataloging, and ordering of information, an aspect of knowledge management we might assume would be a part of the depersonalization of knowledge, but it was not. Mayerne’s process of documenting failure was more self-critical, formal and stadial process of documenting failure than that of the author-practitioner. This might be explained in part by the fact that the author-practitioner may have intended his work for publication. Mayerne’s work, for all the glamour of its multi-colored inks and formal mise-en-page, seems destined for domestic and mostly personal consultation.

On a spectrum between recipes and experimental essays, we might order these four manuscripts thus: Bordeaux Ms. 1213, Sloane 3426 , Ms. Fr. 640, and Sloane 3423. This is not a simple progression. As we noted, Sloane 3426 is ambivalent in many ways, with different recipe authors varying in their usage of the first-person, of causal explanations, and of references to printed works. In general, however, both Ms. Fr. 640 and Sloane 3423 emphasize the first person and the practice of trial, or essay, far more than was common in recipe collections, commonplace collections, or books of secrets in the period. They participate in the general preference for first-person narration, self-reflexivity, experimentation, and speculation found in essay writing in the period in general. The use of the first person functions more to situate the account as the questionable practice of a particular individual rather than to assert its truth or efficacy. These texts might be better categorized as collections of experimental essays.

Conclusion: Thickness, Doubt, and Bacon’s Method

The evolution of the experimental essay from the recipe bears implications for the nature of early modern experimental knowledge, the manner in which it emerged, and what it might have to suggest to us about contemporary scientific rhetorical practice. Personalizing knowledge, and making clear what the steps were in producing it in a particular time and place, situated knowledge as the product of individuals. Personalization thus offered greater tools for query and doubt than those afforded by texts appearing in an impersonal, omniscient voice. Rather than increasing claims of certainty, we see in the experimental rhetoric that developed over the course of the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries an increase in uncertainty. Characteristics of the emerging experimental essay that responded to such uncertainty might include adaptability to unexpected outcomes, tentative and probabilistic claims, and increasingly formalized and sophisticated mechanisms for doubting.

As Porter has discussed, large-scale narratives about the thinning of modern scientific knowledge link certainty to an impersonal method. For Bacon, as generally in the sixteenth and early seventeenth centuries, method primarily meant textual techniques for storing, arranging, accessing and transmitting knowledge, rather than a particular series of cognitive processes or forms of argumentation.119 Above all, the term method in the late sixteenth and early seventeenth century connoted a Ramist dichotomous chart, a shortcut to knowledge organization and visualization that could be utilized in any form of textually-based knowledge, no matter the epistemology. Ramist dichotomous charts were the antithesis of the essay; they were purposefully extremely systematic and impersonal, thinned knowledge that could efficiently mechanize the process of pedagogy.

Bacon, however, opposed this thinned-down, impersonal method, which he termed a “magistral” method in opposition to his own suggestion of an “initiatory method.” By adopting these two terms of magister and initiate, Bacon intervened in the mystery-cult dynamic upon which recipe culture was based, as well as in current Ramist pedagogical practices. The “magistral” method presented knowledge in the omniscient, unquestionable voice of the master. The initiatory method, Bacon claimed, denied the authority of the teacher who presented knowledge as though it were already known, forefronting instead the ability of the student to ask critical questions.

We might relate this to the use that the author-practitioner and Mayerne made of the command voice. Commands in the recipe tradition, such as recipe (“take”) itself, appear in an authoritative voice that does not make clear what doubts and failures might exist within the recipe—even if such recipes are attributed and users do not hesitate to scribble questions and criticisms in the margins all around. The author-practitioner and Mayerne sometimes use the command voice in a different fashion, that is, as a reminder to themselves of speculative other ingredients and processes to try in the future. This use of the command is, as it were, the initiate taking charge and personalizing the command to themselves; it is a mode more explicitly probabilistic and uncertain about future success compared to the typical commands found in recipes.

As a mechanism allowing the student to question knowledge by tracing the course of its development, Bacon suggested thickening knowledge with the particular details of its origins. Bacon “compared the magistral method to the piling of dead wood,” because it offered cleaned building material, efficiently prepared for building storehouses of knowledge and for transmission.120 This is what we might call, in Porter’s terms, thinned knowledge. Bacon complained that such pre-packaged lumber, by wiping away the messy dirt around the roots, that is, all the subjective, local, questionable and failed characteristics of knowledge trials, prevented the future growth of knowledge. Better to start small, with the incipient little shoots with their dirt still clinging to them, and grow knowledge step by step. Developing literary technologies with the dirt still on means thickening and personalizing.

This was precisely what the author-practitioner did. The increasing personalization we encountered in the manuscripts of the author-practitioner and Mayerne would be surprising if we expected a thinning of knowledge away from a thick, local embodiment. However, such personalization is not surprising if we expect a shift in the meaning of experiment from general experience to a particular event.121 Along its journey from a recipe to an experimental essay, experimental rhetoric passed through a period of hyper localization and first-person narration.

Currently the development of the experimental essay is typically connected to a notion of Baconian “experimental philosophy” taken up by the Restoration Royal Society and often granted great agency as the ancestor of modern scientific rhetoric.122 This chronology of its development suggests that a certain epistemology came first, and Restoration experimental philosophers adopted fully fledged literary technologies, including the experimental essay as well as forms of natural history, adapted to their epistemological needs.123 However, the story of a slower, wider, and messier development of detailed first-person historical experimental reports complicates that narrative.124 In this essay, I have argued that how-to literature slowly evolved to become more personalized and historicized as tacit knowledge gradually was rendered increasingly textual. This would suggest that experimental philosophy, an explicit form of limited, probabilistic speculation developed through adaptive, reiterative experimentation, grew out of such developing literary technologies and not the other way around.

The increasing personalization of how-to texts in the early modern period can be set in the social context of an entrepreneurial trading zone, in which artisanal figures made overtures to more textually-based scholarship and vice versa. Ursula Klein has described a process of depersonalization of craft expertise at the end of the eighteenth century (one arena in which this occurred was the eighteenth-century Lyon silk industry founded two centuries earlier by the Turquet family). However, according to Klein, that development followed an increase in a variety of new roles and crafts in which individuals could claim personal mastery; these figures “were neither ordinary craftsmen nor traditional scholars,” writes Klein. “Like scholars, they were familiar with textual traditions as well as mathematical and graphical representations; but, like craftsmen, they actively participated in the world of labor in addition to their contacts with, or even citizenship in, the Republic of Letters.”125

Prior to eighteenth-century depersonalization, the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries offered a period of often idiosyncratic inventiveness, as individuals departed from the ancient genre of the recipe in different ways. This comparison of four manuscripts highlights the innovations of Ms. Fr. 640 within this snowballing stream of creatively diversifying textual practices. A thousand flowers bloomed when it came to creative ways to document experimental knowledge in the early modern period. Much more research remains to be done in unearthing this diversity and making a case for what it says about the emergence of new scientific genres and what it might have to suggest for current scientific rhetorical practices.

Not all of the innovations of the author-practitioner, or Mayerne, or Bacon, or of the many others who took pen to paper to document their trials in the period would continue on in the future. One aspect of early modern experimental practices which has noticeably dropped away over the intervening centuries is the use of first-person narration in scientific articles, which rely instead upon heavy citation as a means for attaching knowledge to the viewpoints of individuals in particular times and places. Authors of scientific journal articles, like researchers in other fields, often follow a convention of using passive-voice constructions in ways that appear to suggest a thin objectivity and replicability. The use of the impersonal voice in scientific journals has declined in recent years,with some journals calling for an increased use of the first person.126 To some scientists, a return to the thickly personalized experimental essay appeals, especially as a replicability crisis has encouraged scientists to reckon with the falsely reliable air of current journal rhetoric.127

This study of the evolution of the experimental essay suggests that the history of experimental rhetoric has never relied upon a single transition from thick to thin, and from the personally subjective to the impersonally objective. In praise of what we might call the “thickness” of a personal essay in contrast to the thinness of information overload and scattered attention, the essayist Scott Russell Saunders wrote,

In this era of prepackaged thought the essay is the closest thing we have, on paper, to a record of the individual mind at work and play. . . Feeling overwhelmed by data, random information, the flotsam and jetsam of mass culture, we relish the spectacle of a single consciousness making sense of a part of the chaos.128
