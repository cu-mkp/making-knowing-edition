Sulfuric Acid for the Scribe

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/h470-b808
← Back to Essays

Abstract

Oil of sulfur, composed primarily of sulfuric acid, is a corrosive mineral acid with many applications. In Ms. Fr. 640, however, the material is almost exclusively mentioned on a single folio (fol. 46r–v) in a series of recipes possibly copied from a source referred to as “Urb.” This essay studies and recreates a selection of these recipes, focusing on those in which oil of sulfur is used as a material for a scribe—as an invisible ink, in creating openwork in paper, as a cleaning agent for quills, and for removing ink from parchment. While numerous contemporary books of secrets include recipes for invisible inks and ink removal, often requiring acids like citrus juice, no other known sources specifically use oil of sulfur for these purposes. This novel application of oil of sulfur serves as a valuable case study of the use of mineral acids in early modern craft processes, as well as early modern techniques for invisible ink and erasure.

Introduction

All seven recipes on fol. 46v, and three on fol. 46r, outline uses of “oil of sulfur,” a material predominantly composed of what is now known as sulfuric acid.1 Aside from the ten oil of sulfur recipes on this folio, only one other entry in the manuscript mentions the material.2 Of these recipes, the four on which this essay focuses (all from fol. 46v) deal with oil of sulfur as a tool of the scribe, describing its applications as an invisible ink (“Writing without ink”), in creating openwork in paper (“Making a letter à jour on paper and other work”), as a cleaning agent for writing quills (“Scribe, Oil of sulfur”), and for removing ink from parchment (“Erasing a letter”).3 While sulfuric acid is frequently mentioned in early modern recipe books and medical treatises,4 there is scant literature on the use of sulfuric acid in writing processes. These recipes therefore offer insight into sixteenth-century writing techniques, as well as the specific methods with which the author-practitioner was familiar and which he may have used in the creation of Ms. Fr. 640. Many questions are raised by the fol. 46v recipes: why would they have been included in a compendium such as Ms. Fr. 640, and how do they relate to other recipes in the manuscript? Is the use of sulfuric acid as a scribe’s tool described in other books of secrets or writing treatises? Could the Ms. Fr. 640 recipes have been copied from another source, and if so, what do they tell us about how the author-practitioner compiled Ms. Fr. 640? The fol. 46v recipes contain meaningful clues, which facilitate a greater understanding of the author-practitioner’s working methods, the use of mineral acids in early modern craft processes, and early modern techniques for invisible ink and erasure.

While there are multiple ways of contextualizing the fol. 46v recipes within the manuscript, I chose to compare them to other recipes in Ms. Fr. 640 that use similar materials or that lay out techniques of scribes and illuminators. Numerous recipes in Ms. Fr. 640 address working on paper and parchment, including recipes that focus on technique and optical effects (e.g., “Mathematical figures without a ruler or compass,” fol. 19v; “Painting large figures,” fol. 65v), and recipes that describe illumination processes such as gilding parchment (e.g. “Seat for gold leaf on parchment or paper,” fol. 10v; “Lettering of gold on paper,” fol. 8r; “Layer of burnished gold on paper,” fol. 29v). However, these recipes are widely dispersed throughout the manuscript, making connections between the recipes on fol. 46v and these other applications on paper and parchment tenuous.

The immediate context of the recipes on fol. 46r–v is of interest given that it is the only folio in the manuscript to include oil of sulfur (aside from a note much later in the manuscript). Compared to other sections of the manuscript, the writing on this folio is very clear, with no marginal notes or visible corrections, which might suggest that the author-practitioner copied the recipes from another source rather than recording them from observations of a practitioner or from personal experiments. Further, the author-practitioner writes “Urb” toward the end of all but one of the recipes on fol. 46v,5 raising the possibility that “Urb” is an abbreviation for a source from which this group of recipes is drawn. Indeed, “Comment. urb. rom.” is mentioned on fol. 1r among a list of authors.6 Whatever the meaning of “Urb,” it does not appear anywhere else in the manuscript. If “Urb” is a source, it could provide a valuable piece of information about the transmission of knowledge in Ms. Fr. 640 and about the working practices of the author-practitioner; as yet, however, the identification of “Urb” remains obscure, and requires further investigation. The fact that the fol. 46v recipes are composed neatly on a single page and use a material that is only mentioned once otherwise in Ms. Fr. 640 reinforces the idea that the author-practitioner drew them from another source.

Though the largest group of the oil of sulfur recipes on fol. 46r–v are relevant to the scribe, the others include forays into medicine, cosmetic treatments, cobblery, and practical jokes. This essay focuses on four writing recipes from fol. 46v, but it is worth noting that two more recipes on that page also address writing or drawing with oil of sulfur. A recipe titled, “Shoemaker” (“Cordonnier”), for example, describes decorating leather shoes with a “writing quill” dipped in oil of sulfur:

If he wants to do some tanned work on black leather shoes, he can dip in oil of sulfur a writing quill and paint with it what he would like on shoes, boots, and colets. And he will obtain a reddish color which no effort will erase. Urb.

While this recipe uses leather footwear as its object rather than paper or parchment, the process of using oil of sulfur and a quill to create text or designs remains the same, and presumably parchment and leather might react similarly to the acid.7 Another recipe describes creating “Black letters on stone,” by writing on stone with oil of sulfur and then heating until the text “becomes black and penetrates.”

In contrast, the remaining four oil of sulfur recipes on fol. 46r–v are more disparate, reflecting a grouping of recipes by material rather than effect. The recipes on fol. 46r address treatments for bodily ailments or imperfections: “For the teeth, oil of sulfur” uses oil of sulfur as a tooth whitener;8 “Against the falling sickness” is a medical treatment for epilepsy or seizures;9 and “Against cold gouts” is a medical treatment for gout. The least similar recipe among the oil of sulfur recipes is “Making someone’s boots burn” on fol. 46v, which promises that if you, “Rub with the aforesaid oil, & as it becomes warm, they will burn without a flame. Urb.” Significantly, after 46v, the author-practitioner returns to medical recipes on fol. 47r, including different treatments for tooth-whitening and “epilepsy or dizziness.” The fact that the author-practitioner deviates course on fol. 46v only to return to medical recipes on fol. 47r might suggest that he encountered these applications of oil of sulfur while writing the fol. 46r recipes, and recorded them on fol. 46v before returning to his original topic.

While oil of sulfur is almost entirely restricted to fol. 46, its single other occurrence in Ms. Fr. 640 is on fol. 117v, where it is mentioned in a note on a metalcasting recipe. This application falls in line with the author-practitioner’s use of other mineral acids, which he discusses making and using throughout Ms. Fr. 640, most notably aquafortis. These acids are used in Ms. Fr. 640 almost exclusively for metalworking. The use of oil of sulfur for writing and erasing could therefore be considered atypical for the use of mineral acids in Ms. Fr. 640. However, since the author-practitioner’s other applications of mineral acids rely on their reactivity with metal, it may be useful to consider the fol. 46v recipes “Erasing a letter” and “Scribe, Oil of sulfur” as relying on a similar chemical process, since the iron sulfates in iron gall ink cause its reaction with sulfuric acid. While this would not explain the use of oil of sulfur in the other fol. 46 recipes, it nonetheless raises a question about the author-practitioner’s understanding of the properties of and relationships between materials.

In any case, the fol. 46r–v recipes are thus unusual for several reasons: they represent almost the only mention of oil of sulfur in Ms. Fr. 640, perhaps suggesting that oil of sulfur was not a material with which the author-practitioner was familiar or to which he had access; they contain what appears to be a citation of an outside source, “Urb,” that is otherwise unmentioned in Ms. Fr. 640; they represent a grouping of recipes by material rather than effect; they are cleanly written. The evidence that these recipes are copied from elsewhere, combined with the fact that the author-practitioner only mentions oil of sulfur once elsewhere in Ms. Fr. 640, suggests that the author-practitioner probably did not attempt the recipes himself. Yet if the author-practitioner did not attempt the fol. 46v recipes, was it because of lack of opportunity or lack of interest? Did the author-practitioner include these recipes because invisible ink and ink erasure recipes were common in books of secrets? Further, were they considered to be practical applications for the scribe, or should they be categorized more as novelties or illusions?

Recipes

The oil of sulfur recipes concerning writing describe two opposite effects: making black letters from invisible ink and making black ink invisible. “Writing without ink” uses oil of sulfur to write on paper with a quill; though the letters are initially invisible, they emerge “black as this one” (i.e., as black as the author-practitioner’s ink was at the time he was writing)10 when the paper is exposed to the heat of a flame. This recipe fits into a long tradition of invisible inks,11 which would have been useful for passing secret messages, whether as a novelty trick among friends and lovers or for transmitting more clandestine correspondences. Many books of secrets contain recipes of this kind, demonstrating their popularity and ubiquity. Giambattista della Porta’s 1558 Natural Magick, for example, contains several recipes about “inks” that require heat to become visible (e.g., “Letters th-- cannot be read unless the paper be burnt,” “Write letters -- of fire”), including one that uses the acid of citrus juice, “To make letters visible by fire.”12 Another recipe in which heat produces the appearance of ink can be seen in a 1628 Book of Pretty Conceits,13 in a recipe titled, “To write a letter with such Inke as cannot be perceiued, vnlesse it be holden before the fire:”

Take a sheet of fair white paper, and write thereon with the iuice of a red Onion, well mixed or temperd with the white of an egge and then dry it well, and then it cannot bée perceiued to be any other then plaine white paper without any writing on it: but if you hold it betwixt you and the fire, you may the more easily read it, and perfectly perceiue the letters or contents thereof.14

Various other methods for transmitting secret messages also appear in recipe books. For example, William Phillip’s 1596 Book of Secrets,15 itself translated from earlier sources, includes a recipe, “To write without ink, that it may not be seen, vnlesse the paper be wet with water.” Phillip’s recipe describes writing on paper with an “ink” made of vitriol and water, and subsequently revealing the writing by immersing the paper in a solution of water and gall powder, producing the same chemical reaction that turns iron gall ink black:

Take pouder of victriall, and put it into a cleane inkehorne, put a little cleane water to it, when the victriall is dissolued, write with it either vpon paper or parchment, and let it drie, and it cannot bee read: when you will read it, take halfe a pint of water, and put thereto an ounce of pouder of gaules, mix them well together, then straine them through a linnen cloath into a cleane pot, then draw the paper through the water, and the writing will be clacke, as if it had ben written with inke.16

This technique is also described in John Bate’s 1634 The mysteryes of nature.17 While these processes are different from the one described in Ms. Fr. 640, their intended effects are the same.

“Making a letter à jour on paper and other work” reiterates the “writing without ink” recipe with one additional step: abrading the blackened letters with a “sponge,” which causes the weakened areas of the paper to fall out and produce openwork letters. The phrase “à jour” may refer to an enamelwork technique, plique à jour (Fig. 1).18 This was well-known during this period, mentioned, for example, in Benvenuto Cellini’s 1568 treatise on goldsmithing,19 referring to a delicate filigree style of metal tracery fused with colored enamels to produce the look of stained glass. The Ms. Fr. 640 recipe does not include instructions for what to do with the paper once the openwork letters are produced, but the mention of plique à jour suggests that a colored background similar to plique à jour enamel might be intended. Alternately, the term may simply refer here to delicate openwork that creates a lace-like result. It is also possible that rather than being a finished decorative product, this technique was used to create a stencil for transferring letters onto other surfaces.20

Although this technique on paper does not appear in other sources, the intentional use of sulfuric acid to corrode or weaken paper does appear in other recipes, as in a recipe in Natural Magick titled “How letters are made, that eat the paper,” in which sulfuric acid is added into ink. However, though the effect outlined is similar, the intent of della Porta’s recipe seems markedly different than that of the à jour letters:

in few days by corroding the paper, or the ink it self, the letters will vanish, or in a moneth, as you put in more or less of the oyl; and this you may try before you send away your letter: If you would have it work more slowly, add but a little oyl; if faster, put in more: you may, when it is too strong, put some water to it.

In other words, della Porta’s recipe is not used to create a decorative effect, but rather to create a message that will self-destruct within a specified and predictable timeframe.

While the fol. 46v recipes described above produce effects by applying oil of sulfur and heat to paper, the other two conversely use oil of sulfur to remove ink.21 “Scribe, Oil of sulfur” describes cleaning a quill by dipping it in oil of sulfur, while “Erasing a letter” is a method for making letters “disappear from the parchment” by retracing the letters with a quill dipped in oil of sulfur. Methods for ink erasure are found in numerous books of secrets, and frequently utilize acids. Natural Magick includes a recipe titled “Take letters off the paper” which, though it does not use oil of sulfur, features another acid often derived from vitriol, aquafortis. Significantly, della Porta specifies that this works best with “letters, written with Gall and Copras,”22 indicating an observation of the interaction between acid and iron-based ink. Recipes for erasing ink can further be traced to medieval sources, including a chapter in the Algerian prince and art patron Tamim Ibn al-Muizz Ibn Bādīs’s 1025 CE bookmaking treatise.23 Although the chapter entitled “On the work in which the writing is eradicated from manuscripts and parchment, from paper and bound books”24 does not mention sulfuric acid, other acids including “acid of the citron” are suggested as effective ink removers. There is no evidence of Ibn Bādīs’s Arabic treatise having been translated into European languages, but the fact that these techniques were described in the eleventh century demonstrates a long history and suggests that they may have circulated in Eurasia prior to being recorded in recipe books.

A variety of ink removal recipes are discussed in books of secrets and writing treatises, including two in William Phillip’s Book of Secrets, “To take inke out of paper or parchment:”

Take Colofoniam, which is called pix graecum, beat it small, and cast it on the paper that is written, then wet a cloath, and lay it on the Colofoniam, vpon the cloath lay some fresh horse dung, & vpon that set a smooth tile stone, then if it be in winter let it stand a whole night, but if it bee summer, let it stand but from mor|ning till nine of the clocke.

This recipe is directly followed by another method, “Otherwise”:

Take Salarmoniacke, and alum, still it in a

limbeck, and with this water wet the writing and it will goe out.25

These contemporary examples demonstrate the widespread and varied nature of invisible ink and ink removal recipes, some of which are similar to the fol. 46v recipes. Despite the prevalence of these recipes in early modern sources, however, I have found no contemporary recipes using sulfuric acid used in the ways described on fol. 46v, perhaps indicating that these applications were uncommon, or that alternate ingredients achieved better results or were more widely available.

Ingredients and Reconstructions

The recipes on fol. 46v include ambiguous materials and instructions, which were investigated through reconstruction.26 For example, there is considerable variation between different preparations of parchments, papers, inks, and quills. While such variation likely would have been expected in an era before standardized materials, it is important to note because the results of recipe reconstructions may have been affected by such variables. In the case of ink, while there are two recipes for printers’ ink in Ms. Fr. 640, there are no recipes for writing ink.27 The printers’ inks differ considerably from iron gall ink, the preferred writing ink during this period and the type used in creating Ms. Fr. 640. Additionally, I hypothesized that it would be necessary to use iron gall ink to produce the chemical reactions found in the fol. 46v recipes.28 Though ink could have been purchased easily in this period, I prepared ink following a recipe from Jack C. Thompson29 for a standard early modern heated iron gall ink. I also used two other historically-appropriate inks, a cold-prepared iron gall ink from the 2015 Columbia University French Paleography workshop (to test the variability of a different preparation of iron gall ink), and a carbon ink from the Ancient Ink Laboratory.30 The presumption was that the iron sulfate in iron gall ink would react with the sulfuric acid whereas carbon ink would be unreactive, allowing for a control group. Additionally, I used 50-50 hemp and cotton paper with gelatin sizing, and both goat and calf parchment.31

Safety considerations compelled me to be very precise when dealing with sulfuric acid. I began the reconstructions with a 15 percent, 3 Molar sulfuric acid solution. This low concentration solution was effective in two of the four recipes attempted, those for “Writing without ink” and “Making a letter à jour on paper and other work,” which are closely related. Both of these recipes include writing on paper with oil of sulfur and heating the paper until the letters appear black; “Making a letter à jour on paper and other work” extends this recipe one step further by using a “sponge” to gently abrade the surface of the paper until the blackened letters fall out, creating openwork letters.

I established proof of concept for these recipes by drawing circles on paper with the sulfuric acid solution, which only revealed themselves when held over a hot plate with tongs (Fig. 2). I also tested the recipe on goat and calf parchments, which did not react (Fig. 3). Furthermore, parchment curled and warped quickly at low temperatures, which suggested that not only is paper necessary for the desired chemical reaction with cellulose, but also because parchment is destroyed at low heat whereas paper tolerates heat well. To produce the à jour effect, I abraded the surface of the paper with a makeshift brush until the blackened circles fell out of the paper (Fig. 4). Since these techniques were effective while the other two were not, it is possible that the “writing without ink” and “Making a letter à jour on paper and other work” recipes required a less intense concentration of sulfuric acid to begin with, or that the process of heating the sulfuric acid in these experiments concentrated the sulfuric acid as water evaporated, producing a more effective result.

The other two recipes, “Scribe, Oil of sulfur” or “Erasing a letter,” did not produce the effect that the author-practitioner describes when attempted with the 15% sulfuric acid solution.32 The diluted sulfuric acid solution was not noticeably more effective for removing ink from paper, parchment, or quill than water, the control in these experiments (Fig. 5). I therefore attempted these reconstructions a second time with a higher concentration of sulfuric acid, using a 96% 18 Molar sulfuric acid.33 Repeating the “Scribe, Oil of sulfur” and “Erasing a letter” experiments with the high concentration sulfuric acid proved much more effective. In “Scribe, Oil of sulfur,” the quill became very clean (Fig. 6), while the control quill cleaned in water remained black. I was surprised, however, that the “Erasing a letter” experiment was not more effective; since the concentration of the sulfuric acid was very high, I assumed that the acid would immediately react and remove the ink completely. The sulfuric acid did react with the iron gall inks in a way that it did not with the carbon ink, and was much more effective than the water control; however, the letters did not disappear upon contact. While the letters lightened and became a reddish color over several minutes, they did not fully disappear (Fig. 7, Fig. 8). This experiment was most effective in the middle of a letter where the natural curvature of the parchment allowed the acid to pool a bit. Even in this case, however, the ink was not immediately removed. Based on these observations, sulfuric acid certainly aids in the removal of ink from paper and parchment, but it did not result in a totally clean surface. It is, of course, also possible that the author-practitioner omitted further steps or treatments that would have more fully removed ink traces.

Upon observing the reconstructions a week later, the concentrated sulfuric acid had eaten through the parchment, but not the paper (Fig. 9, Fig. 10). This also occurred after two weeks in the reconstructions with the lower concentration of sulfuric acid (Fig. 11). While this might suggest that this method would be used with paper rather than parchment, the recipe for “Erasing a letter” specifically mentions parchment. Further, long term effects of the reconstructions on paper have not yet been observed, so it is possible that the paper will also be eaten away over a longer period of time.34

The reconstructions demonstrated that the applications of oil of sulfur as a scribe’s tool are viable at a low concentration for “writing without ink” and “Making a letter à jour on paper and other work,” and with concentrated sulfuric acid for “Scribe, Oil of sulfur” and “Erasing a letter.” Assuming that the sixteenth-century workshop would have had access to a concentrated solution,35 the reconstructions verified that oil of sulfur could have been an effective addition to the scribe’s tool kit. However, the degradation that it causes, at least in parchment, also limits its applicability. While many contemporary recipes describe intended effects similar to those in the fol. 46v recipes, I have found none that specifically use sulfuric acid, suggesting that if these applications of sulfuric acid were used, their scope may have been limited. Perhaps the corrosive properties of sulfuric acid prohibited its widespread use as a scribe’s tool, especially given that a variety of materials were said to achieve the same effects.
