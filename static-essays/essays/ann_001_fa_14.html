Wax and Tallow: Material Explorations

By , DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/0cp6-7h54
← Back to Essays

Abstract

Wax and tallow are easily transformed from solid to liquid and back again. Their properties, such as hardness, opacity, malleability, and carvability, can also be transformed through the addition of various substances, including resins, earths, and pigments. These versatile materials were used for many purposes in early modern Europe, and also possessed a range of symbolic, sacramental, and other cultural meanings. Taking inspiration from the entry on fol. 109r for “Modeling in wax,” this essay provides a preliminary exploration of the broad uses and meanings of these important materials.

“The light is God, the wax is man, Christ is both.”

– Cornelio Musso (1579)1

“It is as precious as it is ambiguous in its duality, for wax—poised between a solid and liquid state—fluctuates between presence and absence, strength and weakness, will and obedience, virtue and vice, memory and oblivion, death and resurrection.”

– G. A. Guerzoni (2012)2

Wax and tallow are mentioned throughout BnF Ms. Fr. 640, and some entries focus on transforming the qualities of the material itself, in particular, to alter its hardness or softness. On folio 109r, for example, an entry for “Modeling in wax” recounts “When wax is too hard, one mixes in turpentine or a little butter, which sont pl renders the wax more amiable, and more appropriate than the tallow that the Italians put in....” Given the frequency of wax (cire) and tallow (suif) in the manuscript, an exploration of their material properties, of their comparative performance in processes, and of the ways in which they combine is in order. We also explore how these mixtures relate more broadly to themes of material transformation in Ms. Fr. 640 and other early modern sources.

In the early modern period, wax was a commonly purchased commodity that saw a variety of uses in workshops, apothecaries, and ecclesiastical spaces, as well as in the household.3 It could be used as a means of imitation or trickery, as wax easily imitates other materials.4 Tallow, or rendered animal fat, was less expensive than wax and more easily sourced. It shared many of the material properties and uses of wax, although it was considered a material of lower quality.5 Both wax and tallow were used regularly by early modern craftspeople: both could be used to carve and model patterns for sculptures,6 and could be employed in other processes, such as copper etching, bronze casting, and gilding.7 Both materials were used to make candles for illumination, although the greater expense of wax meant it was more likely to be used in candles for sacred spaces. Wax and tallow readily change from one state to another, from solid to liquid, evoking the transformation of other materials, especially glass and metals, which also could be melted down and solidified into new objects. This ability to transform states lent to wax a spiritual dimension, as seen both in alchemical and devotional8 practices. Moreover, the central place of wax as an ingredient in many medicinal remedies, creams, ointments, and cosmetics9 also highlighted its place in making substances that themselves could bring about transformation in health or in appearance.

Wax and tallow share more than the ability to move readily from liquid to solid and back again. Both substances ideally undergo a purification process before they can be used. In the early modern period, wax was purified and bleached in earthenware pots, boiled in a mixture of “fresh seawater, alum, and saltpetre several times, until no traces of impurities remain[ed].”10 Tallow also underwent a purification process before being used, but early modern sources on rendering tallow are scarce, perhaps because this process was considered common knowledge and thus unimportant to record, similar to recipes for making bread.11 Modern recipes that describe tallow rendering techniques, however, are plentiful12 and widely available on the internet.13 In our experiments, we rendered our beef fat and pork fat in a modern slow cooker, but this could have also been done over many hours in an oven or on a stove. It is essential in rendering to apply heat to the fat for many hours without burning it and then strain the rendered fat through cheesecloth to remove the impurities. This straining process can be repeated multiple times to achieve an ever more purified and perhaps firmer substance.

Variations in wax and tallow reveal different sensory and functional properties, and these are noted in early modern sources. In “The Use and Abuse of Beeswax,” Guido Antonio Guerzoni writes that in order to assess the quality of wax, “one must carefully examine the color. The finest is yellow: the lighter it is, the better, and the darker, the worse.”14 In Ms. Fr. 640, the author-practitioner refers to “white wax” in many entries, referring to a higher quality, more purified wax.15 Scent could also play a role in determining the quality of the wax, as a “rotten or mouldy” scent could indicate that tallow had been added to the wax in order to make the expensive material go further.16 Tallow was much easier to procure and less expensive than beeswax; the fat of different animals could be used, and each of these fats possessed different material properties with their own distinct advantages. For instance, lamb tallow is mentioned in Ms. Fr. 640 in an entry on burnishing gold, and Biringuccio notes goat tallow as an ingredient in a wax mixture.17 In our experiments, we rendered both beef and pork tallow in multiple iterations. In general, the pork tallow had a much smoother texture18 than the beef tallow; the consistency of the once-rendered beef tallow was reminiscent of mashed potatoes, while the consistency of the once-rendered pork was closer to thick yogurt. After rendering the fats for an additional five hours, the twice-rendered pork fat was even creamier in texture (Fig. 1); however, the twice-rendered beef fat had a particulate consistency that could be compared to hard rice pudding. We believe these different consistencies would relate to performance capabilities of the tallow.

On fol. 109r of Ms. Fr. 640, turpentine and butter are listed as softening agents for wax, although it is not clear how much should be added to achieve the desired texture. This led us to conclude that tallow is also a softening agent, although one that, it seems, the author-practitioner would prefer not to use.19 In our experiments, a 1:1 mixture of wax and beef tallow yielded a mixture that was soft and released easily from the molds but was prone to breaking and cracking after it hardened. We therefore opted for mixtures that contained smaller amounts of the softening agent than of wax. We made mixtures of wax and turpentine, wax and butter, wax and beef tallow, and finally, wax and pork tallow. (Table 1) Adding two teaspoons of Venice Turpentine to a quarter cup of wax produced a gummy, sticky result that could be carved easily with a stylus. The butter, however, when mixed with the wax, was difficult to carve: the carving tool got stuck in the material, making it difficult to control.20 Despite the misgivings of the manuscript author-practitioner, the tallow mixtures worked particularly well for carving. As mentioned above, a 1:1 mixture was too soft and fragile, but mixtures that included “a little” tallow (in our experiments, two or four teaspoons of tallow to a half cup of wax) resulted in a wax that was more malleable than both the pine rosin and butter mixtures.21 The best mixture was a mixture of wax and pork tallow. Carving tools cut cleanly through the material, making it easy to manipulate and carve, while the overall structure remained solid enough to endure manual manipulation. (Fig. 2)

Ms. Fr. 640 also contains information about hardening wax by mixing a bit of resin (fols. 120r, 160v, among others). Resin, too, can change states, and it appears in several entries in Ms. Fr. 640 (as rousine, resine, or la gemme), for, among other purposes, in making imitation coral, as an ingredient in purpurine, adhering lead to glass, and using resin candles to smoke molds. The material we used to try the wax-resin mixture was sold as “pine gum rosin,” a refined form of resin. In our experiment, we were concerned about mixing the two substances due to the higher melting temperature of the pine gum rosin. Would the wax burn or smoke if poured into melted rosin? We melted the wax first and then dropped in pieces of the rosin. The rosin, like an ice cube in cool water, slowly dissolved and became more gelatinous. The resulting mixture once hardened was resistant to the impression of the stylus and thus more difficult to carve.

The softness and hardness of wax and its ability to change from one state to another, and to take on different appearances was often remarked upon by early modern artisans. Cellini, for example, notes that the seasons affect the conditions of wax, and the temperature of the workshop could determine how the material responded to the craftperson’s hands and tools.22 On fols. 150v–151r, Ms. Fr. 640 contains a process by which charcoal and white wax is mixed to give the mixture particular properties ideal in lost wax casting, and the manuscript recommends for a “Very strong wax” (fol. 150r) to mix wax, rosin, and Armenian bol. Hugh Plat also mentions the mixture of wax and charcoal: “Note also that you must first cast all your curious patternes in yellow wax tempered with the fine powder of smale cole.”23 Plat hardens wax by adding resin to it,24 and mixes in red ocher to color the wax, which makes the pattern more visible.25 Biringuccio mentions adding Grecian pitch or “ship’s tar” to wax,26 while both Biringuccio and Cellini write about the benefits of mixing white lead with wax to soften the wax.27 The remarkable ability of both wax and tallow to easily transform and combine with other substances made them useful as well as symbolically important in the early modern workshop and in early modern culture more generally, where much evidence of a fascination with transformation can be found in early modern literature and other realms of cultural production.
