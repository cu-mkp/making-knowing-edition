What is Or Mat in Ms. Fr. 640?

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/dv6y-7c72
← Back to Essays

Abstract

The term or mat occurs several times in Ms. Fr. 640, often alongside mentions of gold, gilding, and gold color. This essay reconstructs a recipe on fol. 66r, “Gilding molding for panels with or mat,” to flesh out the semantic richness of or mat in relation to the manuscript, contemporary artisanal and archival sources, modern conservation literature, museum objects, and the strange etymological development of the term. Ultimately, this essay argues that or mat should be considered neither as a material nor as a technique exclusively. Rather, early modern conceptual understandings of the term seem to point to the inextricability of the optical effects of the material from the processes of its making.

Introduction

Describing practice was likely a difficult task for any early modern artisan. It entailed the simplification of a complex process, one that might involve minute adjustments, numerous trials, and unstable variables into a single linear narrative. Practitioners who took to writing were acutely aware of the ambiguity that any translation from the realm of practice to that of written words engendered. Taking into account the variability of techniques among artisans, the distortion inherent in the translation of practice into written text becomes even more pronounced.

While the meaning of certain terms can evolve alongside the processes they describe, others can develop quite independent lives from their original meanings. This appears to be the case with or mat, a concept we encounter in early modern gold and gilding recipes as well as in a particularly detailed entry on fol. 66r of BnF Ms. Fr 640.1 The term or mat appears simultaneously to denote “matte gold,” the optical effect caused by an unburnished gilded surface, and the process of “mordant gilding,” a technique that uses an oil-based, often golden colored mordant2 upon which gold leaf is applied, followed by a layer of varnish. Due to this ambiguity, it is often unclear whether the term refers to a process (gold leaf application onto a still wet or tacky mordant) or to gold metal’s éclat—its highly valued lustrous effect, which can be enhanced with burnishing but, in the case of or mat, is an unburnished finish created on the gold surface of mordant gilded substrates. This ambiguity exemplifies not only the difficult translation of process into words but also the disparate definitions and connotations that a single concept can acquire over time.

Scholars and conservators seem divided on how to define or mat, but all agree that it involves gilding. In the early modern period, there were four primary techniques used for gilding: burnished leaf (or water gilding), flat unburnished leaf (or mordant gilding), gilded applied relief (or mordant gilding on a raised or molded surface), and shell gold3 (which used ground gold leaf suspended in a medium and applied as paint).4 Doris Oltrogge, conservator at the Cologne Institute of Conservation Sciences, suggests that or mat refers to the process known as mordant gilding and that the translation into “matte gold,” suggesting a matte or dull finish, is a misattribution or incorrect translation.5 Jill Dunkerton, paintings conservator at the National Gallery in London, agrees that or mat refers to a gilding technique, but she suggests that a matte or dull finish is indeed produced with mordant gilding, since the gold leaf laid on the oil mordant cannot be burnished (i.e., polished to produce a shine) without damaging the metal’s surface.6 Dunkerton also states that mordant gilding generally involves the use of yellow ocher, red lead, linseed oil, and sometimes varnish.7 Notably, these ingredients are all present in the Ms. Fr. 640 recipe.

The or mat recipe on fol. 66r of Ms. Fr. 640 exemplifies a conspicuously ambiguous use of or mat. This is already apparent in its title, “Gilding molding for panels with or mat” (Dorer mouleure de tableaulx dor mat), implying at first sight that or mat refers to some substance or material, probably a thin gold metal leaf, or perhaps indicating the use of an or mat technique or process to gild a molded painting frame. To complicate matters even further, the bizarre etymologies that emerge from research into or mat recipes suggest that this concept also gained a range of connotations that seem at first to be only remotely related to materials or processes.

This essay attempts to illuminate the meaning(s) of or mat in Ms. Fr. 640 with close reading, and practice- and object-based research. It explores two primary questions: what is or mat, in terms of materials and techniques, and what was it used for? To begin, I first show what the term or mat meant in the context of Ms. Fr. 640 and to its author-practitioner, first through an in-depth analysis of the folio with the recipe for or mat and its extensive marginal annotations, and then by contextualizing the term in the manuscript as a whole. Next, through a recipe genealogy, I compare the recipe on fol. 66r to contemporary and earlier sources, and I explore the meaning of or mat in dictionaries, encyclopedias, and early modern documents, tracing the etymology of the term. Finally, I examine the use and optical effect of or mat by comparing objects with mordant gilding in museum collections. This investigation of the intriguing concept of or mat also touches on the broader question of the apparent inextricability of histories of materials and their properties from histories of techniques. I ultimately argue that or mat was understood within an early modern conceptual system that entwined material and process, and it is unproductive and historically fraught for us to consider one apart from the other.

What is or mat?

Reading and Reconstructing Fol. 66r

The difficulty of writing down an artisanal process is illustrated on fol. 66r (Fig. 1). The density of the text in the margins demonstrates the need to explain, rephrase, and revise the linear narrative provided under the heading “Gilding molding for panels with or mat.” The recipe begins near the bottom of the page and terminates in an asterisk, and it continues with the uppermost marginal note in the left margin, which begins with a matching asterisk. The recipe continues down the margin, skipping over a marginal note that belongs to the entry “Colors in oil that are imbibed” (i.e., “On canvas & in distemper, one gilds with bole & honey & a little garlic juice”), and when there is no more room in the left margin of fol. 66r, it picks up again in the upper left margin of fol. 66v, where it finally ends with a horizontal line to underscore its bounds. Yet there is more. An interlinear note positioned between the heading and the body text on fol. 66r, and which ends in another asterisk, directs the reader to the following page (fol. 66v), where, below the horizontal line in the left margin, the discussion picks up again at a note that begins with a corresponding asterisk. It seems likely, in fact, that this latter set of additions may have been composed before the former.

The central text of the recipe is written in a small, cramped script, which suggests that it was added to the page after it had already been completed.8 That said, despite its status as a later addition, the author-practitioner’s treatment of the or mat recipe is anything but minor. The main text of the recipe, in line with the other four recipes on the page, describes the gilding method used by les flaments. Rather than instructing the reader, it describes in the third person how “the Flemish” apply a layer of distempered glue and lamp black on “the molding.” The recipe does not specify what sort of molding this is, but it seems to refer to the moldings of the wooden frame of a painting.9 The recipe continues with the observation that, after the lamp black layer has dried, the moresque ornamentations are polished and then painted by the Flemish artisans with a mixture of pigments bound together in huile gras (“fatty oil”), which, the author-practitioner notes, is the thickened oil that the Flemish used to clean paint brushes.10 They then apply gold leaf on top of these painted ornamentations and varnish the entire surface, gold leaf and lamp black together, making it appear that “all this black is painted in oil.” At the bottom of the recipe text, however, the author-practitioner cautions against the direct application of the gold onto the freshly painted moldings, since the process is imperfect and the gold leaf can attach to surfaces other than the moresque ornamentations, a problem that can be solved by allowing more time to dry, as he notes in the margin.

The author-practitioner calls for painting the moresques “with minium, massicot [avecq du minium du massicot], & ocre de ru [...] because the three aforesaid colors are gripping & dry immediately.” He later squeezed the words “and a little yellow ocher” between the recipe’s lines, above the list of pigments. The French phrasing avecq du minium du massicot is ambiguous. First, it could indicate that the Flemish use both minium and massicot to create a gold color, as reflected in the Making and Knowing Project’s translation. Alternatively, since minium (more commonly known as red lead) is produced through the heating of massicot (yellow lead), “avecq du minium du massicot” could mean to use red minium derived from bright yellow massicot, rather than using both color pigments, minium and massicot, and thus excluding massicot completely from the gold color recipe.11 Another ambiguity is the meaning of ocre de ru. Recent scholarship sheds light on this point. Jo Kirby suggests that “oker de rowse” or “de rux” might have been a brownish-yellow color.12 R. D. Harley agrees, writing that it was used as a hair color in painting and cites Hilliard as saying that it was used for yellow shading, which suggests that it was a darker yellow, perhaps a brown shade.13

At the end of the main text of the recipe is a note, “Go back to the top to *” (Retourne hault a ce *), that directs the reader to the margin at the top of fol. 66r. Interestingly, this long note, consisting of seven dense paragraphs running parallel to the recipes from top to bottom and even continuing onto the verso of the folio (fol. 66v), presents a marked change from a descriptive to an instructional voice, perhaps indicating that the author-practitioner had since performed the experiment.14 In this marginal note, the author-practitioner again lists the ingredients for the colored underlayer, here including yellow ocher along with the other three pigments, and without specifying any amounts. Having removed the qualifier “un peu” in this second iteration of the recipe, we may ask whether he now recommends using more yellow ocher than the Flemish do, or an amount more comparable to that of the other three pigments.

In the marginal note, the author practitioner also specifies that the gold leaf is not applied immediately after the underlayer has been painted; rather, one should wait one day before gilding.15 The gilding should then be allowed to set for another day, at which point “Flanders varnish” mixed with spirits should be applied to the entire frame, enhancing the gold color and changing the optical effect of the glue-based black paint to make it look like oil paint. Below this, the author-practitioner continues with another short note about varnish. He observes that the varnish will increase (augmentera) the color of the gold and notes the length of time required for the varnish layer to dry: one hour. He then observes that the varnish makes the or mat water-resistant, provided one rubs the surface after waiting eight or ten days for the varnish to dry. The author-practitioner specifies using Flanders varnish that is mixed with spirits to make it more dessicative.16

Just below the note on varnish, the author-practitioner clarifies the type of “fatty” or “thickened” oil (huile gras) to be used for “gold color” (or couleur).17 While in the main text, the author-practitioner had noted that the Flemish use the thickened oil in which they clean their oil brushes, here he recommends using oil that has been thickened in the sun (i.e., stand oil) instead. He writes that this stand oil will not tarnish the final gold leaf layer whereas the oil made from cleaning brushes will, due to the presence of verdigris in the mixture.18

At the bottom of the marginal note on fol. 66r includes a number of seemingly miscellaneous observations. These are squeezed into the final inches of the folio’s margin, and this limited space may account for the proximity of these disparate observations. First, the author-practitioner writes that the mordant layer should not be touched with one’s fingers or else the gold will not adhere to the surface.19 Presumably one would touch the mordant in order to determine its tackiness and ensure that it is dry enough to be gilded upon. In a subsequent note on fol. 66v, the author-practitioner specifies that the reader can determine the appropriate tackiness of the mordant by breathing on it. If one’s breath condenses on the surface of the mordant, it is ready for the gold leaf to be applied.20

The author-practitioner then writes two further notes that compare this or mat technique to other gilding methods. First, he compares the water resistance of or moulu to that of or mat.21 Then he advises that one should not burnish the gold that has been applied to an oil base, a rather common practice in gilding techniques, otherwise the gold leaf surface will be damaged. Next he instructs the reader to collect the baves & petites lambeaulx (“protrusions & small flakes”) that are removed by the cotton when the gold is dry. He then finishes this paragraph on “gold color” writing, “it is more beautiful when it is a little thick, but in France they beat it too delicately.” Presumably the first “it” refers to the visual effect of the or mat, and the second “it” refers to the gold leaf that is a little thick.22

The last two notes on fol. 66r return to the tackiness of the mordant. In these notes, the author-practitioner departs from his previous advice to use the tactile quality of the mordant or the visible condensation of one’s breath on the surface to determine the appropriate time to apply the gold. Instead, he writes that one must not gild or mat directly after having applied the gold colored mordant; rather, one should wait one day or one night for the mordant to dry so that it can better grip the gold leaf.23 The recommended waiting time prevents the gold leaf from adhering to the black paint so that it will only attach itself to the now tacky mordant, since the oil-based mordant dries much more slowly than the glue distemper used for the black background. Having run out of space on fol. 66r, the author-practitioner continues at the top of fol. 66v, where he specifies that when the gold colored mordant begins to adhere (gripper), this is a sign that it will be ready for gilding in ten or twelve hours.

Finally, it appears that the author-practitioner returned to the recipe a third time. Just beneath the heading of the or mat recipe on fol. 66r, there is a directive note: “see the other side of this folio this mark: *” (voy de laultre part de ce foeillet ceste marque *), which sends the reader to the margin of the folio’s verso (i.e., fol. 66v). The author distinguishes this new note from the long marginal note that had continued from fol. 66r by drawing a line above the new note marked with an asterisk mark. This new note on fol. 66v introduces two new ingredients to the or mat recipe, instructing that in addition to stand oil (which he again specifies is not the kind used to clean brushes), one can also use a little varnish or ceruse (lead white) for the mordant. For his final note, the author-practitioner compares or mat to burnished gold, saying that or mat can be made quickly and lasts longer, it is water resistant, and is scarcely less beautiful than burnished gold.

Experiment

Due to the numerous ambiguities in the or mat recipe, I designed my experiments to test a variety of variables. First, I wanted to test the optimal combination of the pigments listed in the recipe. Would the best gold color be produced using all four pigments? Which pigments should be used in greatest quantities, and which should be used sparingly? I designed three combinations for the experiment, labeled T1, T2, and T3, varying the proportions of the pigments in each (Fig. 2).24 Second, the drying time seemed essential to the setting process of or mat gilding, so I designed an experiment with two trials: one that allowed the mordant layer to dry for twenty-four hours, and another in which it dried for ninety-six hours.25 Third, I wanted to see the effect of the varnish not only on the gold but also on the gold color. I hypothesized that the gold color of the mordant layer would make flaws or losses to the gold leaf less apparent. I hoped to determine whether the varnish layer contributed to this effect.

First, I experimented with various combinations of red lead (minium) and yellow lead (massicot) to determine the meaning of avecq du minium du massicot. It became immediately apparent that the second reading of the phrase, which excluded the use of yellow lead and specified that the red lead was derived from the yellow, was incorrect. The red lead was far too bright an orange without the yellow lead to temper it into a more subdued golden color (Fig. 3). Further, a combination with more yellow ocher (which already had a color quite close to the desired gold hue) and little to no yellow lead appeared promising on the clear, glass mixing plate; however, when applied to the lamp black distemper layer, the yellow ocher-based gold color appeared to be almost fully absorbed by the black underlayer. After I had established that the opaque property of yellow lead (massicot) was essential to achieving a gold colored mordant, I started to experiment with the addition of red lead. In the second and third combinations for the gold color, I increased the amount of yellow ocher to temper the bright yellow of the massicot, and included (4C) and excluded (4B) red lead from the mixture. While in both cases, the golden color improved with the addition of more yellow ocher, it was obvious that the red lead could not be excluded entirely, since it gave a necessary warmth and opacity to the golden color (Fig. 4). This is evident when comparing the appearance of a gold mordant produced with and without red lead (Fig. 5). The combinations without red or yellow lead were far more transparent and required additional coats to achieve the same amount of coverage as the mixture that included lead-based pigments. Thus, it became clear that the use of both red lead and yellow lead was essential for or mat gilding. Moreover, the consistency of the paint mattered as well. Thicker mixtures with lead-based pigments achieved much better coverage than thinner ones.

This experimental observation directly coincides with an observation made by the author-practitioner on fol. 66r. Just above the heading of the or mat recipe, at the conclusion of an entry entitled “Colors in oil that are imbibed,” he writes, “it is necessary that gold color be laid down thick, for if it were clear [cler], it would be imbibed & would run.” In this note, he may be referring to the optical effect caused by painting a gold colored mordant on a lampblack surface (Fig. 4). The vibrancy of the color is absorbed by the dark lamp black layer. In the first trial I performed, only one layer of the mordant was applied. As a result the mordant dripped down the panel and became translucent or cler. This concluding note to the entry “Colors in oil that are imbibed” also gives us a clue as to why the author-practitioner included the or mat recipe on fol. 66r, squeezing it into the bottom and margins of the page: he seems to have seen a connection between the two final entries. The concluding observation to “Colors in oil” about how gold color is imbibed bridges from this entry to the more in-depth discussion of gold color in “Gilding molding for panels with or mat” below it.

After twenty-four hours, I gilded the first mordant layer (Fig. 6). The mordant was slightly tacky to the touch, and moisture condensed on the surface of the mordant when I breathed on it. The gold leaf laid relatively flat on the surface, with a fair amount of crinkling. After ninety-six hours, I examined the gilding, and the irregular surface of the gold leaf seemed to have smoothed itself out, while the color had taken on a slightly orange or golden tone (Fig. 7). The second and third mordant layers were gilded after ninety-six hours, with little difference from the twenty-four-hour trial. In both trials the mordant was less tacky and no moisture collected on the surface when I breathed on it. However, the gold leaf still adhered to this drier mordant, though with a bit more crinkling. Observed twenty-four hours later, this gilding had also acquired an orange tone. This change in the color causes the gold leaf to tend towards the color of the gold mordant, perhaps contributing to the diminished visibility of losses in the gold leaf or flaws in its application.

I next applied turpentine varnish, which the author-practitioner seems to treat as an all-purpose varnish, to one half of the gold leaf and gold color swatches.26 The turpentine varnish had an immediate and significant effect on the appearance of the gold leaf. The irregularities of the unburnished gold leaf became much less pronounced after applying a single layer of the slightly viscous varnish, thereby enhancing the gold (Fig. 8). The gold leaf also appeared to take on a darker color after the varnish was applied (Fig. 9). This darker color seemed to bring the gold leaf closer to the color of the gold mordant layer. Because of this similarity of color, damage to the gold leaf became less apparent, a feature that could also be considered to “increase” the color of gold.27

Unexpectedly, applying the varnish enabled me also to observe the strength of the oil mordant. The secure “grip” of the mordant became evident, as it stopped the gold leaf from being removed as easily as the gold leaf that I had applied with a water size in a problematic comparative trial (Fig. 10).28 This “grip” also helps to explain why the unburnished surface smoothed itself out over time. Whereas the water-based sizing allows for slight movement as the gold leaf smooths itself out and settles on the surface, the tacky oil mordant seems to prevent the gold from laying smooth on the surface when first applied, despite the care with which the gold leaf is laid, and it smoothes itself out at a much slower pace.

Inadvertently, the phrase baves & petits lambeaulx was also clarified after the application of the varnish. The excess flakes of gold leaf that I had not removed with the cotton became suspended in the varnish and speckled the lampblack panel (Fig. 9). Taking this into consideration, the term baves becomes clearer, referring to the gold flakes that seem to “dribble” through the varnish on the black panel.29

Manuscript Context

The manuscript context gives us insights into the author-practitioner’s distinction between the application of gold color and application of gold leaf, both indicated by the term dorer. On fol. 66r, he writes:

One ought not to gild with or mat after with having gilded [dorer dor mat apres avecq avoyr dore]. But wait one day or one night, But take heed not to and when it is as if dry, it grips the gold. Next, one varnishes.

The author-practitioner uses the verb dorer in two ways in this sentence. First, he uses it to refer to the gold leaf of or mat, and subsequently, he uses it to refer to the gold color upon which the leaf is applied. This reuse of dorer exposes an ambiguity, or flexibility, in its denotation, as it refers variously to gilding, gold leaf, and gold color.30 Randle Cotgrave’s 1611 French-English dictionary provides little insight into the particular usage of these terms. Cotgrave defines the term dorer as “to gild, or doe over with gold;” this infinitive form of the verb seems to refer to the act of applying a leaf of gold, but it could also mean to paint over with a gold color.31 In addition, Cotgrave gives the word doré(e) three distinct definitions: “gilt over” is simply the adjectival form of a surface laid with gold leaf; “of gold” could refer either to an object made of gold metal or another material of gold color; and “faire, beautifull, brightly shining” lacks any direct relation to the metal gold.32

Or mat appears in four different recipes in Ms. Fr. 640. First, the author-practitioner mentions it very briefly in his recipe “For laying down and seating burnished gold and giving red or green or blue” on folio 6r. In the original French title, he forgoes the use of either term, dorer or dore, instead opting for the more cumbersome Pour poser et assoir dor bruny. In the first few lines of the text, he mentions the suitability of ceruse for or mat and introduces the adjective dorees (“gilded”), but he only refers to the application (aplique), not the gilding, of or mat. Throughout the rest of the recipe when referring to burnished gold, he alternates between the verbs dorer and apliquer.

On fol. 65r, in the entry “Flesh color” (Carnation), the author-practitioner mentions or mat in a marginal note, here using the term dorer to refer to applying or mat. Significantly, the subsequent marginal note discusses the importance of “fatty colors” such as minium and massicot and the utility of “fatty walnut oil” for making “gold color” (or couleur). The two terms are paired again on fol. 100r, where or mat is mentioned in an entry titled “Gold color.” The recipe lists the ingredients for the color of the mordant layer but makes no explicit reference to gold leaf application.

The recipe on fol. 99v, “Gilding your moldings for panels without gold,” (“Dorer ta moleures de tableaux sans or”) most clearly displays the ambiguity of the author-practitioner’s language, using the word dorer to refer to the application of gold color without any gold leaf involved at all. The significance is even more pronounced when compared to the usage in the recipe “Color of gold without gold on silver” (Couleur dor sans or sur largent) on fol. 29v, which one might think is closer to gilding in its application of a metal foil but here refers to gold color without gold leaf application.

Historical Context

The author-practitioner’s classification of this recipe as Flemish is significant for our understanding of the technique. The technique was used not only in frame decoration but also in oil painting and fabric printing, with variations appearing in a number of Netherlandish and German sources.33 By the end of the fifteenth century, the term or met has become associated with this technique in guild statutes in the Low Countries, while or de couleur and aurum madide could be found in other contemporary sources referring to gilding on an oil mordant. Statutes from Antwerp’s Guild of St Luke, dated November 9, 1470, indicate that mordant gilding, along with many other gilding methods, was well established by the late fifteenth century at least.34 One statute actually specifies that or met could not be laid without first applying the gold-colored ground.35 The guild statutes of Tournai from 1480 detail the various types of gold available to painters and glass makers and carefully delineate which golds should be used for which purposes.36

A late fifteenth-century German recipe refers to gilding on an oil mordant as aurum madide, which roughly translates to “wet gold” and alludes to the tacky or slightly wet texture of the mordant necessary to adhere the gold:

For laying down wet gold: Take chalk and saffron in equal amounts, and take as much as these two are in weight of burnt ocher. And rub strongly together with beaten olive oil on a stone slab. After, write with this same oil, and place your gold on top.37

Though it is unclear when madide transformed into mat, the Oxford English Dictionary traces the etymology of “mordant” in the English language through French (mordant) and Italian (mordente) to post-classical Latin (mordans).38 The Latin stem mord- means “to bite, or nip”; in this context, it likely refers to the substance’s ability to grip other materials. Perhaps the two root words became confused at some stage of development, or the biting root was attributed to the technique upon observation of the way the oil gold clings to the oil layer.

In Il libro dell’arte, Cennino Cennini includes a short section on mordant gilding, notably followed by a small group of varnish recipes. The mordant, as described by Cennini, includes a heat-bodied oil mixed with lead white and verdigris. Among the several discrepancies between the recipe in Ms. Fr. 640 and Cennini’s is the latter’s use of very thin gold leaf for mordant gilding, whereas the author-practitioner recommends the use of a thicker leaf. In addition, Cennini recommends using one’s fingers to apply the gold leaf, while the author-practitioner specifically cautions against this technique lest it spoil the gilding. Cennini makes no indication of a gold color for this mordant. Rather, he describes the importance of verdigris for the drying time of the mordant; one imagines that this would color the mordant a bluish-green.39 The author-practitioner, on the other hand, advises against using verdigris, writing that with time it tarnishes the gold leaf.

By the seventeenth century, even more ambiguity surrounded the mordant gilding technique. The recipe compilation of Swiss physician Sir Théodore Turquet de Mayerne includes instructions to prepare “oil for applying gold leaf.”40 This recipe closely resembles that on fol. 66r of Ms. Fr. 640. Both include a heat-bodied oil, but De Mayerne specifies linseed oil with the addition of garlic, lead white, and an onion. Yellow ocher and umber are likely included to create the gold color and De Mayerne specifies that they be bound in the linseed oil, applied to the surface, and that the gold leaf be added to it when it is tacky to the touch. Later, in his recipe simply titled “Or Couleur” for the trim of a picture frame, De Mayerne recommends using “oily paint like the residues from brush cleaning” for the base coat. He continues:

Note, if you want to do your layout with a gold color on black or another dark paint, first the black should be mixed with glue and applied, and on that do your leaf work with oil. Let it dry as above, lay on the gold, remove the excess and finally varnish [the whole thing]. Then the black will look like ebony. Common varnish with a little spikenard oil for drying [is suitable for this].41

This description corresponds closely to the recipe in Ms. Fr. 640, even mentioning the black coat prepared with glue, with which the author-practitioner begins his recipe.

The Brussels manuscript, dated 1635, mentions several recipes for mordant gilding (or de couleur).42 The first describes a process by which the residue from brushes is boiled and strained through a cloth to produce a yellow color, to which are added yellow ocher and “coarse” massicot and minium if the yellow color needs to be enhanced. The second calls for armenian bole ground with linseed or nut oil. The third includes yellow ocher and a little lead white, which the recipe asserts will make the yellow ocher “more similar to gold in color.” Much like the recipe on fol. 66r, the Brussels manuscript recommends fatty oil (huile gras) to be used for or de couleur and instructs the reader to wait the “appropriate length of time” before applying the gold. As seen in Ms. Fr. 640, the Brussels manuscript advises waiting before applying varnish to the gold:

Note, that the or couleur must be nearly dry before the gold is laid on it, for otherwise the gold would become dull without any brilliance or splendour.43

The Brussels manuscript includes an intriguing definition of or mat: the term mat is derived from the Italian word meaning silly, “so that ‘or mat’ is silly gold, without brilliancy and splendor.”44 The manuscript also traces the term’s roots to the French matois or a “do-nothing fool,” presumably because the gold does not shine as it should, and also to the Indian or Persian term Schamate, meaning “the king is dead,” “so that ‘or mat’ is a dead or dull gold.”45 Cotgrave provides a similar definition, defining mat as “deaded, mated, amated, quelled, subdued, overcome; also as Flaccide,” with a supplementary definition of or mat as “unpolished gold.”46

These rather bizarre etymological definitions betray the semantic ambiguity that the mordant gilding technique had acquired by the early seventeenth century. It is unclear to the compiler of the Brussels manuscript how this technique acquired the name or mat or how this name is related to or de couleur, and he attempts to reconcile this confusion by defining or mat as the optical effect that is achieved through the process called or de couleur. Judging from the Brussels manuscript and Cotrgrave’s attributions of or mat, the term could be related to the Latin mat, referring to the “unripe” quality of the gold. Base metals were believed to ripen into gold and, though doubtful, perhaps mat alludes to the less lustrous appearance of impure gold. The associations of mat with death could also be due to an incorrect association with the Latin stem mort- meaning “dead.”

What was or mat used for?

Object comparisons

The decoration of frames changed over time according to available materials and techniques but also tastes. Before the middle of the fifteenth century, frames were painted in bright colors, most often red; later, black frames, often with gilded decorations, became popular.47 From the recipe title in Ms. Fr. 640, “Gilding molding for panels with or mat,” it would seem that the author-practitioner is describing an applied relief technique of mordant gilding. The accurate description of the materials and processes involved, the text’s alignment with recipes from Flemish manuscripts discussed above, along with the attribution of the technique to the Flemish suggest that the author-practitioner may have drawn the recipe from a Flemish written source, acquired it directly from a Flemish artisan, or perhaps studied with a Flemish-trained artisan himself.

A number of sixteenth-century Netherlandish examples of or mat ornamentation on black frames survive, and these frames give us an idea of the desired visual effect of the author-practitioner’s or mat recipe. The frames produced by the workshops of the Claeissens family of Bruges and the Francken family of Antwerp offer good examples.48 The frame surrounding the Crucifixion attributed to Frans II Francken and dated between 1581 and 1642 is a near contemporary example of the sort of gold detailing on a black frame that the author-practitioner describes (Fig. 11, Fig. 12). Another example frames a Banquet by Anthonis Claeissens, dated 1574 (Fig. 13). Claeissens was a member of the Bruges Guild of St Luke from 1575, and he served multiple terms as dean of the guild.49 If the guild statutes in Bruges were similar to those of Antwerp and Tournai discussed above, it seems likely that Claeissens would have used an or met gilding technique on a gold-colored ground much like the one described by the author-practitioner.

In the recipe on fol. 66r, the author-practitioner refers to the gold patterning in or mat as “moresque” (moresque). This term, in use from at least the fifteenth century in France, came to designate a particular kind of ornamental design inspired by Islamic metalwork (Fig. 14).50 Moresques, however, were by no means confined to French decorative arts. By the mid-sixteenth century, they were in use across Europe, with model patterns circulating in print. Thus, the development of ornamental designs of geometric, sinuous, or interlaced line took on an independence from any originary context in Islamic art, and the author-practitioner’s familiarity with the term “moresque” and the patterns it came to designate need not be related to direct contact with Islamic arts and artisans per se.

Accordingly, despite the author-practitioner’s designation of the or mat recipe as Flemish, the use of ornamental gold patterns on black frames is attested well outside of the Low Countries. One such frame, held at the Victoria and Albert Museum (Fig. 15), is believed to have been created in Venice when this style of frame was most in vogue (roughly 1580–1620). It is noteworthy that this vogue for light on dark decorative framing was also visible in contemporary Limoges enamels, which often display framing bands of light-colored grotesque ornament—an ornamental mode historically associated with moresques and arabesques—against dark backgrounds (Fig. 16).51 Scientific analysis indicates that a layer of animal skin glue was used in the black paint layer, just as described in Ms. Fr. 640 and the recipe collected by De Mayerne.52 The use of the or mat technique in Venice suggests that this method could have been available to the author-practitioner by means other than direct contact with Flemish artists. It also suggests some level of standard practice across the workshops of Europe.

While ornate black frames with gold decoration seem to have been produced across Europe, they are often associated with the workshop of Corneille de Lyon (1500–1575). Also known as Corneille de La Haye, this Netherlandish portraitist to the European nobility received his early training in the Low Countries and was in Lyon by the year 1533, where he would establish a highly successful workshop.53 The artist’s aedicular frames, often accented by marble inlay, give a monumentality to their small portraits and are similar in style to the V&A example.

The possibility that the author-practitioner might have been familiar with either the work or person of Corneille de Lyon is tantalizing.54 Corneille’s showroom, well-trafficked by elite patrons and artists, garnered the praise of the Venetian ambassador to France, while his active workshop attracted the talents of foreign artists such as Jan van der Straet (Stradanus) of Bruges.55 The author-practitioner, who was not likely a native of Toulouse,56 travelled within France and perhaps visited the mercantile city of Lyon. It is also possible, though admittedly improbable, that the author-practitioner studied under the portrait painter. Such contact with a Netherlandish master would be one way to account for the author-practitioner’s frequent references to the techniques of Flemish artists. Of course, more research is necessary to make any such claim.

Regardless, one might imagine the author-practitioner either observing a Flemish painter who was decorating a frame using the or mat technique or in conversation with such an artist. The author-practitioner could have recorded the process in the manuscript and later, upon trying the technique himself, added the numerous marginal notes to the recipe, clarifying and refining the practice he had previously encountered second hand.

Conclusion

Well before the early modern period, artists, artisans, goldsmiths, and alchemists have all ventured, by various means, to imitate, manipulate, and create gold. The complex history of gold and its value inevitably meant that multiple terms with conflicting definitions and connotations developed to describe gold and gold coloring processes. Notably, the author-practitioner does not use the word imitation or counterfeit (contrefaict) to describe any of the gold color recipes in the manuscript.57 This suggests that he does not consider gold colors, or couleur, couleur dor, la couleur de lor, to be fakes or imitations of gold.58 Rather, it seems gold color and gold metal are distinct yet closely linked ontological entities. Ms. Fr. 640 illustrates the complexity of the author-practitioner’s relationship to the materiality of gold. He refers to gold over 150 times in multiple forms including gold color, gold leaf, and gold metal in the context of metal casting.59

The ambiguity of the language surrounding gold, as seen through the lens of or mat, makes evident the complexity of the problem. Philological analysis of written techniques provides an elaborate explanation for what the term or mat means. Likewise, reconstruction of the technique provides insight into the sources of these ambiguities but does not clearly distinguish process from material. What becomes clear is that the histories of the process for and the materials of or mat are inextricable from each other, and this fact must be considered before any attempt to translate or decipher it and similar terms.60

Consideration of these ambiguities as indications of a complex and interrelated conceptual system of understanding and interacting with gold helps to circumvent the unhelpful dichotomy between material and process. Or mat does not simply refer to “mordant gilding” or “matte-finished gold,” but rather to a multivalent system of layered meaning and material—that is, meanings inextricable from material—that are best understood in relation to each other. In the case of or mat, this layering begins with the surface treatment of the frame, layered with animal skin glue and lamp black which provides a stark contrast to the gold, both in color, due to its dark opacity, and in finish, due to its gloss after varnish application. Next is the mordant layer, which at once imitates gold in color and also contains dessicative materials such as the heat-bodied oil and lead-based pigments which make the gold grip onto the surface. The gold leaf is the third layer which, despite its thickness, acquires a bright color from the gold color of the mordant and also diffusely scatters light due to its uneven, unburnished surface texture, caused by the dessicative properties of the mordant. The fourth and final layer protects the gold from water damage and tarnishing and offers specific optical properties that add to the gold’s brilliance and its éclat.

While in its modern translation or mat might refer to any gold surface that has a matte finish, I would suggest that for the early modern artisan, the author-practitioner included, this was not in fact the case. Or mat referred to the optical effect created through this specific mordant gilding technique, and all of the steps and materials involved contributed to the substance-process of or mat. The material’s optical effect and the process of creating it can only be understood within a system that does not distinguish the one from the other. It is when an attempt is made to rationalize the two, separately and distinctly, that “wild philologies” occur.61
