Molding a Rose

Abstract

Of the many entries in Ms. Fr. 640 that contain instructions and observations on the lifecasting of plants and animals, those on fols. 155r-v deal with molding and casting a rose. Like other lifecasting entries, these appear to be a record of the author-practitioner’s first-hand experience, perhaps even evidence that he was learning as he experimented with various plants. This essay follows the author-practitioner’s instructions for molding and casting a rose, an endeavor that required close and repeated reading and trying of many of the manuscript’s other entries on the lifecasting of plants and animals.

The recipes “Rose” and “Molding a rose” (BnF Ms. Fr. fols. 155v and 155r) give advice on how to cast a rose by making a mold for each of the flower’s components and joining the resulting parts.1 This essay recounts our reconstruction of techniques for the lifecasting of a rose, in which we experimented with both one-piece and two-piece molds (the latter can be reused to produce multiple copies). A puzzle-like reconstruction of information, based on close and iterative reading of Ms. Fr. 640 as a whole, was necessary to carry out our experiments. Our efforts revealed the significance and extent of the author-practitioner’s experience with and interest in lifecasting techniques.

To reconstruct the procedure from the text, we had to locate information on the system of gates (gect), vents (souspirails), and wax “veins” (i.e., sprues, filons de cire) necessary for both molds, which we did by correlating the clues on fols. 155r and 155v with the instructions more explicitly presented in other Ms. Fr. 640 recipes for lifecasting. We found molding techniques for plants in entries related more generally to casting flowers on fols. 117r and 145v. The two-part mold process is described on fol. 159v in a recipe related to lifecasting a crayfish. Drawings in the margins of fols. 142v and 124v illustrate the system of veins, vents, and gates needed to mold crayfish and lizards; these were helpful in constructing a two-part mold used to cast the leaves of the rose. Several of these recipes will be discussed below.

Our work not only resulted in a beautiful rose cast (Fig. 1), but also in a reconstruction of the author-practitioner’s evolving thoughts on this process as he self-corrects during the course of the activities he recorded in the manuscript. We argue that this self-reflection reveals him to be continuously learning through his own experiments, seemingly motivated by the aspiration both to imitate nature and to transform an ephemeral thing into a tangible and fixed artistic creation that will remain unchanged. His discussion of the gates, veins, and vents in the mold construction reveals his attitude to lifecasting as a process requiring great delicacy, and his account as a whole stresses the need for repetition and self-correction in order to gain mastery. We argue that this self-correction can be seen over the sequence of the folios of the recipe. Detailed instructions and changes to the procedure that the author-practitioner inserts shows that he may be learning through his own making.

Our goals for the reconstruction included trying to understand the author-practitioner’s experimentation with materials in common use, including tin, lead, and fish glue, as well as to reconstruct his experience as he developed his lifecasting expertise.

Assembling and Combining the Texts of the Recipes

On fol. 117r (“A means of molding flowers and plants”) the author-practitioner suggests that it is possible to mold a flower or a herb using a one-piece mold, but on later fols. 155r (“Molding a Rose”) and fol. 155v (“Rose”), he describes a different casting method. Because a rose has several components (flower, stem, leaves, and buds), the author-practitioner suggests making a separate mold for each part, leaving “little tips” of the small branches at the stem intact in order to facilitate joining the pieces after the casting process is complete. The reader is instructed to place the flower and its petals as low as possible in the mold, instructions which imply that the mold must be deep and perhaps that the object be oriented in a vertical position. No further instructions are provided regarding this mold. Other recipes in the manuscript must be consulted for additional information.

Fol. 117r (“A means of molding flowers and plants”) describes the use of a one-piece mold. It is made by shaping a clay slab in the shape of a mandore (a type of musical instrument like a lute) surrounded by thick walls. The whole flower is inserted into a “a wax stick pointed like a large peg” which is in turn supported by a thick iron wire inserted through the center of one of the mold walls. In this way, the flower is positioned horizontally without touching the mold walls, surrounded by space on all sides. This airspace is then filled with wet sand (i.e., plaster of Paris mixed with brick powder), that “will be of equal thickness as everywhere & [is] exceed everywhere by the height of two good fingers the top of the such plant.” Once this wet sand is in place, it must set before the external clay walls can be removed. After it has set, it should be dried for a few days, at which point the mold will be hard and dry enough to be put in the oven without cracking. Once in the oven, the original rose is reduced to ashes, and the wax elements melted out. This then allows the liquid molten metal to flow through the now empty “veins” of the former wax sprues and fill the space left by the live rose during the actual casting. If the mold is made in one piece, it can be used only once, since it must be broken to remove the final cast object. If it is designed to separate into two pieces, it can be used again.

In the margin of fol. 117r, two drawings show the flower stem inserted into the wax peg and positioned in the mandore-shaped mold. Similar text and drawings explaining the arrangement of the casting infrastructure are repeated elsewhere in the manuscript, including fol. 124v (“A means to make the gate for small female lizards”), fol. 141r–142r (“Molding a crayfish”), and fol. 145v (“Molding plants and flowers”). On both fols. 117r and 145v, the author-practitioner suggests casting a delicate flower or plant oriented vertically. Additionally, on fol. 117r, he says to “adapt two vent holes with two small sticks” to the sides of the wax peg and on fol. 145v to “pass a thread through the mold” to keep the object upright and in place when the plaster mixture is poured into the mold (Fig. 2).2

By the time the author-practitioner wrote fol. 155r, he appears to have refined his mold making technique, since he adds specific details like how to arrange a system of wax “veins” or sprues that will help the liquid metal flow evenly between the leaves and petals. He also suggests making “a vein of wax” from the back of the first petal to be joined to the main gate, a description corresponding to the wax peg more fully described on fol. 117r. He seems to assume the reader is aware of the instructions and marginal drawings provided on previous folios.

There are some particular hints regarding the difficult casting of the delicate leaves. On fol. 155r the author-practitioner briefly mentions making a two-piece mold that may be reused, while on fol. 155v he specifically recommends using a two-piece mold for the leaves and the buds. On fol. 155r, he also suggests applying a thin layer of melted butter on the outside of the petals to strengthen them during the pouring of the wet sand and placing a thread between each petal in order to separate them during the casting process. In a note, the author-practitioner suggests applying melted butter to the end of the rose stem as well, to make it strong enough to support the weight of the flower and the leaves once the components are joined. For the same reason, he also suggests that the stem be cast in “tinned latten” rather than the softer (and cheaper) tin or lead. Otherwise, on fol 155r, he suggests tin and lead as casting metals. By contrast, on fol. 155v he specifically indicates tin as the medium for stem, flower, and leaves.

Other suggestions in the marginal notes include using eau-de-vie to moisten the rose and oiling the “wax gate” before pouring in the wet sand to diminish the adherence of sand to the casting infrastructure. For cleaning the ashes from the mold, the author-practitioner suggests waiting for the mold to cool down before blowing out the ashes via the small sprues. If the mold is still hot, the ashes will stick to the remnants of wax. After casting, the reader is advised to soak the cast object in water in order to remove any trace of sand from the petals.

The instructions on fol. 155r join those on fol. 155v again when the author-practitioner describes the process of attaching the various parts of the cast rose. The procedure of joining the rose components is in fact the main subject of fol. 155v. Soldering with latten wire is presented as a first option. However, tin “is troublesome to solder” without damaging the cast. The author-practitioner thus proposes a second option: fish glue. Since “because the cast flowers & especially the rose are not beautiful without being painted,” he explains, there is no point in soldering when the glue works just as well and will be hidden by the paint. The substance may also be used to join other elements to the flower (such as for repairing a lifecast fly, or attaching a fly to a plant). Once the components are joined, “white wax mixed with much well-ground ceruse or, even better, lead white” can be melted and placed on the joins with a “small hot point of iron;” this can also be used to fix imperfections such as “little barbs that are in the middle of the rose or the holes of the that may be in any petal.” White lead also creates a white ground for colors and embellishment.3

Reconstruction Experiments

Our experiments in March 2015 followed the two-week expert maker residency in the Making and Knowing Lab of the sculptor and scholar, Andrew Lacey. In our first trial we made one-piece molds for all the components of the rose: the flower, stem, and leaves. For our second experiment, we made a two-piece mold for the leaves with the intention of reusing it. In both cases we decided to solder the rose flower to the stem and use fish glue to fine-tune details of the cast rose, and then apply titanium oxide as a white ground for the painting (time did not permit the latter two steps).4

Sixteenth-century Roses

We made our decision to use a rose as our flower from the titles of the recipes, “Rose” (fol. 155v) and “Molding a Rose” (fol. 155r). We selected a modern commercial rose, a choice that might seem inauthentic, but, through our research, we learned that by the sixteenth century the modern domestic hybrid rose already had been cultivated to its now familiar shape. Leonart Fuchs describes two species of rose in his 1558 L’histoire des plantes: the wild field rose and the domestic rose cultivated in gardens (Fig. 3).5 The wild rose has a single layer of five petals, but various subspecies of the two basic rosa had already been hybridized to obtain different colors, fragrance, and petal formations (Fig. 4). Elsewhere in sixteenth-century literature, illustrations of domestic white roses exist with several layers of petals.6 The author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 does not specify the use of a wild or domestic rose, but the marginal drawing of fol. 155r shows a flower with more than five petals, which led us to choose a multi-layered hybrid for our test subject (Fig. 5). Our modern commercial rose, rich in petals, is thus a relatively authentic representation of a sixteenth-century hybrid rose flower.

Making the Mold

At the beginning of both experiments, we cut the rose blossom and a bunch of leaves from the stem, leaving a small bit of the flower stalk on each to facilitate the joining process (Fig. 6). Then we prepared to cast the three components according to the information collected from fol. 155r. Following the earlier suggestion on fol. 145v, we immersed the flower upside down in a tall glass of eau-de-vie to moisten it before placing it on the clay of the mold.

On the stem and on the back of the petals and leaves, we applied a thin layer of melted butter using a brush. We applied wheat oil to the wax peg and veins of all three molds.7 This ensured a smooth surface for the sand to flow over. The author-practitioner does not specify a type of oil, but he mentions wheat oil in previous recipes involving the casting of delicate things.8 We used melted butter to coat the stem, as we were curious to experiment with it on the thicker parts of the rose.9

Although the author-practitioner suggests using just one thread to separate the petals, we found this too difficult and instead used several short threads, trying to reach the deepest area between adjacent petals (see Fig. 7). In this way, we opened up space that allowed us to apply melted butter to small petals that would have otherwise remained hidden.

To make the system of wax veins or sprues, we connected the outside of the first petal to the main gate (as suggested on fol. 155r), while adding two other thin wax sprues to connect the entire flower to the clay base of the mold.10 We followed a similar procedure for the stem, making a wax sprue that connected the top of the stem to the mold base (Fig. 7). While the flower and stem looked well positioned and stable on the base of the clay, with their stems running through the wax peg and supported on both sides by two wax sticks), the spray of three leaves seemed fragile (Fig. 8). The melted butter on their reverse sides seemed to have made them heavier and more frail than they really were. Moreover, the system of wax veins built to keep them vertical, as described on fol. 117r, failed when the sand was poured into the mold. As soon as the sand touched the base of the mold, the wax peg detached from the clay, the little wax sticks and the sprues (connected as shown in the marginal drawing on fol. 155r) broke, and the leaves were lost within the mold. This failure spurred us to experiment with casting the leaves in a two-part mold. We were encouraged by a remark on fol. 155r to open the molds and clean the ashes out. This implies that the author-practitioner had been using a two-part mold for the leaves, the same method he recommends for lifecasting small animals, such as crayfish, found elsewhere in the manuscript (e.g., fol. 159v).

In our second experiment, we placed the spray of leaves on the base of the mold and built thick clay walls all around.11 We did not use melted butter on the leaves because there was no need to stiffen them in this position. On the contrary, we needed to prevent the clay from attaching to the bottom of the leaves. For this purpose we used eau-de-vie, following the instructions on fol. 112v–113r (“Making the arrangement and disposition of the animal”).

If, after you have placed your animal on your clay cake, you are distracted with other occupations, rub your animal with eau-de-vie, & under the belly, so that it does not dry & attach itself to the earth (fol. 113r).

We brushed eau-de-vie on both sides of the leaves before laying them down on the clay (Fig. 9).

Our next challenge was to construct a system of wax sprues that would connect the leaves as illustrated in the marginal drawing on fol. 155r (Fig. 10). Deviating from the diagram, however, we did not make the two souspirails (vents) right away, because, according to fol. 155r, the vents should be made after the object being cast had been burnt out: “You them can also mold well the leaves of rose bushes, strawberry plants, & similar things that are flat & can be flattened without spoiling them with two gates, for opening your mold when it is reheated, & cleaning the ashes from it, All and making vents & several gates.” Here, the author-practitioner seems to suggest incising the vents directly into the mold after opening it and cleaning out the ashes (Fig. 11).12 Finally, we cut registration marks into the wall of the mold next to the gate to ensure we could reassemble it correctly (Fig. 12).

Of the several kinds of sand suggested for casting in Ms. Fr. 640, the author-practitioner specifies one type on fol. 159v (“Carnation”), writing that when it comes to molding flowers and delicate things “one needs very thin sand & to blow strongly.” On the following fol. 160r (“Sand for flowers”), he notes,

If it is cast thick, it bears down too much on the flowers & constricts them & reduces them as if massed together. Take heed therefore to temper it until thin, & blow strongly before the flower is covered. Cast at the side of the mold. And tempering the sand, one ought not only to turn the spatula but to beat the sand in water as if you were beating glair of egg.

Based on previous experiments in the lab, we decided that the mixture described in “For casting in silver” (fol. 121v) was most suitable for our use. It is the most detailed recipe and is used for lifecasting an animal, and specifies “4 parts of plaster, of two parts reheated brick, & one part reheated feather alum.” We used a proportion of two parts plaster of Paris to one of brick dust and increased the quantity of sal ammoniac from two teaspoons to four for this trial (we did not use “feather alum” for any trial).13 Consequently, this mold took much longer to dry before we could put it in the oven and burn out the leaves (Fig. 13). We also had to pour the sand into the mold in two goes, as we did not mix enough at first. The final cast of the rose reflects this addition through a sharp line on the outside of the first petal (Fig. 14).14

Casting the Metal

We were now at the point that we had to select the metal alloy for casting the components. While on fol. 155r, the author-practitioner let the reader choose between tin or lead, on fol. 155v he assumes that tin will be used to cast all the rose elements. For all our experiments we decided to use an alloy of tin and lead, following the author-practitioner’s recommendation on fol. 116v for “Mixture of tin and lead,” for “delicate plants & other flowers or thin foliage,” because we had already experimented with casting in pure tin during the residence of expert maker Andrew Lacey.15 Fol. 116v specifies 3 parts tin to one part lead, but we used an equal amount of tin and lead, with the result that the cast rose and its stem are much heavier than the small medal we had cast in pure tin during a previous class experiment. This result has the advantage of resolving the author’s concern that the stem, if cast in tin or lead, would be too fragile to support the weight of the rose’s blossom.16 It is possible that this is why the author-practitioner changed his opinion on what metal should be used in lifecasting during the writing of the manuscript. A mixture of tin and lead is suggested earlier on fol. 116v, but by fol.155v the author-practitioner, in viewing tin as “troublesome,” seems to share Vannoccio Biringuccio’s low opinion of the alloy. The latter author states in his Pirotechnia that tin is “not better for any work when mixed than when pure, except for the advantage of the master, who … sells lead that is worth little for tin that is worth much.”17

The beauty of the final rose cast derives from the richness of those lifelike details stiffened by the melted butter and fixed by the metal alloy (see Fig. 1).18 Moreover, although casting the spray of leaves in a one-piece mold was a failure, those cast in the two-piece mold turned out well and contributed to the final detail of the cast object (Fig. 15). That part of our experiment also proves the efficacy of laying the fragile leaves down in the mold and using eau-de-vie to keep sand from adhering (Fig. 16).

When we poured the molten metal, we used two strips of copper to press and hold the two halves of the mold together. We then wrapped the mold in cheesecloth dipped in a thin plaster mix (Fig. 17). Unfortunately, despite the warning on fol. 116v (“And make sure that there is more of the metal than is needed, in order that some is left. However, if there is not enough, finish casting, & it will take. But it will not be so neat”), we ran out of metal during pouring. Our final cast object thus shows a fracture in one leaf and a small broken stem (Fig. 18).

Since the final components—the stem, the rose flower, and the spray of leaves—were heavy, we had no choice but to solder them together, with a solder made by melting the small parts of sprues removed during cleaning (Fig. 19). We speculated that fish glue would not have been strong enough to keep the entire rose together,19 but that it could have been useful in fixing the fracture that appeared on the leaf.20

Conclusions

Our conclusions coincide with those on fol. 159v (“Carnation”), where the author-practitioner says that, while it is possible to successfully mold any kind of flower whole in a one-piece mold, if multiple casts are desired, the plant should be cut into its components and a one-piece mold made for the stem and flower, while a reusable two-piece mold is made for the leaves: “[The carnation] is molded like the rose, marigold & all other flowers thus, as is said, namely, whole-mold flowers, & all the leaves, if you want, in two halves, joining them by solder for the most secure way, even if you could mold them together in a closed mold.”21 The text from this folio, late in the manuscript, expresses not only the author-practitioner’s technical achievement in flower casting, but perhaps also communicates his increased confidence in the process as a way to fix the ephemeral in a physical object.

We studied this development of the author-practitioner’s skill in casting delicate things by analyzing the details provided throughout the manuscript on the materials and techniques used to create lifecasts of plants and animals. Our research presented here aims to provide a better understanding of lifecasting techniques and of the author-practitioner’s process of learning through practice that we detected throughout the manuscript. But we also seek to contribute to the study of casting practices for small and delicate things in early modern Europe, a subject scarce in primary sources.22

In sixteenth-century Europe, flowers like roses were associated with the concept of physical beauty subject to the damage of passing time.23 With this in mind, our work of reconstructing the author-practitioner’s experiments demonstrates that the lifecasting process is not merely a sequence of technical steps. On the contrary, lifecasting is a unique opportunity to give permanence to an animate, delicate thing in a manner that preserves its intrinsic form and beauty (Fig. 20). This artistic practice thus becomes one of the author’s main preoccupations throughout the manuscript. His intensive effort to perfect the process of lifecasting through experimentation with “veins,” “breathing holes,” and many varieties of alloys and molds reveals the goal to suspend the ravages of nature and fix the ephemeral form of a living rose in metal.
