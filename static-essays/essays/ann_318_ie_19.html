Turning Turtle: The Process of Translating BnF Ms. Fr. 640

By , DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/1pw7-ax09
← Back to Essays

Abstract

By focusing on the translation of the entries on molding and casting turtles from life in BnF Ms. Fr. 640, this essay seeks to underscore and bring to light the chronology, choices, and processes behind the Project’s translation of the manuscript. Our translation serves two main goals: to accurately represent the author-practitioner’s intentions and voice, as unhindered as possible by twenty-first-century assumptions, and to provide a window onto the vivid world described in the manuscript.

The two central processes at the heart of the Making and Knowing Project are experiment and collaboration. This Paleography Workshop is convened very much in the spirit of both. The work you complete in the workshop will have implications for several generations of students and researchers… We also welcome you to contribute your own ideas about how to modify and refine these practices for the use of future paleographers.

            — Pamela H. Smith, Paleography Workshop Introduction Guide, 2014

In the fall of 2013, a call for applications went out from Pamela Smith, Seth Low Professor of History at Columbia University and Founding Director of the Making and Knowing Project, asking for paleographers who might be interested in participating in a Middle French Paleography Workshop the following June. This workshop was the first in a series of broader “Text Workshops,” tasked with the transcription, translation, and markup of BnF Ms. Fr. 640 for digital publication. Moreover, these workshops had been conceived as one of several components of the Making and Knowing Project.1 When June 2014 arrived, fifteen paleographers convened in Fayerweather Hall on the Columbia campus, having traveled from various parts of the United States, Canada, France, Germany, and Italy (Fig. 1).2 Diversity was sought when choosing participants of different backgrounds because Ms. Fr. 640 deals with such a wide range of topics, from lifecasting to pharmaceutical entries. The cohort consisted mostly of graduate students whose research interests and expertise varied widely, but did not extend as far as professional translation experience.

The authors of this article, one an art historian, the other a medievalist, met and struck up a fruitful working partnership at the first workshop, and found themselves returning every year to participate in the transcription and translation of the manuscript, eventually narrowing their focus down to the translation of the metal casting entries (Fig. 2). In this essay, we hope our two voices will represent those of the forty-nine paleographers who worked on Ms. Fr. 640’s digital edition. We will be using the first person as a means to acknowledge our own agency in this process as well as the subjective nature of any translation work. Similar to the way other essays might take the reader into the “workshop” (boutique) of the author-practitioner, this essay was very much written as a way to guide the reader into the “workshops” that produced the digital edition.

Who’s in the Race: Translating a Sixteenth-Century How-To Manuscript

Ms. Fr. 640, with an accepted date of the last quarter of the sixteenth century, fits into a wider literary genre of technical “how-to” literature, one of the earliest examples being Il libro dell’arte (ca. 1400) by Cennino Cennini.3 The fifteenth and sixteenth centuries saw a great number of these kinds of treatises being written, among the more well known De la pirotechnia by Vannoccio Biringuccio in 1540 (Fig. 3), ‘Li tre libri dell’arte del vasaio by Cipriano Piccolpasso in 1548, Di Secreti by Alessio Piemontese in 1555, De re metallica by Georgius Agricola in 1556 and in the case of France in 1563, the Receptes véritables by Bernard Palissy.4 The list is long, and many of the examples cited were re-edited in the decades following their initial publication.5 As well as being re-edited, these treatises were also frequently translated. Di Secreti was published in French under the title Les secrets du reverend Alexis Piémontois in 1557.6 De re metallica, written in Latin, was translated into German only a year after its publication, and De la pirotechnia was translated into French in 1556 (Fig. 4).7 English translations of these texts at the time of their publication—excepting De’ Secreti—were less common, and these sources were for a long time only accessible in their original language.8 Because of their continued importance for art historians and historians of science and technology, a new wave of English translations emerged in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries.9 An example from the authors of the 1912 translation of De re metallica offers a rare insight, albeit sparse, into their translation process:

…. many methods and processes described have never been practiced in English-speaking mining communities, and so [we] had no representatives in our vocabulary, and we considered the introduction of German terms undesirable; other methods and processes have become obsolete and their descriptive terms with them, yet we wished to avoid the introduction of obsolete or unusual English; but of the greatest importance of all has been the necessity to avoid rigorously such modern technical terms as would imply a greater scientific understanding than the period possessed.10

Beyond the anomalous example above, translators often neglected to make their editorial decisions explicit. This is a recurring pattern that can be found in many of the translations of how-to literature, the major exception being Lara Broecke’s recent edition of Il libro dell’arte in which she explicitly addresses the “excruciating English” sometimes found in her translation of Cennini’s work, underlining that she wants to “reflect Cennino’s writing difficulties in [her] own writing.”11 Broecke addresses issues of structure in the original text, punctuation, choice, and consistency of specific translated words as well as the general editorial guidelines she applied to her text.12

When translating these types of books, a translator, in general, is necessarily confronted with having to make decisions in terms of vocabulary, syntax, audience reception, and comprehension. However, when translation moves from singular scholar to several, as with the Making and Knowing crowd-sourced text workshops, a different working method is required. Since the practice of translation has traditionally been a solo endeavor, a translator will often make decisions without articulating the reasoning behind them; and the reader may only be able to intuit these decisions retrospectively through the implicit evidence of the text itself. That a given translator chooses one option over another is not always made apparent, nor is it expected to be made so. For the translation of Ms. Fr. 640, however, the Making and Knowing team was forced to bring the decision making into the open and to provide an opportunity for iterative and interactive exchange. In this way, a series of guidelines and practices developed and were documented throughout the workshops, henceforth referred to as the “translation protocols.” 

This essay is structured around the division of the text workshops into two phases: the first three, in which the transcription of Ms. Fr. 640 was completed and an initial translation was made; and the last two, when the translation process materialized into a codified set of protocols that both drove the translation process thereafter and shaped the finalized digital edition. In the first phase, the translators did not enter into the task with a high sense of self-reflection, nor were they inclined to document their decisions. In the second phase, however, with a heightened awareness of the collaborative environment and its consequences, the thinking behind the translation process shifted. Sharing authorship of the translation also meant sharing ownership, and provided a more objective perspective that made all participants answerable to a third entity, the translation protocols themselves. In addition to the organic evolution of the protocols, the freedom from singular ownership of the translation and the growing awareness of significant elements in the author-practitioner’s voice meant that a distinctive translation was produced. In practical terms, this means that the reader of the English translation of Ms. Fr. 640 might be surprised, at times, by the choices made. In this essay, we explicitly articulate the protocols and principles used in translating Ms. Fr. 640 into English, and document the translation process in a transparent manner, explaining why the translation appears in the format that it does. Because we worked mostly with casting entries, specific examples come from the process of lifecasting, particularly those of turtles and tortoises.13 The tortoise, it turns out, is the perfect representative of the translators and our work, encapsulates our vision of what a translation should be, and, as La Fontaine notes in the fable: “she gets going // Creeps graybeard-like, makes her slow haste …”.14

The Hare: The First Three Workshops

In looking back at the development of the translation protocols, the first three workshops (2014, 2015, and 2016) form a cohesive unit based upon the most immediate objective at that time, namely the transcription and initial translation of certain manuscript entries for the following fall and spring laboratory seminars.15 In the spirit of the Making and Knowing ethos, experimentation played a key role in the first three years, by guiding both the day-to-day workflow and the development of a digital infrastructure held in a dedicated Google Drive folder.

When the first workshop got underway, project directors and paleographers created a daily workflow that included collaboration in both formal and organic ways. In formal group morning meetings, the paleographers benefited from the expertise and knowledge of others to create a shared understanding of certain vocabulary and artisanal processes, such as casting in wax and making imitations. The large group meetings also provided a venue to discuss the previous day’s methodological questions and problems, including specific concepts and vocabulary the author-practitioner describes in the manuscript (Fig. 5). After the morning meetings, work continued in groups of two, each including French and English native speakers (Fig. 6). In order to divide the work up efficiently, each pair was assigned a certain number of pages from the manuscript, and tasked with updating an already semi-completed diplomatic transcription, creating a normalized transcription from it, and finally updating a minimally-completed translation.

With no other specific instructions, each working group experimented organically with its own style and process. We developed what was later revealed as a singular working method involving one of us reading aloud from the semi-complete transcription while the other read along with the manuscript facsimile page to verify and update the transcription text. When anomalies occurred, we conferred with the manuscript, made an executive decision, and updated the transcription. Likewise for translation, while one of us read the partially-prepared translation out loud, the other followed along in the newly-created normalized transcription to confirm and update the translation text. Once the translation was complete, we then reread it with an eye to rendering it as fluently as possible, often imagining ourselves in the position of the student trying to carry out the written instructions. In retrospect, it was a “principle of student comprehension” that drove the initial phase of translation in the first three workshops. Given the nature of the working environment—everyone was working in Fayerweather Hall throughout the day—informal conversations also took place between various groups to see how others were transcribing or translating unfamiliar words. 

The general workflow was closely linked to the Google Drive infrastructure that had been set up for the paleographers. The manuscript was divided into units of pages, not units of entries. As a result, in the first three workshops, each group was assigned a certain number of pages and worked through the entire page, even when the entries on it had no relation to one another. For example, on fol. 10r, the author-practitioner moves from how to make imitation jasper to making hourglass sand (Fig. 7). The Google Drive, too, was organized according to manuscript page number, each page having an individually-named folder that contained the facsimile image, the diplomatic/normalized transcriptions and translation documents (Fig. 8). Once a folder, i.e., a page, had been completely updated, the paleographers placed their initials in the title of the folder, indicating it was ready for revision by Marc Smith and Pamela Smith. Progress was measured by looking at the number of folders that had initials next to them, indicating they were complete.

The first three workshops intentionally provided the space and scholarly support to experiment with collaborative workflows. Only through the act of transcribing and translating did the team of directors and paleographers begin to understand how best to accomplish the task at hand. Different groups practiced different workflows and this led eventually to a general methodological approach and a complete and accurate transcription of the manuscript (Fig. 9, Fig. 10). It also led, however, to the realization that while the initial translation objectives had been achieved, i.e., the “principle of student comprehension,” the translations as a whole were sorely lacking consistency. The experiential and experimental nature of the first three workshops had produced different translation voices. Dotted throughout were a variety of English idioms, grammatical structures, and vocabulary applied to identical French phrases and words. If one of the strengths of the project had been the vast array of specialized knowledge the participants brought to the manuscript; the drawback was the very distinct, sometimes isolated translations that this knowledge produced.

The Tortoise: A Slow Haste in the Last Two Workshops

Over the course of the last four days of the 2016 workshop, a small group of participants who had attended all three up to that point, and who were therefore familiar with the questions and challenges that translating raised, gathered together to brainstorm and design an effective collaborative methodology in line with a new translation objective. The outcome of these four days resulted in a new workflow that would “consistify” the translation, as well as create standardized documentation for use by all translators.

For the following two years, the workshop took place in Toulouse, at the Université Jean Jaurès. From ongoing research on Ms. Fr. 640, it had been discovered that the manuscript was in all likelihood written by an author-practitioner who worked in or near Toulouse, and moving the workshops there offered the participants, as well as the Making and Knowing team, an opportunity to gain insight into the manuscript’s environment. While this did not impact the translation process directly, we were able to gain insight into the processes, places, and materials that the author-practitioner described in Ms. Fr. 640, and we were greatly indebted to the expertise of Pascal Julien, Professor of Art History, Colin Debuiche, and Sarah Muñoz on Renaissance Toulouse. We toured contemporary buildings, the city at large, as well as museums (Fig. 11). We visited marble quarries and a woad museum (Fig. 12, Fig. 13). Knowing that we were working in the same milieu as the author-practitioner was inspiring and created a great sense of motivation and interest among the team.

By 2017, the paleography team consisted only of experienced paleographers, i.e., people who had already participated in one or more of the previous workshops. We all knew each other quite well and by that time were all deeply engaged and committed to finishing the translation. It had become a joy to work for a few weeks a year on this project, with a dedicated group that took pleasure in meeting up and seeing each other (Fig. 14, Fig. 15). This working environment meant that we felt comfortable asking questions, providing critical feedback, and exchanging information. In other words, the team really coalesced into a very efficient and congenial working environment that not only offered mutual understanding but constantly encouraged new solutions to problems that came up. It was within this new environment that the new translation workflows and protocols were put into practice.

The new workflow, which included the transparent documentation of explicit translation protocols, in effect underscored three theoretical outcomes that emerged as translation progressed. First, just as the “making” that happened in the laboratory seminars enhanced the “knowing” of the manuscript entries, new translation groups acquired a more refined “knowing” of a process through the “making” of its translation. For example, in order for us to understand the “Molding turtles” entry, we had to work through our own “making” of the process over several attempts (Fig. 16). Second, it became evident that the translations depended not only on understanding the sixteenth-century vocabulary and grammar of the author-practitioner, but also on understanding his work, his goals, and his cultural and physical environment. In addition, paying attention to the preservation of the agency of language used by the author-practitioner took on greater importance. Third, the team decided to keep the translation as close to French syntax as possible, guided by the strong belief that we should not impose twenty-first-century concepts onto a sixteenth-century text, but rather work to capture the voice of the author-practitioner.

The first changes in the workflow applied to group composition and tasks. The translators shifted from working in pairs, to working in groups of three, always including at least one native speaker of English (Fig. 17).16 In order to build expertise within each group, the manuscript was divided up into entries, as opposed to the previous division of pages, so that each group could focus on a single theme rather than a linear progression by page number.17 A parallel shift in digital infrastructure occurred with the creation of a spreadsheet by then Project Manager Naomi Rosenkranz that tracked the progress of completed entries (Fig. 18). In this way, each group was able to become an “expert” in the translation of certain processes, such as casting or painting, following the entries throughout the manuscript and making sure that the translation was consistent across all of them (Fig. 19). Our work with the casting entries allowed us to make consistent decisions, such as keeping crocum ferri in Latin instead of using the previous translation of “iron dross.” Platine d’ardille was consistently translated as “clay slab,” replacing other translations such as “clay base,” and luther became “to lute,” instead of “cover with clay,” among other translations. 

The shift in workflow was accompanied by the formalization and standardization of project resources. First, a general vocabulary list was generated for everyone to consult, including protocols for variants of spelling and function, citations for references, and page number examples (Fig. 20).18 This list was to be consulted for entry-specific vocabulary and for certain recurring words, and formed the basis for what is now the Glossary. For example, words such as après and car were given a consistent translation in English: “next” and “for.” Likewise, a distinction was made between puis and ainsi, which became “then” and “thus,” respectively. Advise bien was winnowed down from four different translations to one: “take heed.” The vocabulary list garnered its own set of best practices for making entries and became indispensable for creating consistency among the translation voices. Second, a default line was drawn for translating French vocabulary and expressions when faced with a variety of options in English: stick as closely to the French as possible. For example, a pointe de fer in French became an “iron point,” rather than the previous translation of “iron needle.” While the meaning of iron needle is perfectly correct when translating pointe, it inferred too much information on the size and shape of the object, layers of interpretation that were not explicit in the French.

In practice, the thematic translation groups, because they focused on one type of technique, were able to develop expertise on translating a particular type of entry: whether in terms of technique, materials used by the author-practitioner, or, more importantly, in terms of language used to express these techniques. An example of this came with the understanding of the word gect, which had two different meanings that had sometimes been conflated. We had previously translated the word gect as “casting,” as in the verb “to cast” for gecter (jeter). This was clearly a comprehension problem on our part that only resolved itself after having translated a series of casting entries and gaining a contextual understanding. On fol. 125v in “Molding turtles,” the first set of translations from 2014–2016 show a lack of understanding about the process taking place: “To cast in sugar without making a casting, they are molded from plaster, as works for anything you like.” (Elles se moulent de plastre pour gecter en sucre sans faire gect comme font toutes choses que tu vouldras.) Here gecter was translated as “casting.” But as our expertise in the casting process grew, and our understanding of these entries sharpened, it became clear that gect did not always mean “cast,” but sometimes referred to a “gate,” the entryway for the hot metal to enter the mold, usually made out of wax. The use of “gate” clarifies the process, and various illustrations show the author-practitioner making channels, or gates, of wax for casting (Fig. 21).

The final translation therefore reflects a more accurate understanding of the techniques and vocabulary at work: “They are molded in plaster to be cast in sugar without making a gate like done for all things that you want.” With this new understanding of gect, we could make choices in translation that were consistent across all entries from this group according to context. Similarly, the fact that teams were working thematically provided a deeper and broader contact with the entries included within a category: so broad that connections between them that had not been made beforehand were still being uncovered in the 2018 workshop. For the turtles entries in particular this meant the discovery that the “Molding hollow” entry on fol. 150v was actually a direct continuation of “Molds” from the previous folio, even though its title had no direct link to it. The initial translation of both entries was confusing in terms of the processes described in each, as we were not aware that the text continued on from a previous page. Being able to connect fols. 150v and 150r within their wider structure vastly improved our translation, as well as our understanding of the “Molding turtles” group of entries.

The expertise that was being developed by the paleography teams happened iteratively and often through the consultation of a very wide range of sources and experts. In translating the casting recipes, we often turned to videos in order to gain a visual understanding of the process. For example, a video of early twentieth-century practitioners illustrated how bells were cast and helped to clarify the processes of certain casting entries in Ms. Fr. 640.19 Then, partly on the basis of the translation work done with the casting entries, the Making and Knowing team created their own video showing the lost-wax casting process that helped the paleography team integrate the techniques into further translation work. We also relied on the wide range of research expertise held by the project members themselves: among them Pamela Smith’s understanding of casting processes, Tillmann Taape’s knowledge of medical history, and Tianna Uchacz’s expertise on Netherlandish art. Expertise was also sought by the project team during the working group meetings. In addition to these meetings, experts in various domains were also called to assist the translation team, especially for the arms and armor entries (Fig. 22, Fig. 23). The vocabulary for these entries was very technical and specific and required the help of Jonathan Tavares, Associate Curator of Arms and Armor at the Art Institute of Chicago, and Pascal Brioist, Professor of History at the University of Tours, who were able to explain the nature of the entry content and its importance in the manuscript and the field as a whole (Fig. 24).

The guiding principle of holding true to the French syntax, structure, and grammar led to two important consequences. First, the use of cognates increased throughout the translation. Indeed, the list of cognates became wide-ranging: for example, “corrupt” was used for corrompre rather than “spoil,” and “adapt” for adapter instead of “arrange.” Second, fidelity to the French syntax became an essential tool when trying to translate the agency of materials and processes described by the author-practitioner. For example, the often used boire—when meant to describe a material absorbing a liquid—was initially translated as “to absorb.” However, this translation removed the agency of the material, which was no longer actively interacting (drinking) with another, but instead passively reacting to it (absorbing). To preserve the understanding of materials held by the author, the new translation was amended to “to drink.” Another example can be found on fol. 144v: “S’il y a du crocum dans le sable les joinctures des molles ne se cognoissent presque point.” Here, initially connaitre was translated as “will not join well.” This conveyed the meaning of the word in context, but in 2018, this was changed to: “If there is some crocum in the sand, the joints of the molds will almost not recognize each other” in order to preserve the implied agency of the reflexive. After a consideration of all instances of connaitre across the manuscript, the translation changed again to “If there is some crocum in the sand, the joints of the molds can hardly be perceived.” While this translation decision in the end resulted in a passive construction in English, on the same folio, in another passage of the “Molding turtles” entry, notions of “agency” or “embodiment” were preserved. When discussing how to prepare a mold, in order for it to release well, the author-practitioner specifies, “L’eau chaulde faict que le sable destrempé en eau s’assoit mieulx sur l’huile, qui aultrement refuseroit.” Initially refuseroit, which implies a certain will on the part of the material—in this case the sand, was translated as “would not work,” the literal translation of the consequence of that refusal. In the second round of translation, however, this was changed to: “hot water makes the tempered sand in water set better on oil, which otherwise would be refused.”

For some words, while the translation might have been quite awkward in the English, the use of cognates allowed us to translate the different material properties and connotations that were expressed in the French. For example, an English word like “appropriate,” often used because of its adaptability, actually turned out to be quite problematic, and had to be used sparingly. In “Turtles,” on fol. 144r, the author-practitioner cautions the reader to mold “celles de bas de deulx ou trois ou quattre selon l’exigence.” We initially translated “selon l’exigence” as “according to their appropriateness.” However, “appropriate” and “appropriateness” were also being used as the cognate of propre, an expression that appears often in the text and holds a different set of meanings. Since l’exigence only appears once in the entire manuscript, on fol. 144r, we decided to retain the word “appropriate” only for the translation for the more common propre, and to translate l’exigence as “according to the need.” Contrarily, there were some cases where the cognates failed to express the full meaning of a word. For example malaisé, in “Molding turtles,” on fol. 147r, was initially translated as “difficult,” but was later changed to “awkward.” Similarly fantasque, found in “Molding hollow” on fol. 151r, was not translated as “fantastical” but rather as “fanciful.” 

The attention to linguistic agency ran parallel to a new markup policy that sought to emphasize key elements in the manuscript and needed to remain consistent across all versions of the transcription and translation (Fig. 25, Fig. 26). The renewed conceptual work occurring on the markup protocol at the same time as the re-translation of the manuscript implied a certain amount of crossover between the two types of working groups, most notably when body parts, for example, were being mentioned in French. In “Turtles,” on fol. 144r, the French reads: “Doncques pour mectre la main à l’oeuvre....” Here the mention of the hand is of extreme relevance to both the concept of embodied artisanal practice and in regard to the markup. The body part, the hand, had to be kept in the translation in order to keep the markup consistent between the different language versions of Ms. Fr. 640. In 2015, we translated it as: “So to get started...,” but changed it to: “Therefore, to put your hand to the work...” in 2019.

In the first three workshops, we were often overinterpreting the text for the sake of clarity and fluency, but with the second translation, we made efforts to avoid superfluous information. For example, we endeavored to keep verb tense and passive/active voices the same in English as in French; pronouns that may have been added in the first translation were removed, even if it meant retaining in English a certain lack of specificity present in the French text. These translation protocols—keeping the syntax, grammar, and structure of the French, and refusing to add additional information—resulted in what may appear as an awkward English translation. We translated sentences such as “Aultant en peulx tu faire à tous aultres animalets avecq de la graine de grand passevelours, du petit, et la graine de chou navette...” (fol. 144r), where it is unclear whether the author-practitioner was referring to both big and small amaranth or if he simply forgot to write the name of a plant after petit, as: “You can do this as well with all other small animals, with some grain of large amaranth, some of small, and grain of rapeseed ....” The uncertainty present in the French remains in the English. In some rare cases, staying close to the French syntax actually helped with comprehension in English, illustrating the perils of overinterpretation or of over-translation. For example, in “Molding turtles,” on fol. 146v, we have “Comme tu as bien descouvert la partie du ventre de la tortue, les dessoubs de la gorge & des pattes, & bien nettoyé le tout....” This was initially translated as: “Since you have cleanly uncovered the part of the belly of the turtle, its underparts, its throat, and its legs, and having cleaned it well...,” implying that both the underpart of the turtle, as well as its whole legs and throat, were to be uncovered. The author-practitioner, however, is talking about cleaning the underpart of the legs and the throat, and not the entire turtle, hence the second translation: “When you have well uncovered the part of the belly of the turtle, the underneath of the throat & of the legs, & having cleaned it all well….” We took similar care in respecting the original French punctuation, with the result that while the first set of translations favored shorter sentences, the final version mirrored the manuscript’s longer sentences. 

The overarching consequence of using these translation protocols meant that previous traces of individual translators’ writing styles were erased. As a group of academics, our personal translation styles were as much a matter of taste as of training: they varied widely and wildly, and came across even when translating a single manuscript written by a single author-practitioner. All the guidelines discussed above helped to excise personal style in favor of a more unified and “guided” translation. A final example that sums up the shift in thinking is the relatively straightforward sentence: “Maistre Alexandre dict ne s’estre jamays trompé …” (fol. 151r), which we initially translated as: “Mr. Alexander says he has never gone astray with ….”. The “has never gone astray” was too embellished for the new translation protocols, and we amended the sentence to the perhaps dryer, but more faithful: “Master Alexandre says that he has never gone wrong with …” in 2018.

Conclusion

In September 2018, we were invited to participate in a final week-long workshop in which the Making and Knowing team completed the full translation of Ms. Fr. 640. There, we were asked to write an essay about the translation process since we had attended all of the workshops and had translated all the casting entries, which figure as over a third of the manuscript. A year later in September 2019, the final working group meeting took place at Columbia University and all scholars writing essays for the digital edition gathered to read and provide feedback on each other’s work. In between the two meetings of 2018 and 2019, the Making and Knowing Team completed a full readthrough and revision of the English text, “consistifying” translations of all entries. Even at what some might consider this late date, some translations were amended. What emerged at the working group meeting was that translating Ms. Fr. 640 has been and continues to be an iterative process, open to interpretation and to the layers of meaning that can be uncovered in its words. Even after the digital edition had “officially” been completed, certain translations were still being revised and reworked due to new inputs from the working group scholars’ comments and discoveries. And, as the Making and Knowing Team re-edited student research essays to correct versions of the translation used in past lab seminars, translations were often revisited and amended again. In this process, it became clear that the digital nature of the Edition makes possible a new level of consistency: text editing software allows a search for all occurrences and conjugations of any given word and a comparison of the various translations. This capacity gave new insights into the meaning of obscure terms, but it also posed new and thorny questions about consistency of meaning, language variability, and principles of translation and interpretation, only made more complex by the compiled nature of a text such as Ms. Fr. 640. Like much of the process described in this essay, such questions would probably never have been raised by a translation that was undertaken in a non-collaborative and non-digital environment. The translation presented in the digital edition is thus one of the many translations that could be offered up to the public, and in this essay we have sought to explain and articulate why this translation and not another. In line with the Making and Knowing ethos, the translation is not meant to be authoritative but, rather, a truly working document that will have lives after this one.

In their translation of the Lives of Vasari, art historian André Chastel and his team note: 

The version we are presenting is one we want to be readable by all.... We struggled against the wearing repetition of monotonous terms, resigning ourselves to using a variety of substitutions; we always chose the most concrete and colorful word to render uneasy expressions, for it seemed to us unfaithful to the spirit of Italian literature that lifeless words should be used to translate a formulaic expression.... We put all our efforts into producing an expressive fit, rather than a translation molded on the text, the essential thing being in our opinion to reproduce the rhythm, movement, the remarkable strength of style …. 20 

This statement—that the “expressive fit” should be privileged over a “molded translation,” i.e., a more literal translation of the text—resonates with historian Peter Burke’s view of the dilemma faced by all historians in their translation of past into present:

If the past is far-away distant country, it goes without saying that that even the most unilingual of historians is a translator. The latter figures as a bridge between the past and the present and should confront the same dilemmas as other translators, each serving two masters, and each seeking to remain faithful to the original, keeping in mind the reader.21

The Making and Knowing project, in contrast to Chastel’s solution, made the intentional decision to impose literal fidelity to the original on its translation, but that decision was made by taking into account the nature of the historical text being translated: a manuscript that expresses the trials and experiences of working in a workshop, a testament to the early modern comprehension of materials, and the written expression of an author-practitioner at work. Yes, the translation demands a lot of its readers; but, in this choice our translation is aligned with the position taken by Lara Broecke: 

It pained me to remove from my first translation some of the happier solutions I had found for Cennini’s verbal muddles, and it bothers me to picture the reader stumbling over every sentence and wishing that the text were a little easier to follow. However, it pains me more to think of the earnest, humble, affectionate, humorous Cennino whom I have come to know in the course of making this translation being erased from his own treatise, to be replaced by a smooth-tongued imposter.22
