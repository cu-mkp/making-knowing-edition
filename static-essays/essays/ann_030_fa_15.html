Colors for Green Leaves and Painting on Metal

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/s0h6-bs33
← Back to Essays

Abstract

This essay explores three recipes in Ms. Fr. 640 (on fols. 158v, 10r, and 63v) that describe the making of a yellow-green paint to render metal casts of flowers more lifelike. The author-practitioner blends verdigris with an organic yellow lake pigment known as stil de grain yellow or scudegrun, and my reconstruction explores the different plant-based dyes and substrates he used for the yellow pigment. The author-practitioner’s experiments suggest a need for both precision and flexibility when imitating nature in the workshop: while the recipes can be specific about ingredients and ways to assess the naturalism of the pigment’s color, they also leave important components such as ingredient quantities and pigment application techniques up to a maker’s “judgment & discretion” (fol. 158v).

Introduction

This essay explores the instructions for “Colors for green leaves” on fol. 158v in the context of other pigment making instructions, in particular two entries on stil de grain yellow (scudegrun) on fols. 10r and 63v. At first glance, these recipes seem like straightforward suggestions for expanding the color palette. Careful reading and consideration, however, shows us that the author-practitioner was interested in creating naturalistic works, and relied on a range of materials and color-making techniques to produce the desired visual effects. Fol. 158v is primarily about modifying green paint (made with verdigris) because, as the author-practitioner states, verdigris “has depth” and is “too dark” for lifelike plants. To make suitable colors, he suggests mixing verdigris with yellow. Yellow has long played a role in making green pigments. As Mary P. Merrifield explains,

it was generally considered that mixed green, composed of blue and yellow, was more permanent than any of the before-mentioned green pigments. They were frequently compounded of ultramarine and orpiment, of azzurro della magna and giallolino, of indigo and orpiment, and of one of the mineral blues with a yellow lake.1

In Ms. Fr. 640, the author-practitioner did not mix yellow with blue but instead used a few specific yellow pigments—stil de grain yellow, yellow ocher, and massicot—to broaden the range of green hues available to him. The variety of options for yellow contrasts with the consistency with which verdigris is used for green throughout the rest of the manuscript.

Yellow had long been a problem for artisans because the plant-based dyes and pigments that are nontoxic also tend to be unstable and prone to fading. While the author-practitioner does not appear to have invented new ways of deriving yellow pigment (his recipes are consistent with those in manuscripts dating to before and after Ms. Fr. 640, as discussed in this essay), he does apply these yellows and greens to painting metal—a technique that few artisans discuss. My experiments focused on the various types of yellow the author-practitioner used to modify green, also referring to fols. 10r and 63v (both titled “Scudegrun”), and how this range of yellow pigments might produce different visual effects when painted on cast metal objects. These recipes lead us to consider the following question: how do the qualities and characteristics of these materials produce different yellows? For the yellow lakes called scudegrun, how do different substrates (ceruse and chalk mentioned by the author-practitioner, powdered eggshell in contemporaneous manuscripts) alter the pigment? The variety in yellows—and even in the substrates and dye sources for yellow lakes—hints at the range of materials the author-practitioner was able to draw on in his permutations of color-making processes. For example, on fol. 10r, he uses “chalk, or better yet ceruse” as the substrate for his yellow lake. His phrasing suggests a noted difference between the two materials and the superiority of the ceruse. However, both fols. 10r and 63v have recipes entitled “Scudegrun” (“Stil de grain yellow”) which make use of different plant sources (weld and broom flower), but the author does not suggest they are interchangeable, nor does he express a preference. While we cannot know for certain the author-practitioner’s imaginary of materials, my examination of this recipe through reconstruction provides a better understanding of the flexibility of the recipes, and how the author-practitioner modified them (using his judgment and experience) to suit specific needs.

History of Yellow and Ms. Fr. 640

Perhaps the difficulties artisans experienced with the fugitive instability of yellows led to experiments, such as those in Ms. Fr. 640, with a variety of materials to try to achieve a stable color. Pigments, composed of small particles varying from .01 to 1 micron, are “pure colors in powder form, which must be suspended in a medium in which they are insoluble (such as oil) in order to make paint.”2 The author-practitioner used earth pigments (ochers), lead-based pigments (massicot, ceruse), as well as lakes (stil de grain yellow) to make yellow paints.3 While the recipe on fol. 158v does not specify how these paints might be used, it is grouped with recipes that refer to mold making and casting, which suggests that the author-practitioner was perhaps looking for ways to color cast metals. He specifically instructed practitioners to paint in oil, “because distemper colors have no hold.” Also, he cautioned practitioners to “layer it transparently, so as not to cover the lines of the work.” These details in his instruction indicate that the paint is meant to be overlaid on a material warranting special precautions. The use of specific pigments for different purposes is typical of the author-practitioner’s instructions. For example, an entry on fol. 62r titled “Ocher” simply states “it is put for faces, hair, skulls, and rocks.” In “Shadows,” on fol. 63r, he notes that “German painters make their shadows on flesh color of men with ground jet, stil de grain yellow & ocher.”4 These recipes give specific instances in which certain types of yellow are appropriate for achieving a sense of realism in painting, even noting examples where a technique has been used to good effect (as with the “German painters”).

On fol. 158v, the author-practitioner apparently seeks to expand his green palette. According to Daniel V. Thompson, one of the “chief functions [of yellow] was to modify the qualities of greens and, to a lesser extent, of reds,” meaning the author-practitioner was likely recording a commonly practiced technique.5 In “Colors for green leaves” on fol. 158v, however, he deliberately instructs practitioners to “put the color on the natural flower or leaf to see whether it comes close.” This recipe shows special attention to naturalistic effects, related to his interest in casting lifelike plants and creatures. The author-practitioner intends to use yellows to brighten “dark” verdigris and evoke a more lifelike effect in his cast plants.6

A number of well-known sources of yellow pigment are not included among the manuscript’s paint recipes. While the author-practitioner mentions marigold and saffron (fol. 38r), he does not suggest that they can be used for dyes or pigments. Perhaps this is because French saffron was not considered good quality, as is noted as early as the fourteenth century in the art technical writing of Petrus de S. Audemar from the north of France. The best kind—known as corsicos—was brought from Sicily, making it more expensive and less practical to use in bulk.7 Terra merita (turmeric) is mentioned in Ms. Fr. 640 in a recipe for imitation gold (for painting over silver foil), among others, but it is never used as a yellow paint on its own.8 Lastly, the author-practitioner only refers to orpiment (arsenic trisulfide, from the Latin auripigmentum) once, although its widespread use dates to Old Kingdom Egypt (2700–2200 BCE) and was present in medieval Europe.9 On fol. 99v, he uses it as a substitute for gold to gild a frame. Perhaps he does not use this popular pigment for painting because by the Middle Ages, artisans had begun to move away from arsenic-based yellows like orpiment to lead-tin yellows.10 It is also possible that he was aware that orpiment was unsuitable for modifying green tones because its sulfur component could react with the copper in verdigris.11 The author-practitioner’s selective treatment indicates that the type of yellow pigment mattered: he considered certain materials more effective when used with green or when used on metals.

Lake Pigments

The author-practitioner suggests using stil de grain yellow, a lake pigment. Since the time of Pliny the Elder, who was one of the earliest in antiquity to write about lake pigments, artists had expanded beyond limited mineral pigments, experimenting with vegetable- and animal-based dyes to make lakes.12 To make a lake, artists typically take a white mineral powder (usually clay, aluminum, or calcite) as the pigment substrate and dye it, similar to dyeing a textile.13 Merrifield explains that artisans knew these extractions could be used for both pigments and fabric dye.14 Ms. Fr. 640 mentions dyeing a fabric yellow on fol. 15r, but it does not specify a color source.15 A similar recipe for giallo santo (a yellow lake made from weld) in the Paduan Manuscript, a codex likely written in the mid-seventeenth century, suggests using “the berries of buckthorn towards the end of the month of August,” while another giallo santo recipe in the eighteenth-century color manual Nuovo Plico calls for “the flowers of the Erba Lizza, Barbi de Becco (yellow goat’s beard).”16 Kirby et al. acknowledge that the “names of yellow lakes rarely give any clue as to the dyestuff content” and that while “schüttgelb, schietgelb [...] the Dutch schijtgeel” as well as “scudegrun” might relate to the pharmacological properties of the Rhamnus (buckthorn) berry, it also refers to a yellow color resembling excrement.17 Terms like schüttgrün and schüttgelb (called “stil de grain yellow” in English) have also been used interchangeably with “Dutch pink.” Recipe 64 from the Mayerne Manuscript (compiled 1620–1646) calls for “Dutch pink” (rendered as schüttgrün in Ernst Berger’s 1901 German translation) mixed with linseed oil and “turpentine varnish and oil of turpentine” for a “green varnish, like emerald.”18 Although no other contemporaneous source spells the term as the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 does, scudegrun is likely related to schüttgrün, schüttgelb, and other variations on the term for yellow lakes. In Ms. Fr. 640, there are two separate recipes for scudegrun, a term the author-practitioner mentions on fol. 158v but does not explain. Fol. 63v calls for “broom flower boiled well in water, putting in enough alum, then ceruse,” and fol. 10r states that scudegrun is made “from the juice of weld & chalk mixed together or better yet with ceruse.” Both weld (Reseda luteola) and dyer’s broom (Genista tinctoria) are plants commonly used for dyeing fabrics yellow, producing “rather acid lemon to brownish-yellow lake pigments.”19 When invoking scudegrun, the author-practitioner might be suggesting a hue of yellow lake pigments rather than a specific recipe.

Reconstruction

Drawing information about proportions of ingredients from the seventh chapter of the so-called Bolognese Manuscript (dated to the fifteenth century), which mostly deals with pigment-making, I adapted the following recipe for the reconstruction:

To make good and fine arzica Take 1 lb of weld, which the dyers use, cut it very fine, then put it into a glazed or tinned base, and add it to enough water to cover the herb. Make it boil until the water is half wasted, and if there is not enough water add a sufficient quantity and no more; then take 2 oz. travertine (q.v.) finely ground, or 2 oz. white lead, and a half oz. of roche alum ground very fine, then put all these things together a little at a time to boil in the vase directly, before the water cools, and stir the water continually, remove the vessel from the fire, and when nearly dry, pour off the water. Then take a new brick hollow in the middle, layer the arzica on it, and let it settle perfectly; then put it on a small and well polished board to dry, and it is done.20

Like the instructions on fol. 10r in Ms. Fr. 640, this recipe indicates that weld is a suitable plant base for making this pigment. The original Italian term for weld in the Bolognese Manuscript is gualda, a Spanish and Portuguese term that is also etymologically similar to the author-practitioner’s use of suc de gaulde on fol. 10v.

Similar yellow lake recipes consistently recommend chalk, white lead, or powdered eggshell as a substrate. Like Ms. Fr. 640, the Bolognese Manuscript recipe also suggests that travertine (a chalk-like mineral) and white lead can be used interchangeably. White lead as a substrate is mentioned in the Paduan Manuscript, which explains that “yellow is made with white lead and gialdo santo.”21 The Pigment Compendium explains that “the pigment is produced by strewing the whole dried plant in a weak solution of alum, then precipitating this with the addition of calcium sulfate, eggshell, or lead white,” adding that “the colour was most beautiful when somewhat opaque but that when precipitated onto eggshells or lead white it produced a very brilliant colour.”22 The National Gallery’s analysis of yellows in seventeenth-century paintings has revealed that they often contain chalk as a substrate for the yellow dye.23 In the Libro secondo de diversi colori, there is a recipe for “arzicha” which states, “take very well ground eggshells and put them in the hollow of a new brick. Take the weld herb of the textile dyers and let it boil in water with a little bit of alum. Pour it on the eggshells and thus make it as light or as dark as you wish.”24 This recipe is almost identical to the one in the Bolognese Manuscript, except it recommends precipitating onto “well ground eggshells.” In the reconstruction, I tested all three of these commonly mentioned substrates.

When testing the recipes, I had to make adjustments owing to the season because both weld and broomflower are harvested in the summer, and the author-practitioner likely referred to fresh plants.25 Air-drying weld reduces the total colorant content, but dried weld was the most readily available at this time of year (fall 2015).26 I first boiled the pouches of dried weld before adding potash alum and the substrate (Fig. 1). When I added eggshell and ceruse to their respective potash alum/weld liquids, a small amount of opaque foam formed, whereas when I added the chalk, the entire mixture turned an opaque yellow (Fig. 2, Fig. 3). The chalk, which was the most finely ground of the substrates, painted on smoothly with few particles visible on the gesso board (Fig. 4). In contrast, the eggshell was the least finely ground, since I ground it by hand in a mortar and pestle (Fig. 5). Although it initially seemed to be fine enough, it felt very gritty when I mixed it with the muller on the glass board, and it did not paint out smoothly (Fig. 6). The ceruse pigment turned greyish upon drying, darkening the color of the weld.27 When painted, it produced a dull light yellow color (Fig. 7). Although ceruse might be valued as a pigment base because of its desiccative properties (discussed below), it is uncertain if this process produced the desired hue of yellow. Further trials could help determine if this recipe consistently produces the same grey-tinged yellow.

The substrate made an obvious difference to the color tone of the weld pigments. The finely powdered chalk painted the most smoothly and produced the brightest yellow. The eggshell produced a darker yellow, but it was difficult to grind finely. In a recipe for rose-colored lake pigments from the Jehan le Begue manuscript, compiled in the fifteenth century, the author instructs the reader to “take egg-shells, pound them well, then grind them fine on a porphyry slab.”28 He separates the process into “pound” and “grind,” with the second step meant to refine the substrate. We re-ground the dyed eggshell and realized that a longer grinding time made the pigment fine enough to paint smoothly, leaving fewer large particles (Fig. 8). This showed us that workshop tasks like pigment making were time-consuming and required patience and muscular strength but seems tasks that might easily be delegated to apprentices or assistants. In Joannes Stradanus’s late sixteenth-century engraving of a print workshop, the grinding of pigment falls to the two well-muscled assistants rather than the young boys shown drawing at lower right of the print (Fig. 9).29

Another issue with yellow lakes is their light sensitivity. The Mayerne Manuscript includes a method for cleaning oil paintings by placing them in the sun, and it cautions that “places painted with schietgroen and lake will be ruined by the sun and water. For this reason, these parts of the painting are covered with glued paper. The same applies to the trees and other kinds of green that are made with schietgeel and masticot.”30 When I was testing the yellows, the pigments were painted on a gesso board, rather than onto metal casts, to first compare differences in the yellows and greens themselves and then to show the range in greens made available by adjusting the yellow content. Although the colors did not fade over the three to four weeks after they had been painted, long-term observation is needed to determine which—if any—are durable.

Ochers

Yellow ocher as paint dates to the Middle Paleolithic period (around 40,000 BCE), appearing at sites in Arcy-sur-Cure and Yonne in France.31 Although earth pigments such as ocher occur widely, “certain regions, including France and central Italy, were the source of yellow ochers of particularly good colour.”32 While ocher was traded between regions in Europe, the French ocher may have been more accessible to the author-practitioner than a foreign import, although he does not specify the origins of his ocher nor mention any variety in types. Cennino Cennini discusses working with ocher “found in the earth of mountains” and notes that “there are two kinds of ocher pigment: light and dark. Each colour [should be treated] the same way of mulling it with clear water. And mull it hard because it becomes more perfect all the time. And know that this ocher is a common pigment, particularly when working in fresco […] And this pigment is coarse in character.”33 These descriptions indicate a range of yellow ochers that can be used to achieve different yellow hues. Kremer Pigments carries two types of yellow ocher from France, Yellow Ochre Sahara and Burgundy Yellow Ochre, both of which I tried. Although the author-practitioner instructs us on fol. 158v to use oil for paints, I first mixed Yellow Ochre Sahara and Burgundy Yellow Ochre with water according to Cennini’s instructions.34 The Yellow Ochre Sahara produced a darker yellow with a more brownish tone compared to the Burgundy Yellow Ochre (Fig.10). As the author-practitioner instructs, I had to rely on my own “judgment & discretion” when mixing the ocher with verdigris and linseed oil to decide if the resulting green was suitable; I mixed the paint directly on the glass plate with the paintbrush and adjusted as I painted (Fig. 11). The ocher oil paints were more opaque and produced a richer color tone than the lake oil paints.

Massicot (Yellow Lead)

At the beginning of the fourteenth century, a new yellow derived from tin appeared in northern Europe and was known in Flanders as masticote. This lead-tin oxide “had the brightness of orpiment but was chemically neutral with regard to other pigments (some lead-based paints turn other colors gray) and only mildly toxic.”35 Cennini also discusses a color called giallorino, annotated as massicot, which is lighter than ocher and “is a very beautiful yellow colour. And lovely greenery and grass colour is made from this pigment with other mixtures.”36 Thompson believes that “probably most of the references to giallorino in medieval writings mean massicot, a yellow oxide of lead, prepared in the Middle Ages by gentle roasting of orange lead as an intermediate product in the preparation of white lead.”37 Later, in a section titled “The way to paint trees and grasses and greenery in fresco and in secco,” Cennini tells artisans to “make a green with giallorino which should be a bit lighter and do some leaves […] Then touch in the lighter bits on the crests just with giallorino and you will see the reliefs on the trees and on the greenery.”38 Perhaps using massicot in painting plants was common practice and the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 adapted it to color metal casts in a lifelike way.

Also evident in contemporaneous recipes is the range of methods artists used for making these pigments. In the Paduan Manuscript, the author includes a recipe for “straw” color: “take lead yellow (massicot), wash it with a very strong and clean lye, then decant the lye, and distemper with the colour with parchment glue.”39 Unlike Cennini, who says that the massicot “needs to be ground […] with clear water” and is good with temperas, the Paduan manuscript suggests lye and parchment glue binder.40 It seems clear that practitioners were adjusting the ingredients, suspension media, and binders to suit the material onto which they applied the paint. For example, while mixing pigments with water and tempera might be good for fresco painting, oil would be a more suitable medium for metal casts.

When I mixed ready-made massicot with linseed oil, it turned into a pale yellow paint. Like the two French ochers, the Kremer massicot pigment was already finely ground and needed only a few minutes of being worked with the muller to become usable (Fig. 12). When painted, the massicot is actually very thin, pale, and translucent, much more so than the ocher oil paints. When combined with the verdigris, it makes a sunny bright green that is translucent and pale in tone (Fig. 13). I also tested the massicot in combination with minium, or red lead paint, because the author-practitioner tells us that this would be a suitable color for marigolds (fol. 158v): “for marigold flowers, lightly ground minium for some, & for others which are more yellowish, a bit of massicot with it” (Fig. 14). The ground minium was a bright, neon red that turned into a light orange when mixed with the massicot. The author-practitioner could blend these two colors together to convincingly imitate the marigold colors, especially since the mixed pigments darkened as they dried.

Perhaps part of the appeal of using massicot was that it would help the oil paint dry more quickly.41 The author-practitioner was aware of this property, mentioning on fol. 59v that “lead white & massicot are the most desiccative, nevertheless they need a good two days.” The color of massicot, although also based on white lead, is very different from that deriving from the weld pigment precipitated onto white lead. If the author-practitioner wanted a bright-looking yellow, he might have considered massicot superior to weld on ceruse because of ceruse’s tendency to turn gray. In the final view of all the painted and dried pigments, each yellow has a distinct hue and tone, and it modifies the green, giving the author-practitioner an expanded palette with which to work (Fig. 15).

Painting on Metal Casts

At the same time that we tested massicot, minium, and weld-ceruse pigments, we also painted directly onto a metal cast of lead-tin alloy that had been made in the Spring 2015 Craft and Science lab seminar.42 Although we considered the green colors reasonably lifelike, the paints did not apply smoothly to the metal and slid off easily, perhaps because there was too much oil in our paint mixture. We realized after conducting the trial that the author-practitioner actually warned us on fol. 129r in “Painting plants of metal” that

If you are in a hurry, it would be better to mix your color with a not-too-thick gum or because oil takes a long time to dry and runs if it is layered thinly, as the plants require. And if you still want it done promptly, temper your color with glair beaten with peelings of the fig tree, and your work will soon be dry. But layer it thinly.

Despite our not having heeded author-practitioner’s advice here, our paint was sufficiently translucent to leave the features of the metal visible, as he instructed (Fig. 16).43

While this method of cold painting might be convincing at a distance, it is inferior in appearance to other methods of coloring metal, such as with vitreous enamel, a technique that existed in the sixteenth century. Sixteenth-century metalworking experts Vannoccio Biringuccio and Benvenuto Cellini both wrote about firing enamel on metal. Biringuccio considered enameling a difficult art, stating, “there are few in our regions who can perform this well because they are dependent on enamels and on the thing they wish to enamel, for each kind of gold, silver, or copper requires enamel harmonious to its nature, else it does not respond.”44

Another sixteenth-century method for coloring metal casts was cold enameling—a misleading term since the technique actually refers to paint that is applied directly to the metal rather than a vitreous enamel applied through heat. Neither Biringuccio nor Cellini wrote about “cold enameling,” which was used as an alternative to traditional enameling.45 Even though this method of coloring metal is less durable than fire enameling, a number of examples still survive in museums. Tamar Davidowitz, who has written extensively on the surface paint on Jamnitzer’s Merkel table piece in the Rijksmuseum, notes that paint is often found with silver substrates because silver, unlike gold or copper, “does not diminish the brightness of translucent paints.”46 Not only was enameling a difficult skill, as Biringuccio mentions, but paint was also a cheaper alternative that could be used on objects that were difficult to enamel in a traditional way. In addition, very thin casts, or those with a low melting point, could easily melt during the enamel firing, making cold painting more suitable.47 In comparison with the rim of the basin on the Merkel table piece (Fig. 17), our paint on the metal cast is less even. The leaf on the Merkel table piece shows different shades of green and yellow, perhaps indicating that the paint was layered to achieve opacity and an even tone as well as a more naturalistic appearance. However, on both our painted metal cast and Jamnitzer’s table piece, the details of the cast as well as the muted shine of metal were still visible under the translucent paint.

Conclusion

The challenge of finding a light-fast, suitable yellow encouraged artisans to experiment with a broad range of materials and methods: each yellow pigment produced a paint with very different qualities of texture, opacity, and color. While we found it frustrating to work with the author-practitioner’s inexact language, by following his instructions as closely as possible, we were able to better understand his methods of measuring and judging results and to suggest ways in which his written records reflected his embodied knowledge. For example, because he was not specific about how to mix the paints, we had to rely on our own experience and understanding of color to make a suitable shade of green to approximate living plants. In addition, with the wide regional variation in materials, extremely precise instructions might not result in the desired effect in any case. We realized that his inexactness was not simply an omission. On fol. 158v the author-practitioner instructs us to use our “judgment & discretion” when applying the colors, showing an understanding of the role of variety in available materials, personal preference, and workshop variability in craftwork. What the author-practitioner offers instead of standardized materials and modern quantitative rigor is a set of guidelines. As in other color making recipes, there is an expectation that other practitioners will adjust and experiment, tailoring the recipe to their own needs.

Our experiments focused on the issues surrounding the yellows that the author-practitioner found suitable for mixing with green and for painting on metal. His suggestion to use the charcoal of peach pits or the “black of ox foot bone” to darken the green warrants further exploration. In addition, the technique of cold painting on metal also merits further study. Further scientific analysis could show the correlation between instructions in Ms. Fr. 640 and extant artworks. For example, Davidowitz was unable to explain the presence of chalk in the yellow paint, but perhaps her results suggest that the yellow was a lake pigment precipitated onto chalk, as the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 recommended.48 The author-practitioner did not invent new techniques for pigments (nor for the various methods he uses to make yellow). However, as one of the few surviving texts that records techniques of painting on metal, his recipes demonstrate that traditional pigment making practices could be adapted to a new medium. As other research essays have pointed out, this kind of versatility and experimentation based on practice and experience was valued in early modern artisanal workshops.49 Adapting painting techniques to metal exemplifies the way in which craftspeople were building on their learned knowledge—and the knowledge passed to them from previous generations—to invent and expand their repertoire.
