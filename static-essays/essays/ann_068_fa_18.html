Abstract

A three-line entry on fol. 15v notes the material qualities, ornamentation, and use of metalpoint tablets or “writing tables.” This essay seeks to understand the physical characteristics of the tablet that the author-practitioner had in mind and the context in which he might have used it. The essay surveys the history of tablets, from ancient and medieval wooden tablets inlaid with wax to early modern varieties made of coated paper and parchment for writing and metalpoint drawing. Several contemporary manuscripts feature recipes for a ground that could be applied to different supports, written on with a metal stylus, erased, and reused. As the author-practitioner makes no mention of any ground, the reconstruction examines possible reasons for such an omission. The essay speculates that the author-practitioner may have used a tablet like the one he describes—perhaps an erasable notebook made by an expert in which he could record his observations temporarily before he transferred them to his final manuscript.

Introduction

For the twenty-first-century reader, “tablet” is a multivalent word, evoking objects from Moses’s stone tablets inscribed with the Ten Commandments, to the ancient wooden tablets excavated in the last two decades in South Korea,1 to modern computer tablets in all shapes and sizes. The common denominator of all these objects is their flat surface for writing. The same holds true for the author-practitioner’s entry on “tablets,” which is a three-line description of some features of a flat writing surface, including the materials that constitute it, its ornamentation and its use.

Tablets

Some are made from boxwood inlaid with ebony, sandalwood, ivory, or gold and silver like damascening. Then one writes on it with silverpoint, and next erases it with a cuttlefish bone by rubbing them (fol. 15v).

Although seemingly straightforward, the entry raises questions about the object’s construction, the functionality of its writing surface, and why it was included in BnF Ms. Fr. 640. This essay aims to understand the meaning of the word “tablet” as it was understood by the author-practitioner in sixteenth-century France. (Fig. 1)

Randle Cotgrave’s A Dictionarie of the French and English Tongues defines tablette as “[a] little table, or boord; also a childes horne-booke,” and the plural, tablettes, as “writing tables.”2 The Oxford English Dictionary records historical definitions of the word “table,” among them, “a small portable tablet for writing upon, esp. for notes or memoranda; a writing tablet,”3 while it defines “tablet” as “a smooth stiff sheet for writing on, usually one of two or more fastened together, originally made of clay or wax-covered wood, later of ivory, cardboard, etc.; a number of such sheets fastened together.”4 The close connection between the two terms—“table” and “tablet”—demonstrates that they were used interchangeably, to designate a flat surface for writing on.

A comparison of the entry to others in Ms. Fr. 640 reveals its singularity. This is the only time that the author-practitioner mentions the term “silverpoint” (poincte dargent)—a silver stylus that was used for writing and drawing and which is associated with an artistic technique that flourished in Renaissance Europe.5 In addition, this is the only use of wood as a support for writing; other entries that refer to writing mention different types of support, such as paper,6 parchment,7 and even black leather shoes.8 The recipe also offers a unique use of cuttlefish bone for erasing; other entries of the manuscript mention it as a mold for casting.9 Moreover, the author-practitioner does not discuss the process of making a tablet, but only describes its appearance. He does not elaborate on the technique of drawing with silverpoint, or any other metalpoint, nor does he mention that using a metalpoint necessitates preparing the support with a ground, as other contemporary authors do. The details that the author-practitioner discloses, as well as those which he neglects to include, raise questions about the degree of his acquaintance with the use of tablets and the technique of metalpoint drawing. Exploring the historical composition of tablets, their material qualities, and the different contexts in which they were used, in addition to reconstructing the recipe, will assist us in understanding the place of the recipe within Ms. Fr. 640 as well as within early modern European culture.

Historical Context: Tablets

Textual, archeological, and visual evidence demonstrates that tablets were a common writing surface from the late Bronze-Age Near East through Greek and Latin antiquity. Ancient tablets were usually made of wood, had a small raised frame around the edges of the tablet and often featured a recessed center containing a thin layer of wax; they are therefore often referred to in the literature as “wax tablets.” The supporting material had to be sturdy enough to provide a firm base for the wax layer and sustain the action of writing but lightweight enough to be portable. From antiquity, boxwood was preferred for this purpose because of its fine, dense grain. Other tablets that did not feature a wax layer were made of a light-colored support or were colored with a white substance such as white pigment wash, gesso, plaster, or stucco to allow for ink writing to stand out (I will refer to them as “whitened tablets”). Tablets could be joined together to form diptychs and polyptychs, but they could also be used alone. Their sizes varied, from small and medium-size tablets that could be held in the hand, to large tablets that had to be balanced on the thigh while writing upon them.10 (Fig. 2)

Wax tablets were written on with styluses made from hard materials such as bone, ivory, horn, wood, iron, or precious metals, which were capable of incising marks into the wax. Marks could be erased by heating the wax and smoothing the surface, a quality that allowed wax tablets to be reused. Ink writing on whitened tablets could also be erased by applying another layer of white ground to cover it. Consequently, both types of tablets served for ephemeral activities: to learn and practice writing, to write drafts, lists, and accounts that would be later transferred to a permanent support, or to send a letter and receive an immediate reply on the same surface. Only in Mesopotamia were wax tablets used as the final support for literary texts, as demonstrated by the Nimrud tablets that were discovered in excavations in 1953.11

Wax tablets made of boxwood, bone, ivory or metal continued to be widely used in medieval Europe. Sets of tablets hinged together usually had all interior leaves coated with green, red or yellow wax on both sides. (Fig. 3) Still serving as they did in antiquity for ephemeral activities, medieval tablets were also used to make drafts of musical compositions and were a central pedagogical instrument for teaching children the alphabet. Andrea Morgan argues that “wax tablets were alternatives to expensive parchment, thus allowing everyday people to participate in the act of writing.”12 Peter Stallybrass and his co-authors argue that the decline of wax tablets coincided with the increased use of paper in Europe. While wax tablets were still common in England and Germany in the sixteenth century, they were rare in Spain and Italy, where the paper industry developed earlier. Although paper eventually became cheaper than wax tablets, it was not sufficiently inexpensive to displace more durably erasable materials until the invention of wood-pulp paper in the nineteenth century. From the sixteenth century on, erasable paper or parchment tablets (or “tables”) came to the fore.13

“Tables,” “table-books,” or “writing tables” were an alternative to wax tablets in early modern Europe. Essentially, they were portable notebooks or sketchbooks of blank paper or parchment leaves coated with a ground of gesso and glue, and they could be written on, erased, and then reused. (Fig. 4) These tables were usually bound by a leather cover, and some were conveniently clasped shut with a metal stylus. Frequently, although not always, white-coated leaves were bound into printed texts for note taking.14 (Fig. 5) Writing tables bound with printed texts were manufactured and sold on a large scale especially in the Low Countries and Germany. The earliest writing table that Stallybrass et al. have traced was manufactured in 1527 in Antwerp, and bound with a printed almanac.15 From the late 1520s, tables were imported to England, and by the 1580s they were being published in London in multiple editions.16 Writing tablets often included charts of weights, measurements, and distances, pictures of coins, and calculation instructions; these proved especially useful for merchants, who—as Cennino d’Andrea Cennini mentions in his Libro dell’Arte of 1390—were users of coated-parchment tablets.17 Nevertheless, writing tables could be purchased by all literate social groups thanks to their wide range in price, and they were used for various purposes, as were the wax tablets mentioned above. Some writing tables were very simple, while others featured luxurious bindings, which included gold, silver, and precious stones; the latter were popular as gifts among the elite.18 (Fig. 6)

Writing tables were convenient because they could be used on the go. Until the fountain pen was invented in the mid-seventeenth century, writing on paper with ink required a quill pen and an open ink container; it was therefore impossible while standing or travelling, or without an organized writing setup.19 Writing tablets, by contrast, needed only a metal stylus, usually a silverpoint, to write on its prepared leaves of paper or parchment, which rendered ink and its inconveniences unnecessary. The traces that metalpoint styluses left on a table could be easily erased, thus making it reusable.20 (Fig. 7) Is it possible that the author-practitioner referred to this type of writing table in his entry? Before we turn to this question, it is important to discuss more thoroughly the term “metalpoint” and its relation to tablets.

Historical Context: Metalpoint

The term “metalpoint” refers not only to a thin metal stylus, but also to the technique of drawing with it and the image that it produces. The technique of metalpoint drawing is associated with the Italian Renaissance, although it was also practiced intermittently in Northern Europe between the mid-fourteenth and the eighteenth centuries.21 (Fig. 8) In fact, metalpoint drawing is still practiced today. Metalpoint styluses could be made of silver, copper, lead, and a variety of alloys. The composition of the metal affects the properties of the stylus, the way its traces “flow” on paper, and the tone it creates. Strictly speaking, the term “silverpoint,” therefore, refers to a stylus made of silver, or at least one with a silver-coated tip. Since pure silver was usually too soft, an alloy of silver and copper or mercury was commonly used.22

The technique of metalpoint was used on different supports: wooden tablets, parchment, and paper. Crucially, in order that a metalpoint drawing appear on its support, its surface had to be prepared with a special ground, typically made of calcined bone and an adhesive substance. Such a ground was usually white but could be tinted by the addition of a pigment. (Fig. 9) Thea Burns explains that “grounds are necessary for drawing in silverpoint because the surface of unprepared paper is not sufficiently abrasive to detach from the drawing stylus the fine deposit of silver particles that constitutes the distinctive drawn line.”23 However, a sufficiently soft metal, such as lead, will deposit a line on an uncoated paper as well.24

Metalpoint drawings on ivory or wooden tablets, coated with a white ground instead of wax, survive from the late Middle Ages onward.25 Ernst van de Wetering argues that these tablets were used between the fourteenth and seventeenth centuries, mainly as erasable supports for apprentice artists to practice on. Compared to the dark color of wax, the white ground on the wooden tablets, drawn on with metal styluses that created dark lines, must have been experienced as a great improvement, he presumes.26 Van de Wetering’s thesis is supported by the first couple of pages from Cennini’s Libro dell’Arte, which are devoted to the preparation of a white ground made of bone ash mixed with saliva to be applied to either a boxwood panel, a panel of fig wood, or a piece of parchment, and then to be drawn on with a metal stylus. The location of this recipe at the beginning of Cennini’s book suggests that this is the most basic technique used by beginner artists. Cennini suggests that one should practice this technique for about a year before one turns to draw with a pen on paper.27 Van de Wetering assumes that Cennini took for granted that the ground could be easily removed and the support recoated to be reused by the apprentice.28

Yet metalpoint drawing was also practiced by master artists, who exploited the technique for its particular advantages. Art historians have characterized metalpoint as “a linear medium often used to capture subtle effects of light and render forms precisely with lines of astonishing delicacy.”29 Skilled artists often combined metalpoint with other materials at their disposal, such as pen and ink, brush and color washes, chalks, and charcoal. (Fig. 10) Burns argues that some artists may have chosen metalpoint in an attempt to reconstruct what they believed to be the appearances of ancient drawings. Since no artifactual evidence of drawing from antiquity existed in the early modern period, artists sought their traces in other sources, such as in ancient literature, on decorated vases, and on metal and stone artifacts.30

The numerous surviving recipes for a basic white ground for tablets, which appear in manuscripts from the period, suggest that this technique was common. Some recipes refer directly to the erasability of the surface, supporting van de Wetering’s assumption that Cennini’s ground could be easily removed. The Secretum philosophorum, a popular Latin text originating in England c.1300-1350, includes a recipe “to make white tablets,” which explains how to apply a mixture of linseed oil and white lead onto calf parchment, and when

this [is] done, shape from these leaves: make tablets of whatever size you wish, and use them as you please. And know that you may write on these tablets with a stylus of lead or of tin or of silver, just like on other tablets. You may also write on them with ink, just like on parchment, and erase the letters with saliva.31

This recipe was followed by one for glue, perhaps implying that such whitened parchment was to be adhered to a wooden support.32 A version of the Secretum philosophorum recipe for white ground was included in a Bavarian manuscript approximately a century later.33 At the end of the fourteenth century, Johannes Alcherius recorded a recipe “to prepare parchment, or paper, primed panels, and linen, … to draw upon them in black, with a pencil or stile [stylus) of gold, silver … as is done upon panels of boxwood,” which explains how to make a mixture of white bone ash and size or glue to coat a surface for drawing.34 Another formula appears in the manuscript of the physician at the English court, Theodore de Mayerne (1573-1655), this time for preparing paper with a mixture of bone ash and gum arabic to be written on with silverpoint and erased using a brush and water.35 These recipes demonstrate the interchangeability of the different supports: the same ground could be applied to wood, parchment, and paper. Similarly, a tablet could be made of any one (or more) of these supports. In addition, the regular appearance of such recipes in manuscript compilations of artists’ materials and techniques suggests that preparing a support for a tablet was quite a usual process in artisanal workshops, in which materials such as bone ash and adhesive substances were regularly available.

Because of their erasable surfaces for drafting and drawing, surviving tablets have been interpreted as either model books or sketchbooks. Van de Wetering has analyzed a curious series of artifacts: small pieces of parchment prepared on both sides, which appear to have been cut from a larger whole and often feature a meticulous metalpoint drawing on one side. He argues that in most of the cases, the drawings were only partially finished, with the head of the figure extremely elaborated and the rest only scantily defined. Some of these were finished by a later hand, though some bear traces of earlier use. These pieces were most likely parts of tablets, small booklets, which could be carried in the artist’s pocket and used to make drafts or quick notes when outside of the workshop.36 Leonardo da Vinci recommended using such a notebook to record quick body movements:

for this reason be sure to take with you a little book with pages prepared with bonemeal, and with a silverpoint briefly note the gestures of the bystanders and how they are grouped. This will teach you how to compose narrative paintings.37

In addition to the aforementioned parchment pieces, wooden tablets coated with a white ground and bound into a sketchbook survive and were considered for many years to be model books, “an archive of stock pictorial exemplars … made available for craftsmen in workshops for use in various artistic formats.”38 Van de Wetering argues that these wooden tablets were identified erroneously and are in fact sketchbooks containing unfinished sketches.39 Whether such tablets were model books or sketchbooks, portability, in addition to reusability, seems to have been an important part of a tablet’s function, whether to capture impressions of ephemeral phenomena or to circulate models within and perhaps even between artists’ workshops. (Fig. 11)

Reconstruction

The tablet that the author-practitioner refers to on fol. 15v is made of boxwood and inlaid with precious metals and expensive materials, such as ebony and ivory. The author-practitioner does not describe the shape of the tablet: is it a single leaf, a diptych, or a booklet? Does the boxwood function as an exterior cover for paper or parchment leaves? Or is boxwood the main support on which writing would be carried out? The details that the author-practitioner gives are not sufficient to determine the answer. Is the author-practitioner’s tablet more similar to the premade writing tables that could be purchased or received as a gift, or is it more like the erasable support made by apprentices in their masters’ workshops? The language of the author-practitioner, “some are made from boxwood” (fol. 15v), may suggest that he only observed other people’s tablets and had not used one himself. The lack of information about grounds could point to his superficial knowledge of the technique. On the other hand, the prevalence of ground recipes in other contemporary sources makes it difficult to imagine that the author-practitioner was unaware of the need for a coating; it seems more likely that he took this for granted. Another possibility, which I explore in my reconstruction, is that, contrary to the scholarly literature, a ground was not always necessary for writing with silverpoint, and therefore the author-practitioner did not mention it.

The main purpose of my reconstruction was to understand the physical characteristics of the tablet that the author-practitioner had in mind and the context in which he might have used it. As a starting point, I decided to conduct the investigation along two trajectories, each relying on a different assumption. The first one considered that the author-practitioner’s description of the components of a tablet was accurate and sufficient, and that one could use a tablet exactly as he describes it. The second hypothesis was that the author-practitioner neglected to mention important aspects of the tablet and its use, either because he was not aware of them or because he took them for granted. Since the manuscript entry in question is not a typical instructional recipe but more of a general description, the reconstruction required extracting details from the entry as well as from external sources.

Following the first trajectory, I assumed that writing would be carried out on the boxwood itself and that the other expensive materials (sandalwood, ebony, ivory, gold, and silver) would serve to decorate the frame or the exterior of the tablet. Since the author-practitioner mentions no ground, I examined the viability of writing directly on the boxwood block. The second trajectory led me to examine two additional options: 1) that a ground would have been applied to the boxwood, or 2) that additional leaves would have been added to the boxwood, e.g., coated paper or parchment, to assemble a “writing table.” Consequently, this second examination included applying a ground to boxwood, paper, and parchment. The reconstruction consisted of three stages: preparing the support, writing on it, and erasing it.

Two recipes for a white ground were chosen for comparison, based on the recipes of Cennini (Ground 1) and Alcherius (Ground 2), mentioned above. Both called for the use of bone ash, while the adhesive substance varied between saliva (Ground 1) and rabbit skin glue (Ground 2). I had two boxwood blocks on which I could examine the application of ground. Consequently, I was able to use one of them to test Cennini’s instructions to wash the boxwood panel and rub it with cuttlefish bone before applying the ground.40 This step proved to be valuable for the reconstruction results, detailed below. I divided the two boxwood blocks in a way that allowed me to apply a ground to two thirds of each block’s surface, and leave one third without a ground (Fig. 12). In addition, I prepared three pieces of parchment so that I had one coated with each of the two grounds and one with no ground. I did the same with three pieces of paper.41 The next step was to write on each support with two types of silver—pure silver and sterling silver.42 Afterward, I erased all drawn surfaces with two materials: bread and cuttlefish bone.43 I repeated the process of writing and erasing on the same supports to test my first observations and to examine the materials’ durability. As a final step to verify my results, I repeated the process of applying Ground 1 (saliva and bone ash) on boxwood and parchment, wrote on them, and erased them again with both media.

The results of the experiment were surprising: I discovered that I was able to write on each of the supports, with or without a ground, although the amount of force I had to use to obtain a mark on the support and the quality of the results differed.44 (Fig. 13) (Fig. 14) (Fig. 15) The most convenient support for writing and drawing with either silver stylus was parchment coated with Ground 1. As to the coated surfaces, generally, Ground 1 (saliva and bone ash) was easier to make and apply; it was equally easy to remove by both cuttlefish bone and bread. Ground 2 (rabbit skin glue and bone ash) was more difficult to produce, since it required heating and waiting for the layers of ground to dry. In addition, its consistency and texture as a ground did not make writing easy, and I had to use much more force to write on it. However, I presume that my inexperience preparing the ground and achieving the ideal consistency might have affected my results; further iterations of making and applying Ground 2 might improve its function as a support for metalpoint writing. Nevertheless, Ground 2, as I prepared it, was more difficult to erase; neither the silver marks nor the ground itself were easily removed by erasing. (Fig. 16)

As to the uncoated surfaces, writing with silver on the flesh side of a piece of uncoated parchment was not only convenient but also most effective. On the hair side of the parchment, the silver left only colorless scratches. Writing with silver on paper was not easy, although pure silver, which is softer, created silver marks better than the silver alloy. As to the use of silverpoint on boxwood, writing on a clean block seemed almost impossible, and the lines that I managed to create were visible only from a certain angel of light. In contrast, writing on a clean block that had been rubbed with cuttlefish bone (per Cennini) was much easier, as if the cuttlefish bone particles functioned as a ground. Nevertheless, in my overall experience, writing was still most comfortable and effective with Ground 1. (Fig. 17) (Fig. 18)

Conclusion

The results of the reconstruction confirmed that both hypotheses are viable: following the author-practitioner’s spare description could yield results, but supplementing information from other sources (namely, the need to coat the support with a ground) also led to a successful outcome. The author-practitioner mentions cuttlefish bone in the entry. It is possible that he used cuttlefish bone both as a simple ground (through dry rubbing on the surface of the boxwood) and an erasing medium in order to write directly on a boxwood block. At the same time, I imagine that using the technique of applying bone ash mixed with saliva (Ground 1) on any support would have been very easy and convenient for any apprentice or artisan in the workshop. Although the author-practitioner does not mention it in the “tablets” entry, bones in general and calcined bones in particular appear in the manuscript numerous times.45 This strengthens the suggestion that the author-practitioner took a bone ash ground for granted. Ground 2, on the other hand, seems more durable and suitable for a notebook in which the leaves are prepared in advance and are easy to write on quickly. Although it is possible that artisans made their own erasable notebooks, the unskilled person could turn to an expert to make and even repair his or her tablet, as is evident from the title page of a Flemish almanac:

you may write here with a stylus of gold, silver, tin, copper, or brass, and you may erase [what you have written] with a wet finger. And when you have worn out [the erasable surface], so that you cannot write on it any more, you can get it repaired by Jan Severszoon, parchment maker, for a little money, and you can then write on it as well as if it were new.46

It is possible that the author-practitioner used a premade notebook, and hence never dealt with applying a ground.

Although all these scenarios are equally plausible, my intuition based on the historical research and hands-on experiment leads me to favor the conclusion that the author-practitioner used a premade writing tablet that he purchased or received as a gift. If he were interested in practicing metalpoint drawing on a reusable support, it would have made sense to use any of the recipes mentioned above (and especially Ground 1) to recoat the tablet himself. However, this entry is the only time he mentions silverpoint in the manuscript, and in the context of writing rather than drawing. It seems more plausible that the entry refers to one of these widespread portable notebooks named “writing tables.” Such a notebook could have served him to record his observations quickly and ephemerally, before he transferred them to his final, clean support. Moreover, the author-practitioner dedicates almost half of the entry to describing the luxurious appearance of the tablet, which might indicate that he was more interested in its decoration than its practical use. As I mentioned earlier, writing tables with luxurious bindings were common as gifts among the elite. (Fig. 19) It is conceivable that the author-practitioner had received such a tablet as a prestigious present from a patron, or that he was used to pulling one out of his pocket to record quickly the results of his investigations. Has the “tablets” recipe given us a peek into the life of the author-practitioner? This question, like many others, will remain open.
