Imitation Rubies and Failure in Ms. Fr. 640

By , DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/7qxw-2832

Abstract

A recipe for ruby on fols. 100v–101r specifies mixing gold leaf with materials for making glass and heating to a temperature higher than for any other (imitation) gemstone. The author-practitioner failed at making ruby, as did most seekers of “gold ruby” glass, including multiple cohorts of Making and Knowing participants who reconstructed the process detailed in Ms. Fr. 640. This essay considers the crucial role of “imitation” and “failure” in stimulating the investigation of and experimentation on natural materials and processes.

Ruby

The recipe to make “ruby” on fols. 100v–101r is one of several in Ms. Fr. 640 for making imitation gemstones. The author-practitioner’s interest in imitating precious and semi-precious stones such as rubies, emeralds, topaz, jacinth, diamonds, jasper, coral, among other stones, underscores that mimesis—and more specifically, material mimesis—was not just the aesthetic goal of artisans in representing nature in general, but more significantly, it was a mode of work by which processes of nature were explored and harnessed to produce new, more workable and useful materials.1

Rubies are first mentioned in Ms. Fr. 640 on fol. 8v in a brief explanation of how to polish a balas ruby (polissement du ruby balay).2 The treatment of the ruby on fol. 8v concerns a genuine balas ruby, a semiprecious stone ranging in color from deep crimson or purple to pale rose, now classified as a kind of spinel. The only other instance of ruby in the manuscript is that on fols. 100v-101r.

Vannoccio Biringuccio, the author of the 1540 text on metalworking, De la pirotechnia, describes the ruby as native to Libya and India; a lustrous transparent stone, whose color is “full red, dry, and not unctuous, but very brilliant.” He regards it as a stone not quite as hard as a diamond and notes that “speculative physicians, judging by its color, say that its nature is hot.”3 Such an understanding of the ruby presumably helped to inform the author-practitioner’s aim in his entry for making ruby on fols. 100v–101r. The recipe for making ruby is part of an unusually cohesive set of nine recipes on glass and stone making processes (fols. 100r–101v), and, like the other recipes in this set, the author-practitioner seems to regard it as a glass process, but his title for these recipes is “pierrerie,” meaning not “imitation gemstones,” but simply “gemstones.” We discuss how the author-practitioner might have understood these glass making procedures in relation to genuine gemstones in our essay on “Gemstones and Imitation.”4

Failure

The author-practitioner failed to make a ruby, as did we in our reconstructions, for reasons of historical and practical interest. This essay examines both the author-practitioner’s aspiration to make a colored glass ruby by using gold, and his failure to do so, in the context of a very long history of failure and experimentation stimulated by a millennia-long search for imitation ruby.

Like most other entries in the manuscript, the author-practitioner’s account leaves out much that would be useful to a historian: where did he obtain real rubies? How convincing did he expect these glass gemstones to be? For what purpose were they used? Were they intended for sale, and if so, in what markets? Was their status as glass gems openly acknowledged? On the other hand, as explained in our other essays on gemstones in this edition, the entries on Pierrerie are relatively clearly written, and contain—for Ms. Fr. 640—quite detailed information on ingredients, equipment, and process. The nine entries are evidently informed by sequential experimentation, and thus form an unusually coherent set of recipes in Ms. Fr. 640.5 Moreover, the entry on ruby is written in the format of an already well-established recipe genre, which typically contains the imperative Recipe (abbreviated as Rx) or “Take”: “For ruby take some gold leaf (Pour ruby prens de lor en foeille)” (fol. 100v). The author-practitioner thereby immediately establishes a relationship between these two precious materials, ruby and gold, intimating that from gold something approximating a ruby can be made. The gold to be used is identified as the “gold leaf of what the painters gild,” but this is qualified in a marginal note: “Others say that it is necessary that the gold be cemented several times, then beaten into leaf.”6

The recipe specifies to “take some gold leaf,” then abruptly returns to a discussion of furnace construction started in the recipe for pierrerie just above it, on which the ruby process builds: “Raise your furnace by two tiles all around, because one needs more heat for making rubies than for emeralds” (fol. 100v). Like the furnace illustrated on fol. 100v, the author-practitioner illustrates these new furnace instructions with a sketch in the left margin of fol. 101r that shows tiles propped up against one another, captioned as if speaking to the reader: “If you need greater heat, put, on the edge of the opening of the glowing furnace, tiles, one against the other, for making the heat reverberate” (Fig. 1). It is not so unusual in the gemstone recipes that the author-practitioner returns to the previous recipe, for this set of nine recipes between fol. 100r and fol. 101v refer to each other constantly, in matters of ingredients—they all include the same basic ingredients, calcined quartz pebbles and minium (red lead)—and in their comparisons of heat and cooking times across the recipes. This method of cross-referencing between different gemstone recipes is apparently not uncommon among other contemporary compilations, whereby the reader is instructed “it is made, like rubies,” or “proceed as if for ruby.”7

In our reconstruction (which necessitated multiple substitutions), rather than raising “our furnace by two tiles,” we removed the top of our furnace, constructed of three firebricks, to bring the crucible into more direct heat and to reduce the size of the firing chamber.8 The recipe then instructs the reader to cover the crucible “with an appropriate tile, a bit thick, in order that heat reverberates better there,” then specifies further furnace conditions: “fill your furnace to the opening with charcoal, so that it will be heaped, and let it kindle, and always maintain the heat evenly without letting the charcoal decrease. And for this effect, always make it so that the furnace is heaped & full of charcoal, and maintain it thus for one day.”9 We did not heat the crucible for a full day, but we did cover the top of the furnace in order to increase the temperature. We left the crucible itself uncovered in the furnace to better observe changes while heating the mixture for a period of about five minutes, which was long enough to more or less melt the contents of calcined quartz (“white calcined pebbles”) and minium in a 1:3 ratio, ground together with a small amount of gold leaf (Fig. 2).10

The author-practitioner succinctly records his experience at this point: “The first experience that I made of it, it only returned a yellow mass, as is vitrified minium alone, & some grains of gold in a mass at the bottom” (fol. 101r). This precisely matches our first (and only attempt) at making ruby. Like the author-practitioner, we were left with gold pieces suspended in a slightly yellowed substance enclosed in a layer of glass at the bottom of the crucible (Fig. 3).

A year after this first failure, the Making and Knowing Project collaborated with the Rhode Island School of Design (RISD) Glass Department to reconstruct the process for making ruby with the goal of approximating more faithfully the original conditions, with regard to the furnace, charcoal, and a full day’s heating (Vid. 1).11

Project members incorporated the ingredients in the amounts specified in the ruby recipe (1 oz. calcined quartz, 3 oz. red lead, 1 grain leaf gold, and potash) on fols. 100v–101r, using, as instructed, a glass mortar and pestle (Fig. 4, Fig. 5). RISD faculty and students constructed two different furnaces to reach sufficiently high temperatures (Fig. 6, Fig. 7), and we arranged the tiles (Fig. 8) as instructed on the margin of fol. 101r (see Fig. 1), which did in fact raise the heat significantly.

All this very intensive work and study, and our many trials and experiments led to a perfect and faithful reconstruction result: like the author-practitioner, all our trials for ruby failed (Fig. 9, Fig. 10).

The author-practitioner’s failure unleashed a torrent of reflecting, hypothesizing, extending, experimenting, and apparently failing again: He begins this intensive activity immediately after his first failure, recording, with emphasis, a note to himself: “Try cemented gold and accompanied with antimony.” Looking again more closely at the notes in the left margin on fol. 100v, where the ruby recipe begins, it is clear that both statements are in a different ink, and probably were added after his first failed experience: “Some say that, mixing the gold with the pebbles and the saltpeter, it makes the color of a peach tree,” and “Others say that it is necessary that the gold be cemented several times, then beaten into leaf” (fol. 100v). Moreover, his directive to “Raise your furnace by two tiles all around, because one needs more heat for making rubies than for emeralds” seems also to have been added later. After collecting the experience of others and recording his own, the author-practitioner then seems to have turned to experiment with creating other gemstones, first topaz, then jacinth (a reddish-orange gem variety of zircon).12 During both these processes, he is thinking about his ruby failure, coming back repeatedly to ruby, for example, in noting his success with topaz, which furnished him with further information on the ruby process: “The ruby en wants more time & more fire & colored with gold leaf. I believe that pumice stone or firestone for the ruby would be better” (fol. 101r). And, in the jacinth recipe (fol. 101v), he seems only to be thinking about ruby:

Jacinth

Is made like ruby, with gold, but one does not need such a great fire. The ruby wants fire for a whole day, and if it does not have enough fire, it will only have red veins.

He adds in the margin that “one holds that rubified antimony makes jacinth,” in a hypothesis similar to that which had produced his note to himself to try antimony and cemented gold as a colorant for ruby. And he adds, for the second time in as many folios, that crucibles must be reheated before putting them into the furnace.

These accounts seem to indicate that the author-practitioner attempted and failed to make ruby more than once, although he succeeded with topaz and apparently also with jacinth. His experimentation after the first failure also shows that he hypothesized that some other form of gold may have been needed (perhaps combined with antimony), and, additionally, and more insistently, that ruby demands a high heat, maintained over many hours (“one day”).13 He also recorded notes about how to increase the heat in his furnace, and what he seems to have heard or read about the experience of other practitioners with making ruby.

Red and Gold

The author-practitioner clearly believes that gold is necessary to create red color. This association between gold and red was the core of a material imaginary that informed humoral therapies, alchemical theory and practice, and a variety of metalworking and pigment making techniques. In medical theory, the most well-balanced temperament was that of the humor of blood—the sanguine—and it could be stimulated by ingesting potable gold or even by wearing gold on the body. In alchemy, the philosophers’ stone was usually described as a red substance, which, when projected over a metal mass could transmute it into gold. Many metal and pigment recipes call for red substances to produce gold colored pigments and metals.14

Recipes for Rubies

Many recipes for rubies associate the creation of a red color with gold, although they are for dyeing or “tincturing” crystals, rather than for making red glass.15 For example, of the seventy-one recipes for making precious stones in the Stockholm Papyrus (Greco-Egypt, 200–300 CE), only one is dedicated to the production of ruby. It promises “the treating of crystal so that it appears like ruby,” whereby the stone is heated in “gold-founder’s waste,” then dipped in cedar oil, mixed with “natural sulfur,” and left in the “dye, for the purpose of absorption, until morning.”16 The composition of the “gold-founder’s waste” suggested in the Stockholm Papyrus is unclear. The method of dyeing a stone had also been mentioned by Pliny the Elder, in his Natural History (ca. 79 CE) as a method for making gemstones, using transparent minerals such as crystal as a carrier for color,17 a process later described as “tincturing” (a term also used by dyers).

The eighth-century treatise, Kitab al-Durra al-Maknuna (The Book of the Hidden Pearl), by Jabir Ibn Hayyan (c. 721–c. 815) contains fifty-one recipes for the production of colored glass, including one purported to be a “ruby (yaqut) without equal.”18 Recipe 46 on fol. 7b of the Kitab al-durra describes a process for making ruby that calls for the use of copper and “ingot,” taken to mean a block of some type of metal, in addition to many more ingredients than described in Ms. Fr. 640, including red lead, various salts, red minerals commonly used as pigments (cinnabar and realgar), vinegar, magnesia, and pulverized bloodstone.19 The tenth-century Persian physician and philosopher Al-Razi (ca. 854–ca. 925), in Kitab al-Asrar, provides many recipes for the production of colored glass and the coloring of gemstones, several of which pertain to ruby (yaqut) and some of these involve the use of gold.20

French author and historian Jean d’Outremeuse (1338–1400) included many recipes for gemstones in his fourteenth-century lapidary, Trésorier de Philosophie naturelle des pierres précieuses. Five recipes for making red-colored gemstones, including hyacinth (or jacinth), balas ruby, oriental ruby, and a “pale red” stone, perhaps resembling the peach-tree color mentioned in Ms. Fr. 640, are found in chapters V (Hyacinthe, grenat et autres pierres rouges), VII (Rubis d’Orient, rubis balais et autres pierres de couleur rouge), and IX (Couleur rouge pâle).21 The processes fall into the category of tincturing (with pigments). Several of the recipes require red substances such as iron oxide or lead.22

The sixth book of Giambattista della Porta’s (1535–1615) Magia Naturalis (first published in Latin 1558, much expanded in 1589, and translated into many other languages in the following century, including English, as Natural Magick, in 1659),23 “Of counterfeiting precious stones,” provides a recipe “To stain crystal with the color of a Jacinth, or a Ruby, without breaking, or wearing it.” This recipe describes a process of dyeing—or “tincturing”—crystal that does not call for the use of gold.24 Related to ruby, della Porta also supplies a recipe for jacinth that is “beautiful enough, and not much unlike a true one,” requiring only the vitrification of lead in “a hard earthen pot [...] set on the fire in a glass-makers furnace [...] for some days.”25 This glass making process resembles that of the recipes in Ms. Fr. 640.

Gold Ruby Glass

The author-practitioner not only associates gold with producing red, but, in his insistence on strong heat, also seems to believe that with sufficient heat, the gold will undergo a transformation of state that will produce red. That compounds of gold could impart a red color to glass had been known for many centuries in written form since at least Greco-Roman manuscripts composed in Egypt.26 Red glass of some sort was produced almost from the time that glassmaking began, but, from antiquity through the early modern period, red remained the most difficult of all colors for glassmakers to produce, and the written accounts of the search for it are full of reports of failure and experimentation.27 The story of ruby glass is that of a technique that was known, lost, and rediscovered on several occasions, before it was revived commercially in Brandenburg in the late 1670s–80s by Johann Kunckel (Fig. 11).28

Objects made of gold ruby glass are known from the late Roman period (fourth century CE), including the Lycurgus Cup, now in the collection of the British Museum (Fig. 12). This object is particularly unusual: in reflected light, the cup appears opaque green, but, when transmitted light shines through the glass, it changes to a rich and vibrant red.29 The effect is caused by nanoparticles of gold and silver within the glass.30

In early modern Europe, the relationship of gold to red-colored glass appears in writing in the so-called Bologna Manuscript (first half of the fifteenth century), which contains many “secrets for colors” (segreti per colori).31 In a section dedicated to “mosaic colours,” the recipes to make “red mosaic,” “a rose coloured mosaic,” and “a pomegranate-coloured mosaic” all call for the addition of “calx of gold,” referred to as both calx letitiae and calx solis.32

Gold Ruby Glass and Alchemy

Since Antiquity, alchemical descriptions linked gold ruby glass to descriptions of the philosophers’ stone, believed by some alchemists to be a red substance that could cause the transmutation of metals, principally from base metals into gold.33 In the tenth century, the Persian physician and philosopher Al-Razi recorded the earliest known written account of gold ruby glass, described in vaguely alchemical terms as being able to attract gold and silver like a magnet and convert one-thousand times its weight into gold.34

Roughly seven hundred years later, enthusiasm for gold ruby glass reached a peak, stimulated in large part by the increasing importance of alchemical practice, especially at noble courts.35 Andreas Libavius, a German physician and humanist (al)chymist wrote in his Alchemica (1597) that “from the red coloured tincture of gold dissolved in liquid or oil it is possible without much difficulty to make red crystal,” although whether he actually practiced this is not clear.36 Shortly thereafter, in 1612, Antonio Neri published a recipe for making “a wonderful red obtained from gold.”37

In his Collegium Physico-Chymicum Experimentale, oder Laboratorium Chymicum, published posthumously in 1716, chymist and natural philosopher Johann Kunckel (1637?–1703) offered a detailed report of gold ruby glass made by Andreas Cassius (1605–1673), published in chapter 10, “De Operationibus Circa Aurum Chymicis” (Some Experiments in the Chymistry of Gold) in his De Auro (1685). According to Kunckel, Cassius created a purple-colored gold pigment, subsequently called Purple of Cassius, by mixing gold chloride and stannous chloride in aqua regia (i.e., a mixture of HCL and HNO3).38

It began in this way. There was a Doctor of medicine called Cassius who discovered how to precipitate Solis cum Jove [gold with tin]; I believe that Glauber may have given him the idea, or so it seems to me. Dr. Cassius tried to introduce it into the glass, but when he tried to form glass from it or when it was taken from the fire, it was as clear as any other crystal and he was unable to achieve a stable red [....] As soon as I heard of this, I immediately took it up, but only I know what trouble I had to find the composition, to get it right and to obtain a durable red.39

Earlier in the book, Kunckel described his own process of making the purple precipitate:

I take two parts of good aqua fortis and one part of spirits of salt, or failing this some strong salt water. When these are mixed, throw in from time to time a little of the purest tin so that it does not heat up but dissolves slowly [....] With this solution the gold can be precipitated in such a beautiful colour that it cannot be more beautiful, and thereby can crystal glass be given the finest ruby colour if the gold has previously been dissolved in three parts of aqua regia and one part of spirits of salt.40

Purple of Cassius, as described by Kunckel, however, was not invented by Cassius.41 The process was already described in 1659 by German artisan and alchemist Johann Rudolf Glauber.42 Glauber developed the process of coloring glass with a gold solution, practically identical to Cassius’s purple pigment, not to produce vessels, as Kunckel later did, but because he saw a link between this process and the transmutation of metals. Glauber believed that metals showed their true colors only when melted into glass, and he hoped that by extracting these colors, he could reveal the secret of the philosophers’ stone.43

In the mid-seventeenth century, Glauber explicitly linked the production of gold ruby glass to alchemy, believing that the color of metals indicated their “soul” or essence which could be revealed if metal salts were added to glass and extracted to effect real alchemical transmutation.44 Based on this theory, Glauber claimed that the soul of gold was captured in the red color of gold ruby glass and, along with other practitioners, he regarded the making of gold ruby glass as akin to the process of alchemical transmutation because the philosophers’ stone turned red before it transformed into gold.45 This was also believed to be a demonstration of the multiplication of gold, as invoked earlier by Al-Razi, for only a small amount of gold was required to color a large amount of glass.46 While there is no evidence to suggest Glauber and Kunckel ever met, it is well known that Daniel Crafft (1624–1697), who had worked as Glauber’s assistant for about ten years and became a glassmaker, worked with Kunckel in Dresden after 1673.47

Failure

A German version of Neri’s book, complete with commentary on the outcome of each of Neri’s recipes, was prepared by Johann Kunckel under the title Ars Vitraria and first printed in Wittenberg in 1679.48 Kunckel admits to having tried Neri’s proposed method of coloring glass red by means of gold powder more than once but was not able to achieve a stable color:

I should like to describe a better method and to show in detail how to make a red or ruby glass if it were not considered such a special rarity by my worthy Prince and Lord. Whoever does not want to believe that I can make it can come and see it in my workshop. The truth is it is still too rare to make it known.49

And again,

This dear and expensive method has certainly been tried by many but it has brought joy to few; it would be better to put the gold into it in such a way that it imparts red tincture to the glass [...] and the author would have been nearer the mark if one could suppose that he had done this or could do it.50

Kunckel offered advice on how to adapt Neri’s processes to northern European practices and raw materials, reminding his readers that all reconstructions are undoubtedly affected by environmental, material, and procedural differences between the original recipe and the moment of reconstruction.

Sixteen of the 133 chapters of Antonio Neri’s L’Arte vetraria (1612) are devoted to the making of red glass: nine offer formulas that produce a more violet or brown color, based on the use of manganese; six employ copper, traditionally incorporated as a red glass colorant in cathedral windows and in translucent red enamels such as rouge clair used by goldsmiths in the Middle Ages and Renaissance.51 Only one of Neri’s recipes for making red-colored glass, called rosso trasparente (book 7, chapter 129), mentions the use of gold, demonstrating that the potential of using gold as a red colorant was at least known in early seventeenth-century Italy, although doubted by the author himself, who concludes, “but one should experiment to find it” (peró si esperimenti per trovarlo).52 Neri’s remark was translated incorrectly in the Latin version Kunckel used as “ut experimento compertu est,” which turned the disclaimer into a statement of success (also rendered incorrectly in Merret’s 1662 English translation, “as by experience is found”).53 Kunckel thus thought that Neri had succeeded, and tried in vain to reproduce this recipe.

Failure and Experience

Neri’s and Kunckel’s points about experiment and experience return us to the intensive hypothesizing and experimenting that the author-practitioner’s failure in creating a ruby brought on. The recipe for gold ruby glass in Ms. Fr. 640 is yet another testament to a very long enduring material imaginary within which gold and red existed in a reciprocal relationship. The ruby recipe in Ms. Fr. 640 also testifies to the very long history of human desire that drove a search for hard-to-attain transmutational processes using precious, meaning-laden materials. The author-practitioner thus takes his place within a very long cycle of seeking, trying, failing, hypothesizing, extending, trying, and, at least for him, ultimately failing, that, over the long term, demonstrates how generative failure can be in the history of science and technology.

Metalworker and author of De la pirotechnia, Vannoccio Biringuccio noted that even if the art of alchemists was entirely futile—a history of failure—it was nevertheless very ingenious and “delightful to students of natural things,” as well as being “very useful to human need and convenience,” for

it gives birth every day to new and splendid effects such as the extraction of medicinal substances, colors, and perfumes, and an infinite number of compositions of things. It is known that many arts have issued solely from it; indeed, without it or its means it would have been impossible for them ever to have been discovered by man except through divine revelation.

***

Thus, in short, it can be said in conclusion that this art is the origin and foundation of many other arts, wherefore it should be held in reverence and practiced. But he who practices it must be ignorant neither of cause nor of natural effects, and not too poor to support the expense. Neither should he do it from avarice, but only in order to enjoy the fine fruits of its effects and the knowledge of them, and that pleasing novelty which it shows to the experimenter in operation.54

In the deep history of human interaction with the materials of nature, alchemical theory and practice was central in driving forward and providing a framework for the investigation of natural materials and their transformation.55 In that investigation, and in the cascade of experimenting that its failures stimulated, the striving to imitate materials and processes of nature—such as a ruby in glass—was a core mode of organizing and understanding the work. In that effort, the process of “failure” was equally, if not perhaps more significant than the successful product.

  1. Stockholm Papyrus
    19. Production of Ruby
    The treating of crystal so that it appears like ruby. Take smoky crystal and make the ordinary stone from it. Take and heat it gradually in the dark; and indeed until it appears to you to have the heat within it. Heat it once more in gold-founder’s waste. Take and dip the stone in cedar oil mixed with natural sulphur and leave it in the dye, for the purpose of absorption, until morning. [Quoted from The Leyden and Stockholm Papyri: Greco-Egyptian Chemical Documents from the Early 4th Century AD, trans. Earle Radcliffe Caley, ed. William B. Jensen (Cincinnati: The Oesper Collections, University of Cincinnati, 2008), 53.]

  2. Kitab al-Durra al-Maknuna (The Book of the Hidden Pearl) of Jabir Ibn Hayyan (c. 721–c. 815), fol. 7b
    Recipe 46 – Making ruby (yaqut ahmar) without equal: Take one hundred dirhams of cornelian stone (‘aqıq), two hundred dirhams of rock crystal (billawr) and twenty five dirhams of magne- sia. Heat each one alone and throw it in sour vinegar. Pound and cook with sour vinegar to which al-qalı (alkali) has been added. Cook very well for half a day until it becomes dry and roasted. Throw it in cold water and wash it in water and salt until its water and jawhar become clear. Put it in a luted pot (qidr) with one hundred dirhams of natrun, twenty five dirhams of al-qalı (alkali) salt, forty dirhams of Armenian borax and ten dirhams of coarse salt (milh jarısh). Light up fire on it in the furnace two days and two nights or one day and one night. If it melts take it out when it cools and throw on it iqlımiya. Take out the melted ingot (nuqra) and pulverize it with sixty dirhams of red lead (isrinj), five dirhams of cinnabar (zanjufr), two dirhams of realgar (red zarnıkh) pulverised in vinegar, five dirhams of magnesia, five dirhams of copper scales (rusakht), and ten dirhams of pulverised blood stone which is shadanaj. Mix and put in a luted pot (qidr) and place in a furnace. Blow on it continuously until it melts and becomes mature. The sign of its maturity is that you put out one carat (qırat) of it on a clear surface until it cools.     Of Several Tinctures of Crystall (p. 183)
    I have declared divers tinctures of glass, and those no vulgar and common ones, but such as are rarely known, and gained, and tried with a great deal of labour. Now I will relate some ways of staining Crystal, and especially those that are choice, and known to very few; if not onely to myself.

To stain Crystal with the colour of a Jacinth, or a Ruby, without breaking, or wearing it. (p. 183)

Take six parts of Stibium, four of Orpin, three of Arsenick, as much of Sulphur, two of Tutty; bear them all asunder, and sift them through a fine service: put the into a pot: hang your Crystal by wires, or cover it over with the powders, and so set it on the fire, that it may be hot, four or five hours; but use no bellows, lest it break in pieces or melt. It is a certain sign of being perfectly coloured, if you take out a piece, and that be of a bright and shining color; otherwise deliver it to the fire again, and after some time try it again. But you must have a great care, lest it cool too suddenly when you take it off the fire, for it will crumble and fall to pieces. If a violet colour pleaseth you, take it soon from the fire: if you would have a deep purple, let it stand longer: we can make a violet with Orpin onely.
