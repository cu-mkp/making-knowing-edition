Arms and Armor in Ms. Fr. 640

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/9rye-d152
← Back to Essays

Abstract

BnF Ms. Fr. 640 offers a unique glimpse into the production and consumption of late sixteenth-century arms and armor. Most period sources, such as guild regulations, court documents, commissions, and inventories, provide only hints about the materials, techniques, cultural experience, and tacit knowledge that clearly once existed. A more vivid picture emerges from Ms. Fr. 640, which is discussed in this essay alongside surviving objects. The essay first addresses the craft of the furbisher or sword cutler discussed over four folios (fols. 94r–97r), and then the use and maintenance of firearms, their projectiles, and plate armor referenced in several entries. Ms. Fr. 640 reveals an industry reliant on a myriad of specialized sub-trades, where no single craftsmen can be credited with the finished product and sale of a sword, and a world in which plate armor and heavy cavalry were steadily giving way to advances and standardizations in firearms.

Within BnF Ms. Fr. 640, arms and armor represent more than five per cent of the total entries. This includes extensive discussion of artillery, its use and material, which is interpreted in another essay by Pascal Briost.1 The focus for this essay is on personal arms and armor, both civilian and military, a subject that can be organized around two main areas of interest dispersed in the text. The largest of these is a distinct section on the trade of the furbisher (sword cutler or retailer) (fols. 94r–97r). The second represents practical notes on the use and maintenance of the arquebus (firearm) (fols. 43v–45v, 55r), together with scattered references pertaining to plate armor (fols. 32r, 56r).

These are not instructions for teaching entry into a craft. All of this content, as arranged by the anonymous author-practitioner, appears to be aimed at a reader or readers from the burgeoning middle and/or noble class in the late sixteenth century, instructing how to discern quality workmanship, as in the furbisher and armor entries, or conveying practices, as in the entries relative to firearms care and shooting.

In the text of the manuscript, there are hints that the author-practitioner was not a “practitioner” per se of the art of furbishing, but experienced the craft through direct conversation with or observation of a local member of that trade. In one instance on fol. 94r he references information given “according to the most competent,” undoubtedly a practicing furbisher. These notes on the furbisher in particular are perhaps found nowhere else in surviving sixteenth-century records.2 Conversely, on the practical notes on firearms the author-practitioner might very well have had personal experience cleaning, maintaining, and handling firearms as well as producing projectiles. Though rarely written down so explicitly, this realm of knowledge would not be out of character in the period for a gentleman or noble, in touch with all things military or pertaining to civil defense. In the discussion of firearms, the author-practitioner’s voice is discernibly factual and authoritative.

The Furbisher

The section of Ms. Fr. 640 dedicated to the craft of the furbisher or sword cutler occupies over four folios (fols. 94r–97v). Fundamentally, furbishers—as conveyed in the manuscript text— were not makers but purveyors and refiners of swords, as in the English verb “to furbish.” They retailed and refined the semi-finished parts produced by specialized sword makers. That is, furbishers fitted/refitted or sold swords, daggers, and their hilts or guards, blades, and scabbards. This is made clear on fol. 94v: “Those who create sword guards are artisans separate from furbishers.” On the next page (fol. 95v) the author-practitioner notes that unfinished or partially finished guards can be bought by the furbisher by the dozen and then mentions several types that are available. The furbisher was also reliant on specialized craftsmen who prepare beech-wood scabbard halves called estelles, sold by the hundred (fol. 95v). Leather for scabbards and grips were also purchased in bulk. The author-practitioner mentions not only the costs of various skins, but also how many scabbards they might yield (fols. 94r and 96v). Further still, the text states that semi-finished blades were imported by the dozen—presumably by the furbishers of Toulouse, where the author-practitioner worked—from Spain, but also from Vienne in southeastern France (fol. 95r).

Like so many urban professions of the early modern period, furbishers came together as a guild, a corporation with a governing body that kept officers, controlled enrolment and rules for advancement from apprentice to journeyman (or companion of the guild) and, for some, to master furbisher. The guild also could offer aid to members and family members in need. Statutes or articles of the guild survive for Paris and, closer to the context of Ms. Fr. 640, for Toulouse.3 These documents are similar for both cities, with those surviving from Toulouse dated to the early to mid-seventeenth century. Little information about actual techniques and skills is given in these sources, making the information recorded by the author-practitioner all the more unique and important.

Fortunately, there are some contemporary visual sources that grant a glimpse of the furnisher’s work. Most notable is a fresco in the vaulting of the Uffizi palace in Florence, painted within a decade of Ms. Fr. 640 (Fig. 1). In the center foreground a master furbisher or journeyman is seen furbishing (i.e., polishing) a sword blade clamped into the very sort of bench described and illustrated by the author-practitioner on fol. 94v. In the background is another worker spinning a large wheel powering a grinding wheel on which another furbisher grinds a sword blade. Along the back wall, men are found working at a forge and anvil. The text of Ms. Fr. 640 provides a means of understanding the postures and actions of all these men, and more.

The Culture of the Sword in Late Sixteenth-Century France

Despite the present popular image, swords were not commonly worn in the Middle Ages. It was not until the second half of the sixteenth century that, throughout Europe, a new civilian form of longer-bladed sword—the rapier or sword of the robe—was developed and carried by various levels of society.4 Precisely at the time the author-practitioner was writing, France was in the thick of the Wars of Religion between political factions of Catholics and Protestants (1562–98). Conflict and its material culture were widespread and commonplace, experienced by all levels of society. Weapons and armor in this age were not just tools for self-protection and defense, but finely-made objects of personal adornment and fashion. Schools teaching gentlemen the art of defense also grew in popularity. For example, in 1573 the Provençal fencing master and military professional, Henri de Sainct-Didier, published a treatise in Paris on the use of the sword.5 In a woodcut portrait from his book, the master strikes the pose of the ideal fashionable gentleman—with scabbarded sword at his left side and cape-like doublet jauntily slung over his shoulder and across his back (Fig. 2).

Carrying the sword and knowing how to use it were integral to a gentleman’s social status. The diaries of an English gentleman, John North, from around 1578, show that his sword and its fittings were important parts of his wardrobe.6 He records expenses for upgrading his swords, for example for covering his scabbard in velvet, having his dagger, rapier, and belt (girdle) russeted (that is either varnished or heat-treated to a dark color), also for having his sword gilded and fitted with a new scabbard and for having other swords re-gilded. Another entry shows buying iron hilts for use in swordplay practice as well as paying for fencing lessons. The furbisher’s trade grew to accommodate the needs of bourgeoning middle-class gentlemen such as these, just as they served the urban elite (patricians) and nobles for centuries.

The text of Ms. Fr. 640 offers the ability to discern quality among the products of this essential trade through the strata of materials and refinement of a sword relative to the client’s wealth and social status. Whether your sword guard was heavily worked and burnished with thick gold leaf, custom fit and garnished with a calf leather scabbard and a silk and gold-threaded grip, or a simple sword with a hilt varnished in black and a grip covered in iron wire, fitted to a ready-made scabbard—this is all accounted for in the author-practitioner’s text, along with the relative costs of the associated materials for the furbisher. In the period there were many who complained, like the Elizabethan gentleman Philip Stubbes, of those aspiring middle-class men who carried “swords, daggers, and rapiers guilte, and reguilte, burnished, and costly ingraven. With all things else that any noble of honorable, or worshipfull man doth, or may weare, so as the one cannot easily be discerned from the other?”7 There is an acknowledged wide range of products, something that resonates with our own modern age of production and consumption, just then flowering in this trade of self-defense and personal adornment. Social status was gauged and, on occasion, falsely gauged by the details of the furbisher’s art.

The Late Sixteenth-Century Sword and its Design

As techniques of fencing gradually developed in the sixteenth century, so too did the sword. Form and function were entirely intertwined. For military use, general trends preferred the cut over the thrust, while the reverse was true for civilian purposes. Debates between contemporary fencing masters over this principle were many and often regionally driven.8 Personal preferences aside, the shape and length of the blade along with its point of balance together with the hilt dictated how the weapon was to be used. Shorter blades of two feet or longer and with broader maximum widths of about three inches were generally intended for cutting blows in close compact formations of military engagements. In civilian one-on-one combat, like duels, longer, slender blades of three feet length or more, and a width of one to two inches or so were designed in favor of thrusting with linear movements intent on keeping distance from an opponent in open space. In the period both forms were generally known as swords without distinction. However, the term rapier began to find currency in the sixteenth century as a sword to wear with your civilian robes—literally as a contraction of the Spanish espada ropera or robe sword—rather than a military sword.9

The term rapier has garnered some confusion since its broader use in modern times has associated it more with the hilt form—swords with guards that comprised many complex iron or steel branches—rather than considering the entire sword (hilt and blade) and its proportions. The manuscript text, however, does not use the term nor make such distinctions, and the small sketch of a sword guard or hilt drawn in the margins on fol. 94r could be either military or civilian. As the sixteenth century progressed, hilts developed ever more complex arrangements of bars meant to guard the unarmored user’s hand against an opponent’s cuts and thrusts. Addressing these design elements, the author-practitioner provides the terms for various parts of the hilt (fol. 94r).

In modern translation, some terms remain an interpretation based on the author-practitioner’s order and association between the parts. The diagram in Fig. 3 pictures a contemporary late sixteenth-century rapier labeled with the author-practitioner’s terms in red beside modern English terms in parentheses (Fig. 3).

The term pontet or “bridge” likely refers to the forward ring or port ring of the guard. In the text it is described as a strip of iron that connects the rings, as well as the part that prevents thrusts from entering into the guard (fol. 94r). Beginning around the 1580s, the period of this manuscript, the port ring could be fitted with a steel plate to close the ring and prevent thrusts from entering. This added further protection to the user’s hand, especially when attempting certain attacks commonly found in sixteenth-century fencing manuals. In modern fencing, such a movement is termed a counterattack in opposition, that is, simultaneously diverting or parrying an opponent’s thrust by sliding against their blade while thrusting towards the opponent’s body. Though not the main focus, the text offers this one brief insight into the intended function of the hilt’s design.

The author-practitioner also defines three types of blades. Those with a sharp ridge running down their middle, those with flutes or channels called fullers, and those with beveled edges but with a flattened center (fol. 94r). The latter he deems the least likely to break, whereas the other two are both difficult to polish and break more easily. Many blades in this period use a fuller or series of fullers in what is called the forte or the heavier third of the tapered blade closest to the hilt. This is the part that must be strong and stiff to successfully push aside or parry an opponent’s blade. Fullers as well as the sharp ridge are designed to act like corrugations to brace the blade. Without them, the blade is too wobbly to maintain point control in fencing. The third type, although devoid of a fuller, would have a diamond cross-section in some parts of the blade that acts in a similar way.

There was a fashion precisely in the time of Ms. Fr. 640’s composition and into the early seventeenth century for sword and dagger blades with elaborate groupings of fullers, some even pierced. Considering the author-practitioner’s note on how these are prone to breaking, these fullers were perhaps a decorative expression of the furbisher’s art rather than a useful advantage as a weapon. A means of showing virtuosity in their trade as well as displaying wealth for the consumer. To achieve a bright polish in the crevices of these deep fullers, a specially shaped attachment was applied into the polishing stick called the fustée. An extreme example of a pierced blade with three fullers of about 1600, formerly in the armory of the Duke-Elector of Saxony, is now in the collection of the Victoria and Albert Museum (Fig. 4). The forte of the blade may be elaborately worked, but the remainder of the blade is shaped like the author-practitioner’s preferred type with a flattened center. Many blades exhibited a combination of the types described.

Another essential part of sword design discussed in the manuscript is the handle or grip. The binding materials of the grip varied widely, though they were all applied over wooden pieces, or”scales,” cut with a hole for the blade’s tang or queue (tail) to pass through. Gummed wax or a wax mixed with pitch was used to stick down the bindings. Iron wire is described as the cheapest but also the most hard-wearing. Finer silk cord stranded on a spinning wheel and mixed with gold and silver threads were most costly, but certainly more fragile (fol. 94r). Relatively few period grips remain with silk binding as the organic material is extremely light sensitive and shatters over time. An example of a surviving grip with a mix of green silken cord and copper with iron wire from a later period, about 1630, is in the collection of the Art Institute of Chicago (Fig. 5).

An unexpected, more durable option for sword grips discussed in the text is made from the dried skins of dogfish, a type of sand shark found throughout the Atlantic. It apparently “gives a good grip even if the hand is sweating” (Fol. 94r). This quality is due to the fine wart-like bumps, the calcified papillae of the dried skin, similar to stingray skin commonly known as shagreen. This material was often used in South East Asian arms and armor, particularly in China and Japan. Whether its use was developed independently in Europe or spread from China via trade with the Ottomans in the Mediterranean, it is difficult to be sure. Among the earliest examples of daggers and swords with shark- or ray-skin grips are those produced for the Duke-Electors of Saxony to equip their personal guard unit. Ordered by the hundreds from blade smiths and assembled by court cutlers for the Duke-Elector, these pieces were exceptionally preserved in the Dresden armory and are now dispersed in many public and private collections. An example of a rapier with a shark- or ray-skin grip nearly contemporary with Ms. Fr. 640, held in the Art Institute of Chicago (Fig. 6), was used as the basis for the diagram above (Fig. 3).

Outside of the Dresden armory group, period shark-skin grips seem exceptionally rare, though relatively few late sixteenth- and early seventeenth-century swords and rapiers are as well preserved and intact with all their fittings as this group. The survival rate is vastly disproportionate to what once existed. Ms. Fr. 640 fills a considerable void in understanding the materials, as well as the tools and techniques of the furbisher, to which this essay now turns.

Furbishing a Late Sixteenth-Century Rapier: Tools and Techniques

With some helpful sketches as well as material descriptions, the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 goes to considerable lengths in laying out the tools of the furbisher’s trade. Among the first pieces of notable equipment is the chameau, a long parallel bench used to clamp down a sword blade while polishing (fol. 94v). This important device and its use with the polishing tool or stick called the fustée is depicted in the center foreground of the furbisher’s workshop in the fresco from the ceiling of the Uffizi galleries in Florence mentioned above (Fig. 1). The author-practitioner notes that this bench was made of rowan or mountain ash, a hard, even-grained wood. Balanced on four legs connected to a long plank or body it resembles a quadruped, hence the name chameau or camel. This low bench made it possible for the furbisher to use their upper body while adopting a forward posture over the clamped blade. Some rare depictions of the chameau, as in a blackwork enamel design print for a sword fitting, shows a furbisher using the long plank to rest the knee as an anchor for more leverage while polishing (Fig. 7).

These postures, pieces of tacit knowledge particular to this trade, are all but lost aside from these few depictions and this text. One note in Ms. Fr. 640 mentions that a sword is given a better cutting edge by the motion of “pulling upward [with the burnishing tool] from the point [of the blade]” (fol. 94v). The polishing tool used in this operation, called the fustée, is also sketched and described in detail. It appears to have an upper and lower half—the stick and the arson—that acts like a bow-shaped clamp to hold a piece of square boxwood “three fingers thick,” used in conjunction with oil for lubrication and emery as an abrasive (fol. 94v). Presumably the hard boxwood would take hold of a film of emery and rub it flat against the beveled edges and sides of the blade. For fullers or grooves, as noted above, a specially made fustée with a narrow boxwood insert would be used (fol. 94r). Other tools for polishing are mentioned: the grateau—described as of steel, square in section, used to remove grinding marks as well as honing a cutting edge; the fresil—a willow stick used with iron scale, the ferrous material collected from a blacksmith, as an abrasive for cleaning off rust; then two sketches in the margins show a bruisson or burnisher and a bloodstone, the hardstone burnishing tool used for a glass-like polish. At the top of folio 94v, there is also a sketch of a scabbard bench, that appears to be a large plank lifted on two legs at one end used for hand-planing or shaping the estelles, that is, the two beechwood halves that form a scabbard. The procedure for using this bench and making the scabbard is detailed on folios 95v–96r.

On fol. 95r, the author-practitioner gives a step-by-step account of the process of furbishing a sword, that can be broken down as follows:

  • The first step is drawing out the queue (tang) of the acquired semi-finished blade, either by the furbisher himself if he has a forge, or contracting a farrier (blacksmith) or locksmith to do it for him. This will be covered by the grip and topped by the pommel on the finished sword.

  • The blade is then passed over to the grinding stone likely to establish the cutting edges and rough shape.

  • Next, the blade is set into the chameau (furbishing bench) where fine flour-like emery powder “soaked in oil to make it hold” is used with the fustée to remove the striations left by the grindstone.

  • A drop or two of oil is applied to give the blade luster.

  • The furbisher begins polishing the blade again on the chameau with a felin—another kind of burnishing stick that has a hardstone mounted on it.

  • With the oil already on the blade, a fine polish is given with chalk followed by wiping the blade down and a dry (oil-less) polish with chalk.

  • Once the blade is completely mounted, the final sharp hone is given with the grateau or square-section steel burnisher.

  • Before the final mounting, the polished blade is tried on the intended hilt, passing the tang through the opening on the escusson (quillon block) and holding it in the hand to assess the weight of the complete sword. Though not discussed in depth in the text, this step is likely intended to feel the balance of the blade and hilt together.

  • The blade is placed between two pieces of wood and tightened into a vise while the pierced opening of the quillon block is widened with a file to fit the tapered tang of the blade.

  • To complete the process, the finished hilt, and—although not mentioned in the text—also the completed grip, is passed over the tang of the blade that is fixed in the vice. To clench or peen the tang over the pommel, described as riveting, the furbisher uses a piece of wood over the button or top of the pommel, and then, “with a hammer, one beats on it to align & secure the pommel well” (fol. 95r). 

The procedure of riveting the tang is similar to forging the head of a nail, and some furbishers facet the head of the riveted tang into a diamond point similar to decorative nail heads of the period. The author-practitioner warns that these are not as good as round heads, as the facets are more likely to break (fol. 95r). Most riveted tangs on extant rapiers of the period are rounded or flattened at the button end of the pommel, though many rapiers and daggers of the group produced for the court and guard unit of the Dukes-Electors of Saxony, about 1590–1600, have tangs filed into a diamond shape on top of the button.10

From these concise step-by-step notes, a better picture of the labor involved in polishing and assembling swords and rapiers emerges. Not unlike a woodworker hand-planing wood, the furbisher uses a series of ever finer steps including heavy polishing with abrasives (emery, then chalk together with oil, then polished dry without oil), and then with burnishers (the hardstone felin and the hardened steel grateau), for a final polish and hone. Few sources give as much detail. In article seven of the Toulouse furbishers’ guild (1625), a sword submitted as a masterpiece for judgment by the guild officials was to be “well polished like silver.”11 Though most remaining swords and rapiers from the period have a worn patina of age from corrosion, when new, depending on their quality and value, they might have had a mirror-like polish with the traces of forging, grinding, and filing all but completely removed.

Furbishing Sword Guards

The text of Ms. Fr. 640, details another important aspect of the furbisher’s trade, the finishing and decoration of sword guards or hilts. Furbishers relied on the guard makers to forge-weld the complex arrangement of barred guards mentioned in the text. The author-practitioner gives some account of how guards could be bought by the furbisher “by dozens,” either in a cheaper semi-finished state or “worked,” which costs more, “depending on its fashion” (fol. 95v). A list of the various types of guards is given with descriptions:

Worked

Guttered, which is with round moldings

Pearled

Scarfed, when the bands are crosswise

Onioned, which is with a flat head

In the King’s manner, which are plain

These short but alluring descriptions are difficult to pair with surviving hilts. Nevertheless, they give a rare view into a late sixteenth-century world of defined styles and trends. Rapier hilts are now generally difficult to attribute to any particular nationality or region, though distinctions certainly existed.12 The group of rapiers for the guard of the Duke-Elector of Saxony exhibit features that resonate with some of these descriptions. For example, a guttered hilt, likely meaning covered in fluted branches and perhaps even onioned (in terms of the shape of the pommel) might describe a sword like a rapier now in a private collection (Fig. 8).

Some of the most substantial information on the craft of the furbisher given in Ms. Fr. 640 concerns the furbishing or finishing of sword guards. They include instructions for achieving the “color of water” which is a heat treatment to turn the polished steel hilt a dark, almost iridescent blue-black (fol. 96v), several colors of varnished finishes (fols. 4v, 96v), and lastly, the technique of mechanically plating the steel surface of a guard with silver or gold, a process known as “false” damascening (fol. 97r). The purpose for all these finishes were twofold: to protect the iron or steel of sword guards from corrosion due to moisture and the sweat of those who handle them, and to ornament or decorate the hilt and other fittings, as these weapons also functioned as fashion accessories indicating one’s wealth and place in society.

Plain steel hilts were used, especially for practice weapons that would receive considerable dents and mars that would damage even a hard-wearing finish; but for daily dress and even military use, protective finishes were more practical. Today, many swords and rapiers survive from the late sixteenth and the first half of the seventeenth century in a plain steel state. Occasionally, traces of a finish might be found in some of the deepest crevices between branches and hidden nooks in pommels. These finishes generally do not survive well through the centuries. The contemporaneous group of rapiers from the Dresden court of Saxony are unique in their excellent preservation, and coupled with the text of Ms. Fr. 640, it becomes clear that these finishes were much more commonplace. An example is the rapier in Fig. 8. It shows some minute traces of silvering, a mechanical plating of the entire hilt with a layer of silver leaf, almost entirely rubbed off apart from some tarnished traces at the base of the pommel and at the join of some branches. Considering this, it is evident that this rapier looks appreciably different now than it did when it left the late sixteenth-century furbisher’s hands. Indeed, we know from some period sources that gentlemen might send their rapiers to be re-silvered or re-gilt as they would have worn down with wear and excessive polishing.13 Looking at sixteenth-century male portraits shows why this is the case, as the left hand is often found in a posture engaging the grip or resting along the side of the hilt. One documented case of an Englishman, Arthur Throckmorton, highlights the importance of these finishes: in 1583 he had his plain steel rapier silvered for the first time in preparation of attending court, no doubt concerned about a decorous appearance.14 Supplementing such consumer accounts, Ms. Fr. 640 provides a rare means of understanding the other side of the story: the work done in repairing or adding finishes to swords.

The author-practitioner discusses the black varnish used on guards very early on in the manuscript on fol. 4v and then cross-references to his section on the furbisher and fol. 97r. This varnish, used on guards as well as other iron objects, trunk bands, and plate armor, was intended to resist rust and seal the iron or steel off from moisture. The essay by Celine Camps in this edition explores this recipe in great detail, attempting a reconstruction and showing that a fair amount of experience and judgment of heat and materials was required to successfully produce the desired even and glossy-black surface.15 The recipe calls for boiling linseed or walnut oil with garlic, hog’s fennel or bread crumbs to degrease the oil, then adding pitch and grains of wheat until the pitch is fully melted. This mixture is then applied with a brush or feather over the steel guard heated in a charcoal fire. In several coatings, the varnish is burned on until it “no longer smokes” (fol. 4v). Some extant sword hilts and armor have traces of this or a similar black varnish. Here again, a large subgroup of swords from the guard unit of the Duke-Electors of Saxony are coated in a black varnish (Fig. 9). Along the swelling bulge of the pommel on the sword in Fig. 9, some of the lustrous varnish has rubbed away revealing the “gray” color achieved by the preparatory heat treatment detailed on fol. 97r.

The most basic, yet labor intensive, finish is to use heat through a charcoal fire to oxidize the steel surface of the guard. Though not understood on a molecular level by furbishers and blacksmiths of the period, heating the surface of steel produces a film-like oxide layer causing a refraction of light to appear. Depending on the temperature reached on the steel surface, the depth of this layer of oxide refracts the light differently, ranging from straw color (about 204°C) to purple (about 282°C) to blue (about 310°C) then to gray (about 371°C). Fittingly, the author-practitioner refers to this temperature index as “passing” from one color to another (fol. 96v).

Aside from adding an attractive color to the hilt or guard, this technique has the benefit of oxidizing the steel surface, thereby forming a protective barrier to resist rust. The barrier, however, can be rubbed off and broken down if not cared for or maintained. Therefore, varnish was a more practical but less attractive alternative, as relayed in the text: “grey is the lesser color [to blue] after varnish” (fol. 96v). There was an evident hierarchy of hilt colors relative to the color’s brilliance as well as the expense of labor and maintenance required, a part of the visual language of conspicuous wealth and status in the period.

The simplest to achieve was the color grey, used as a base layer for the varnishes as well as for applying gold and silver leaf in the process of false damascening (discussed below). The author-practitioner warns not to go beyond the gray color as, in the furbisher’s empirical experience, it will “throw off filth” and is then unable to accept the layers of applied precious metal plating or any of the surface colors (fol. 96v). This can be interpreted as reaching so thick a layer of oxide on the surface of the guard—at a temperature beyond perhaps about 390°C—that it can no longer refract light and appears opaque or “filthy” and perhaps difficult to work.

The first step in producing a heat treatment finish on the guard and pommel is to polish all the parts bright. This provides a mirror-like base to increase the depth of refraction in the oxide layer. Then, the furnace or charcoal fire must burn for one-to-two hours to produce the hot ash into which the hilt is placed (fol. 96v). Once covered in the hot ash and visibly passed to a blue or gray color, the guard is rotated. In an age before thermometers, achieving an even color finish by maintaining a level temperature on surfaces of variable thicknesses must have presented a significant challenge, perfected only through experience. If the color “passed” beyond gray, presumably the whole process would have to start again, laboriously polishing down the surface to bare metal.

To obtain what the author-practitioner refers to as “the color of water,” the process is even more labor intensive, according to the text: “One needs almost one day for preparing, softening & setting in the color of water” (fol. 96v). From the description, the color may be interpreted as a deep iridescent blue-black that resembles the sunlight color of deep ocean water. What might correspond to this finish is preserved on some of the rapiers from the Dresden group, for example on the pommel of the rapier in Fig. 3 and Fig. 10. The labor was not only in filing and burnishing the guard bright or “white” in the first stage, but also after passing the guard to blue or almost gray. In the process, the guard is taken while still “moderately hot” to a vise and burnished with a bloodstone (heliotrope) burnishing tool. This is done in multiple passes of heating and polishing until the depth of finish is obtained. So sensitive and fragile is this finish that, as the author-practitioner warns, the surface of the guard must remain hot while burnished or “the bloodstone would scrape & undo the color” (fol. 96v).

The most labor intensive of all guard finishes was silvering or gilding by the method of false damascening, discussed on fol. 97r. An example of a silvered guard plated in this method is depicted in Fig. 11.

To begin, the guard is filed all over to a slightly rough state. This is what gives the surface tooth for the soft metals—silver or gold—to key into the surface of the harder steel by smoothing or burnishing the precious metal over the burred steel surface in three or more built-up layers or passes.16 On the extant hilt in Fig. 11, the red arrow points to the filed cross-hatching exposed where the silver plating has worn away. The exposed steel on this rapier guard also has a slight blue tone, further evidence of the preparatory heating process described in the text. After this heating process,

while the guards are hot, one attaches them to a vise, & with some tweezers, one layers the gold, & one sets it with bloodstone that is quite clean & rubbed with putty. And when the guards are cold, one reheats them, then one layers the gold as above. And first, one gives two layers, until the entire guard is well-garnished. And next, one reheats as at the beginning, & one gives the third covering of gold (fol. 97r). 

The twelfth-century text of the goldsmith-monk, Theophilus, describes much the same procedure for use in silvering iron horse fittings and stirrups, returning the pieces to be silvered or gilded to the fire multiple times.17 One detail that Ms. Fr. 640 adds is the importance of working or burnishing the layers of gold or silver while the steel surface of the piece is still hot. The author-practitioner even elaborates on how one should bring the guard close to the nose to judge the temperature from the radiant heat. As with the technique of burnishing the brilliant blue in the “Color of water,” the text suggests that the heat helps in the burnishing process.

What is so striking about the author-practitioner’s text regarding the profession of the furbisher is how connected and reliant the trade was on the work of a wide range of specialized sub-trades: guard makers, bladesmiths, scabbard blank makers, in addition to possibly blacksmiths, locksmiths, those in the trade of textiles and velvets for grip and scabbard coverings, as well as those who made charcoal or oils and produced polished hardstone burnishers, among others. Even fishmongers were likely involved to obtain dried dogfish or sand shark skin for grips. The network was complex and far reaching as much as local. References to trade amongst craftsmen, costs relative to the distances materials traveled, as well as the compensation for the degree of refinement are scattered throughout the text. The author-practitioner has given a picture of an industry where no single craftsmen can be credited with the finished product. In museums and collections everywhere, there is an attempt to identify the bladesmith or, in some cases, a goldsmith or cutler (furbisher) responsible for certain swords. This is perhaps a misleading emphasis. In truth, the production of these weapons, especially those of the finest craftsmanship, are products of complex collaborative processes. The more elaborate in the decoration and materials, the more compound in their execution. Understanding the nature of the production of these pieces goes a long way in interpreting their material composition and explain the inability to generally attribute these pieces, aside from their occasional—and sometimes fraudulent—bladesmith and goldsmith marks.

Firearms and Armor

In the folios of Ms. Fr. 640 that discuss firearms or armor, the narrative is not one of trade or production, but of consumption. The author-practitioner is recording practical knowledge useful to military men as much as the middle-class bourgeoisie who might be part of civic militia or called upon to defend the city. Although perhaps not intentionally, the author-practitioner’s scattered entries also record the tensions between two technologies: an arms race between the ascendance of offensive firearms versus the tradition of defensive plate armor which was in gradual decline at the time. Considering the context of the wars that consumed France in the period of the manuscript’s composition, the presence of entries on firearms and armor are not surprising.18 What is surprising is the practical information about the material culture of weapons and warfare scarcely addressed in other period sources. The entries relative to firearms and armor, though few, are valuable for the perspective they offer.

The references to firearms, that is, personal weapons or guns rather than cannons or wallguns,19 are generally associated with one form of long-barreled gun—the arquebus. After a few hundred years of development since the end of the thirteenth century, gunpowder production in sixteenth-century Europe was becoming regularized, as were ordnance sizes (for artillery) and standards in firearms. The author-practitioner refers to at least two sizes of firearm as well as the appropriate measure of powder relative to the weight of the bullet (lead ball). These were: l’harquebus de chasse, a longer-barreled fowling piece associated with hunting (chasse) with a long range of about 66 yards (80 paces), and the harquebus or haquebute, the arquebus, a slightly shorter-barreled gun with a range of about 50 yards (60 paces) that was in common military use for most of the sixteenth century (fol. 43v). Concepts of standards had been developing gradually for some time, mostly specific to regions, but here there is a clear intent.

On fol. 45v, the author-practitioner provides a sketch of what is described as an arquebus, with a long barrel fitted or inlaid into a hook-shaped wooden stock. In the period, this distinct form of stock shape with its hook-butt end was also referred to as a petronel in both English and French. The term derived from the French for petrinel or poitrinal, referring to the chest. The hook shape of the butt end was pressed against the breast or sternum of the shooter to fire, rather than the shoulder as in modern long-barreled firearms (Fig. 12, Fig. 13). Early arquebuses from the late fifteenth century into the first half of the sixteenth century were fired from the cheek. By the middle of the sixteenth century, as the sizes of the projectile, charge of black powder, and relative recoil (back-shock from the explosive power) gradually increased, stocks with breast hooks were an improvement to help a shooter both aim down the barrel and absorb the recoil. Firing from the chest, however, was relatively short-lived as a design solution to this problem. Shooters or harquebusiers would experience chronic pain and bruising of their sternum. Consequently, this stock shape was only in common use from about 1560 to 1590, supplanted by shoulder stocks found on heavier muskets and shorter carbines that developed around the same time.

Ms. Fr. 640 was written precisely in the time and place when this form of hook-shaped stock was particularly favored, but not without recording its associated issues. In multiple entries, the text recommends a means to reduce recoil. Rather than attributing the issue to the design of the stock, the entries diagnose other features. On fol. 43v, the author-practitioner suggests that the culprits were barrels made with chambers—smaller cavities to accept the charge of black powder—that caused a stronger explosive force, and recommends barrels without chambers, the alternative being a flat breech plug or end to the barrel tube. He also suggests reducing the fouling—that is, the filth or burned-up residue of black powder—by shooting with oiled linen wadding (the plug inserted to keep a bullet or lead ball packed behind the powder charge), cleaning one’s gun with oil every eight days (fol. 44r), as well as a peculiar form of ramrod that charges the powder down the barrel without sprinkling it around the walls of the barrel. This is accomplished with a ladle-like device that screws into the end of the ramrod, similar to the method used for charging powder into a cannon barrel. The author-practitioner suggests that different sizes should be available for different ranges. To load, one fills the small pipe-like ladle with powder, points the gun muzzle downwards and loads the rod vertically without spilling the powder (fol. 45v). Once in place, the arquebus is turned right side up and the rod is removed, leaving the powder at the breech. To this writer’s knowledge there are no extant examples of late sixteenth-century arquebuses with this type of ladle-tipped ramrod, though perhaps they were more common in the period. Nevertheless, it seems an impractical means of reducing fouling especially in the heat of battle where space could be a constraint as well as the additional time it would take to load in this fashion.

Among the most noteworthy information concerning firearms are the entries and sketches on “hail shot,” a cartridge prepared in paper tubes with layers of lead shot or pellets—rather than a single lead ball or bullet—separated by wads or patches of cardboard, felt, leather, or linen. Of these, the author-practitioner notes, if strong wadding material is used, the cartridge “barely scatters & makes for great piercing against a door, cuirass body [breastplate] and others” (fol. 45r). In this statement, the arms race to defeat heavy plate armor with improved projectiles is evident. This hail shot was like a modern shotgun shell without its self-contained charge of explosive powder; instead, the powder charge had to be loaded down the barrel first with the hail shot cartridge packed over it. In the instructions for producing these cartridges on fol. 45r, the wooden mandrel for creating the paper tubes as well as the cutter for producing the wadding is illustrated in detail and even labeled with letters referenced in the text (fol. 45r).

Experimentation on whether such shot was indeed effective against breastplates and at what ranges would prove a worthy investigation.20 From the late sixteenth century well into the seventeenth, some breastplates were tested or “proofed” by armorers to show whether they could withstand the shot of a pistol, arquebus, or larger musket at close range. The dents found to the left or right center of various surviving breastplates attest to these measures. Some metallurgical scientist-historians, such as Alan Williams, have posited that most of these so-called “proof” marks were likely the result of undercharging the powder producing a weaker explosive force behind the projectile, or even faked altogether, thus giving a false impression to wearers and consumers.21

In 2017–18, the Art Institute of Chicago embarked on an experiment to reproduce one of the armors in its collection and test it with a .60 caliber lead ball, modern black powder, and a reproduction matchlock musket. The breastplate of the armor was a copy of one produced at the royal workshops of Greenwich, England around 1588. It was made of smelted medium-carbon steel resulting in a similar metallurgical composition to the original.22 It also closely matched the weight of the original within a pound (about 14 pounds total, 6.35 kilograms) plus an over breastplate, known as a plackart, that added an additional “shot-proof” layer weighing an additional ten pounds (4.5 kilograms). The result of the test, shooting the matchlock musket at sixteen yards, produced about a half-inch or 1.2-centimeter dent on the outer breastplate and a slight mar to the surface on the inner breastplate (Fig. 14).

The experiment showed that armor could be effective in stopping a lead ball projectile from even the largest of personal firearms—the musket—comparable if not more powerful than an arquebus. The reality, however, is that not all late sixteenth-century soldiers could afford such protection, nor would they necessarily desire to carry the requisite weight, the breastplate alone weighing about 20–25 pounds. In an extreme case from the period, the Duke de Guise’s siege armor weighs in excess of about 88 pounds or 40 kilograms, and has three bullet dents on the breastplate (Fig. 15).

In Ms. Fr. 640, the author-practitioner addresses the quality of armor on fol. 32r:

The high plates of the pauldron of the brassard are like a masterpiece of the masters [armorers], for it is necessary that these pieces be very precisely hollowed & adjusted in order that the movement may be free. Cuirass bodies must be very evenly beaten & driven with the hammer.

The armor of the Duke de Guise is an example of the highest quality custom-made armor available in France at the time of the Ms. Fr. 640.23 The pauldrons (shoulder defenses) and brassard (arm defense or vambrace) are indeed “precisely hollowed” with overlapping segments called lames that overlap and articulate in such a way that they present minimal gaps between them. The plates are generally evenly hammered on this armor, though the breastplate is intentionally forged thicker at the front where impact is most likely. Strategically, armorers spared weight for where it was most practically necessary. As with swords, judging quality relative to wealth and social position was important not only for appearance but to protect life and limb. Lesser forms of plate armor might be made of thinner metal, weigh much less, and be sold in sizes of small, medium, or large. These common or munitions pieces of plate armor might protect from sword cuts and thrusts and even projectiles from long range, but were of little protection from firearms at closer ranges of twenty yards or less. Considering the entry in Ms. Fr. 640 on dented gun barrels, “cannon” and an imperfect means of adjusting them with a hammer on the outside on fol. 45v, these firearms were not particularly accurate beyond a range of twenty or thirty yards, and some much worse than others.24 Blanket fire, or gunfire en masse, was the more effective means of defense, and consequently the concept behind effective projectiles like hail shot as well.

Quality in manufacturing processes for armor is commented on in one other note after discussing the breastplate on fol. 32r, mentioning morions, the most common open-faced steel helmet of the second half of the sixteenth century: “Morions from Milan are all in one piece & thus better than those that are of two” (fol. 32r). The morion, with its characteristic sweeping brim and rounded comb of raised fin-like projection at the top was first produced in two halves down the center rather than raised from a single flat billet of steel in France around 1550–60.25 This method not only allowed armorers to produce morions in the French style with higher combs—four or five inches (10–13 centimeters) tall—without breaking through and overstretching the metal, but also cut down significantly on production time. It allowed an armorer to produce several rather than one a day. Significantly, the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 is warning against local production, advising the reader to turn to the armor making center of Milan, Italy, where morions were extensively exported and generally made in one piece throughout the period (Fig. 16). Without a seam running end to end, it is conceivable that one-piece morions could better withstand swords and even projectiles.

Ms. Fr. 640 draws one other connection to the technological arms race between firearm projectiles and plate armor: tempering steel. In this metalworking technique, iron or steel is heated to red hot allowing carbon to be absorbed into the surface, producing a hard-crystalline structure when quenched or submerged into water or oil. The result is carbon steel. For some applications, quenching might produce a steel that is too brittle or hard and so the piece can be tempered, reducing some of the hardened carbon crystal structure on the surface, by heating it back up to a certain temperature judged by the colors straw-to-purple-to-blue-to-grey—as discussed with guards above—and allowed to air-cool more slowly. Molecularly, this process was not at all understood in the period, and so the text of Ms. Fr. 640 reveals the empirical nature of experimentation. An entry entitled “Excellent tempering bath for cuirass bodies [breastplates]” (fol. 56r) includes such ingredients as earth, pigeon dung, and horse dung among other organic material to be mixed in water. Presumably, this was the water used as the bath for quenching a breastplate after heating it in a forge.26 This is reminiscent of the twelfth-century recipe for quenching steel tools in the urine of a red-headed boy from the treatise of the goldsmith-monk Theophilus. Whether or not this or other recipes for the quenching bath helped to harden the steel is debatable, though the organic material present in the water may or may not have had an effect on the subsequent carbon absorbed in the steel.27

Experiments to find a better tempered steel were undoubtedly many and idiosyncratic from craftsman to craftsman. Surprisingly, the author-practitioner gives a recipe for tempering projectiles—addressing the other side of the arms race—in an entry on fol. 55r, “For tempering a ball that will pierce and will make healing difficult”: “Mix fat & strong vinegar & throw your ball very hot into this mixture.” It is not specified if the ball or bullet is made of lead or iron. Earlier in the sixteenth century it was not uncommon for projectiles to be produced in forged iron, in which case an iron bullet could have been case-hardened and tempered into steel by quenching in such a liquid. However, if a lead ball was intended, as a nonferrous metal this recipe would have had no effect, if not melting the ball in the process. Aside from piercing armor, the added advantage mentioned of inflicting a worse wound, real or perceived, underscores the ominous nature of these materials and the culture of conflict at the time of writing.

Aside from a thriving arms industry, the period of Ms. Fr. 640 was rife with changes in battlefield tactics and advancements in firearms. This gradual development of weapons and armor underpins the choice of arms discussed by the author-practitioner. For example, it is telling that plate armor is only given a cursory mention in a few places, particularly in regard to firearms.28 Armor and the heavy cavalry that generally used it was just then in decline. The weight and performance of plate armor were steadily giving way due to advances in firearms. Thicker plates were required to offset the higher firepower and larger charges in muskets or harquebuses, the ranges of which are commented upon on fol. 44r.29

The sixteenth-century military—infantry and cavalry—tactics are not broached in the text of the manuscript aside from some few comments such as the note on fol. 45r on the use of hail shot intended to damage breastplates. Perhaps the author-practitioner was aware of these developing tactics but not concerned with recording that type of knowledge. Treatises on warfare and the development of tactics were already well published by the period, especially in the genre of Kriegsbücher in Germany beginning in the late fifteenth century.30 Conversely, these treatises on warfare and tactics give only passing reference, if any, to the production and maintenance of armor and weapons, and are more concerned with training and tactics. Against that backdrop, Ms. Fr. 640 becomes an important complementary resource.
