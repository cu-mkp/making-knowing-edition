Ms. Fr. 640: The Toulouse Context

By ,
← Back to Essays

Abstract

Ms. Fr. 640 belongs to the Béthune collection in the Bibliothèque nationale de France, and the names of three master artisans living in Paris are mentioned on the first page, thus appearing to connect the text to a Parisian context. Several personal and place names and other clues in the manuscript indicate, however, that it was written by an individual familiar with the Languedoc who probably lived in Toulouse at the end of the sixteenth century. This essay discusses the activity of Toulouse’s gunners, founders, painters, and goldsmiths to shed light on the political, economic, and artistic context in which the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 wrote. Several entries in the manuscript and the interests of its author-practitioner echo the activities, collaborations, and ambitions of the most prominent craftsmen of the Languedoc capital, such as the goldsmith Masse brothers and the Arsenal metal founder Barthélémy Fraysse. Toulouse

The political, geographical, and commercial characteristics of Toulouse underpinned an expansion of artistic and cultural production during the sixteenth century.1 BnF Ms. Fr. 640 contains many implicit and explicit references that intersect with this cultural production, and research on the artistic production and the historical context in Languedoc in the second half of the sixteenth century makes possible a reconstruction of the author-practitioner’s cultural environment.2

Since the thirteenth century, Toulouse was the administrative capital of Languedoc (Fig. 1) and harbored a university where Roman law was taught. Located in the south of the kingdom, close to Spain, Toulouse also benefited from a strong royal presence, for both military and judicial reasons. Hence, a Parlement was established there as early as 1443, attracting several noble families of the kingdom. Municipal authority, embodied each year by eight capitouls, generally members of the bourgeoisie, was in charge of administering the city and a large arsenal. Being a military stronghold and a strategic supply point, Toulouse was considered a “bonne ville’’ of the kingdom. The town had the privilege of casting cannons, which explains why a number of engineers or masters involved in the royal artillery either settled or resided there throughout the sixteenth century.3 This particular feature is extensively echoed in the manuscript.

A distinctive characteristic of Toulouse is its long-standing urban memory, materialized in the twelve registers of Annales, drawn up each year at the capitouls’ request to record major events and political decisions from 1295 to 1787.4 These manuscript chronicles were illuminated, particularly with portraits of the town councilors. At the time such portraits were considered a privilege of the nobility, the jus imaginum. In the first half of the sixteenth century, a growing interest in history manifested itself in Toulouse as a proclamation of identity. Like many cities in the kingdom, Toulouse was depicted, both in handwritten chronicles and in print, as an ancient and ideal city, inclined to prosperity and intellectual endeavor. Its climate, environment, and the heavens (Mercury) that ruled over the city characterized a particular genius loci. According to early modern historians, the essence of this genius was incarnated by the figure of Pallas, the protectress of the antique Tolosa (Palladia Tholosa), who influenced the character and the culture of the city dwellers, their local institutions, their literary and artistic productions, as well as their crafts.5

The claim to a glorious past was also rooted in religion. The conversion of Toulouse to Christianity was an important element in its urban memory: the martyrdom of the city’s first bishop, for instance, proved that Toulouse had been a capitolium (seat of a bishop). Numerous relics were also tangible evidence of the grandeur of Christian Tolosa. According to written documents dating back to the fourteenth century, as well as later fifteenth- and sixteenth-century records, the abbey church of Saint-Sernin was said to house the relics of the bodies of six apostles.6 This belief was strengthened by the fame of the legendary donor: Charlemagne, the Christian emperor, the symbol of the struggle against paganism (the Saxons) and Islam (the Muslims of Spain). Thus, beyond its antiquity and its legendary foundation, the city’s piety was its most revered treasure. An engraving in Nicolas Bertrand’s Opus de Tholosanorum gestis (1515), the first realistic description of Toulouse, established a portrait of a fervently religious city studded with church spires. This depiction shows why the city was so attractive for craftspeople, including goldsmiths, stone-cutters, stained-glass artists, and carpenters, and it helps to explain the early and extremely uncompromising attitude of Catholics toward Protestants.

The dynamic economy of Toulouse can also be explained by its location, on the road to Spain and, thanks to the river system, within reach of the Pyrenees and the Atlantic. Capitalizing on the company system, the mostly Spanish foreign merchants who had settled there were able to connect the areas that produced raw materials (wool, pastel woad, spices, and cloth) to European regions where they were already established (including Aragon, Castile, Flanders, and England). The quality of the woad grown in Lauragais at the time was particularly appreciated in Europe, and thus a few families (Bernuy, Cheverry, Assézat, among others) were quickly able to climb the social ladder.7 In the second half of the sixteenth century, some of the heirs of these merchants became powerful royal officers (e.g., Cheverry).8

Several active artisans in Toulouse were also involved in the exploitation of the underground riches of the Pyrenees over the second half of the century.9 To this end, they were granted royal licenses, and they served the economic and symbolic interests of the kingdom. For instance, they gave a new impetus to the use of Pyrenean marble in royal decorations. They also tried to take advantage of the mineral resources (gold, silver, lead, tin, lapis lazuli, copper, alum) of the mountains, which were called the “indes françoises.”10 Toulouse was thus characterized by strong social, political, intellectual, artisanal, and artistic creativity throughout the Renaissance.

Ms. Fr. 640 in Context

The first folio of the manuscript opens with three names that initially guided research toward a Parisian context: “Mestre Nico[illegible] Costé, in the Rue de la Heaumerie, at the sign of St Claude / Mirrors. Mestre Jehan Cousin, who resides in the Faubourg Saint-Germain, knows of the master. Mestre Jehan Garnier, in the Rue des Escrivains by St. Jacques de la Boucherie, currier. Try pastel woad flowers” (fol. 1r).

Jean Cousin, also known as Jean Cousin the Younger, is one of the better-documented members of the famous family of Renaissance artists and artisans. He was the son of the painter Jean Cousin the Elder. (c. 1503–1560/1562) a native of Soucy, near Sens. Jean Cousin the Younger (1536/1537–1593/1595), also a painter, lived, like his father, in Saint-Germain des Près.11 He is credited with a few paintings and etchings, as well as the drawings (with a few exceptions) contained in the Livre de Fortune kept at the library of the Institut de France, together with the drawings contained in the new edition of Dominique de Sera’s Livre de lingerie, first published in 1584.12 He is the author of the widely successful Livre de pourtraicture, first published in 1595 and reprinted several times. There are fewer known paintings by Jean Cousin the Younger than those credited to his father. While he only gained renown in painting and drawing, his relatives, particularly his brothers-in-law, were mainly craftsmen who specialized in metalwork. Pierre Dufour and Jean Daussonne were gunsmiths, Girard de Camp was a locksmith, and Élie Lincre was a goldsmith in Nuremberg.13 Among Jean Cousin the Younger’s customers were a number of noblemen, including François de Béthune (†1575), whose sons were Maximilien, the Duke of Sully, and Philippe, the owner of Ms. Fr. 640.14

Nicolas Costé probably belonged to the Cousté family, also from Sens. The Cousté and Cousin families had ties dating back to the first decades of the sixteenth century. Jean Cousin the Elder was certainly married to a daughter of a Nicolas Cousté (†1527),15 and Cousin was the guardian of the painter Jacques Cousté, who later followed him to Paris, where they remained close.16 Another painter from Sens named Nicolas Cousté was a member of the family, but his relation to Jacques Cousté and the aforementioned Nicolas Cousté is not clearly established.17 In 1563, the second Nicolas Cousté assisted Jean Cousin the Younger in preparing the King and Queen Mother’s solemn entry into Sens.18 His son, also named Nicolas, was a journeyman gunsmith in Jean Daussonne’s workshop in 1587. He is the Nicolas Costé, or Cousté, mentioned in the first folio of the manuscript.19

The third name on fol. 1r is Jean Garnier. He lived in the Rue des Écrivains, near Saint-Jacques-de-la-Boucherie, and was a currier (a leather craftsman who made cuirasses and helmets, for example). A contract dated January 31, 1588, and kept in the Archives Nationales de France, testifies to the marriage of his widow to another currier, Pierre Richard, who also lived in the Rue des Écrivains.20 As it confirms Jean Garnier’s death, this document strengthens the idea that the manuscript was written before 1588. According to Marc Smith’s essay on the structure and the different handwritings of the manuscript, the three names on the first folio (fol. 1r) belong to the first sections written by the author-practitioner.21

If these three names contribute to setting the manuscript in a Parisian artistic context, many references seem, instead, to point to the former province of Languedoc, and Toulouse in particular.22 A few mentions show a good knowledge of the South of the kingdom (Fig. 2). The author-practitioner alludes to “tartar from Montpellier” (fol. 48v) and mentions the city’s pan (0.248 m) as a unit of length used in the founding of cannons: “It is true that this is a pan of Montpellier & not a King’s pan, which is not used in the founding of cannons for making them good. For the pan of Montpellier being of shorter, the cannons more proportioned there are shorter also, & compensate in thickness ce the length that they would have by the King’s pan, by which measure they will be found longer” (fol. 17r). Montpellier is 260 km east of Toulouse, on the Mediterranean. Although less important politically than Toulouse in the sixteenth century, Montpellier was nevertheless particularly renowned for its medical school. Pliny compared Montpellier to Italy because of its pleasant climate and exposure, and his praise encouraged its university to turn to natural sciences and plant-collecting as early as the mid-sixteenth century.23

Further references to places or geographical particularities help narrow down the location to southwestern France. For instance, the author explains that: “Candlemakers never make a good candle when the Autan wind blows because the candle always tends to melt, however good the tallow they use for it” (fol. 13v). This wind mainly blows in the southwest of France and is mostly felt in and around Toulouse, and as far as the Lauragais. The city of Foix, in the Pyrenees about 90 km south of Toulouse, is named twice: the author mentions the “forges of Foix” in an entry about steel (fol. 50v), then alludes to the “Molders from Foix who cast their ur medals crosswise” (fol. 90r) and even gives one of their recipes (fol. 143r). In a short entry, he also refers to the “Bell tower of Rodez, church of Albi, bells of Mende” and to “the bell of Toulouse named Cardaillac” considered “as one of the most beautiful in France” (fol. 20v). Here he mentions Rodez, the capital of Rouergue, located 150 km north-east of Toulouse, Albi, halfway between the two cities, and Mende, 100 km northeast of Rodez. The “region of Albi” is mentioned again concerning its plaster which is whiter than “that from the mountains” (fol. 12r). The author-practitioner also explains that pastel woad is grown in the Lauragais region and specifies that “the best pastel woad of Lauragais is the one from Carmail & the one from Auragne” (fol. 39r). The reference to Caraman and Auragne, two small towns about 30 km from Toulouse, show how familiar he was with the region. Likewise, by stating that “earth for molding” is to be found “at Fosseret & in another place called Ox “ (fol. 81r), he mentions two other small towns, Ox and Le Fousseret, located respectively about 25 and 50 km southwest of Toulouse.

The city of Toulouse is mentioned more than any other place in southwestern France. The author-practitioner evokes the city’s “mat maker[s]” (fol. 32v), as well as “the Toulouse molder” (fol. 69r). He also reports that the people of Toulouse source their “pierres de filières” (sharpening/polishing stones) from the city of Carcassonne (fol. 71v). However, the more frequent and technically significant mentions of Toulouse concern the use of sand from the hillsides of Pech-David above the city: the author first explains that “one can find an excellent one on the little hill of Puy David” (fol. 81r) (Fig. 3),24 and he repeats, a few folios later, that “The sand from Thoulouse, which is taken, from the depths of the earth, from the small hill of Puy David, is excellent in itself” (fols. 84r and 87v). By claiming to “have experimented with sand from Th{oulous}e” (fol. 85v), the author gives reliable evidence of his presence in the city. Referring once again to the “sand from Thoulouse” he observes that “The usual  bo  good one is the one which is found in a vineyard near Puy David, but the one which is most excellent is the one is from the Touch, near Sainct Michel & toward Blagnac” (fol. 87r). His knowledge of the region proves even more specific when he mentions the river Touch, which rises on the borders of Comminges, and flows into the Garonne, on the outskirts of Blagnac, near the district of Saint-Michel-du-Touch. The author even specifies that there is “a vineyard that is quite high up” (fol. 87r).

Such familiarity with Toulouse is underscored by references in the manuscript to identifiable inhabitants of the city. Two of these references are in a scribe’s hand not otherwise used in Ms. Fr. 640, and they indicate a use of the manuscript as an account book, a use that predated the beginning of the writing of the compilation of techniques.25 These entries occur on the last folio of the manuscript and refer to the Ouvrier family: “In the year fifteen hundred seventy-eight, the second of July, the heirs of the late Sieur Ouvryer received two hundred and five écus  the rent of Saint-Frajou, amounting to twelve hundred and thirty livres” (fol. 170v). This family was prominent in Toulouse from the late fifteenth to the eighteenth century.26 Some of its members were called d’Ouvrier from the early seventeenth century. Rigail Ouvrier, a distinguished member of the family, was a capitoul in 1541 and 1544. Other members were councilors in the local parlement, such as one of Rigail’s sons, Jean Ouvrier, who died in 1580, and two of the latter’s sons, Rigail and Jean. The latter Jean, a fervent supporter of the Catholic League, died in 1592 at the battle of Villemur, alongside his friend Duke Scipion de Joyeuse, and the commander of the artillery, the Sieur de Pardeilhan.

Benoît Ouvrier, another of capitoul Rigail’s sons, was an influential merchant. He lived in the “rue des Fleurs,” also known as “rue de la Sénéchaussée,” in the capitoulat of Saint-Barthélémy, near the Mint (Fig. 4 and Fig. 5).27 In 1578, the date associated with “the late Sieur Ouvryer” in the manuscript, Benoît was the only recently deceased Ouvrier (†1573), so he is probably the man mentioned in this last folio of Ms. Fr. 640. Some documents kept at the Municipal Archives in Toulouse are proof of payments made, first to this merchant between 1570 and 1573,28 and then, from 1573 onwards, to “the heirs of the late Benoist Ouvrier, merchant.”29 Benoît’s sons were named Gabriel, Jean, and François, and his two daughters Jeanne.30 The merchant’s heirs carried on their father’s trade with the capitouls. In 1589 they were paid a significant sum of money for the cloth intended for the livery of the capitoul of Saint-Barthélémy, and the taffeta for the banners of the capitoulats of Saint-Barthélémy and Saint-Sernin.31 In light of this, it is intriguing that two entries in Ms. Fr. 640 deal with painting on taffeta (fols. 10v and 42v). Among Benoît Ouvrier’s children, two sons had major functions: Gabriel was Capitoul in 1610–1611, and François was “Master of Ports and Passages” in 1608.

The Ouvrier family was related to the Ducros family, whose name also appears in Ms. Fr. 640, in an entry on merchants: “And first, François du Cros owes from 2nd September 1581/ for 1 cane of cambric at 8 livres per canne I make this” (fol. 9v). Two marriages sealed the alliance between these two families: one in 1586 between Jean Ouvrier (the supporter of the League who died in 1592) and Anne Ducros; the other in 1592 between Gabriel Ouvrier (Jean’s brother) and Françoise Ducros.32

Ducros, sometimes spelled Du Cros or Delcros is the surname of several families in Rouergue and Quercy. The branch of the Lords of Planèzes includes a “François Ducros de Bérail,” baron of Belcastel and Guitalens, who died in 1600.33 The branch owes its name to the château de Planèzes, located in Luc-la-Primaube, in Aveyron. In 1577, during the Wars of Religion, François Ducros led twenty horsemen to Toulouse for the King’s service and the defense of the city, by order of François de la Valette de Cornusson, governor of Rouergue and Seneschal of Toulouse.34 In 1598 François Ducros bought the estate of Lestang, also in Aveyron, from La Valette’s widow.35 François Ducros’s son, also named François, was Lord of Guitalens.36 A member of the family, Guyot Ducros de Planèzes, was made a Knight of the Order of Malta in 1571, which finds an echo in the manuscript in the entry concerning the Cross of the Commanders of the Order of Malta (fol. 40v). No other “François Ducros” was found in the archives of Toulouse. Another branch of the Ducros family, named “Villenouvelle,” included several merchants, but none of them named François.37 The Archives départementales de Haute Garonne harbor a “commonplace book” (dated 1539–1564) concerning the Delpech and Ducros families, merchants in Toulouse.38 It deals with the business of Antoine Ducros, a nephew and partner of Pierre Assézat, one of the most influential pastel woad traders in Toulouse, famous for building the Hôtel d’Assézat (Fig. 6). However, no link can be found between this merchant and the one mentioned in Ms. Fr. 640.

In contrast, a precise reference to founders in Toulouse helps connect this manuscript to the artistic context of the city. Concerning “the gunner,” the author explains: “The composition of cannons of France is of one quintal of metal for two of rosette. But those of Toulouse & Poncet puts iii of rosette & one of metal” (fols. 17r–v). Here, he points to a composition of the metal used for founding cannons specific to Toulouse, as attested by a founder of the Poncet family (Fig. 7). A little later he devotes a marginal note to the Poncet family and uses their name as its title: “Poncet. They cast with solder that pewterers glassworkers use” (fol. 49r). There were at least four founders from the Poncet family documented in and around Toulouse between the 1530s and the 1610s. Pierre started his apprenticeship in 1537, was a “master founder” from 1540 onwards,39 and a “master artilleryman” and cannon founder after 1561.40 He died in 1574.41 His shop was in the capitoulat of la Daurade, near the rue des Couteliers, where the furbishers had their workshops.42 He was the father of the founder Nicolas Poncet, documented from 1570 to 1619,43 who worked in the 1610s with his cousin Jean Poncet, also a founder.44 Finally, Étienne, another of Pierre Poncet’s sons, carried on the same trade, before appearing as a cannon founder in documents from 1570.45 All of them, throughout their careers, were referred to as “Mirepoix,” in documents.

The author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 also describes a cure for the plague, prepared by “m{onsieu}r de montorsin” (fol. 44v), who most probably is César de Montourcin, who served as master of the Toulouse Mint in 1590–91. As such, he supervised the minting of coins that bore the effigy and the legend of Henri III (Fig. 8), part of an attempt to maintain the military power of the Catholic League, led by cardinal François de Joyeuse (1562–1615), archbishop of Toulouse, and marshal Guillaume de Joyeuse (Fig. 9). The aim of this venture was to challenge the legitimacy of Henri IV and confront the threat he represented for “the preservation of Religion.”46 The treasurer’s accounts for 1589 report that Montourcin lived in the capitoulat of Saint-Barthélémy, where the Mint and Benoît Ouvrier’s house were also located.47 It is no surprise that a cure for the plague was recorded by an inhabitant of Toulouse: the city experienced several epidemics, particularly in the 1580s, as reported, for example in the 1586 and 1587 Annales of Toulouse.48 Cardinal de Joyeuse, who had just been appointed archbishop of Toulouse, canceled his entry into the city because of an epidemic. A decree issued by the capitouls describes the scope and the consequences of these episodes: the officials ordered that the inhabitants who had left their homes or closed their shops to flee to the country return to the city, or be declared deserters.

Finally, although the name of Jean Prévost does not appear in the manuscript, several sketches in Ms. Fr. 640 seem to be related to engravings from Subtiles et plaisantes inventions (1584), the first printed book devoted to sleight of hand (Fig. 10).49 Prévost, reportedly born in Toulouse around 1520, is said to have distinguished himself in the art of poetry,50 but there is no trace of him in the archives. Wishing to serve the “public good,” he revealed the secrets of mountebanks in order to “tear the blindfold of ignorance off the eyes of the common people” who were robbed and tricked “in all the finest towns of […] France” by “a band of impostors” who moreover were guilty of taking “out of the kingdom […] quite significant amounts of money.”51 The author-practitioner may have met Jean Prévost in Toulouse. The drawings in the manuscript could thus be evidence of his role as a compiler, which contrasts with Prévost’s aim of unveiling the secrets of conjuring for political and social aims.

All such direct references to identifiable personalities in Toulouse helped direct extensive research into the artistic context of the capital of Languedoc, which is covered in the next section.

The Author-Practitioner and the Artistic Context of Toulouse

From the 1560s to the turn of the century, the city’s historical context bore the imprint of the Wars of Religion, which influenced the developments of crafts. The accidental death of Henri II during a tournament in 1559, and a succession of several inexperienced kings on the throne of France in the second half of the sixteenth century, resulted in the weakening of royal authority and a series of political and religious conflicts (Fig. 11). The Wars of Religion, in which Toulouse played a central part, opposed Catholics and Protestants, servants of the king and insurgents, and troubled the provinces of the kingdom from 1562 to 1598. Religious tensions, already tangible in the 1530s, quickly festered as the Reformation took root in different segments of society. Protestantism spread throughout southwestern France, due to the conversion of Antoine de Bourbon, governor of Guyenne, and Jeanne d’Albret, queen of Navarre.

The coexistence of the two religions gave rise to growing hostilities and became increasingly bloody in Toulouse as Protestants narrowly failed in an attempt to take over the city hall in 1562. The Catholic reaction was rapid and in accordance with the fear inspired by the prospect of a great spiritual and social upheaval. In the early 1560s, a large faction of the Catholic nobility of Guyenne, Gascogne, and Haut-Languedoc rose up, spurred on by war leader Blaise de Monluc and Cardinals d’Armagnac and Strozzi.52 Catholic circles in Toulouse stood out for their intransigence and their zeal as the League’s supporters. To overturn the Reformation, some sought the protection of Charles IX and the pope, while others appealed to Spain, hoping for intervention by Philip II.53

In this context, and up to the siege of Montauban in 1621, the prerogatives of the city concerning arms were of paramount importance. The proper maintenance of the Toulouse Arsenal and the part played by artisans (artillerymen, founders, engineers, architects, and carpenters) became a key political issue. Moreover, the officials took great pride in the Arsenal and, when eminent figures came to the city, they never missed an opportunity to show them around. When the king’s brother, Henri d’Angoulême, grand prieur of France, came to Toulouse in 1579, he was shown the municipal archives and the Arsenal: “which he greatly admired, and particularly the artillery, saying that nowhere was there anything like it except the Castel Sant’Angelo in Rome.”54

Recent research has shown that, despite the religious upheaval, this period was not synonymous with artistic decline.55 The affluent classes of the city loosened their purse strings for new buildings, despite their significant contribution to financing the war for forty-odd years.56 Among the wealthier citizens involved in the management of the city by the capitouls, Benoît Ouvrier, the merchant named in Ms. Fr. 640 should be mentioned.57 After the Guise brothers were assassinated in December 1588, Catholic intransigence took a more radical turn in Toulouse, first against King Henri III, then against his contested successor Henri de Navarre. The contention was fanned by Urbain de Saint-Gelais, bishop of Comminges, who had been appointed governor of the city in January 1589. Benoît Ouvrier’s nephew Jean, a councilor in the local parlement, was a leading figure of the movement: in 1589 he went to Muret to preach for the League. He was fully committed to the war and met with death at the battle of Villemur alongside the Duke of Joyeuse in 1592.58

The career paths of several artisans show how they managed to adapt to the needs and the economic and political challenges of the times by becoming involved in the exploitation of natural resources or the manufacture of arms, such as the architect and engineer Dominique Bertin or the ironmaster Hélie Bachelier.59 The focus in Ms. Fr. 640 on casting, founding, arms, and armor perfectly illustrates this economic and political context.60

All through the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, the art of founding flourished in Toulouse and became one of the city’s signature industries.61 At the beginning of the sixteenth century, repair of the strongholds near the Spanish border and those easily reached from the Mediterranean was a crucial issue for the kingdom. Toulouse was part of this: in 1525, an Italian engineer was sent to the town to inspect and improve its defense works. It was equally essential for Toulouse to ensure its military striking power and an efficient deterrent force, hence the effort put into war equipment. The King sent men to develop and strengthen the municipal Arsenal: Jean Clichy, ironmaster and king’s canonnier ordinaire (cannon founder), Jean Chantereau, carpenter and king’s canonnier, Michel Pelhot, master artilleryman from Lyon, Jacques Périgon, master armorer and canonnier ordinaire, as well as Jacques Barrau, master wheelwright and canonnier ordinaire.62

In 1536, as the threat of a Spanish invasion loomed once again over Toulouse, the capitouls commissioned the city’s master artilleryman Claude Pelhot, a cousin of Michel, to cast a dozen artillery guns.63 A few years later, his mastery of the art served the city’s classical ambitions with his creation of a life-sized, gilded bronze statue of Dame Tholose, which proudly adorned the Archive tower of the town hall (Fig. 12).64 Pierre Poncet served as an apprentice in Claude Pelhot’s workshop from 1537 to 154065 and married Pelhot’s daughter, Claudie, in 1542.66

The city’s accounts for 1570–1590, during the Wars of Religion, give the names of around forty artillerymen, cannon founders, armorers, and founders charged with the maintenance of the Arsenal and with the casting of new guns and the supply of ammunition.67 One founder, Barthélémy Fraysse, stands out among these artisans for his multiple activities, his links with prominent individuals of Toulouse, and his role in the Arsenal.68 His status as artilleur ordinaire echoes the very precise references to guns in Ms. Fr. 640 (cannons, culverins, falconets, cannonballs, ammunition, etc.) (fols. 17v, 19r, 21r–28r, 32r, and 45v).

Alongside guidelines on painting, varnish, and casting, instructions and observations in the manuscript concern goldsmithing, including the molding and casting of small objects, especially to imitate nature. These instructions evince an interest in lifecasting plants, insects, reptiles, and for creating hybrid animals, such as winged rats (fols. 120v, 129r, 154v–156r, and 159v). They are consistent with mannerist aesthetics, a taste for the “rustique,” and a penchant for fountains and grottoes that flourished in the leading artistic centers of Renaissance France during the reigns of Charles IX to Henri IV, spurred by royal entries and prestigious projects (e.g., at Verneuil). This set of interests are also found in compilations and books by Androuet du Cerceau, Bernard Palissy, Hugues Sambin, and Joseph Boillot (Fig. 13, Fig. 14, Fig. 15).69

The many recipes about goldsmithing and the fact that the author-practitioner himself speaks of owning a goldsmith’s forge leads to comparison of the content of Ms. Fr. 640 with the activity of the numerous Toulouse goldsmiths (fol. 12v). With 280 registered goldsmiths in the sixteenth century, Toulouse was in this regard one of the most active centers in Renaissance France.70 The treasurer’s books and supporting documents for the Town Hall’s accounts give the names of about twenty-five goldsmiths who worked for the capitouls between 1570 and 1590. Two of them stand out because their know-how matches some of Ms. Fr. 640’s recipes. In several entries, the author includes instructions on molding flowers from nature (fols. 120v, 129r, 154v–156r, 159v). It is interesting to note that the poetry contests held in Toulouse since 1323 were called Jeux Floraux (Floral Games) as the winners were awarded gold or silver flowers (marigold, wild rose, violet). These flowers were made by two goldsmiths, Jérôme Véret (from 1576 to 1582 and from 1584 to 1589), and Arnaud Cornayre in 1583.

The Livre Rouge (Red Book) of the Academy of the Floral Games specifies the order in which the prizes were awarded, an order established in the sixteenth century: “And so it was agreed that the marigold will come first, then the wild rose, and lastly the violet.”71 The awards ceremony was the occasion for lavish displays of pageantry. In 1565, when King Charles IX made his entry into Toulouse, a rustic theatre was erected on the place de la Pierre to celebrate the nine Muses, whose number evoked the King, and to glorify the legendary Clémence Isaure, credited with founding the medieval poetic jousts (Fig. 16). As the King went past the theatre, a device triggered a “great cloud” from which a “globe made with great artifice” seemed to emerge. A child dressed as a nymph stepped out of it and offered the King the flowers of the floral games.72

The author-practitioner briefly mentions the molding of marigolds from nature (fol. 159v) but places greater emphasis on other flowers, like roses (fols. 116r, 129r, and 155r–v). This may be attributed to the fact that in 1570, the authorities of Toulouse decreed that goldsmiths were no longer allowed to cast flowers like those of the floral games for private customers:

by common agreement, it was decided […] that the town officers can be mandated to search the homes of goldsmiths and goldbeaters of the said town to see if there are any flowers there other than those commissioned each year by the city for the floral games. And if so, they will be seized and brought to the town hall and will be dealt with as is necessary, and the said goldsmiths, goldbeaters and others will be forbidden to make any flowers similar to those of the said College, otherwise a penalty of five hundred livres and imprisonment and other sentences may ensue.73

The author-practitioner also seems to have interests in common with the merchant-goldsmith Raymond Masse, active in Toulouse from 1585 to 1631.74 Masse was in close contact with the d’Ouvrier family and was in business with Jacques Boulbène, the most important painter of the Toulouse milieu (Fig. 17). They both had shares in the lotteries called blanques. Masse also worked on a regular basis with Parisian and German goldsmiths, as well as lapidaries from Antwerp and Switzerland. Moreover, Philippe de Béthune’s niece Louise, who was married to the baron of Mirepoix, was one of Masse’s more distinguished patrons. Masse’s connections and expertise overlap with many of the author-practitioner’s interests and concerns.

The author of the manuscript also mentions molding, casting, varnishing, and repairing medals (fols. 12v, 15v, 43r, 50r, 57v, 68r, 69r, 72r–v, 80v–81r, 82v–83v, 84v–85v, 86v, 89v–90r, 91r, 92r, 101r, 103v, 110v, 116r, 117v, 118v, 120r, 122v, 125r–v, 129v, 132v, 135v, 136v, 137v–139r, 140r–v, 145r, 149r, 150r, 153r, 156r, 158v, and 169v–170r). He reports using sand from Toulouse to mold “a large piece of a portrait of Jesus” (fol. 85v) and includes many instructions for making “medals.” An entry also deals with “Currency medals,” apparently to do with striking coin-like medals (fol. 170r). These details and the fact that Benoît Ouvrier lived near the Mint suggest a connection to the Masters of the Toulouse Mint. The treasurer’s books, supporting documents, and the notary Massiot’s notes (containing transactions involving several masters of the Mint) give the names of twenty-six men who performed the duties of “master, “general,” “assayer,” “moneychanger,” “cutter,” “engraver,” “warden,” or “comptroller” at the Mint. The names include César de Montourcin, the “master of the Mint” (fols. 116r, 122v, and 137v), and also his predecessor Louis Saporta,75 “master of the Mint” in 1586 and 1587 (Fig. 18). One of Benoît Ouvrier’s nieces had a daughter, Françoise de Perdriel (before 1573–1631), who married Louis de Saporta in 1587.76

Conclusion

The author-practitioner’s identity and his professional profile are not easy to decipher. Clues contained in some entries of the manuscript make clear, however, that he was quite familiar with the Toulouse region and its resources. He perhaps had close links to a wealthy family of the city, and observed or worked with artisans whose activities were relevant to the political, military, and economic events of the late sixteenth century. The author of Ms. Fr. 640, who apparently had the opportunity to meet with practitioners linked to the Arsenal and the Mint, also seems to have had contacts with Toulouse League circles, particularly with such staunch supporters as members of the Ouvrier family and César de Montourcin. Like many versatile artisans and other members of society in the late sixteenth century, the author-practitioner also seems concerned to increase knowledge and to improve and spread know-how that could have significant political and economic consequences in this context of war and financial crisis.
