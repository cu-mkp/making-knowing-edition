Pinking Satin

Abstract

This short essay investigates the meaning of “scratching satin” on fol. 42v and identifies it with the technique known as “pinking,” that is to say, creating a pattern by means of cuts in the fabric.

Reconstructing the entry “Pounced design for scratching satin” (fol. 42v)1 presented an opportunity to develop a clearer understanding of what the author-practitioner meant by the term “scratching satin,” or esgratigner sur le satin. Based on the language of the recipe, it was unclear whether the penknife or piece of glass was intended to cut entirely through the satin or to scratch a line onto the surface of the satin by cutting through some (but not all) of the threads. My reconstruction suggested that both interpretations create a design on the satin fabric (Fig. 1, Fig. 2). However, researching the terms like esgratigner sur le satin, esgratigner le satin, and satin esgratigne revealed that the word esgratigner or “scratch” was commonly used in relation to satin to describe tearing small rips in the fabric, a technique known in English at the time as pinking satin (Fig. 3). For example, a seventeenth-century French–Italian dictionary specifically translates esgratigner when used in the phrase esgratigner le satin to squarciare in Italian, meaning to rip or to tear.2 Garments made with satin slashed in this way were very popular at the time (Fig. 4, Fig. 5). Based on this interpretation, my reconstruction of this pinked satin technique demonstrated that the egg white and fig milk mixture which is painted over the pounced design serves as a reinforcement.3 The cuts I made on the lines painted with the mixture were much cleaner than those I made on portions of the fabric that had not been painted with the mixture. The cut threads did not fray as much (Fig. 6). It is unclear how common the use of reinforcements for pinked satin were at the time, and further research situating this recipe within its larger context would be very valuable.
