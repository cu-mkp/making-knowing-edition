Abstract

In this essay, former Making and Knowing Postdoctoral Scholar Donna Bilak presents selected aspects of the pedagogy used by the Making and Knowing Project in its creation of Secrets of Craft and Nature. She discusses structures and principles of the Making and Knowing Project, and highlights two central pillars of the Making and Knowing Lab course: skill building and student reconstruction of the processes contained in Ms. Fr. 640. She underscores the importance of failure in pedagogy more generally, and provides examples of extensions of Making and Knowing pedagogy to a variety of other settings.

Introduction

The Making and Knowing Project devoted 2014–21 to the material and intellectual exploration of an anonymous, late sixteenth-century French manuscript, a compilation of almost 930 entries about artisanal technologies known as BnF Ms. Fr. 640.1 The Project brings together people from across the academy and diverse professions, all with different skills and backgrounds, to interpret and understand the craft technologies recorded in this early modern text. This collaborative process creates multiple lines of expertise that help shed light on artisanal processes and tacit knowledge that would otherwise be largely opaque to a modern reader.

The Making and Knowing Project is an international enterprise involving many people and moving parts, a central tenet of which is collaboration.2 The Project is built upon the interplay between four spheres of activity: the transcription, translation, and encoding of the manuscript; critical commentary and research through historical reconstruction; regular scholarly exchange and open peer review; and digital development. Each of these elements involved the pursuit of original research in regularly scheduled courses, along with training of students (e.g., in paleography, laboratory skills, digital techniques). These spheres worked in concert from 2014 to 2020 to produce Secrets of Craft and Nature in Renaissance France: A Digital Critical Edition and English Translation of BnF Ms. Fr. 640, https://edition640.makingandknowing.org (first release 2020). The decision to publish the research as a digital critical edition is noteworthy because it made the Making and Knowing Project into a digital humanities project in addition to its physical research and historical reconstruction goals. A critical edition is a scholarly tool that offers critical analysis of a text rendered accessible in transcribed and/or translated form in which a scholarly introduction functions as its critical apparatus. The Project’s born-digital Edition had many advantages over a print format, especially for research into embodied techniques and knowledge that is better visualized than verbalized. The Edition’s inclusion of student-driven content, derived from practice-based pedagogy and collaboration, makes up the extended in-depth commentary of Ms. Fr. 640’s entries, and brings to light many facets of the manuscript’s genesis, and the artisanal knowledge it contains. Its digital format activates its content and can capture the dynamism of the students’ material exploration of Ms. Fr. 640, which was integral to their research process, in ways a printed publication cannot.

This essay explains the concept and practices that structured the graduate Laboratory Seminar, Craft and Science: Making Objects in the Early Modern World. It discusses the Making and Knowing Project’s core pedagogical practices of collaboration, the apprenticeship model, and skill building exercises. It also makes suggestions for the adaptation of the Project’s teaching methods for use in different learning environments, and presents reflections on how learning happens, intellectually and emotionally, as a consequence of material engagement employed as a tool of textual analysis. These observations stem from my own experience with the Laboratory Seminar as a Making and Knowing Postdoctoral Scholar in the inaugural years of the Project from 2014 to 2017. My perspective has also been shaped by the recognition of failure as a productive and generative force and as a catalyst in the learning process. This constitutes a significant personal and professional takeaway from my time on the Project.3

Laboratory Seminar: Structure and Principles

The Making and Knowing Project was designed to create a scholarly resource that would shed light on the manuscript Fr. 640 and its remarkable artisanal content, with the goal of publishing it as an open access born-digital edition hosted by the Columbia University Libraries. Much of the Edition’s critical content is based on the historical reconstructions of entries (or “recipes”) in Ms. Fr. 640 produced in the Craft and Science Laboratory Seminars, which Pamela Smith developed as a blended learning environment where the text-based analysis of Ms. Fr. 640 meets experiential, materially-engaged research. The Lab Seminar was co-instructed by Smith and three postdoctoral scholars (each serving three-year terms) and supported by Project staff and guest lecturers. Within the overall Making and Knowing Project’s design, the Lab Seminar served to generate student research essays (i.e., a central piece of the Edition’s critical apparatus) which utilize transcriptions and translations of entries produced in the Text and French Paleography Workshops.4 The Craft and Science students reflected the Project’s interdisciplinary nature, coming from such fields as art history, anthropology, architecture, creative writing, English literature, history, medieval and Renaissance studies, textile conservation, and chemistry.

The student essays present the textual and material analysis of specific entries in Ms. Fr. 640 through historical reconstructions undertaken in the Lab Seminar and made an argument about what their research reveals about process, materials, and sixteenth-century culture and society. The student essays also investigated early modern conceptions of nature exemplified by the artisanal technologies recorded in Ms. Fr. 640 as well as current understandings of how science, art, and scholarship interrelate. The reconstruction of Ms. Fr. 640’s historical making processes involved text, document, and object‐based historical research with hands-on work and material engagement.5 The Making and Knowing Project team members have developed a particular approach to “historical reconstruction” in the Lab Seminar as a text-focused approach that uses hands-on, material engagement as a teaching and research tool.6 While different disciplines use reconstruction in a variety of ways, the Project’s method was determined by several limiting factors, including the short time span of a course semester, students’ general unfamiliarity with the techniques they researched, and the necessity of bringing all students up to speed each semester with skill building exercises. The Project came to see the goals of the students’ reconstruction as “proof of concept,” one component of their exposure to “material and technical literacy,” in contrast to an expectation of a completely authentic (or, “scientific”) reconstruction, or an expectation of a student’s development of artisanal expertise. The Project found that reconstruction became a way to generate extremely productive historical questions about material life and culture of the past, questions they would never have considered without the experience and hands-on engagement with materials and techniques in the Lab. The student essays also integrate information and insights derived from the students’ hands-on laboratory experiences that are logged in “field notes,” a style of journaling modeled on qualitative and quantitative techniques of recording used in anthropology/archaeology and the natural sciences, and archived in an online repository.

Smith structured Craft and Science as a multi-year series of courses with annual Lab Seminar themes derived from general groupings of seemingly related entries in Ms. Fr. 640 (the themes eventually were expanded into the categories found in the List of Entries).7 From 2014 to 2019, Lab Seminars explored the following areas:

  • Moldmaking and Metalworking (2014–15: portrait medal casting, sand and plaster molding and casting, bread molding, among many other subjects);

  • Colormaking (2015–16: dyes, pigments, artificial gems, coloring woods, metals, and varnishes)

  • Practical Knowledge: Vernacular Natural History, Practical Perspective, Optics, Mechanics, and Medicine (2016–17: medicinal remedies, preserving plants and animals, arms and armor, plant cultivation, among numerous other topics)

  • Ephemeral Art (2017–18: artificial grottos, painting on taffeta, molding with paper and stucco)

  • Printmaking, Inscription, and Impression (2018–19: printmaking, image transfer; tablet making; etching solutions)

Student reconstructions of manuscript entries were extremely wide ranging. Highlights include fish glue, silkworm cultivation, making a self-playing spinet mechanism, imitation gemstones, lifecasting butterfly wings and pansies, making sand for hourglasses, a “sausage bomb,” and a host of other unexpected things.8 The Craft and Science Lab Seminar is held in a ca. 1940s wet lab in unused lab space in Columbia’s Department of Chemistry, and its fume hoods, sinks, and countertops provide a safe and flexible work space conducive to experimental research of historical practices (Vid. 1).

The Making and Knowing Project sources materials for reconstructions from a wide variety of vendors, weighing authenticity, appropriateness, historical availability, and the time constraints of the semester. Materials are procured from chemical supply companies, craft specialty stores, conservation and archival shops, and online suppliers—including things like jet (i.e., black-colored lignite), beeswax, lye (sodium hydroxide or potassium hydroxide), sulfur, woven linen cloth, and hundreds of others. Local supplies are also tapped: oyster shells from a restaurant in New York City's Grand Central Terminal (Fig. 1), cuttlefish bones from pet stores, cochineal insects (a natural dyestuff), and Venice turpentine (today primarily marketed as a therapeutic substance used by farriers on horse hoofs, but also used in painting and instrument-making) from the artists’ supplier Kremer Pigments, beef bones from a kosher butcher shop, and brandy from a neighborhood liquor store.

These modern materials are approximations of substances referred to, or deduced from, Ms. Fr. 640 entries. Acknowledgement of the uncertainty about the stability and/or authenticity of these materials is fundamental to experiential learning in Craft and Science where the point of reconstructions is material engagement, which often meant “good-enough” material fidelity. Moreover, the manuscript’s entries themselves are very far from a “recipe,” with many steps of the procedure left out or given in ambiguous brevity, and very often suffering from a great lack of clarity, even contradiction. All of which meant that students’ reconstructions were not a process of following instructions, but instead puzzling through interpretation of the text by means of material engagement. Students thus had to grapple with a difficult text as well as the vast divide between early modern and modern materials and technologies. They learned quickly that their experimental protocols might not ever yield definitive results (and generally could never do so in the space of a single semester). The process is thus rife with opportunities for failure, indeed it is pretty much unavoidable. Initial encounters with this reality were often disorienting to students used to an academic system that privileges end-of-term research papers structured around presenting a defensible and/or refutable thesis. Dealing with failure, and developing an appreciation for its generative aspects, is discussed in greater detail below. For now, I will state that recognition of failure as integral to the learning process raises productive questions about the level of certainty in historical research (and that of other disciplines), and encourages students to think about how to acknowledge that uncertainty to reach a judicious interpretation, as well as to help them develop perspective on the always-unfinished process of scholarship.

Historical reconstruction in Craft and Science focuses more on process than product, although students did have to complete a concentrated research essay of publishable quality by the end of a single semester. The emphasis on the process of the reconstruction, rather than on the production of an authentic object at the end of it, brought to light the way in which reconstruction actually formed another facet of close reading and interpretation of a text, and made clear how essential reconstruction is to interpretation of such a “how-to” text. The Project’s emphasis on experimental process, observation, and reflection allowed the guiding questions in the classroom to become: How can material engagement assist us with the interpretation of ambiguous, often tacit knowledge? What new kinds of questions does insight acquired through hands-on experimentation enable us to ask of the text? Experiential learning of this sort enabled students to make an imaginative leap—a leap necessary to all historical inquiry—back into the author-practitioner’s material environment, his knowledge, and his expectations and aims of the processes he recorded in the manuscript’s entries.

Collaboration, Learning through Apprenticeship, and Skill Building

Textual interpretation of Ms. Fr. 640, and by extension its historical reconstruction, is complex. It entails an essential tension between the assumptions of the modern reader with their own skillset and the knowledge possessed by the sixteenth-century author-practitioner. Enigmatic statements along the lines of “as you know [how to do]” are one example of how tacit knowledge of different kinds pervades the manuscript. What may have been obvious to the author-practitioner centuries ago confronts us today with a multitude of questions about practice and materials. The entries are often not explicit about the material or object being produced. This is demonstrated, for example, by references throughout Ms. Fr. 640 to “glue,” “clear glue,” and “very clear glue” that likely represent different substances with specific tensile properties and optical effects as opposed to being mere adjectival attributes.9 Nor are things like time, temperature, and measurements stated in precise terms, although such quantitative ambiguity is in keeping with how recipes were recorded in the early modern period,10 and the qualitative information contained in the recipes actually turned out to be equally useful, indeed often more useful than quantitative statements.

The Making and Knowing Project came to see material engagement as a viable instrument of interpretation for probing forms of veiled information within the textual record about bygone artisanal practices. Knowledge that students gain from hands-on experience opens up pathways to understanding early modern material and bodily ways of knowing. In this, student work is guided by three principal elements: collaboration, an apprenticeship model of knowledge exchange, and the use of skill building exercises that help to prepare the Craft and Science students to undertake Ms. Fr. 640’s recipe reconstructions.

The student’s ability to establish a connection between practice in Ms. Fr. 640 and historical research is fundamental to the Lab Seminar experience, and collaboration is a crucial component in facilitating this. The publication of student research is an essential pedagogical goal of the Making and Knowing Project. Thus, during the creation of the Digital Critical Edition, an enormous amount of work went toward facilitating the students’ publication-ready essays by the end of each semester. Student essays are crafted in stages, integrating input from Craft and Science peers and instructors as well as from scholars and artisans affiliated with the Project for their particular expertise. Feedback thus occurs throughout the reconstruction research and writing process, delivered in different forms. In-class essay workshops guided by the instructors (Smith and the postdocs) engage the students in reading and critiquing one another’s drafts, using Google Docs to collaboratively edit and comment on work online. Additionally, students work in close consultation with academic and museum-based scholars, as well as with “Expert Makers,” in residence for an intensive two-week period each semester, who help with an introduction to the techniques relevant to the year’s theme (e.g., molding and casting for Year 1).11 Oversight of the student research is further provided through annual Working Group Meetings held at Columbia at the conclusion of the academic year. These convene the scholars and practitioners associated with a given year’s theme to review and discuss student essays with the Craft and Science students and instructors, suggesting amendments to student work where necessary. The robust, ongoing intellectual support that students need as they research and write their essays comes from this collective and sustained interaction with individuals with different skills and backgrounds, as well as from the intensive collaborative editing process throughout the semester. Interpreting and understanding the craft technologies and experimental processes embedded in Ms. Fr. 640 is a community effort.

These intersecting spheres of collaboration are also evident in what Smith terms the “apprenticeship model,”12 an innovation of the Laboratory Seminar. Etymologically, apprenticeship incorporates the sense of the French verb apprendre (to learn) and the Latin apprehendere (to apprehend). The concept of apprenticeship, then, refers to the act of gaining knowledge and/or skill through observing and experiencing a process under the tutelage of a skilled practitioner. The Making and Knowing Project’s apprenticeship model essentially consists of knowledge-sharing among experts (artisans, scholars, conservators) and with students. In this process, distinctions about those who teach and those who learn become blurred in the knowledge exchanges around obscure materials and techniques. In the Project’s apprenticeship model, you are always teaching someone else while they are teaching you.

Skill building in the Lab Seminar commences with an exercise termed “historical culinary reconstruction” that aims to introduce the student to the promises and pitfalls of reconstructing practical knowledge contained in historical texts. The exercise is also undertaken in pairs or small groups to underscore the power (and challenges) of collaboration. This is intended as a fun, low-stakes introduction to the methods of the Making and Knowing Project, that begins with the question of what it means to teach yourself to do something by reading about it in writing. In the historical culinary reconstruction, students select a recipe from a variety of early modern culinary recipes (for instance, those readily available in Early English Books Online [EEBO] and the Wellcome Library) in order to attempt to reconstruct the obscure object of these food recipes at home. This exercise aims to have students grapple with how to interpret and obtain materials, with designing a workflow for obtaining an outcome in an often-obscure object of reconstruction, and to consider what are acceptable compromises with regard to authenticity in such a reconstruction (e.g., modern ovens). Students are required to document all stages of the execution and experience of their historical culinary reconstruction in their field notes. This introductory skill building assignment concludes with the students’ presentation of their historical culinary reconstructions at the next class for consumption and reflection (“digest and discuss”) about the experience.

Skill building exercises are also undertaken with an Expert Maker who serves a residency with the Craft and Science Lab Seminar each term. In this context, skill building serves to guide students step-by-step through a historical artisanal process that relates to the theme of a given year. Just a few examples of skill building sessions in the Lab Seminar with Expert Makers include lifecasting (Moldmaking and Metalworking, 2014–⁠15) (Fig. 2), oil painting (Colormaking, 2015–16) (Fig. 3), taxidermy (Practical Knowledge, 2016–17) (Fig. 4), levigation of azurite pigment (Ephemeral Art, 2017) (Fig. 5), and engraving of copper plates (Impressions, 2018) (Fig. 6). This didactic form of skill building also embodies the “apprenticeship model” discussed above.

Just as the Making and Knowing Project examines the early modern workshop as a site of knowledge-making about natural materials and processes, the Project itself can be viewed as a modern-day workshop of experimentation and knowledge generation. Craft and Science students discover through iterative practices the importance of experiment and failure as vital elements of creativity and knowledge-making. Cross-pollination of ideas and knowledge occurs among scholars, artisans, and students through the Lab Seminar. The Lab Seminar creates a heterogeneous, interactive space where learning and innovation happen, enabling the unexpected (and often unexpectedly insightful) to occur.

Failure

My discussion of Making and Knowing pedagogy has thus far laid out the Craft and Science Lab Seminar rationale and its organization. It is also useful to share the ways in which various kinds of failure affect the Project students’ experience of reconstruction as a dynamic agent of intellectual growth. Encounters with failure in the Lab Seminar have also impacted my own views on research and teaching. Like anyone, I have experienced a wide variety of failures, certainly personally instructive. Yet I had never given deep or sustained thought to a philosophy of failure until I joined the Making and Knowing Project team. Failure can open up important learning opportunities in the classroom. What follows are some examples of how different encounters with failure shaped the Craft and Science Lab Seminar experience. This illuminates the importance of first identifying students’ interests and their backgrounds and experiences, and making room for the role of emotion in learning.

The textbook definition of failure essentially describes “a lack of success”—whether in a person, thing, or event. Failure can also refer to the omission of an expected or required action, a deficiency of some sort, or malfunction. Use of the word “failure” tends to be loaded with negative weight: abasement, disappointment, shame. The experience of failure, however, can possess positive value and even foster unexpected success. Asking questions, in fact, invites failure to enter into the research process. You may not end up where you thought you would when you started.

The ability to recognize, and follow, the unexpected paths that failure can generate can open productive avenues of investigation and lead to tangible results and new insights. From this perspective, failure constitutes a necessary component of learning. Certainly, failure comes in many forms.13 Teaching at Columbia University as part of my work with the Making and Knowing Project brought me into close contact with students’ anxieties about encountering failure in any form, and, at the same time, their aspirations to perfection in accomplishing their projects, failure’s alter ego. This failure-perfection binary, an either-or construct, is often reinforced by institutional practices such as letter grades, which judge students’ products with finality and encourage students to understand their learning objectives in terms of the product of short-term testing or knowledge retention, rather than the long process of education. Failure has become a dirty word.

Craft and Science Case Studies in Failure

The following account presents an example of the role of failure and expectations involving our first group of graduate students enrolled in the very first Making and Knowing Lab Seminar. This episode also recalibrated my own views on Ms. Fr. 640 as well as my approach to guiding students in their reconstruction and essay writing process (see Vid. 2).

Historical Culinary Reconstruction

When the Making and Knowing Project began teaching in Fall 2014, Smith had already dedicated enormous effort and energy in setting Craft and Science into motion, years in fact,14 with considerable preparatory work done in conceptualizing the Lab Seminar. It was September, it was “showtime,” but none of us (instructors and students) really knew what to expect. Our first group of Craft and Science students were drawn to the course by its hands-on experimental premise. Smith decided to start the course by introducing students to the Making and Knowing Project with a historical culinary reconstruction, something she called a “skill building” exercise (i.e., the pedagogical concept and practice detailed above). The historical culinary reconstruction was not a graded assignment and was meant to happen in a “no judgement” zone.

This first skill building exercise had an unnerving effect on our students. It created an unavoidable confrontation with the fact that there was no possible way to execute this assignment perfectly. Yet perfection was not the point. Early modern culinary recipes (indeed any kind of early modern recipe) contain too many variables and unknowns for definitive results in a modern kitchen in the space of a week. Several of our students, likely for the first time in their academic experience, presented total failures at our in-class debrief about executing (and if possible, eating) this assignment. “Failure” in this context is definable as both a physical and a psychological state, set up by the students’ need for specificity in executing the reconstruction “perfectly.” That is, they had a deep need for quantitative information or instruction, which they believed crucial to “do” the assignment properly. The students’ feeling of failure in executing this skill building exercise also related to the lack of aesthetic appeal and/or inedibility of their historical culinary recreation (subjective sensory reactions to the creation of an artifact) (Fig. 7). This also generated self-consciousness (subjective valuation around having one’s efforts result in something not identifiable as “good” to eat or look at). The underlying feeling at this stage was: “If I can’t do this right, how am I going to pass this class?” Even students who experienced relative success with their culinary recipe reconstruction (in that it was recognizable as food in appearance and taste) expressed uncertainty about their ability to perform at a high level in the Lab Seminar.

The students’ reservations tentatively emerged during our in-class historical culinary reconstruction debrief, and I fielded several nervous enquiries thereafter about how the reconstruction-based essays should be written. The recipes in Ms. Fr. 640 are typically unhelpful in providing identifiable materials or explicit instructions for measurements and temperature. From the students’ perspective, this presented obstacles to obtaining the conclusive results they believed were necessary to validate their research, and they had just experienced significant research barriers in their own home kitchens when trying to interpret early modern culinary recipes. Understandably, they were feeling anxiety about how to produce their research essays, which was met with empathy from Smith and the rest of the team.

What the historical culinary reconstruction exercise in fact revealed were deeply ingrained issues and anxieties around what is “acceptable” and expected in graduate-level research. Students exhibited the extent to which they had internalized the judgmental nature of letter grading and the Failure-Perfection Binary. While messy iterations, loose strands, and dead ends (hallmarks of failure) are a normal part of the research process, the current conventional model for the research essay expects any trace of these “failures” will be expunged in the submission of an essay in its final, polished form.15 Based on their graduate experience to date, our students had no point of reference for working with failure as a research methodology. On paper, the historical culinary reconstruction presented a useful introductory exercise. In practice, it created anxiety. This kind of work in Craft and Science was daunting for students and instructors alike. Yet we had to move forward as a class, and as a team, and go deeper into working with materials as an instrument of textual investigation.

Bread Molding

By the time we undertook our next reconstruction exercise—bread molding, taken from an entry in Ms. Fr. 640—a couple of weeks later, anxiety was high among our students. This time, it was caused by the enigmatic nature of the recipe, “For casting in sulfur” (fol. 140v):

To cast neatly in sulfur, arrange the bread pith under the brazier, as you know. Mold in it what you want & let dry, & you will have very neat work.

This entry from Ms. Fr. 640 is accompanied by a marginal note with an equally unhelpful suggestion:

Try sulfur passed through melted wax, because it no longer inflames & no longer makes eyelets.

As was the next passage that immediately follows on the folio, “Molding and shrinking a large figure”:

Mold it with bread pith coming from the oven, or as the aforesaid, & in drying out, it will shrink & consequently the medal that you will cast in it. You it can, by this means, by elongating and widening the imprinted bread pith, vary the figure & with one image make many various ones. Bread coming from the oven is better. And the one that is reheated twice retracts more. You can cast sulfur without leaving the imprint of the bread to dry, if you want to mold as big as it is. But if you want to let it shrink, make it dry, either more or less.

This entry posed many challenges: what was the goal of the recipe? What was it for? How to reconstruct a process for which we could not recognize an aim or object? We set off on trying to follow the process, although we had only barely begun to visualize what might be the intended product or outcome. Although the first mention of the process is entitled “For casting in sulfur,” we had to reconstruct an almost wholly unmentioned process which would allow the casting of that sulfur.

Our first reconstruction hurdle was thus making the bread (mentioned only by its “pith” in the entry), an alien undertaking to almost the entire class. Aside from the open question about how bread may or may not have been made during the early modern period, the majority of the group had never made bread from scratch using modern methods. The students showed great concern about the lack of an identifiable early modern recipe for basic bread, and shamefacedly reported their “failures” of dead sourdough starter, non-rising loaves, extremely dense textures, and other problems. They found themselves making loaf after loaf, learning as they went, with several eventually concluding that bread turned out to be a forgiving substance that taught the novice bread maker what it needed and how to treat it.

The second hurdle was making a mold from the bread pith, and students tried a variety of techniques: scooping out the soft inner part (the “pith”) of their loaves and forming it around objects, or pressing their models into a hunk of pith, compressed against a hard surface. Some cut their loaves in half, impressing their models into the still-warm bread—sometimes using the two halves to make two-piece molds. When “B-Day” arrived, we all gathered in the lab with the hunks of misshapen bread—i.e., our bread molds—that we had baked and eviscerated in our home kitchens (Fig. 8). By then, these were dried out little things bearing impressions from objects pressed into the soft inner part of the bread while it was still warm from the oven in accordance with the recipe.

The third hurdle was pouring sulfur into these molds. I harbored reservations about how hot, liquid sulfur poured into bread “pith” (the soft, spongy inside of the bread’s crust) could possibly be a viable casting process. My doubt was rooted in experience with various casting techniques as part of my Metal Arts training, as my previous profession was as a wax model-maker in Toronto’s jewelry industry. I was not thinking about failure per se; I just could not imagine this working successfully (not to mention, what was “very neat work” anyway?). Nevertheless, we melted sulfur obtained from Amazon Prime in our fume hood, ahistorically heated in a saucepan on an electric hotplate, and poured the hot liquid into the bread molds we jerry-rigged with masking tape and wedged into paper cups for stability. The moment of truth arrived when it was time to break open our bread molds once we figured that the sulfur had cooled.

It revealed an unexpectedly wonderful result. The sulfur models that emerged from the bread mold displayed a high level of detail—not unlike modern polymer-based models used by contemporary artists in order to produce more permanent, reusable molds of the object. The surprise that the students and I felt at seeing what we produced made me realize that I had expected the experiment to fail all along. The bread molding experiment was actually a success, despite the seemingly unlikely combination of bread and sulfur (Fig. 9). I realized that my own lack of faith in the recipe was a mismatch of expectations about the text, the implausible process it described, and the possibility of actually gaining insight from such an “implausible” recipe.

Suddenly the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 looked really good in my eyes. He knew what he was doing in this entry. His bread molding recipe was cost-effective, practical, and eminently useful in replicating objects and playing around with their shapes in a low-stakes and low-cost medium. I later learned additional facts that further raised the manuscript’s author-practitioner in my esteem when I discussed our successful bread molding result with our Making and Knowing Project colleague, Larry Principe (Drew Professor of the Humanities at Johns Hopkins University in the Department of History of Science and Technology and the Department of Chemistry). Sulfur has certain properties that make it a useful material in experimental casting. It neither forms a dross (the scum that forms on molten metal) nor becomes friable upon reheating. Further research revealed the prevalence of sulfur use in early modern casting. Once I let go of my modern expectations around reading texts and working with materials, I came to see that this was not such an oddball recipe after all.16

The “reveal” with the bread molding experiment was a humbling moment for me. Why was I so astounded by the result? Because I had dismissed the text as implausible before we even tried it, and because my first impression of reading the recipe did not align with my previous studio experiences. In retrospect, this first “official” experiment in the Lab Seminar marks the point where I began to think more deeply about the power of failure to transform intellectual risk-aversion (roughly translatable as “what do I need to do to demonstrate my intellectual competence and pass this course”) into risk-taking (“I wonder what trying X might show me, and how this might inform my project development”). For me, personally and intellectually, the bread molding exercise shone a bright light onto assumptions that I did not even realize I held, and revealed ways that doubt and dismissal had placed obstacles in the way of my curiosity. The bread molding experience not only impacted how I worked with our students, it started to change how I viewed my behavior and actions.

The bread molding experiment also precipitated a shift in attitude in our students around fear of failure. This recipe affected a kind of research paradigm shift through which we started to appreciate the possibilities that material engagement as a research tool presented. Our class began to go off-roading with making bread and casting objects in it. They created complex molds (a Buddha statue, carved in the round (Fig. 10, Fig. 11) and a bust of Marx, among other things) and they combined various mixtures of sulfur, tallow, and beeswax (as suggested by the marginal note: “Try sulfur passed through melted wax”) in order to create different textures, colors, and densities to see what would happen with these material combinations. Some of these mixtures turned out to be impractical as they were too soft to be used as models for creating other molds. On the one hand, they represented failures in a technical sense. On the other hand, they acted as prompts for deeper textual interpretation through more material engagement. Failure from this perspective presented an invitation to experiment, to be curious without restraint or fear of the “wrong” outcome. Failure facilitated an openness to follow new paths of inquiry that might unexpectedly emerge.17

Failure can be a visible mismatch between expectations and results. In the case of Craft and Science, this mismatch involved a tension between the written instructions and their material realization. Also, the successful implementation of a set of instructions to produce a presentable reproduction might in fact yield less insight into the hidden knowledge of the past than failure to do so. The failures invited students to critically engage with the material conditions, sequence, and scaffolding of experimental instructions, and the question of when something was good enough to form a “proof of concept” that demonstrated the plausibility of a written historical recipe.

Adapting the Making and Knowing Pedagogy

The interdisciplinary nature of the Making and Knowing Project provides several ways in which its educational program can be adapted and transferred to other learning contexts. The centerpieces in this regard are the Lab Seminar and the dynamic community created by the expert maker residencies and the annual meetings of working groups. The potential transfer of elements from the Making and Knowing Project into other educational settings thus requires a note on their structure and organization.

In the context of the Lab Seminar, students crafted their research essays for the production of a Digital Critical Edition of a specific, and complicated early modern practical text. This goal necessitated major funding and support for institutional coordination, project management, designated research space, conferences, staff, and so on (all the costs that also go into natural scientific research groups). That said, you do not need a sizable, major research grant to “do” the Making and Knowing Project. Adapting the Making and Knowing pedagogy is not about recreating the Making and Knowing Project, but rather about researching texts in a material way, and focusing on process in order to ask new kinds of questions. In my opinion, skill building and scaffolded project research constitute the most powerful and transplantable elements of this pedagogy for other projects at different educational levels and in other institutions. Two exercises from the Project exemplify this: the historical culinary reconstruction and bread molding, as described above. These exercises are relatively easy (and safe) to bring into any classroom and are readily modifiable based on the students’ access to a kitchen. The big takeaway here is not only about the importance of material engagement but bringing experiential learning back into humanities pedagogy.18

The Making and Knowing Project’s methodology has also been successfully transferred to other educational contexts, notably in the example of Yingyi Liang, a student at the Princeton International School of Mathematics and Science (PRISMS), a STEM high school in Princeton, New Jersey. In 2017, Ms. Liang’s teacher Dr. Roxanne P. Spencer had read about the Making and Knowing Project in a Chemical & Engineering News article.19 Dr. Spencer discerned Ms. Liang’s interest in working with metals and suggested she look into the Making and Knowing Project’s translation of Ms. Fr. 640 for her PRISMS capstone project. Dr. Spencer saw this as an opportunity for Ms. Liang to explore technical processes through analytical techniques included in the PRISMS curriculum, such as SEM (scanning electron microscope) imaging, made possible through a partnership with Princeton University that makes scientific equipment and training available to the PRISMS high school students.

In creating her experiments, Ms. Liang toggled between trying to reconstruct the experiments of Making and Knowing Project students from their research essays and detailed field notes, and her own reading of the manuscript in English translation. What resulted was Ms. Liang’s own interpretation of the entries in Ms. Fr. 640 for sandcasting, defined by her own experimental impulse around conducting “erosion trials” of her own design that utilized SEM to observe the elemental compositions of the results (e.g., comparison of crystalline structures and identification of chemical components). Ms. Liang’s erosion trials introduced water into her tests, presenting her attempts to accelerate, mimic, and study the aging processes of her cast metal materials. This marked a departure from the manuscript’s sandcasting recipes even as Ms. Fr. 640 provided a springboard into new questions about metallic structure that Ms. Liang went on to explore. None of the “failures” Ms. Liang experienced over the course of her experimental casting deterred her in the least; rather, they served to spur her curiosity. Ms. Liang’s work presents a fascinating example of a high school student’s curiosity and delight in pushing boundaries. She was not content with simply following procedures discussed in the Craft and Science student essays, but more interested in pushing material limits to see what might result, and then to think about why that might be.20

Interestingly, Ms. Liang found working with the Making and Knowing Project’s student essays relatively challenging, and in my view this mirrored the challenges that the Making and Knowing Project’s students experienced in striving to understand the author-practitioner. As Ms. Liang encountered what she discerned as gaps in information or lack of clarity in the Craft and Science procedures and records, she turned to the manuscript to puzzle out the process. In her own words, the procedures as described in the essays still required her “to do work to understand it,” and she was “looking to try to figure out the process” because the descriptions of the casting processes provided by the Project students were often hard for her to penetrate.21 These kinds of obstacles seem to have galvanized Ms. Liang’s curiosity and experimental impulse, and driven her to deeper engagement with the manuscript.

Overall, the laboratory-based teaching of historical scholarship as embodied by the Making and Knowing Project and its offshoot with the PRISMS high school experiment emphasizes the power of open-ended exploration that can happen in this type of material investigation. Students and teachers need to engage the issue of risk-aversion in today’s educational structure. Hands-on research can be one way to encourage intellectual risk-taking—a skill that can be carried into any future professional pursuit a student may follow.22

I have found that students tend to be very good at answering questions based on their knowledge of existing theoretical frameworks, rather than posing questions that stem from their own curiosity. My own pedagogical practice at the Gallatin School of Individualized Study at New York University provides examples of how skill building can foster community and teach intellectual risk-taking at the undergraduate level. My courses there in the history of science examine early modern knowledge-making practices, modes of investigating the natural world, and the role of intuition and imagination in the experimental process. They are structured around the student producing a final essay. With this capstone assignment, my students learn intellectual responsibility through the design of their own essay question (in consultation with me) that connects topics and readings from our course with their own academic concentration, which can be anything from music, biology, religious studies, computer science, or dance. The seemingly straightforward undertaking of posing an intelligent question is supported through two kinds of editing exercises: “Draw Something” (using images and objects) and “Draft Exchange” (text based). These exercises bookend the semester: usually, I start the term with the former, and run the latter towards the end of the course. Both exercises are weighted Pass/Fail: completion of the exercise criteria merits “Pass” (e.g., 5% towards the student’s participation grade); non- or incomplete participation results in 0%.

Draw Something prepares the student for developing their final essay question by directing their attention to the editing process through visual and haptic engagement. The exercise has three components:

  1. Go to a museum exhibit; choose any object that captures their interest; focus on an element, and draw it—on site, in the gallery. In a remote learning setting, household objects or things situated within the student’s vicinity provide useful models as well.

  2. Write up a one-page reflection about the drawing experience.

  3. Present your reflections to the class in a two-minute account of the experience that includes showing both an image of the object you chose to render as well as your drawing. Should a student be uncomfortable showing their drawing, they are welcomed to keep that element of the assignment private from the class.

In explaining the pedagogical rationale behind this skill building exercise to the students—that it is designed to help them explore and develop ideas and skills for writing their essays—I de-emphasize the need for perfection or even drawing ability. The point of the exercise is to observe and reflect upon what happens when you connect looking, thinking, and doing. I encourage the students to actively strive to disengage from concern over producing a picture that “looks good” and to focus, rather, on what they experienced during the process.

The seemingly simple action of trying to render an image on paper attunes a person to the editing process. As you draw, you intuitively evaluate your work, instinctively making adjustments and changes. This manifests in different ways, such as adding or erasing lines and shadow. Or in making adjustments to your original plan (e.g., the realization that your original intention to draw that Bernini statue at the Met in its entirety was overly ambitious, hence your decision to focus only on the pedestal). Perhaps you will grasp details revealed through the necessary close looking. Perhaps your work produces feelings of “not good enough,” or you discover an ability you did not realize you had. All of this is relatable to the process of essay writing, where initial ideas must be made into manageable form, and the ability to recognize and develop insights gives depth to the content. Draw Something invites the student to take the time to think about the process of doing and the kind of knowledge this produces. This simple exercise exerts a profound effect on the students. I have run Draw Something in numerous classes since 2014 and I am continually struck by how the drawing experience works to establish community in the classroom. Hearing one of your peers give voice to something that you yourself are feeling fosters trust and invites dialogue, essential ingredients for a vibrant seminar experience and a positive learning environment. The experience of the drawing exercise also serves as an important touchstone for the students as they develop their essays in written form.

Writing requires a lot of feedback. The second skill building exercise, Draft Exchange, presents a different kind of model to students who are used to writing as a solitary endeavor, and where the instructor’s feedback is given as assessment of the completed assignment. I generally schedule this editing exercise about a month in advance of the final essay deadline. The exercise thus works to circumvent end of term panic where the final essay often gets produced in a sweaty mess the night before it is due.

Draft Exchange requires the student to come to class with a printout of three to four double-spaced pages of their essay (the final essay length is approximately twelve pages, based on the challenging nature of producing a dozen pages of concise, meaningful, and insightful content). As with Draw Something, I de-emphasize polish and perfection in this draft, and stress the “work in progress” nature of the exercise. In class, I organize the students into pairs and give the partners time to read each other’s draft (approximately fifteen minutes). Thereafter they turn to comment on each other’s work (the robustness of the idea, organization of content, intellectual engagement with course readings). Students comment and make edits directly on their partner’s draft either with pencil and paper, or using tracked changes in an electronic copy. In remote learning situations, breakout rooms and collaborative editing on Google Docs works brilliantly for this exercise.

As with Draw Something, in-class reflections constitute an important part of the Draft Exchange experience. We discuss what it feels like to have pushed through the first stage of pulling together the essay topic and its attendant ideas (always hard, always messy!), as well as what it is like to both give and receive feedback in a collaborative environment. In my experience, students come out of the Draft Exchange experience with a clearer sense of direction about how to proceed with developing and writing the final essay, and, significantly, with confidence that they have something worthwhile to say.

Knowing how to ask an original thought-provoking question and understanding how to effectively develop it constitutes a valuable and transferable skill. Draw Something and Draft Exchange work together to provide the student with targeted opportunities during the course in which to engage in the dynamic and often organic process of working with an idea: it is not enough to have one, you need to know how to put it to use. Draw Something and Draft Exchange also humanize the process of thinking, writing, and communicating ideas. It gives students agency and allows them to share with one another the challenges, frustrations, and breakthroughs that are part of the process of making knowledge. Skill building in this context also provides the space where students learn to appreciate the careful labor that goes into developing an idea and seeing it through different iterations en route to completion.

Conclusion

The strength of the Making and Knowing Project is its processual and collaborative nature. It focuses on texts as guides toward practice, thereby raising a multitude of questions and opening up possible avenues to interpretation. Learning how to read a practical text as codified material practice invites us to rethink certain educational practices. Traditionally, humanities have taught close reading and interpretation by exploring the intellectual context and intertextuality of texts; Making and Knowing pedagogy inserts an active step into this close reading that forces students to confront the nature of a practical text as always inadequate in conveying its action in words. Instead, close reading of such a text must involve “doing” as well. This essay argues that incorporating “doing” into historical research has benefits beyond the study of practical texts such as Ms. Fr. 640. These benefits have to do with developing close reading, writing, and interpretive skills, such as explicit scaffolding of research assignments to make the processual nature of research and writing explicit, skill building as part of iterative design, and the consideration of evidence, which develops awareness and explicit acknowledgment of different levels of certainty in research. They also include more general pedagogical techniques, such as community building, the power of collaboration, asking new questions, open-ended explorations and risk-taking, appreciation of process both in material exploration and more generally in other types of research, and, finally, a recognition of the generative nature of trying, failing, reflecting, and trying, over and over again.

The contribution of Making and Knowing pedagogy to academic training is to introduce into textual analysis ways that a text about materiality and making enables access to a past physical world. What is achieved by the consideration of materiality in Ms. Fr. 640 (be it reconstruction in laboratory seminars or interpretation of the manuscript’s meaning in paleography workshops) is the understanding of the text itself as a physical thing that can give rise to actual material things, rather than just words about those things. Through its amalgamation of archival and laboratory research, Making and Knowing pedagogy establishes historical connections between the early modern workshop and the modern day laboratory as sites of knowledge production. What also emerges from the laboratory seminars are surprising relationships between manuscripts and experiments in the material world. In all this, reflection—in addition to doing—is key.
