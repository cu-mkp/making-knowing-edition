Tutor or Student? An Expert Maker Perspective

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/rzm3-dt19
← Back to Essays

Abstract

Each semester, the Making and Knowing Project invited an Expert Maker to spend two weeks with the Craft and Science course in order to introduce students to necessary skills for their lab work. Silversmith and conservator, Tonny Beentjes (Head of the Metals Conservation Programme, University of Amsterdam) joined the Project in Fall 2014. He led the students in sand molding in frames, following Ms. Fr. 640, fol. 118v. The students then took their molds to be cast in the Ubaldo Vitali Studio. In this essay, Tonny discusses his experience as an Expert Maker and the need to find a via media between his training in contemporary practices and the pursuit of historical reconstructions with novice students.

In the fall of 2014, when the Making and Knowing Project was investigating the moldmaking and metalworking entries in BnF Ms. Fr. 640, I had the honor to be the first expert maker with the Project, and I focused with students on the entries for making sand molds and casting metals into them (Fig. 1, Fig. 2, and Fig. 3). The author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 devoted about a third of the manuscript to the art of casting, and the sections on casting from life actually first sparked interest in the manuscript.1

The author-practitioner uses the term “frame” (chassis) molding. Ms. Fr. 640 is especially interesting for its numerous entries describing the making and use of refractory powders for molding. The author-practitioner also mentions the use of natural sand (or, earth), giving detailed descriptions of the location this sand can be found in the hills near Toulouse (fol. 87r–87v). The molds in the manuscript can roughly be divided into two groups based on their composition: the first uses plaster of Paris to make a liquid mold material, and the second uses fine powders or “sand” (sable) that is compacted in a frame around the model or pattern. The former is comparable to lost wax casting and was used to cast intricately shaped objects, especially objects from nature for lifecasting. The latter, in contrast, was used for casting less intricate objects without undercuts, such as medals, and was the focus of the fall 2014 session.

Casting in a sand mold requires a model or a pattern to be impressed into a mold (see Vid. 1). Unlike lost wax and casting from life, the pattern is not lost in the process and it can be used repeatedly. For frame or sand molding, the model needs to be sturdy enough to have the sand packed tightly against so that it makes a detailed impression. Some of the students made their own model first in wax and then, because the wax was too soft and sticky for a good sand impression, they reproduced this wax model subsequently in sturdier plasters (Fig. 4, Fig. 5, Fig. 6, and Fig. 7).2 The next step was to prepare the frame mold. The entry on fol. 118v describes the use of remnants of old molds, made up from plaster and brick dust, which had to be crushed into powder. Fortunately, parts of molds from previous Ms. Fr. 640 lifecasting experiments were available. We crushed these old molds in mortars and sieved them, a quite messy and laborious activity (Fig. 8).

Then the real molding could commence: the recipe stipulated the use of water with sal ammoniac (ammonium chloride) to moisten the dry powdered sand (Fig. 9, Fig. 10, and Fig. 11).3 Moistening the sand was the pivotal moment in the reconstruction experiment: the sand had to be moistened to a specific consistency, and the information in the recipe was not sufficient for a person without previous sand molding experience to know how to proceed further. The expert maker could intervene in the process, but this will also mean that he influences the interpretation and course of action. An expert maker is a practitioner usually trained in twentieth- or twenty-first-century practices, and, although these practices can sometimes be very similar to historical working methods, one has to be very careful not to impose modern efficient modes of working and thinking on historical practices. Centuries of experimentation and modern scientific research have resulted in generally accepted standardized practices of mold design and casting methods, particularly in foundry processes.

Previous reconstructions using Ms. Fr. 640 demonstrated that some of the practices described in the recipes contradicted these modern views on best working practices.4 The combination of a novice working with an expert or master is therefore a very interesting and useful approach to use in historical reconstructions, often providing a learning opportunity for both parties. The knowledge and skill gains of the novice are quite obvious, but the expert can also learn a great deal from the interaction with the novice. This is especially the case when the novice interprets the historical text first and makes independent observations and reflections, not hampered by previous “modern” knowledge of the subject. In addition to this, an inquisitive novice can, through effective questioning, force the “master” to rethink established practices and produce new insights into historical working methods.

The Fall 2014 workshop on frame molding revealed to me new and valuable insights into the consistency of molding sand. Throughout the manuscript the author-practitioner emphasizes the fact that powders used for the mold should be ground as finely as possible to achieve a good cast. My initial thought was that this aim for minimum grain size was entirely driven by the empirical thought according to which the finer the grains, the finer the imprint on the mold. However, I realized during this workshop that there are other factors at play. For example, the fineness of the powder also has an effect on the consistency of the molding sand, on the way it can be manipulated and compressed, and on the impression it makes on removal of the model. This has to do with the way water disperses between the sand grains: as the grain gets finer, the cohesion improves, especially when the grain size is lower than 0.2 microns and takes on clay-like properties. This realization occurred to me when, because of time constraints, we made molds using powdered molds that were quite coarsely grained (Fig.12, Fig.13). Indeed, when it came to handling and finding the right moisture level, these molds reacted in a distinctly different way from the very fine material with which I had worked before. During my expert maker residency in fall 2014 I was working on my doctoral research into the sand mold casting technique, and working with the recipes from Ms. Fr. 640 contributed greatly to my understanding of the fundamentals of sand molding.
