Ms. Fr. 640 and The Jewell House of Art and Nature (1594)

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/w16g-zd09
← Back to Essays

Abstract

This essay compares BnF Ms. Fr. 640 with The Jewell House of Art and Nature (1594) by Hugh Plat. It considers similarities and differences between these two works and analyzes areas of overlap. I argue that Plat, for all his emphasis on experiential knowledge, probably had less personal experience of casting, molding, and weapons than the author-practitioner. I explore the local contexts and knowledge networks in which the texts were produced, and how the author-practitioner and Plat dealt with the issue of secrets in their works. This comparison of the two texts highlights the importance of experience and exchange in early modern knowledge networks.

Introduction

A comparison between Hugh Plat’s 1594 book The Jewell House of Art and Nature and Ms. Fr. 640 reveals notable similarities.1 While Hugh Plat lived and wrote in London, and the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 seems to have been located in Toulouse, both works consider similar topics, such as casting, molding, weapons, and animal husbandry. Both were written in early modern European cities in the late sixteenth century and were influenced by the urban contexts in which they were produced.

Ms. Fr. 640, the author-practitioner’s unpublished manuscript, contains 170 handwritten folios of recipes, practical instructions, and other tidbits of information with marginal annotations. The entries range from sleight of hand tricks to cures for gout and gonorrhea.2 The contents of Ms. Fr. 640 suggest that the author-practitioner was particularly interested in (or knowledgeable about) casting and molding, cultivation, and weapons, among other topics. The author-practitioner’s origins are uncertain.

We know more about Hugh Plat. Plat was a prodigious inventor, a prolific writer, a capable gardener, and an avid student of philosophy and natural history.3 He was baptized in London on May 3, 1552. Plat’s father, Richard, was a brewer who made his fortune after moving from London to Hertfordshire.4 As a teenager, Plat studied at St. John’s College, Cambridge.5 In the 1571–72 academic year he was admitted to Lincoln’s Inn, an educational institution for students of the law. For young, upwardly mobile Londoners like Plat, Lincoln’s Inn provided both social and educational opportunities that would help to establish Plat as a respectable and scholarly gentleman.6 He was called to the bar (thus completing his legal education) in 1581.7

Plat also published his first book while still a student at Lincoln’s Inn. This book, The Floures of Philosophie, was a collection of self-authored poems and precepts drawn from the works of Seneca. His second book, published in 1584, drew on the works of Petrarch and the church fathers.8 In the 1590s, Plat began meeting with Londoners from all walks of life in pursuit of the various kinds of knowledge they could offer him. This knowledge was usually practical but sometimes philosophical. The information Plat gleaned from these encounters became the basis for his book The Jewell House.

The Jewell House consists of five sections. The first section includes 103 “Diverse new and conceited Experiments.” Like the entries in Ms. Fr. 640, the entries in this section of The Jewell House are a collected assortment of observations, instructions, and pieces of advice. They range in topic from how “To steale bees” to “How to erect or build over any brooke, a cheap wooden bridge…without fastening any tymber work within the water.”9 The second section of The Jewell House is a treatise on soil that includes strategies for the cultivation of farmland.10 The third concerns distillation and the fourth focuses on casting and molding. In the fifth and final section of The Jewell House, Plat presented some “New Inventions” of his own design, such as an easily portable pump and a light raincoat that was nonetheless “sufficient against all rainie weather.”11

Ms. Fr. 640 and The Jewell House consider similar topics, such as casting and molding, cultivation, animal husbandry, and weapons. Of the over one thousand entries in Ms. Fr. 640, over four hundred are concerned with casting and molding.12 The Jewell House includes a comparable section in which Plat describes the processes of casting and molding in forty-four steps.13 About thirty of the author-practitioner’s entries concern the cultivation of plants or how to capture and care for animals (for life casting).14 Plat discusses agricultural cultivation in the second section of The Jewell House. In addition, twelve entries in The Jewell House describe how to capture or care for animals and insects. Fifty entries in Ms. Fr. 640 concern weapons, compared to three entries in The Jewell House.15

For the purposes of this comparison, I systematically matched entries or topics that are included in both works. Using the Ms. Fr. 640 index as a starting point, I identified key areas of overlap and matched relevant parts of The Jewell House with corresponding entries in Ms. Fr. 640. A document listing entries I matched can be found in the Appendix at the end of this essay. This annotation will focus chiefly on the topical overlap that I found between Ms. Fr. 640 and the sections on “experiments” and casting and molding in The Jewell House. These were the sections in which I found the most significant overlap and of which I thought in-depth comparisons with Ms. Fr. 640 would be most useful.

Based on these comparisons, I will argue that the author-practitioner likely had more firsthand experiential knowledge than Plat, especially when it came to weaponry and processes of casting and molding. I will consider how Ms. Fr. 640 and The Jewell House were both shaped by the knowledge networks in which their respective authors operated. Both Plat and the author-practitioner appear to have been interested in the exchange of knowledge and secrets for personal gain, but the differing potential repercussions of publishing secrets in a book as opposed to including secrets in an unpublished manuscript likely prompted them to take differing approaches to sharing secrets.

Comparing Entries in Ms. Fr. 640 and The Jewell House

Sometimes Plat and the author-practitioner took different approaches to the same task. For example, both Plat and the author-practitioner shared strategies for making varnish dry quickly. Plat recommended boiling the oil until it was one-third or one-half consumed and then adding fish bones to it.16 The author-practitioner offered more complicated instructions which involved boiling turpentine oil, adding pulverized mastic, passing it through a sieve, stirring, and adding eau-de-vie.17 This is evidence that, in some cases, Plat and the author-practitioner shared similar concerns but had access to different information (or chose to share different information) about the tasks that they described. This also hints at some of the possible differences between the contexts in which they worked. For example, they may have had access to different materials or they could have been working under differing time constraints. In this instance as in others, the author-practitioner appeared to include more experientially-rooted details in his explanation, while Plat’s instructions were methodical and precise but not necessarily indicative of firsthand experience.

Plat’s work—and its reception by Elizabethan audiences—was informed by his high socio-economic status. Plat’s father had multiple sons, but Hugh was the only one to outlive him and became his primary heir. Thus, although Plat was a lawyer by training, his life of scholarly inquiry was financed by his family’s wealth. His time at Cambridge and Lincoln’s Inn, his pedigree, and his publications all helped him to garner a reputation as an educated and trustworthy man of science. Plat’s early authorial pursuits were not lucrative, but they did win him the respect of his contemporaries and a sizable readership.18

When London suffered a devastating plague outbreak in 1593, Plat produced expensive but effective plague cakes to distribute to Londoners who could afford them, including numerous prominent courtiers. Since he did not have a medical license, Plat was not permitted to charge fees for his cures, but he expected gifts in exchange for his medicines. His amateur medical practice was made possible in part by his ability to procure rare and costly ingredients, such as herbs and ground bezoar stones.19 The success of Plat’s plague cures was therefore predicated in part on his status. Likewise, the subsequent boost to Plat’s reputation can also be partially attributed to his high standing.

Plat thus earned a reputation as a highly educated man who was well-versed in science and medicine. By contrast, the author-practitioner’s manuscript, especially his imperfect Latin, suggests that he had a less scholarly background than Plat. While the author-practitioner may have had less formal education than Plat, his detailed recipes indicate that he benefited from a wealth of experiential knowledge.

One area in which The Jewell House and Ms. Fr. 640 overlap significantly is in their discussions of casting and molding. Their general instructions concerning how to cast and mold are often in agreement with one another. For example, when describing how to cast flowers, the author-practitioner instructed that plants and flowers “are not laid on top, but as in air, without touching on any side anything” (fol. 145v). Similarly, Plat wrote that when casting plants, “no part of your branch [should] either touch your table in the bottom, or reach to the uppermost part of the sides.”20 Additionally, both Plat and the author-practitioner suggested mixing sand and sal ammoniac as a good way to prepare plaster for molding. Plat noted that “some put a small quantity of sal armoniac to a great proposition of water, and therwith temper their pap.”21 The author-practitioner gave a comparable instruction, writing, “sal ammoniac water is very good, is excellent for moistening sands, but for lots of water one only needs a little sal ammoniac” (fol. 106r).

Plat and the author-practitioner also described similar methods for preparing plaster of Paris. Both suggest heating plaster of Paris more than once and mixing it with sands or powders, but Plat’s instructions regarding how to prepare plaster of Paris were more detailed than the author-practitioner’s. The author-practitioner wrote that one should mix the plaster of Paris with sand and that “it is necessary that it endures the fire twice, & is reheated twice” (fol. 106r).22 Plat wrote, “You must first roast or burne the plaster of Paris, before you mix the same with the reste of the powders.”23 After the first heating, Plat continued, one could prepare the plaster in one of two ways. One could break the stones, set them on the coals of a fire and allow them to burn, and then beat them into a powder. Alternatively, one could beat the plaster in an iron mortar until it became a powder, put the powder in an earthen pot, and then set the pot on a fire. Then one should stir the mixture “continually, with a wodden spattle for an houre, or there abouts, and until you see the spattle leave as it were a visible line, or tracte behind it, after you have stirred the powder round there with.”24 Plat noted that this second method was “longer and more troublesome” but that some thought it was “a better way” than the first method.25 Plat said to break up the stones of the plaster of Paris until they became a fine powder; the author-practitioner said they should be “lustrous grains nearing the shape of sugar” (fol. 106r).26 Both the author-practitioner and Plat recommended using sal ammoniac to make the plaster of Paris. Plat gave a specific proportion in his instructions, writing, “[S]ome dissolve an ounce of sal armoniack to every pottle of water.”27

In his instructions for making plaster of Paris, Plat included information that might suggest that he was personally familiar with the process, such as specific notes about which method was “longer and more troublesome” and visual details about how to recognize when the plaster of Paris was sufficiently stirred.28 He also referenced the ideas and opinions of other people, using phrases such as “some men do” and “others thinke.”29 This example is notable because it is an instance in which Plat described a process using more experiential details than the author-practitioner.30

The author-practitioner’s instructions about casting and molding were often more detailed—and more suggestive of firsthand experience—than Plat’s. For example, he included details about when and how to pick the right plants or flowers for molding, while Plat said only “take a branch of rosemarie, tyme, or isope, &c”.31 While Plat considered all plants together, the author-practitioner gives instructions for different types of flowers, including “Daisies” (fol. 117v), “Carnation” (fol. 159v, fol. 143v), and “a marigold with its leaves” (fol. 146r), in separate entries. This suggests that the author-practitioner, unlike Plat, was familiar with specific details pertaining to the casting of different flowers.

Other examples also indicate that the author-practitioner had experiential knowledge of the processes of casting and molding. In one entry entitled “Toad,” the author-practitioner provided instructions about how to use a toad for life-casting. He wrote, “you could make your cast in one piece & large, but it would be awkward to cut” (fol. 143r). He recommended instead that “it is best to do it in three or 4 parts” (fol. 143r). In addition to these practical instructions, he gave advice in another entry that suggested that he was familiar with the sort of mishaps that one might encounter in the process of preparing a toad for life-casting. He wrote, “if by chance you want to mold this vile animal & if it were to piss in your hand, it would burn your hand & itch, as if you had handled nettles. But the most powerful remedy for this is to put your hand into fresh earth, & to cover it as if you wanted to bury it.” (fol. 109v).

Plat was less thorough in his discussion of preparing toads for life-casting. He wrote, “Some doo kill Toades and frogs, which they meane to cast, by leaving them in oyle till they die, and some do put strong water into their mouthes.”32 He did not include additional information about how to cast or mold a toad specifically (as opposed to another animal or object). The author-practitioner suggested a comparable method for killing snakes, lizards, “& other similar beasts” (fol. 109v). He wrote that these animals should be submerged “in a bottle of good vinegar & some eau-de-vie” (fol. 109v). Thus, while the two men had comparable ideas about how to kill a toad for life-casting, Ms. Fr. 640 includes a level of detail that suggests greater familiarity with the process.

Similarly, an analysis of comparable entries in Ms. Fr. 640 and The Jewell House suggests that the author-practitioner had more experience with weapons than Plat. Plat’s descriptions of how to make weapons were succinct and do not include much detail. By contrast, the author-practitioner’s descriptions of how to make and use weapons were detailed enough to suggest that he had firsthand experience with weapons and warfare. In an entry entitled “Defense of a town” (fol. 21v), the author-practitioner wrote, “there is nothing that has a greater effect for the defense of a breach than petards placed at the entryway at the time of the assault.” He went on to describe the best kind of shrapnel to use in a petard (a small bomb) including “cart nails, large steel dice, bits of chain & similar things.” He also recommended using grenades and provided tips on how to make them more effective by “putting coarsely pestled glass either in the molds or in the crucible, when one wants to cast them.” He advised that the grenades “ought not to be too brittle” and that they should be “somewhat alloyed with the substance of the pieces in order that, holding the blast a little rather than breaking, they have more violence.” In other entries, the author-practitioner went into even more detail about how to construct, load, and use grenades, petards, cannons, and other weapons. He included such specific details as how much a petard should weigh if one planned to place it against a door or iron grate—“40 or 50 lb” (fol. 23r)— and how many horses it took to move various types of cannons—for “large culverins [...] one needs fifteen or sixteen horses” (fol. 18r). The specificity of his instructions and the tone of his writing make it seem likely that he was drawing on his own experience.33

By contrast, when Plat wrote about weapons, he made it clear that he was drawing on the experiences of others. In an entry entitled “How to make a Pistol whose barrell is two foote in length to deliver a bullet point blank at eight skore,” Plat wrote a single sentence describing how to load such a pistol with “a bullet a thought bigger than the bore” by ramming it with a “skowring stick.”34 He continued that he had learned “This of an English gentleman of good note and of an approved experiment.”35 In another entry, he provided brief instructions about how to construct a weapon with a “peece,” a hooking iron, and “the bore of a Tennis ball.”36 He noted that he did not come up with this method himself, but learned it from “the fine lymner of Lambeth.”37 He also indicated that his knowledge of this weapon was limited, writing, “I leave the full use thereof to be found out by Martiall men.”38 Thus, while the author-practitioner likely had experience with weapons and perhaps had used them personally, Plat’s experience with weapons was limited, but he was keen to show that the knowledge had been acquired from others, and required testing by those with greater experience.

The significance of space and networks in Ms. Fr. 640 and The Jewell House

The fact that Plat may have had less firsthand experience with weapons and the processes of casting and molding than the author-practitioner did is significant because personal experience was a key issue for Plat. On the title page of the 1594 edition of The Jewell House, just underneath the work’s title and above Plat’s own name, were the words, “Faithfully and familiarly set downe, according to the Author’s own experience.” In the preface, Plat wrote that his work was different from the works of other “excellent Artists of our age” because it was based on personal experience.39 He wrote, “[S]ome of them having written divers particulars, only by a theoretical and speculative kind of contemplation, and not drawn from the infallible grounds of practise, have published whole volumes by imagination only, in their private studies.”40

Plat often noted in The Jewell House when he had witnessed or performed a task himself. Although he was well-versed in philosophy, his writings indicate that he valued practical knowledge over philosophical or theoretical knowledge. In his essay in The Jewell House on soil and husbandry, he wrote, “I must here acknowledge that the best naturall phylosophie that I ever coulde learne at this point was neither out of Aristotles phisicks, nor Velcuries naturall philosophy, nor Garsceus meteors, nor out of any of the old philosophical Fathers, that writ so many hundred years past; but that which I have, I gathered it on the backside of Moore fieldes.”41

Moorfields was an open ground to the north of London that was first drained and leveled in 1527.42 In Plat’s day, the land was owned by the city of London and used by various types of people from around the city. Some used the land for recreation, such as archery or a leisurely stroll, while others used it to graze animals or dry laundry.43 Plat’s claim that he “gathered” the knowledge is ambiguous as to whether he actually worked the fields himself, but his assertion suggests that he may have had experience working the land for agricultural cultivation, or at least that he had witnessed others doing so firsthand. This example illustrates not only the importance that Plat ascribed to personal experience, but also the way in which Plat’s knowledge was locally rooted in the London area. Plat stressed the importance of place in his writing, noting that he had gathered his knowledge “within the lists and limits of London.”44

Deborah Harkness has argued that “London, as a house of science and a prototype of a modern laboratory, worked.”45 She contends that the “urban sensibility” of sixteenth-century London shaped the work of writers and researchers like Plat in important ways.46 Notably, it encouraged collaboration within and among diverse groups of people, and it made these people aware that their work would be studied and evaluated by others. Drawing upon Plat’s published works and his unpublished notebooks, Harkness demonstrates that Plat drew his knowledge from a diverse network of informants. Plat consulted with Londoners from all walks of life, from famed mathematician Thomas Hariot (who told him how to construct a cheap, wind-resistant lantern) to humble laboring immigrants.47 Harkness sees the urban context of London, with its thousands of diverse residents living in close quarters, as having been integral to Plat’s work.

Ms. Fr. 640 was also informed by the context in which the author-practitioner worked and wrote. Several entries in the manuscript indicate that the author-practitioner was probably based in Toulouse. Most of the author-practitioner’s mentions of Toulouse in Ms. Fr. 640 have to do with the acquisition and the nature of specific materials there. For example, he wrote that horse hairs for making sieves were brought to Toulouse from Spain (fol. 44v) and described two different types of mats that were available for purchase in Toulouse at the time of his writing (fol. 32v). In an entry entitled “Common saying,” the author-practitioner noted that the bell of Toulouse was one of the most beautiful in all of France (fol. 20v). This could be interpreted as a note of local pride, or simply as an observation.

In addition to reflecting the local contexts of the places in which they were produced, Ms. Fr. 640 and The Jewell House reflect the knowledge networks in which their authors operated. From his attributions in The Jewell House, we can glean that Plat’s network in London was large, diverse, and knowledgeable about a wide range of topics. Because he considered accounts based on personal experience to be more reliable than other accounts, Plat noted when he had attempted or observed something himself. When he had no firsthand knowledge of the task he was describing, he indicated that he received the information from a credible source. For example, in an entry entitled “A peece whereby to perform some extraordinarie service either by sea or land,” Plat wrote that he heard of the technique, which details how to build a weapon, from “a Gentleman of good conceipt in all ingenious deuities.”48 This attribution, while vague, works to assure the reader that the source of the information was trustworthy. Similarly positive but vague attributions abound throughout The Jewell House.

When he could not cite a person, however vaguely, Plat still sought to assure his reader that the information he was sharing was credible. For example, in describing a means for keeping horses well-fed and well-behaved, Plat indicated that he could not personally attest to the effectiveness of the method.49 Even as he admitted this, however, he included details that emphasized to his reader that the method probably worked. He noted that the informants from whom he received the information were still alive, which suggested that the method was not outdated. Crucially, those travelers “speak of their own experience, and not by bare report from others.”50 Where Plat relies on others’ experience rather than his own, he bolsters its credibility by emphasising the authority of its provenance.

We know much less about the author-practitioner’s knowledge network than we do about Plat’s. One of the reasons for this is because the author-practitioner made attributions much less frequently than Plat. When the author-practitioner referred to other people, he tended to do so generally, using phrases such as “some” and “others” to refer to other practitioners while avoiding any degree of specificity.51 In fact, the only named attribution is given in a recipe for “Excellent water against the plague of Monsieur de Montorsin,” which is described as “a very rare & tested secret” (fol. 44v). Although the author-practitioner seems to have been less concerned with direct attribution than Plat, he was no less concerned with asserting the authority and utility of his recipes. As well as describing them as “tested,” he frequently used the word “excellent” (excellent, excellents, or excellente) and phrases such as “one finds by experience” or “I have experienced” (fol. 88r). Sometimes the author-practitioner combined superlatives with his own eyewitness accounts and even with attributions. For example, he introduced a description of a method for whitening silver casts with the words, “I have seen an excellent German working thus” (fol. 121v). While attribution seems to have been the exception and not the rule, the author-practitioner emphasises personal experience and reliable witnessing as authoritative sources of confirmation, much like Plat did in the Jewell House.

Both authors’ networks involved exchange as well as accumulation of knowledge. Plat was not above bartering one piece of information for another, for instance when he disclosed some unnamed secret to the distiller John Hester in exchange for his method for catching fish.52 The author-practitioner, too, indicated that he hoped to learn from others by making his own knowledge available for exchange. In an entry under the heading “For the workshop,” he wrote, “as small peddlers lay open small wares in order to buy richer ones & to profit more and more, so I, from a desire to learn, am exposing what little is in my workshop to receive, through a common commerce of letters, much rarer secrets from my benevolent readers” (fol. 162r). This suggests that the author-practitioner saw himself and his manuscript as being embedded in a network of people who exchanged knowledge. In this entry, the only instance where potential readers of the manuscript are mentioned, the author-practitioner intriguingly implicates them in his network for exchanging “secrets” of practical knowledge.

Secrets in Ms. Fr. 640 and The Jewell House

The issue of secrecy arises in both Ms. Fr. 640 and The Jewell House. When the author-practitioner mentioned secrets, he often seemed to do so in order to emphasize the value or importance of the information he was imparting, rather than to indicate that the information in question should be kept secret—a rhetorical strategy which was common in the “books of secrets” tradition which rapidly gained popularity in this period.53 Although he called Monsieur de Montorsin’s medicinal water a “secret,” he still shared the recipe for how to make it. Similarly, in an entry entitled, “Against redness of the face,” the author-practitioner wrote, “make a small lead cap & wear it overnight. Excellent secret. Try a lead mask” (fol. 77r). The author-practitioner seems to have been willing to share his secrets, which consisted more in techniques and tricks than in secrets in a modern sense.

Plat also shared secrets in his writing, particularly in the first section of The Jewell House, when he thought that they would benefit others. For example, in an entry about how to harden leather so that it would last longer, he wrote that the method he described was a secret, but that it was so effective and would be so useful to “the whole lande” that he chose to share it.54 He offered his apologies to his “especiall good friend” for sharing the secret.55 Ayesha Mukherjee argues that Plat’s secrets were “designed to be revealed, widely disseminated, and modified by practice.”56 Mukherjee posits that many of the items that Plat described in The Jewell House were for sale in his shop of the same name.57 This suggests that revealing and subsequently monetizing secrets was one of Plat’s chief concerns. Malcolm Thick agrees, writing that Plat marketed secrets both in print and in person.58 We know that Plat sold artifacts from his home, but there is scholarly debate as to whether he actually owned such a shop or whether he simply drew up plans for one.59

Both the author-practitioner and Plat thus valued secrets, and both men were interested in trading their secrets for some kind of gain. The author-practitioner was explicitly interested in intellectual gain, at least by the evidence of fol. 162r, and may also have been interested in monetary gain; Plat was interested in both intellectual and monetary exchange. The author-practitioner’s entry “For the workshop” (Pour la boutique), quoted above, suggests that he envisioned a space in which to exchange knowledge and wares. (The word boutique can be translated as either “shop” or “workshop”). Plat’s notebooks include plans for a shop, which he called his “Jewell house,” in which to market his “Secrets and Wares.”60 Whether a literal shop or a conceptual boutique, both authors seem to have wished to exchange knowledge for money or further secrets.

In some cases, however, Plat was not willing to share secrets, even for monetary gain. In the section in The Jewell House on casting and molding, Plat used the imperative while instructing the reader to cast and mold. The style and tone of his writing suggest that he intended his work to function as a sort of manual or set of instructions for a practitioner. These instructions, however, are incomplete. Plat wrote that he was reluctant to include all he knew in his instructions because doing so would evoke the ire of artisans who may have felt threatened by the sharing of their trade secrets. “And as I looke for thankes of many that are ignorant herein, I am sure to receive blame of those who with long travaile and expence, have scarcely attained much skill as they may find in this work with a few houres study,” he explained. “Neither may I safely set down the infinite use of this arte, for fear of the infinite abuse which would follow by the lewde, and sinister practices of idle, and ill-disposed persons.”61 Given the published, public nature of his work, Plat was concerned about the preservation of his own social capital.

In one instance, Plat referred his reader “to the Goldsmithes” for more information about how to color patterns in gold.62 “Although I could easily set down both the matter, and the manner thereof,” he wrote, “but because therein I should discover a secret, that concerneth the whole trade, I have thought good to suppress it for this time.”63 This is a notable contrast to his apparent lack of concern for threatening the livelihoods of curriers, ironworkers, and brewers by disclosing secrets relevant to their professions in the first section of his book.

The rhetoric of revealing secrets was common in early modern alchemical texts and books of secrets.64 Plat’s choice not to reveal more information about the processes of casting and molding, however, raises some questions. Was he really as knowledgeable about casting and molding techniques as he claimed to be? Did his vagueness allow him to take credit for someone else’s ideas? We can only speculate about the answers to these questions, but they suggest that Plat may have been vague as a rhetorical strategy.

Throughout his section on casting and molding, Plat often implied, and sometimes explicitly stated, that he had more knowledge than he chose to share with the reader out of concern for how his text would be received by the public, particularly those whose trade secrets he described (i.e., his informants, or members of his knowledge network). This degree of reflexivity is absent from Ms. Fr. 640. The anonymous author-practitioner did not seem as anxious as Plat about disclosing the secrets of craftspeople, nor did he worry that setting their secrets to paper might make him unwelcome in certain circles. In an entry entitled, “For the workshop,” the author-practitioner made reference to artisans who might “complain that one discovers their craft secrets.” He wrote that if “the jealous disapprove that these things are picked up & taken from others,” one should remind them that “nothing is yet said which has not been said or done before,” and that no-one should presume “to invent anything new”—all one can do is to “rediscover” long-forgotten secrets and “publish them for those who do not know them” (fol. 66r). This indicates that the author-practitioner was mindful that the sharing of craft secrets might upset some artisans. He does not, however, seem to have been overly concerned about the consequences of sharing these secrets, and in fact provides a philosophical justification for doing so.

Like Plat, the author-practitioner may have been bound by social or professional constraints. He described craft processes that belonged to specific professions, which may have meant that he was a member of a professional group. However, he also described craft processes that did not belong to particular trades, and he included recipes detailing techniques that members of professional organizations would not have been allowed to practice. This suggests that while the author-practitioner had a broad set of skills relating to craft professions, he was not bound by professional constraints, or he chose to ignore them.65

It is worth noting that unlike the author-practitioner, Plat published his work. Although the author-practitioner may have intended to publish his work or at least show it to other people, we cannot know this for certain. In writing that he hoped “to receive, through a common commerce of letters, much rarer secrets from my benevolent readers,” the author-practitioner possibly gestured towards a future readership (fol. 162r). Unlike Plat, however, he did not yet have a clearly established audience at the time he wrote the manuscript. Plat was already a published author before writing The Jewell House.

There is some scholarly disagreement as to how Plat’s audience received The Jewell House. Harkness contends that The Jewell House helped to cement Plat’s status as a reputable author and purveyor of “natural knowledge.”66 Thick suggests that Harkness overstates the importance of The Jewell House in building Plat’s reputation, pointing out that Plat was disappointed that The Jewell House did not receive more acclaim and that it was far from his most popular work.67 His book Delightes for Ladies, published in 1600 (or 1602) by the same publisher that had printed the first copies of The Jewell House years before, would become Plat’s most widely read book.68 Delightes for Ladies was a cookbook intended primarily for female readers, while The Jewell House was aimed at any reader who might benefit from its contents.

Plat had a clear idea of who his audience was and often addressed them directly in The Jewell House. He was also acutely aware that his work would impact how he was received in society and how he was viewed in the scientific community in London. If his claims were faulty, his reputation would suffer; if he alienated goldsmiths, he might face social consequences. This may have also threatened his ability to continue carrying out his processes of observation and investigation, since he drew upon his social networks to accrue this information.69 Since the author-practitioner was writing notes to himself and hypothetical future readers, he was free from these concerns.

Conclusion

This comparison of Ms. Fr. 640 and The Jewell House has shown that Hugh Plat and the author-practitioner were interested in some similar subjects, such as weaponry, casting and molding. In some instances, the author-practitioner’s more detailed instructions suggest that he may have had more experience with the subjects at hand than Plat. This is significant because firsthand experience was a key issue for Plat. Both Plat and the author-practitioner drew on networks of knowledge to accrue the information that they shared in their works. Plat’s network was wide and diverse, but we know relatively little about the author-practitioner’s network.

A close reading of Ms. Fr. 640 suggests that the author-practitioner was engaged in processes of knowledge exchange and that he hoped to learn more via exchange in the future, but that he was fairly unconcerned about revealing trade secrets. Plat, by contrast, chose not to reveal certain trade secrets about casting and molding. This could be an indication of his wish to maintain good social relationships with those in his network, or it could be the result of gaps in his knowledge. Both the author-practitioner and Plat thought about potential readers and attempted to establish the credibility and utility of the information they shared.

Although The Jewell House and Ms. Fr. 640 are comparable to one another in some ways, the differences between them are telling. Based on an analysis of some of the areas of overlap between them, such as weapons, casting, and molding, we can conclude that the author-practitioner had more knowledge about weapons than Hugh Plat, possibly derived from his firsthand experience with weapons. In addition, the author-practitioner may have had more experiential knowledge about casting and molding than Plat. A study of Plat’s description of how to prepare plaster of Paris suggests that he had firsthand experience with some processes of casting and molding. By comparison, the author-practitioner provides extensive details about various aspects of these processes that are suggestive of experientially-rooted expertise. It is difficult to draw conclusions about Plat’s knowledge of casting and molding, however, because he stated that he had more knowledge about casting and molding than he was willing to share. Plat’s unwillingness to share these secrets may have stemmed from his desire to maintain good relations with those in his knowledge network. Both the author-practitioner and Plat worked within knowledge networks, as evidenced by the attributions in their respective works. They also each sought to trade knowledge and secrets for personal gain. This comparison thus highlights the importance of experience and exchange in early modern knowledge networks.
