Paintbrushes in Ms. Fr. 640

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/m26t-hn31
← Back to Essays

Abstract

“Paintbrushes” (“Pinceaulx”) on fols. 66v–67r is one of the few entries in Ms. Fr. 640 that concerns itself with the making of craft tools. By the sixteenth century, paintbrushes (also known as “pencils”) could be purchased ready-made by specialist artisans. Yet technical manuals and treatises on the art of painting continued to include instructions to make them in the workshop. By reconstructing the recipe and situating it in relation to contemporary recipes for brush making, this essay concludes that the level of detail and ready-to-hand materials in “Paintbrushes” suggests the author-practitioner at least witnessed brush making and perhaps even attempted it himself.

Introduction

Research into the materials used in sixteenth-century artists’s workshops is made possible by the survival of many artworks of the period. Research into the tools used to create these works, however, is more challenging because tools are likely to be used until they are no longer functional and then discarded. This essay examines the instructions for brush making in the entry “Paintbrushes” (“Pinceaulx”) on fols. 66v–67r of Ms. Fr. 640. This particular recipe is an especially useful case study for historical reconstruction for two reasons: first, because of the lack of surviving brushes from the period; and second, as historian Jo Kirby has noted, “there is little information on brushes in [the late sixteenth century], and what exists is rather widely dispersed.”1 Our fabrication of paintbrushes based on the directions given starting on fol. 66v gives insight into the descriptions, requirements, and instructions regarding brushes found in related primary source literature; our reconstruction can be seen as a form of supplementary evidence from a practical standpoint to address the lacuna in the historical record.

Many recipes in Ms. Fr. 640 detail techniques of painting, glazing, and gilding. These would have supported contemporary workshop practices, which required artisans to know how to use both workshop-made and ready-bought materials and pigments to produce various colors and effects.2 In contrast, the inclusion in the manuscript of the recipe for making paintbrushes, what the manuscript refers to as pinceaulx or what English sources would call “pencils” until the mid-nineteenth century, is not quite as easily explained.3 The entry is unusual within the manuscript, as it is one of the few times we are told how to make the tools required for painting.4 Given the centrality of tools to the various activities of sixteenth-century artisans, and even the use of brushes and paintbrushes in diverse spheres of artisanal work across the manuscript, the author-practitioner’s comparative lack of concern for their manufacture is somewhat surprising. As historian Susie Nash notes, “indeed tools, as much as if not more than materials, were often the defining feature of a craft, and the means by which guild membership was often dictated.”5 A typical artist’s workshop in the fifteenth century likely had scores and perhaps hundreds of brushes. Jean Lemaire de Belges’s fictional artist in La plainte du désiré (1503–1504) announces, likely with some degree of overstatement, “I have thousands of pinceaux and brosses and tools.”6 Both the brosse (brush) and pinceau (paintbrush) played central and often complementary roles in the workshop as two essential tools of the artist-craftsperson—especially the painter (Fig. 1).

This essay therefore addresses a key historical question raised by the inclusion of this recipe within the manuscript: were artists in France in this period making their own brushes, or buying them ready-made? If they were buying their brushes, why include the instructions for making them in the manuscript? And, whether the brushes are being made or purchased, how does this recipe help us understand the practice of painting and crafting techniques within the world of Ms. Fr. 640?

Brush Making in Europe

Textual descriptions for brush making are not as common as instructions for other techniques related to painting, such as, for example, varnishing or gilding. That said, various extant sources discuss this topic, namely, those by Cennino Cennini (late fourteenth-century), Cipriano Piccolpasso (1548), Valentin Boltz von Rufach (1549), and Henry Gyles (1664), all of whom address the making of brushes in some detail.7 The peculiarities of the recipe from Ms. Fr. 640 emerge when comparing it with these sources. As this section will demonstrate, understanding the intended use of a brush-making recipe is not always apparent from the text of the book or manuscript itself.

Cennini instructs the reader on how to make both miniver and hogs’ bristle brushes under the section heading, “The Importance of Knowing How to Make Brushes,” which he commences with the assertion, “first you have to learn how to make these brushes.”8 This could imply that the reader was intended to actually fabricate the brushes, but we can also understand this chapter within the overall program of Il libro dell’arte, which conveys the art of painting from the descriptions of fundamental techniques and preparation of the most basic materials through to more complex workshop practices and products. It is possible, however, that Cennini intended merely to aid the reader (i.e., “anyone who wish[es] to enter this profession”)9 in choosing brushes and appearing knowledgeable about the craft of painting. Cennini differs from the authors of later seventeenth-century painters’ manuals, who explicitly intended to instruct gentleman painters and to “enable them to speak on the subject of painting with propriety, and without incurring ridicule.”10 It seems likely, however, that Cennini, with his more practically oriented text, considered knowledge of brush making necessary to be fully versed in the painter’s craft.

Certainly, Cennini’s contemporaries in France seem to have made their own brushes, or at least delegated the task to their assistants. In 1399, merchant Pierre Berbisey sold pigs’ bristles and wire to artist Jean Malouel to make “broisses,” and swan quills for making “pinceaux.” Again, in 1402, Berbisey delivered a shipment of brush-making materials to Malouel. Berbisey was not a specialist trader, and his supply of raw materials to make artists’ brushes (both pinceaux and brosses) in the 1390s indicates that at least some artists were not buying their brushes ready-made.11 However, Gerald Ward argues that Cennini’s description about how to make brushes “is perfunctory and hard to follow and does not appear to reflect any first-hand knowledge of the technique.”12 In our experience, the information Cennini provides is sufficient for modern readers with little craft expertise to undertake a reconstruction; presumably, it was more than sufficient to supplement the knowledge and instruction already available to someone working within an early modern workshop.

As Jo Wheeler notes, by the fifteenth century, most Florentine artists were not making their own brushes: “Instead, they bought them ready made from apothecaries or specialist makers. Artists such as Lorenzo Lotto and Titian in sixteenth-century Venice purchased such brushes from the city’s famed color sellers.”13 For Italian artists, the practice of buying pennelli (paintbrushes) was so widespread that by 1587, in his manual De vera precetti dela pittura, Giovani Battista Armenini excuses his excision of miniver brush-making instructions, stating that “they are sold everywhere in shops and by apothecaries; among the best are those which come from Venice.”14 Similarly, the inventory of the Venetian merchant Jacopo de’ Benedetti (who died in 1594) shows that he regularly sold ready-made brushes of all sorts, including brushes in quills, pigs’ hair brushes, and fine hair brushes for miniature painting. As Roland Krischel notes with regard to the inventory, “this wide choice reminds us that Venetian brushes had a far-ranging reputation.”15 There is evidence that Venetian brushes traveled through Europe beyond Italy; Tintoretto, for example, bought Venetian brushes for the Spanish court in 1577.16 By 1586, brush-making had become a specialized profession in Augsburg, where pinselmacher appear to have been on the cutting edge of specialized manufacture, using metal ferrules in place of quills to hold the hairs of the brushes in place.17 The accounts of Wolf Pronner, kept in his capacity as the Duke of Bavaria’s “Administrator of Painting,” list payments made to Caspar Schecks, a pinselmacher or artists’ brush-maker from Augsburg, who supplied “very small artists’ brushes, artists’ brushes of squirrel fur, artists’ brushes of polecat, brushes in metal tubes, and older brushes in quills.”18

The early modern interest in the art of painting led to a growing body of manuscript and printed technical literature, which emerged as a distinct literary genre by the late sixteenth century. Little is known, however, about the intended and real audience for these books. Valentin Boltz von Ruffach’s Illuminier Buoch (Basel, 1549), for instance, includes, among its many recipes for paints and its scores of woodcuts modeling the subjects of art, a very detailed description of how brushes are made;19 however, much as is the question with Cennini, it is uncertain whether these instructions were intended to be followed. By the seventeenth century, manuals appear to be explicitly directed to the amateur painter, and they too delineated the methods and tools for amateur painting. These didactic texts include Henry Peacham’s Art of Drawing with the Pen and his Compleat Gentleman (London, 1622), Etienne Binet’s Essay des merveilles de nature, et des plus nobles artifices (Rouen, 1621, under the pseudonym of René Françoise, which formed the basis for Pierre Le Brun’s 1635 manuscript, “Recuil des Essaies des Merveilles de la Peinture”), Edward Norgate’s manuscript on illumination and miniature painting, “Miniatura or the Art of Limning” (written 1627, and circulated widely in manuscript), William Sanderson’s Graphice (London 1658, in large part based on Norgate’s widely-copied manuscript), William Salmon’s Polygraphice (London, 1672), the anonymous “The Excellency of Pen and Pencil” (published in London in 1688), and York glass painter Henry Gyles’ 1664 manuscript on painting. It is noteworthy that of all these later English works, only the last two instruct the reader in how to make brushes.

Although a handful of early modern texts address brush making in detail, they nevertheless leave the modern reader with questions, particularly about the kind of animal hairs required for fine paintbrushes and the means to secure those hairs. While Cennini recommends that the miniver brushes (i.e., the equivalent of the author-practitioner’s pinceaulx) be made using miniver tails and quills, Cipriano Piccolpasso proposes different kinds of materials. In his Li tre libri dell’arte del vasaio—a description of the techniques of maiolica production authored around 1557 likely at the request of Cardinal François de Tournon—Piccolpasso discusses the kind of paintbrushes used by maiolica painters. These are made out of either the hair of a goat or an ass, and bound and lashed onto handles with waxed yarn. Yet while Piccolpasso provides one of the few early modern descriptions of brush-making, his method does not incorporate a quill ferrule to secure the hair fibers, though the brushes he describes as penelli resemble pinceaulx (Fig. 2). In contrast to Cennini, whose instructions call for the use of the end of the miniver’s tail, Gyles does not recommend using the tips of the tail fur to make “pensills”: “The tip of the end of the tayle is not for making pensills on because the haires are crooked bending toward the poynts, but all the other haire above that joint is usefull.”20

If the advice about brush hair was ambiguous, then the advice about how to bind that hair could be just as divergent. Most authors recommended the use of some sort of ferrule. Indeed, the use of quills to serve as ferrules is what gives the “pencil” its name and thus shares an origin with the word “pen.”21 The early modern brush-making recipes we found all recommended some type of quill to function as a ferrule. Cennini mentions vulture, duck, goose, dove, and hen feathers, depending on what size brush is desired.22 Salmon includes swan, duck, goose, and crow feathers in his list of possible quills.23 Ward claims that metal ferrules were not used until 1655, when tin ferrules became prevalent.24 This point, as we have seen, is contradicted by the 1586 account of Wolf Pronner, although the practice he documents may have been confined to the area surrounding Augsburg.25 Kiddier, writing much later, notes that “brushes made with tin ferrules were not in favor with the old masters: all metals were regarded as more or less chemically antagonistic to the brilliance of the pigments,”26 but he does not provide a citation for this claim.

Once hair and ferrule were procured, the former had to make its way into the latter. Gyles suggests placing the hairs in a cylinder and arranging them by tapping the cylinder until the hairs are arranged, then clipping the bunch at the roots.27 This is not so different, in fact, from the author-practitioner’s instructions on fol. 66v: “putting it thus into the said folded card, tap it & shake it such that the hair comes together & is rendered the same length.” Nevertheless, several of Gyles’ recommendations are idiosyncratic, and this might be an indication that Gyles may have come up with his processes himself after trial and error, and intended the instructions to be used for brush making. As Robert Tittler notes, access to specially made pinceaulx in England during the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries varied with locale. While fine painting materials were relatively easy to procure in London, where pinceaulx were typically imported from abroad, places further afield (like York) often had to make do with home-made materials.28 By 1621, artists’ brush making in England had become such a specialized profession that it gained a royal patent of monopoly, and a “Mr Smyth, brushmaker” of London was so well established in his profession that he was able to take on an apprentice in 1630.29

In sum, clues about the geography of workshop-made and ready-made brushes before the eighteenth century are fragmentary yet suggestive. Hugo van der Goes’s St. Luke Drawing the Virgin (1480) shows two pinceaulx placed on the saint’s window ledge, while beside him on the floor lies a bird wing and a knife, which Nash believes was so placed as to suggest that St. Luke made the pinceaulx immediately prior to painting (Fig. 3).30 In 1521 in Antwerp, Albrecht Dürer is recorded as having bought “13 bristle [brushes] ready-made for 6 stivers, 6 bristle brushes for 3 stivers, and then another 3 stivers for another 3 bristle brushes,” demonstrating that even by the early sixteenth century, at least some masters were relying on specially made brushes rather than brushes made in their own workshop. Tittler notes that the brush makers’ trade had become so specialized in Antwerp and Augsburg, that “by the latter decades of the sixteenth century, it allowed for distinctions to be made between those who made the coarser, bristle brushes, and the finer ‘pencil’ brushes.”31 However, Nash argues that despite the developing profession of brush-making, many sixteenth-century artists likely made their own brushes. It was almost certainly an important skill for apprentices to acquire, a skill which many masters likely knew and they perhaps preferred to construct their brushes in accord with their own needs.32 Thus, the limited primary sources available that describe how to make pinceaulx or brosses do not present a conclusive answer to our question of what use Ms. Fr. 640’s author-practitioner intended to relate to his reader with his brush-making recipe.

Brosses and Pinceaux in Ms. Fr. 640

While this essay focuses on the entry spanning fols. 66v–67r, which discusses the making of paintbrushes (pinceaulx), there are numerous other entries in the manuscript that mention brushes (broisses), paintbrushes, or both. For example, the entry “Brushes” (“Broisses”) on fol. 31v instructs the reader on how to pick brushes: “Take some that have been used a little by white-limers, because the harshness of the quick-lime and usage make them easier to handle.” In “Paintbrushes” (“Pinceaulx”) on fol. 58v, the reader is told how pinceaulx ought to be cleaned:

When the color has dried inside, & you want to clean them, soak them in spike lavender oil and they immediately will turn soft again as before, then you will finish to clean them in walnut oil. Walnut oil is not as not as appropriate to soften them as that of spike lavender, which is clear like water & penetrates & does not have body like walnut oil. The handle of paintbrushes is made by those who work with care from porcupine quills, by others from wood of arrows from Turkey with whom with which they also make small rods to rest their hand when they are painting.

Throughout the manuscript, brosses are seen in use in a variety of applications: to apply varnish (fol. 4v), to polish stones (fol. 13r), to coat molds (fol. 67v), and to clean an oven-dried animal (fol. 129v).33 Pinceaulx, on the other hand, are used for tasks such as gilding (fol. 6v), laying down distempered gold (fol. 32r), tracing outlines on glass (fol. 39v), painting frescoes (fol. 42v), painting fine detail (fol. 58v) as the Flemish painters do (fol. 60v). Flemish painters, the reader is advised, are particularly careful to avoid having hairs from the brush stick to the painting, indicating that the danger of hairs detaching from the pinceau was as present in the sixteenth century as it was in our reconstruction.

Reconstruction

Materials and Tools

The recipe in Ms. Fr. 640 uses the word gris (squirrel) to indicate the sort of fur that is to be used in making the paintbrushes or “pencils” (pinceaulx): “To make them well, cut the hair from the tail of a squirrel’s fur, as much in one go as one can hold in a card folded up like a cannule.” We found the translation of gris as squirrel to be plausible but potentially ambiguous. Other literature, both primary and secondary sources, on the subject of materials used in brush-making helped us to circumscribe the problem. Harley notes, for instance, that “there is generally no problem in knowing when reference is made to bristle brushes, but it is sometimes difficult to establish exactly what raw material is indicated in connection with soft hair brushes.”34 Cennini specifies “minever” brushes, and sixteenth-century English sources generally refer to miniver or calaber tails for brush making; however, Norgate, writing in 1620, refers only to miniver. Gyles notes that calaber fur is finer than an English squirrel’s, which indicates that the two are not identical, while miniver appears to be related to ermine.35 Ward notes that the term “miniver” was used to indicate any sort of grey fur that could come from a stoat (also known as ermine) or a squirrel.36 The important thing is that the brush hair be fine, flexible, and able to be shaped into a fine point. If the animal indicated in fol. 66v is indeed a squirrel, it is almost certainly not one of Gyles’ inferior English squirrels, whose fur was more difficult to shape into a fine point. From this perspective, it is possible that Ms. Fr. 640 points to the Siberian squirrel.37 Its fur was prized by brush makers in the sixteenth and subsequent centuries, as its quality created especially fine-tipped brushes.38 Alternatively, following Ward, it is possible that gris merely refers to any animal with a grey tail suitable for brush making. To further complicate the question, the pelt of the red squirrel from Germany and France changes to a greyish color in the winter months. Thus, it is possible that the recipe in Ms. Fr. 640 refers to a red squirrel whose fur has turned grey in winter.

For our reconstruction, we obtained a grey American squirrel tail from an online retailer, as squirrel tail fur is still used to craft flies for fly fishing. Our reconstruction supported the difficulty in bringing the squirrel pinceau to a point, which Gyles mentioned as being a drawback of using squirrel fur. While it is difficult to parse whether the shortcomings of our brush were due to our novice brush-making skills or the quality of the fur, the fact remains that our brush fibers had a tendency to spread, making fine-pointed work difficult to execute.

The author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 does not specify what sort of bird is to be used to procure a ferrule, saying only a “quill whose size is appropriate, of a duck or a crow for the small ones.” For our reconstruction, we used quills from the wing of a Canada goose, which Professor Pamela Smith picked up along the Charles River in Cambridge, Massachusetts. The manuscript also specifies using silk thread, but given the unexpected difficulty of finding silk thread in shops near Columbia University, our reconstruction used a similarly water-resistant polyester thread.

Technique

Our reconstruction diverges from the instructions provided by the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 in two important ways. First, while Cennini recommends using multiple bundles of hair to construct the brush, the author-practitioner recommends the use of only one large bundle of hair that is tied and inserted into the quill. Because we were concerned that the weight of the pigments in oil would drag the brush fibers out of the quill (an issue we already experienced with modern brushes), we chose to use multiple bundles of hair. This method had the additional advantage of allowing us to shape the brush, as arranging a brush with a large bundle of free hair proved maddeningly difficult. Second, while the author-practitioner does not mention the necessity of boiling the quills prior to mounting the hair, Ward mentions boiling the quills as a common practice.39 Both Boltz von Ruffach and Gyles similarly recommend soaking the quills in lukewarm water, with Boltz noting that this is done to render the quills pliable.40

Having acquired our squirrel tail, we began by cutting the hair into bundles of roughly twenty to twenty-five hairs, cutting them off close to the tailbone as the manuscript prescribes, producing bundles roughly one millimeter in diameter.41 The author-practitioner goes on to say:

Put one or two bristles of a rat’s whiskers in the middle, then, grabbing it with the fist & pinching it well with the thumb & index finger, thoroughly wet the tip of all this hair in water, then, moving close to the light, pull out the hairs which make the tip too long with the ends of your fingernails, and this until the tip seems good to you, & until you notice in it the rat hairs which are entirely black & the hair of the petit-gris is at the whitish from the root to the middle.

Keeping these bundles held tightly between our fingers, we stripped the shorter “guard hairs” from the bundle before wetting it in clear water. Once it was wet, it was much easier to handle, as this caused all the hairs to cohere into one unit.

We then turned to the author-practitioner’s next step: “When it pleases you, bind and tighten it very well with a thread in two places, then cut the excess & fit it in a quill of a convenient thickness for it, of a duck or a crow for the small ones.” We wrapped each bundle of wetted hair securely four to five times in thread, roughly ¼ inch from its base, i.e., the side that was closest to the tail bone on the squirrel (Fig. 4). We carefully kept the hairs from the sides of the tail separate from the hairs that had come from the end of the tail, as Cennini recommends to use the end hairs for the tip and the side hairs for the body. This provides the “architecture” of the brush, which was often supplemented with a rat’s whisker, crucial for executing fine details. Each of our brushes was assembled using seven to nine bundles of hair, depending on the size of the quill to be filled (Fig. 5).

We cut the quill from the goose feather and boiled it for ten minutes in water over a kitchen stove. When we removed it, the quill was much more pliable than it had been prior to boiling, although this only lasted for approximately a minute, after which point the quill had cooled. We used scissors to cut the boiled quill into a ferrule of ¾ inches, cutting two opposing slits into one end of each quill. This slit was not mentioned by any authors, but was necessitated by our first trial. We decided to slit the ferrule because it proved impossible to stuff all of the hairs into the quill without compromising their arrangement. With the slits cut into the quills, the ends became large enough to hold and maintain our arrangement of the hairs, and could be firmly fastened around the girth of the hair bundle with thread.

Our greatest difficulty in inserting the hairs, once we had arranged the small bundles into the desired shape, was to ensure that the entire bundle made it into the quill intact. In order to keep the hairs from escaping the quill, we used a light coat of clear nail polish to keep the hairs in place for our second and third brush. While this was a departure from our sources, the problem itself was certainly familiar to those who made brushes: Gyles emphasizes the difficulty of inserting the hair into the quill in a manner that will not spoil the shape of the brush.42

Once the hairs were inserted into our slit ferrule (the quill), we wrapped the slit quills closed with thread (Vid. 1). It was not easy to keep the thread from sliding off the smooth quill, nor was the wrapping always effective (Vid. 2). While fol. 66v does not mention wrapping the quills, supplementary sources indicate that other practitioners found this process useful to secure the hair fibers in the quill. Piccolpasso’s illustration shows brushes that appear to be wrapped in thread.

Despite our best efforts, without a center whisker support, the arrangement of the hair fibers in the quills rendered our brushes better suited for medium point, rather than fine-pointed work (Fig. 6).

Why is this recipe in Ms. Fr. 640?

As our survey of the geography and primary source documentation of European brush making has demonstrated, it is difficult to draw conclusions about authorial intent in including brush-making instructions in painting treatises and technical manuals before the eighteenth century. Still, a textual analysis of the entry “Paintbrushes” (“Pinceaulx”) on fol. 66v of Ms. Fr. 640 combined with reconstruction offers insights into the process of brush making. Significantly, the author-practitioner describes particular details of physical engagement with the materials, indicating that he intends for the reader to actually perform very specific actions in order to replicate his technique. The reader is enjoined to proceed by “pinching it [the bundle of hair] well with the thumb & index finger,”—a technique that was crucial in our reconstruction and a piece of detailed information that is only important enough to be included if the author expects the reader to be performing the same activity. Furthermore, Cennini includes a “turning stick” in his description of brush-making, while the recipe in Ms. Fr. 640 advises that the hairs be arranged in a folded card. Because the turning stick is a specialized tool used in the manufacture of brushes, and is thus a tool used by professional brush makers, the absence of the turning stick and the improvisational suggestion of the folded card may indicate that the author-practitioner’s recipe is intended for artists who are “making do” without ready-made brushes or specialized tools.

Triangulating between the recipes in Cennini, Boltz von Ruffach, Gyles, and Ms. Fr. 640 allows us to suggest some tentative conclusions regarding the author-practitioner’s intended use for the recipe for brush making on fol. 66v. Gyles’ recipe is the most idiosyncratic of the three documents, recommending the use of a reed to arrange the hairs, thus contradicting Cennini on the desirability of using the end of the tail for the brush fibers. Could this be due to limited availability of materials in York? If so, then perhaps Gyles’s instructions for brush making are aimed at artisans who do not have ready access to London’s markets. Both Cennini and the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 refer to embodied practices, which seems to indicate that they both at least witnessed brush making and, as such, included their particular techniques as part of their instructions. We may also note the presence in Ms. Fr. 640 of “non-specialist” equipment. Rather than recommending a turning stick to arrange the fiber bundles, the author-practitioner mentions a folded card, indicating that the brushes are likely not being made professionally. When viewed as a whole, in conclusion, we propose that the detailed level of brush-making instruction on fol. 66v indicates that the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 had at least witnessed the process—and perhaps even tried it himself—before writing his recipe.
