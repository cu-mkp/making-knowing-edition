Counterfeit Coral

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/3mtj-rk54
← Back to Essays

Abstract

For millennia, coral has aroused the interest of natural philosophers, literary authors, and artisans who strove to imitate it. This essay argues that procedures to counterfeit coral such as the one on fol. 3r shed light on early modern understandings of the workings of nature and the relationship between nature and art.

Metamorphosis

Knowledge about coral and its properties in early modern Europe was the outcome of a long cultural sedimentation of different traditions. One component was a core of literary texts transmitted among the learned, including physicians, artists, humanists, and naturalists. The most important source was Ovid’s Metamorphoses, written in the first decade CE. The poem recounted the origins of coral as the result of a dramatic transformation in a compelling mythological framework: When Perseus laid the severed head of Medusa on the edge of the sea, some seaborne sticks came in contact with the Gorgon’s bleeding head and were soon turned into stone, thus coral was born. Ovid’s poem was exceptionally popular through the centuries, laying the foundation for a persistent tradition that was incorporated into Pliny’s Natural History. This encyclopedic work, written only seventy years after Ovid’s Metamorphoses, was an influential source to which, even after many centuries, early modern European scholars continued to refer. When discussing coral, Pliny stated that it was also named Gorgonia, as its original soft material had turned into the hardness of stone, intertwining the myth of Medusa with the stony nature of coral.

New Questions

Over the course of the sixteenth century, some of this information began to be questioned. Pietro Andrea Matthioli argued in 1563 that the berries of coral mentioned by Pliny were nothing other than the polished beads made by artisans.1 However, the idea of the transformative nature of coral, which turned from a soft material into a hard one when removed from water, persisted, conceivably due to the lack of any direct knowledge of its condition under the sea. Significantly, one of the first texts questioning that information relied on the evidence from coral hunters: Daniello Bartoli in his treatise on coagulation, dated 1681, refers to an entrepreneur in the coral hunt who ascertained that coral was hard even under the sea.2 Nevertheless Bartoli still accepted a hardening process taking place in the reproductive season, which demonstrates the resilience of such a belief and the challenge in meshing new evidence with traditional frameworks.3 Another early empirical challenge to the textual tradition can be traced in the 1609 Gemmarum et Lapidum Historia by Anselmus de Boodt. When discussing the nature of coral, he refers to a specific branch examined in the antiquarium of Rudolf II, which apparently showed both a vegetal part and a mineral one.4 On such evidence, he rejected the common belief of coral turning into stone outside seawater and proposes a theory based on a “stonifying sap” of coral (succus lapidescens). This stonifying sap would gradually turn the vegetal plant of coral into stone. The Nuremberg goldsmith Wenzel Jamnitzer’s use of coral in his portrayal of Ovid’s myth of the nymph Daphne transforming into a laurel tree alludes to these views of the transformative nature of coral (Fig. 1).

In their efforts to organize the complexity of nature in a taxonomical structure, early modern naturalists placed coral halfway between the vegetal and mineral spheres. Ferrante Imperato placed coral after stones but before mushrooms and plants. His definition of coral, similar to De Boodt’s, described a leafless bush made of a “stony substance” (sostanza pietrigna) that could grow as a plant both on rocks and on other shrubs by covering their branches “like a dressing” (in modo di veste).5 This is why, he reports, when coral accidentally snaps, we can occasionally see an inner core of wood (si scopre l’interno legnoso).6 He may well have been examining a branch of imitation coral, as his description coincides precisely with the appearance of the imitation coral produced following the recipe in Ms. Fr. 640 for “Counterfeit coral” on fol. 3r.

Our contemporary knowledge of coral differs from both Imperato’s and de Boodt’s observations. Coral does not have an internal ligneous structure or any vegetal components at all. Unfortunately, it is impossible to trace extant objects to which those naturalists referred, however, in the absence of additional evidence, it is fair to speculate about whether they were examining natural or artificial coral.

Recipes for Imitating Coral

The practice of producing imitation coral appears to have been common, and several recipes for imitation coral from the sixteenth to the early eighteenth century have been identified.7 These recipes fall into three types (some authors provide more than one recipe): the first type is the most common and consists of a moldable paste that is pressed into molds and dried. The paste was a combination of horn powder mixed with ashes and water, with cinnabar added as a red pigmenting agent. This first type was apparently the most appropriate for paternoster beads.8

A second type of recipe involved the use of fragments of natural coral. Coral was crushed into a fine powder and left in a solution of lemon or orange juice, mixed with a coloring agent such as cinnabar, minium, or sinoper, and kept under a layer of horse droppings for a number of days. The paste thus obtained was left to dry. This recipe, as mentioned by Giovanni Battista della Porta, produced material very similar to natural coral, and it was recommended for making small objects like cups but also for repairing broken coral.9 De Boodt also mentions this second method as used by forgers who expressly wanted to imitate the properties and powers of coral. On the other hand, he writes, other forgers were interested instead in imitating the shape and color of coral.10

This brings us to the third type of recipe for imitation coral: the recipe on fol. 3r of Ms. Fr. 640. This recipe is quite detailed as to materials and technique:

One needs to first make the branches of wood or take a bizarre thorn branch, then melt a lb of the most beautiful clear pitch resin and put in one ounce of subtly ground vermilion with walnut oil, and if you add in a little Venice laque platte, the color will be more vivid, and stir everything in the resin melted over a charcoal fire and not of flame, for fear that it catches fire. Next dip in your branches while turning, & if any filaments should remain on it, turn the branch over the heat of the charcoal.

This method (see Vid. 1) does not appear in other sixteenth-century sources, so far as we could determine.11

A recipe mentioned by Anselmus De Boodt and its short copy by Thomas Nicols mentions the use of a twig of a wild pear tree.12 Once cleaned it was covered with a solid, warm paste made of clarified pine resin, white wax, and cinnabar.13 The mixture was then molded around the twig over the heat of charcoal fire.14 This procedure was supposed to produce a perfect imitation of coral that only experts could distinguish. This technique is slightly different and less detailed than that on fol. 3r, but the result must have been similar (Fig. 2).15 Moreover, the details on fol 3r seem to be more practically oriented, as, for example, the author-practitioner mentions the use of Venice lake pigment as an alternative to vermillion.

Nature, Art, and Imitation

The procedure for this third type of imitation coral is significant, not just for its fine results but also because of its process of transformation. It is analogous to the transformative process attributed at that time to natural coral: the viscous red substance, called emplastre in the recipe described by de Boodt, could easily call to mind the “stonifying sap” of natural coral in his treatment of coral. In the same way that the stonifying sap was responsible for the transformation of coral from a soft, yielding vegetal material into the hardness of stone, the thick red mixture of this recipe solidifies like stone. The distinctive way the red substance of the recipe on fol. 3r coats the branch recalls Imperato’s description of coral growing over other sea plants and covering their branches.16 The homologies between the descriptions of coral by these two naturalists and the recipe on fol. 3r are suggestive.

The recipe on fol. 3r can be seen as a material instantiation of the knowledge system in which metamorphosis and transformative materials were highly valued, and were viewed as providing essential insights into the inner workings of nature. The fact that this coral was called “counterfeit” did not indicate that it was seen as a forgery or was meant to deceive. Rather, some methods for making artificial gems, as Imperato tells us, were understood to be similar to the natural processes of the formation of stones, implying the possibility that artisans, through their observation of nature, could harness its processes in order to imitate and understand its processes more perfectly.17 Finally, it is fruitful to ponder how the circulation of high value artificial products made by artisans could, in turn, have influenced the observation of natural phenomena. In the case of coral, it seems likely that the creation by artisans of “counterfeits” that aspired to simulate natural processes and effects stimulated scholarly conceptions of the workings of nature, just as much as the other way around.
