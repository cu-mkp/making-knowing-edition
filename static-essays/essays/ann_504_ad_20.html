Molding, Modeling, and Repairing: Lifecast Snakes Modeled in Black Wax

By DOI: https://doi.org/10.7916/8je7-1124
← Back to Essays

Abstract

Ms. Fr. 640 contains several entries for producing casts through a process of molding and casting in successive molds. One of these is “Cast of wax to represent an animal that one has not got” (fols. 139v–140v). This entry presupposes that the practitioner has already lifecast a natural snake in a straight shape in a plaster mold. This plaster mold is poured with a special black wax which can be bent into the shape of an undulating snake. This black wax snake then forms the model for a “sand” mold (plaster and refractory materials), in which metal is cast. On the basis of this and other related entries, this essay recounts a reconstruction of the plaster molding and wax bending processes, and compares the resulting cast to an extant sixteenth-century cast bronze snake that is a combination of lifecasting and “repair,” or modeling.

Molding Snakes in All Weather

The author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 includes numerous instructions for creating lifelike casts of a variety of animals by means of the process of lifecasting.1 On fol. 116v, he makes the observation that snakes hide in the winter and thus are not available at all times to the founder who wishes to cast them. He lists three methods for overcoming this problem: keeping the snakes in barrels, making several molds with one snake in the summer, or, in a technique that does not, strictly speaking, seem to be related to seasonality, making a lifecast of a snake that one then subsequently casts in wax, twists into a lifelike shape, and then casts in metal:

Molding snakes in all weather

aulcu Because in the winter they hide in the earth, some feed them des in quantity in barrels filled with earth & covered with manure. Others make several molds in summer, because with one snake, you can make four or five. And others mold lengthwise a natural snake, without it being curled, with common plaster, reheated as said pu , en noyau & in two half molds, as said, then they cast it in wax. In that way they have snakes that they can twist as they like, and then they cast them in metal, as is said.

In the margin, he notes “If, in twisting the snake from wax, some feature becomes undone, you can repair it by removing the protrusions from the molded wax.”

Lifecasting a Snake

In this entry, the author-practitioner makes clear that snakes can be lifecast from “a natural snake” and he gives instructions for lifecasting various animals from start to finish in a very detailed yet non-sequential series of entries spanning fols. 111r–116r. His instructions include catching (fol. 111v), keeping, and killing them to avoid injuries (fol. 112r), then posing them (fol. 111v) on a clay base (fol. 111r), to create two-piece molds (fols. 113r–115r) made from what the author-practitioner calls sable (“sand”), with plaster, pulverized brick, and feather alum (fol. 111v). The animal is burned out, and the mold is opened and cleaned of ash and any remaining bones. The mold is then closed and clamped together tightly, and, finally, it is poured with metal (115r–116r).

This entry includes references to and illustrations for arranging natural snakes on the clay base (fol. 113r) and molding them in a curled shape (fol. 115r) (Fig. 1, Fig. 2). Apparently, the author-practitioner determined that it is difficult to burn out a snake in a mold (as would be normal for other molded animals), and he provides two alternative methods. One is to open the mold and cut out all parts of the molded snake that are accessible, and then “reheat the mold so well that what remained inside burns, then pass some quicksilver inside, & attracting ce qui the ashes & the burnt bones from the inside, it will also come out, because the conduit is big, & thus will not sour at all your material.” The other can be done when the plaster of the mold has set and the snake is still pliable within it: “You can pull really strongly the molded snake, because it is soft & malleable & obedient, & the mold, having set, is so firm that, because of this, the imprint will not be spoiled” (fol. 115r).

Lifecasting and Modeling a Snake

After the detailed instructions for lifecasting animals on the foregoing five folios, the author-practitioner recounts on fol. 116r, as noted, that

others mold lengthwise a natural snake, without it being curled, with common plaster, reheated as said pu , en noyau & in two half molds, as said, then they cast it in wax. In that way they have snakes that they can twist as they like, and then they cast them in metal, as is said.

This then is a hybrid method2 of making a pure plaster (rather than “sand”) mold for a lifecast snake, but without the complications of posing and burning out (because it is cast lengthwise with no twists), then using that straight-snake two-piece plaster mold to cast a wax snake, which one then twists as desired, and, finally, in its new twisted shape, uses as a pattern for a two-piece sand mold, into which, after the wax is melted out, metal is cast. The result is a lifelike snake curved into its characteristic serpentine shape. We know of extant bronzes apparently made by bending soft wax, such as a sixteenth-century cast of a snake, curved into a characteristic undulatory shape, in the Kunsthistorisches Museum in Vienna (Fig. 3) (more on this below). This bronze snake further possesses the two hemipenes (male sexual organs) of the animal, which would seem to provide a naturalistic feature, however they appear to be added after the lifecast stage, and possibly modeled from wax rather than cast from life (Fig. 4). Remarkably, the author-practitioner mentions this organ in an entry on molding vipers, noting that “the male ejects from its nature [i.e., its genitals], which is at the bottom, when it is firmly pressed, a little mass like half an arquebus ball made in the genitals and full of very venomous spurs” (fol. 129r).3

This hybrid technique, recounted by the author-practitioner as useful to more easily create a snake with a serpentine curve, and apparently employed in the making of Vienna snake, suggests a kind of duplicitous use of modeling in the lifecasting process. This essay explores this technique, attempts a reconstruction, and poses a hypothesis about the making of the Vienna snake in light of several entries in Ms. Fr. 640.

An Animal One Has Not Got

In a long entry for “Cast of wax to represent an animal that one has not got” (fols. 139v–140v) the author-practitioner recounts a method for casting and twisting a wax snake. This method involves making a special “black wax” from a mixture of wax, charcoal, and sulfur. He notes in the margin that this type of wax is generally employed for objects that cannot be released from a two-piece mold (such as “those which have arms & legs out front or entwined”) and thus must be melted out (fol. 139v). The author-practitioner sees this wax as especially useful because “This wax is very soft & amiable & pliant as copper, and it is so strong because of the sulfur, which renders it meltable much before the other,”4 even asserting that “you can prove it on a hot slate” (fol. 140r).

After giving very specific instructions for making the black wax,5 he then discusses how to cast this wax into a two-piece “mold which is of white plaster” (fol. 140r), cautioning that

before you use it, soak it for a good hour in cold water & for at least as long in water so hot, that, at the beginning, you cannot hold your finger in it. And q take heed that it does not imbibe b more,thus but rather that it shows itself to be very wet everywhere, without the water being imbibed in it.

He notes that “it is good to soak it one night or one day before casting in it, in order that it be well imbibed. The same must be done for fruits of sugar.”6

Then, one removes the mold from the hot water (the two halves of which have already been bound together), and the wax, which must be at just the right temperature, is cast into it (fols. 139v–140r):

take heed if your wax has passed its high heat, which you will recognize when it no longer smokes, when it makes large tracts se re pulling at the edges, & motionless & close to one to the other. For, if you were to cast too hot, you could not separate your wax from the mold, & it would set in the cast. When it is in this good state, stir it with a little stick, in order that the pulverized charcoal is everywhere & not placed at the bottom. And in this way, cast in your mold, little by little, & not in one go, because the wax, by its unctuousness, does not flow like other things, & on this occasion, you can cast two or three times until your mold is full.”

The mold should be “cool before opening it, in order that the cast thing does not break. You will recognize that the cast is good when the wax tipped out of the mold is thin and smooth. Remember to make many gates all along the mold, in order that the wax may thus run better.” (More below on this method of tipping out the wax from the mold.)

Once the wax is cooled and removed carefully from the mold, it can be shaped and modeled. In the margin at the start of the entry, the author-practitioner had alluded to this process: “To make snakes with wax, or another thing to fix on a candle, one needs to cast with modeling wax of all colors” (fol. 139r). The long set of instructions for making the black wax and casting it into a mold clearly forms the main interest and focus of the author-practitioner, and he appears to be especially taken with the potential of this particular technique for multiple reproductions, writing, “And the more you will cast, the neater you will make it, and your mold will serve you for more than a hundred times if it is well managed.” In what seems almost an afterthought, he briefly mentions in the margin the technique for actually making use of the cast wax animal that is produced by this process: “When your animal is cast [...]. if you want to refold it & twist it around on some stick or taper, put it to soften on in hot water, and hold it while twisting it round” (fol. 140r).

Repairing and Modeling

Vasari describes the use of a “black wax” for “roughing out,” or “modeling,” a first version of a sculpture.7 This step of the sculpting process involves adding or removing material to create a first shape, whereas lifecasting consists in taking an imprint of a pre-existing form, thus the entries in Ms. Fr. 640 for hybrid lifecast-modeled forms perhaps represent an adapted or lesser-known use of black wax. Another entry in Ms. Fr. 640, however, points to an important conception of the relationship between repairing and roughing out: having molded the different parts of a bird separately, one can “repair them to first model a form” (& reparent pour esbaucher premierement une forme) (fol. 157v). This apparent equivalence between reparer (“repair”) and esbaucher (“modeling” or “roughing out”) is significant for understanding the author-practitioner’s aims in his art of casting. Reparer, from the Latin reparare, means restoring the molded object to a state resembling the original. More generally, repairing is conceived today as adding material when a molded object has holes caused by air bubbles inside the mold, or in removing excess material, such as the flashing formed by molded material leaking into the joints of the mold. Ideally, those adjustments should not be visible to the viewer. “Repair” thus today consists in adding finishing touches to reproduce the details of the template, as, for example, by chasing.

For the author-practitioner, however, repair and modeling mean something different. He speaks again of modeling and repairing on fol. 125v, in an entry for “Plaster for casting of wax” describing a method for casting a snake in wax: “to model an animal closest to nature, to repair it afterward” (Mays cest pour esbaucher animal au plus pres du naturel Et pour apres le reparer). This appears to be modeling (esbaucher) an animal by molding, with an intermediate retouching of the wax before subsequently casting it in metal.8 In the later seventeenth century, Félibien mentioned this double use of “repair,” whereby the sculptor can still interpret the wax before the metal is cast.9

The equivalence that the author-practitioner sees between “modeling” and “repairing” is closely connected to his primary concern of creating lifelike cast objects in molds that can be used multiple times. With his deep interest and apparent expertise in lifecasting, the author-practitioner may have preferred to make an entirely lifecast snake to preserve the natural detail of scales, but, as he noted on fol. 115r, the twisted serpentine shape of the snake made that difficult, thus he provides a hybrid method on fol. 116v in his entry about “molding snakes in all weather.” If lifecasting relies on the perfection of molding to produce the illusion of life (once the object is painted), the modeling, or “repair” achieves a naturalistic appearance by manipulating the imprint’s faithfulness to its original (natural) pattern.

An artisan making a livelihood by lifecasting would be concerned to mold snakes in all weather, and thus would find techniques that preserved molds and saved time and fuel of great utility.10 There is no evidence that the reproductive technology of lifecasting was regarded in the sixteenth century as a lesser or “mechanical” form of the art of casting. And, rather than striving to display his hand and ingenuity as an original modeler, the sculptor casting from life strove instead to display his virtuosity in hiding the work of his hand.11

My hypothesis is that the repairing of the snake mentioned on fol. 125v and displayed by the Vienna bronze relies on the author-practitioner’s dual use of repair in a technical and interpretive sense, that is, to respond to the technical challenge of molding the serpentine curve of the snake and to display his talent by hiding his hand. To explore the intermediate wax modeling stage through reconstruction, I first piece together the instructions from relevant entries in Ms. Fr. 640. I then describe my experiments with black wax molding and modeling, and formulate hypotheses about the bronze snake from Vienna. We cannot aim to be exhaustive, or to solve the puzzle of the making of the Vienna bronze. Our aim, rather, is to study the case of a collusion between modeling and molding in the case of the snake.

The Process

For the reconstruction, I followed “Cast of wax to represent an animal that one has not got” on fols. 139v–140v, but sought out further details from other entries on casting, on metals, and on the methods of coloring a cast animal.

In reading this entry more closely in order to reconstruct it, I realized that the sketch showing the plaster mold (that had been created from a natural snake) for the wax snake was not straight, but already curved into a serpentine shape (Fig. 5). But, these instructions were for making a straight snake that one could “refold it & twist it around on some stick or taper” by softening it in hot water (fol. 140r). Why is the snake to be cast in black wax shown in this sketch as curved? The answer may lie in a detail of the instructions on fol. 140r, where the author-practitioner notes that “you will recognize that the cast is good when the wax tipped out of the mold is thin and smooth.” This technical detail seems to indicate that he pours out some of the wax from the mold to obtain a thin-walled hollow wax cast (known as slush molding), which could perhaps be used for the molding of a core for a snake that would be cast hollow in metal, as is mentioned on fol. 107v: “When you want to mold hollow, it is necessary that the core be of the same material. And if the snake or the animal is curved or folded, one ought to make the core of several pieces.”

The entry on 139v–140v does not include the aim of making a hollow wax cast. Indeed, as is frequent in Ms. Fr. 640, the author-practitioner does not describe further steps in a process by which a finished object is produced. Instead, one must search the manuscript to glean any hint of subsequent stages. In the entry for “molding an animal that one has not got,” he does not lay out the steps by which the black wax snake itself is molded, simply noting that “When your animal is cast, cut with a hot penknife the flashing & superfluous things. And if you want to refold it & twist it around on some stick or taper, put it to soften on in hot water, and hold it while twisting it around” (fol. 140r).

The steps following this process of producing a wax snake in a two-piece plaster mold (made from the natural snake) would be to mold the wax snake in a two-piece mold made of sand (sable, i.e., plaster and refractory material) as is done with lifecasting natural snakes. The wax melts out during the firing of the mold, and metal is cast into the resulting cavity. Any metal flashing is then removed with various tools such as files and chisels. Lost detail, of the scales, for example, can be repaired using punches, or a burin, as noted on fol. 152v in “Repairing cast things.” Holes or flashing can also be filled with black, grey, or white wax made with ceruse. Finally, paint helps to hide any repairs, while completing the illusion of a live animal with its natural colors.12 Thus, we can view the addition of paint as the final step in “repairing” the cast—in the sense of making it resemble the original natural animal.13

Reconstruction

My goal in the reconstruction was only to experiment with the hollow and solid casting of black wax and to attempt to bend the resulting casts in the curves of the Vienna snake.14 This was of particular interest because the bending of the hollow wax snake seemed quite implausible. My goals were limited, and my results can only be regarded as preliminary.

For the reconstruction, I began by creating the plaster mold. I used an artificial rubber snake as a model because I did not have access to a dead natural snake. Since then, I have been able to recreate the mold using a natural snake (see https://insculpture.hypotheses.org/1183).

I followed the instructions on fols. 139v–140v, and drew details from two other entries treating plaster molds for casting wax, on fol. 125v, “Plaster for casting of wax,” and, on fol. 127r, “Molds of plaster for wax.” The latter includes a sketch of a curved snake with casting channels (Fig. 6), which represents a mold, made from a curved lifecast snake, into which wax is to be cast. In the author-practitioner’s instructions, half the weight of pestled charcoal is added to a given weight of wax,15 as well as a walnut-sized amount of sulfur. I made a first mold with a capacity of 150g of wax which had the form of a straight snake, as described on fol. 116v. I made this plaster mold in three parts to facilitate the extraction of the wax, following the example of the Vienna snake (although the entry on fol. 116v refers to a two-piece mold). I used oil as a release agent to make the wax cast easier to remove from the mold. For 150g of wax, I added 75g of vine charcoal and 18g of sulfur. The author-practitioner says that the sulfur causes the wax to be “very soft & amiable & pliant as copper, and it is so strong because of the sulfur, which renders it meltable much before the other.” I did not experiment with other wax mixtures, and thus could not test the function of sulfur in the process,16 which may also be related to sulfur’s association with experimentation and transformation in the author-practitioner’s material imaginary.17 The snake, made from this two-to-one mixture of wax and charcoal, was extremely hard to bend when the wax had cooled (Vid. 1). This stiffness must be useful during the subsequent sand molding because the shape remains stable when the snake is handled. I tried to submerge the wax snake in hot water to soften it, as noted on fol. 140r, but this technique is difficult to implement without melting the details of the snake’s scales.

Then I followed the instructions on fol. 125v to remove the wax from the mold while it is still hot (“do not wait for the wax to cool completely to release it, but do so when it is still a bit warm”), contrary to the instructions on fol. 140r (“Let it cool before opening it, in order that the cast thing does not break”). The solid wax cast could easily be bent when it was removed from the mold after 10 minutes (Vid. 2). I then cast a hollow wax snake. It was, however, very difficult to bend it without crushing or breaking it (Vid. 3).

The difficulties of bending the hollow snake vanished when I made a second mold matching the sketches on fol. 140r (Fig. 5) and fol. 127r (Fig. 6). Giving the hollow snake a lifelike curvature was then easier because the curves only needed to be bent a small distance (Vid. 4). In this case, it is better to follow the advice on fol. 140r and remove the wax when it has cooled, without, however, waiting until it is completely cold. It is difficult to quantify how to achieve this intermediate state,18 neither too hot nor too cold. The folds of the snake, as it turns out, cover the holes left by the vents of the casting infrastructure, thus reducing the necessary repair. One of the advantages of the black color is that it facilitates the retouching work because the details appear clearly (fol. 139v). Finally, my experiments have shown that the wax is easier to handle in all cases if resin is added.19

It is possible, then, to bend a hollow or a solid wax cast by using quite warm or lukewarm wax. Future experiments might vary the amount of sulfur to determine its role. The sketch showing a snake already twisted could be a case of “roughing out” in the sense that the molded shape contains the beginnings of the serpentine form that the repair completes by folding the hollow wax snake.

Hypothesis about the Vienna Snake

The Vienna snake encompasses two different intentions at the same time. The craftsman represents both the forward motion of the snake and the anatomy of its genitals. While we cannot know for sure whether the caster really molded the genitals, those two divergent intentions seem to be the result of a plan that did not include the hemipenes from the start.

Flashing (metal that leaked out between the mold halves during casting) has been filed off along the casting seams leaving burrs which indicate that the snake was likely molded in a plaster mold comprising three pieces (as I also did in my reconstructions, see the videos). This first (plaster) mold had one piece for the belly and two side pieces for the body. Making the mold in three pieces facilitated the removal of the wax snake. The seam of the mold piece on the animal’s back was hidden by shaping a groove in the wax all along the back of the snake (Fig. 7). On the underside of the snake, two seams are visible, left by the two-piece (sand) mold of the second molding (Fig. 8). These follow the outlines of the side casting seams from the first (plaster) mold. They are located along the juncture of the scales covering the upper part of the snake and the rings covering its belly. This judicious placement hides the seam within the natural pattern of the animal. In addition, the detail of the scales suggests that the wax snake was handled to bend the animal, since the scales appear clearly worn down through handling or by a heat source. The cast appears to be a solid casting, rather than a hollow one.20

The casting seams also pass along the hemipenes, which is odd because the craftsman would well have known how to hide those ungraceful traces within the animal’s anatomy (Fig. 9). From a technical point of view, this can be seen as a mistake, because these burrs distort the genitals. The flashing betrays two competing intentions, however, and raises the possibility that the plaster mold was first made to produce en masse snakes without hemipenes.21 Another detail supports this hypothesis. The snake’s rings are better preserved in the cast than the scales; however, the rings become indistinct on the second to last curve of the snake, where the genitals are located. It is possible that this part of the body of the snake was reinforced with wax to incorporate the molded hemipenes. Although it is difficult to know for certain, this addition could have been a way for the craftsman to overcome some technical difficulty. We cannot exclude the possibility that the craftsman fixed the hemipenes with metal pins driven into the body, however, in this case, any retouching would only be visible on the hemipenes themselves, leaving details of the rings untouched. Adding the genitals between the two moldings is an example of “repair” as modeling, or, in other words, making the wax cast resemble more closely its original lifecast model.

Finally, the snake shows traces of green paint. It is impossible to judge today in what way the paint could have covered up imperfections resulting from the molding or helped to define the snake’s anatomy to achieve a better likeness, but it too is an example of “repair” that is intended to bring the snake closer to its original model.

Based on the entries in Ms. Fr. 640 and in the light of this examination of the Vienna snake and my reconstructions, then, my hypothesis about the molding process for the Vienna snake is as follows:

  1. A natural snake is lifecast in a three-piece plaster mold, with the snake positioned in a straight line.

  2. Flexible, possibly “black” wax is cast into the straight snake mold. Perhaps this mold was slush cast to create a hollow wax snake (although the Vienna snake does not seem to be a hollow cast).

  3. The wax snake is bent into a curve and casting infrastructure of wide wax channels is added (although the Vienna snake does not show evidence of wax channels between the curves), then the modeled hemipenes are added to the wax snake, and an attempt is made to hide the casting seams left in the wax snake by the three-piece mold (thus creating the groove that shows on the back of the Vienna snake).

  4. If the snake is to be cast hollow in metal, a core of plaster would need to be poured into the hollow of the wax snake at this point, and core pins attached (although the author-practitioner says to make this core in several pieces on fol. 107v).

  5. A two-piece “sand” mold (made of plaster and refractory material) is then poured around this curved wax snake.

  6. The wax is melted out from this sand mold, and any remaining wax is cleaned out by opening the mold (as the author-practitioner indicates on fols. 150v–151v).22

  7. Bronze is cast into the sand mold.

  8. After the bronze is removed from the mold, the flashing and casting seams left by the two-piece mold are filed down.

  9. The snake is painted with “cold enamel” (i.e., oil paint) to enhance its lifelikeness and possibly aid in hiding the repairs.

This process may have been employed to create snakes for serial production, as both the plaster and sand molds could hypothetically be used multiple times. Recall that the author-practitioner was especially enthusiastic about this aspect: “your mold will serve you for more than a hundred times if it is well managed” (fol. 140r).

Conclusion

The case of the snake and the black wax shows that repairing is not only a finishing stage at the end of the molding process. It can also be a technical and interpretive technique in the interval between the plaster molding of the natural snake and the sand molding of the wax cast made from it.

The author-practitioner chooses to create a lifecast of the snake by means of direct molding of the animal, in order to preserve all its natural detail. The use of the black wax not only allowed for hollow metal casts, but its qualities also could respond to the technical challenge of the complicated serpentine shape of the snake. It had to bend without breaking or cracking, even when hollow, while keeping the details of the animal intact. The addition of the sulfur to the black wax may be experimentation on the author-practitioner’s part to improve the flexibility of the wax. Such experiments aim to overcome the difficulty of burning and cleaning out a curved snake from a sand mold. Molding a snake in a curved shape in the plaster mold not only allows one to represent the animal “in all weather,” but also allows for further manipulation. The black wax affords a technical alternative to molding the complex contortions of the animal by manually altering the snake’s imprint. This is where the ambivalent status of “roughing out” comes into play to give a new character to lifecasting. My hypothesis is that the realization of the final shape is relegated to the “repairing” step of the process, which is both technical and interpretive: it facilitates a complicated molding project as well as the interpretive manipulation of the snake’s serpentine shape.

The duplicity of the artifice is that it combines the authenticity of a direct imprint with interpretive manipulation of the snake into a naturalistic representation. The creative possibilities of the wax modeling “repairs” are illustrated by the example of the Vienna snake’s genitals. If we follow our hypothesis, the reinforcement of the snake’s body and the hemipenes are interventions at the level of wax modeling. For this reason, the Vienna snake can perhaps be understood in relation to Norberto Grammacini’s concept of “built mutations,”23 that is to say, as an object of curiosity made by the human hand.24 It is hard to judge to what extent this addition was a response to technical constraints and/or simply a creative opportunity. It seems clear, at least, that the hemipenes were not intended to be perceived as modeled by hand; nor should the snake’s serpentine shape appear artificial to the viewer. On the technical level, a repair, however minute, needs to integrate into the molding process. In this way, the hemipenes and the artificial curvature of the snake must appear as true as the surface of the snake as a whole. The stakes of the animal’s genitals or its undulatory shape, apart from its natural historical interest, are the virtuosic use of a modeling step that is integral to the molding process, and produces a lifelike result.

The specific case of the Vienna snake can engage a contemporary viewer in a twofold interaction with lifecasting. We can assume that the act of picking up and turning the object over to look underneath already destroys the first surprise at seeing a lifelike snake apparently moving towards a viewer and perhaps preparing to strike. The next stage of apprehension is that this illusion of lifelikeness is created by a direct molding process. But, if a viewer noticed the hemipenes, would their modeled form be perceived? Or does this constitute a third level of artifice: a viewer thinks they have identified a lifecast before them, but it is actually the product of a hybrid technique? A technical understanding of this object might produce a lasting sense of wonder, like a distant echo of the feeling of astonishment at ingenious solutions known in the sixteenth century as maraviglia,25 as the virtuosic secret to this representation of a lifelike snake is only uncovered layer by layer.
