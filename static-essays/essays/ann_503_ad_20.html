Abstract

Ms. Fr. 640 contains numerous references to Languedoc and Toulouse. The most numerous entries referring to Toulouse by name are about an excellent sand which can be gathered from the Toulouse environs and used to make molds for metal casting. These entries are particularly interesting for their account of firsthand experience and their observations about the properties of natural and artificial sands. They are detailed enough that they can be followed to locate and gather Toulouse sand suitable for making molds for metal casting. This essay recounts the search for Toulouse sand and the reconstructions made with it in the Making and Knowing Lab.

Languedoc and Ms. Fr. 640

Numerous references to Languedoc (Fig. 1, Fig. 2) and Toulouse in Ms. Fr. 640 make clear that at least some part, and likely the entirety, of the manuscript was composed in Toulouse.1

The author-practitioner mentions the sale of silkworms in Languedoc (fol. 53v), and mentions Montpellier twice (Fig. 3) with regard to units of measurement (fols. 17r, 27v), and once refers to the “tartar of Montpellier” (48v).

In an entry on fol. 20v entitled “Common saying” (Commu{n} dire), the author-practitioner lists notable features of the region, including the bells of the Mende cathedral (Fig. 4), the bell tower of the Rodez cathedral (Fig. 5), and the church of Albi (Fig. 6). But he counters this common saying by noting that the great bell of Toulouse, the Cardaillac in the Cathedral of Saint-Etienne in Toulouse, is one of the most beautiful in France, “all the more since it weighs two hundred and fifty quintals.”

In an entry on “Plaster,” he notes that plaster “from the mountains is greyer, and the one from the region of Albi is whiter” (fol. 12r). At the same time, he knows that “there is found in Cominge[s], near the town of Aurignac, a sand white like salt and lean, that glassmakers & potters use,” and he apparently made trials of it for mold making, noting that it “becomes impalpable ground on porphyry & is easy to grind. And once ground, it resembles calcined alabaster. It molds very neatly, and I have not found any that molds as delicately as this one for low relief” (fol. 89v) (Fig. 7).

The author-practitioner also knows the qualities of the clay soils in the Fousseret and Ox areas (fol. 81r) (Fig. 8).

He refers to the iron bars from the city of Foix (fol. 50v), a reference interesting for its discussion of different grades and origins of iron ingots:

The steel that farriers & iron workers commonly use is not refined like that from Germany or Biscay, which is reduced in hardness p in the bath of molten iron, but only in the iron bars which are transported in flat ingots from the forges of Foix & elsewhere, there is a harder & whiter & finer kind than the other, as it comes from the mine. And the workers choose it & use it like the other steel. And because [that] it comes from common iron, they call it strong iron. But it is not as excellent as purified steel like that from Germany & Biscaye, which is sold in small beads.

He makes reference again to Foix on fols. 90r and 143r, where he notes that the molders of Foix “cast their medals crosswise” (Fig. 9),

He refers to the fertile Lauragais plain (Fig. 10) for its pastel woad cultivation (fol. 39r), used to make a blue dye, and to the villages of pastel production of Auragnes and Caraman (Fig. 11, Fig. 12), where he notes that “The best pastel woad of Lauragais is the one from Carmail & the one from Auragne.”

The entry containing this passage (fol. 39r) stands out for its detailed account of woad cultivation:

It is grown in Lauragais where the depth of the earth is so fertile that if one were to grow wheat there every year, it would lie flat for being too vigorous. This is why one alternately does pastel woad and wheat there. For the cultivation of pastel woad, one ploughs the soil with shovels of iron, as gardeners do. Next, one harrows it with rakes, & breaks it up finely as for sowing cooking herbs. One commonly sows it on St. Anthony’s day in January. One makes eight harvests of it. The first ones are better. The best pastel woad of Lauragais is the one from Carmail & the one from Auragne. And sometimes the pastel woad is good in one field & in the one close by it will hardly have worth. The goodness of the pastel woad is known when, put in the mouth, it gives a taste as of vinegar, or when crumbling & breaking it, it has some mold-like veins which are as if golden or silver. One assays it in the dyers’ vat, and to fill a vat with it, one needs six balls of it. In this, one dyes several locks of wool, and if it dyes fifteen times, it is said to be fifteen florins, if it gives xx dyings, xx florins. The good kind dyes up to 30 times & commonly up to xxv or 26.

The author-practitioner mentions woad in eight different entries, underscoring its economic significance for Toulouse at this time.

Toulouse

The author-practitioner refers to the city of Toulouse (Fig. 13) eleven times, and mentions people and places of the city, describing features of the region, for example, a local specialty of mat making and dyeing with woad (fol. 32v):

Mat maker

They make two kinds of them in Toulouse, one to hang on the partition walls of rooms, which they weave almost as fine as the straw hats worn by villagers. And they make them in long bands, some the width of ten straws, others of thirteen. And they work on them mainly in summer. And in winter, when they put it to use, they sew it. But beforehand, they put it in dye and commonly make it in three colors, green, red, and sometimes violet. The green one is made in a single pastel woad dye, because green being made from yellow and blue, the pastel woad discharging itself onto the straw, which shows its dark yellow, one obtains bright green. For the red, they use alum and brazilwood. For the violet, they use pastel woad & a little copperas, which browns the blue with its black tint.

He also notes an interesting trade network of horsehair sieves from Normandy through Toulouse to Spain (fol. 44r):

Horsehair sieves

They are made at Coustance in Normandy with horse tail hair which they clean with lye because they sort them with the mouth & separate out short & broken ones. They do not make the reed longer than the sieve, & the reed is round. They attach to both edges of the reed the hair, either black or white, according to the work they want to make. And the weft is done over & under as for making any other fabric. And passing a small flat stick of ii or three fingers wide between, next they pass two hairs at each step of the pedal and weave with three pedals. There are xviic [1700] hairs in the entire sieve. They sell them by the dozen & each dozen xxx sous. They bring them to Toulouse to transport them afterward to Spain, and take silk tammy in exchange for it.

The author-practitioner refers to the arsenal of Toulouse (Fig. 14) and its family of master founders, the Poncet (fols. 17v and 49r) (Fig. 15), one of whom founded three bells for the Saint-Etienne Cathedral (Fig. 16) bell tower where the wondrous thirteen-ton bell, the Cardaillac, noted by the author-practitioner on fol. 20v, also hung.

The author-practitioner’s references to various identifiable individuals in Toulouse, like the Poncet, add to our understanding of the manuscript’s composition,2 while the most frequent mentions of Toulouse in the manuscript, which are about the excellent sand of Toulouse, give remarkable insight into the author-practitioner’s firsthand experience of the Toulouse landscape, and into his attitudes toward natural materials and the possibility of transforming them by his art.

Toulouse Sand

The entries that refer explicitly to Toulouse sand (Fig. 17, Fig. 18) are particularly interesting for their recounting of firsthand experience and the author-practitioner’s remarks about the properties of natural and artificial sands. The entries begin with a very long set of notes on “Sand” that runs from fol. 69r to 70v, including the comment that “The sand near my area is very excellent. But one ought to, in order to make an excellent work, take it fresh, which has been hardly put to work. For it dries out after having been cast several times & is lean & has no body” (fol. 69v). In the margin at the beginning of this “Sand” entry, he makes reference to “The Toulouse molder,” noting that he “reheats it strongly then grinds it finely on marble and passes it through a linen & moistens it with wine. He makes the cast very broad & flat & barely deep. He casts pure latton used for trebuschets & similar thin things. He casts very hot” (fol. 69r).

On fol. 81r, he pinpoints the location of this sand:

One can find an excellent one on the little hill of Puy David near Thoulouse for lead, tin & copper. It does not want to be too much reheated because, drying out all at once, it loses its bond & burns at the first arrival of metal, which makes the work lumpy & not neat.

At the bottom of fol. 84r, the entry “Sand from a mine of Thoulouse,” which begins another intensive set of notes on firsthand experiments with “sands,” also makes reference to the location of the best earths near Toulouse:

The sand from Thoulouse, which is taken from the depths of the earth, from the small hill of Puy David, is excellent in itself, but to make it withstand several castings, I mix it with pulverized sandiver & moistened, which hardens it, & takes body & bond with it, & makes it withstand more than five as many castings as you wish. Try to moisten the pestled glass and other sands with the waters of the aforesaid salts. It wants to be, like all natural sand, well reheated before putting it to use. One pulverizes it, because it is in lumps, then one reheats it in a copper kettle or similar thing, until it no longer smokes.

His campaign of experimentation continued with the entry “Experimented sands” (Sables experimentés) on fol. 85v, which records that

I have experimented with sand from Th{oulous}e, & after reheating it well twice, in a skillet, I passed it through a fine sieve, like the apothecaries’ double, without finely grinding it further on the porphyry, as I have done previously. I moistened it with wine boiled with elm root and molded with it a large piece of a portrait of Jesus.

Two more entries refer to the sand of Toulouse, the first on fol. 87r, further specifies the location of the sand:

Sand from Thoulouse

The usual good one is the one which is found in a vineyard near Puy David, but the one which is most excellent is the one from the Touch, near Sainct Michel & toward Blagnac, in a vineyard that is quite high up. The latter is finer & a little fatter than the other, & better for small works. It does not want to be reheated too much.

A marginal note on fol. 87v specifies the properties one must look for: “One ought to choose the one which is as in clods & lumps, well deep in the earth, for usually the one that is found higher up is too much a kin of the earth, and the lower is a kin of the rock.” This intensive recounting of firsthand experience of the Toulouse sand ends on fol. 87v with one more entry on “Sand from a mine of Thoulouse,” which perhaps serves as a summary of his experience with preparing this sand to produce successful molds:

Casting sand coming from the mine, once passed through a double sieve, next put in with melted resin, burns & inflames & becomes all red & inflamed like iron. Once cold, it is completely black and can be ground very finely on porphyry. Having prepared it thus & rendered it without asperity on the fingernail, I moistened it with beaten glair of egg & beat it well, until it was not pasty but rather powdery. I found it of very good release, & molded with it in lead & tin very neatly, but it wants to be well reheated & at ease.

Others beat it well in a mortar, in small amounts at a time, & thus it is pressed together and rendered very fine. Then they reheat it moderately, only to dry it. Next, they grind it dry on porphyry. And thus it becomes as if impalpable and not too dried out, it and it retains the body & the bond of the earth to which it is kin and is better than when it is so burnt. Once moistened with glair of egg passed through a sponge, it releases very neatly M very neatly in low relief, but not for figures in high relief. Therefore, since then, I have experimented lexper with moistening it only with very strong vinegar. It released a figure which I could not release previously. And I believe that, moistening the finest in a fashion as with glair, & the coarsest, for filling, with gushes of salt water or wine, that they do not join so well. But as they are of one nature & are moistened the same, they embrace each other & hold together one with the other.

For molding well, after having prepared your sands, mold in a day. Slowly reheat them the next day, then cast them on another.

While his firsthand accounts of sand and mold making pick up again on fols. 88v–90v, and some of these entries discuss experimenting with “sand from the mine,” this entry on fol. 87v is the last explicit mention in the manuscript of Toulouse sand.

The author-practitioner’s experience with Toulouse sand, however, appears to have allowed him to draw more general conclusions about the important properties of any sand gathered from nature for the purposes of mold making. On fol. 88v in an entry entitled “Mineral Sand,” he lays out more general observations about the properties of natural sand, noting that color is not a significant marker, but rather, the bond it demonstrates by coming out in lumps, yet still breaking apart in the hands is the crucial identifying characteristic:

It does not matter if the color is white or yellow. Above all it must be in one piece & as if taken from a quarry or rock formation, & the deeper one takes it from, the better. The signs of its goodness are that it is thus amassed, and that when removing itself in the form of rock, it comes out in lumps & qu bricks, which demonstrate its bond & that it is not too lean.

However, it should break apart between your hands & have very small men & delicate grains & of the same nature.

He then instructs in how to remedy sand that is not quite delicate enough:

If it is not delicate enough, you can pass & grind it finely, either through water or through a sieve & when it or on the porphyry, & in this way, from sil lean they become fatty & well bound. You can mold with it in sa frame or en noyau without cloth waste, & try it with lead, for if with this it does not become porous & casts neatly, it will also behave well with copper. Some say that the fatty sands do not want the metal to be cast too hot. Artisans who work in large works &, who for profit, do not need to grind & seek the curiosities of artificial sands, benefit more from seeking some that is ready-made by nature, which has the finest grain possible, & for small works, they only pass it through a sieve. But those who work in small works, finely grind it & grind it impalpable, because they do not need a lot of it.

In an entry on fol. 90r–v, “That any sand can be rendered good,” he restates these observations on the potential of sands collected from nature to be worked into suitable mold material, noting the free gift nature makes to “her children” in providing sand that needs little modification. He further specifies that such sand cannot be found in all regions, but nonetheless, “you can be certain that in all places you can render the sand from a mine good & proper for molding.” He notes again the characteristics of this ideal sand as “a kin of earth…pulled from the depths of the sand-bed in bricks & clods,” in the same terms that he had described Toulouse sand. This exceptional passage is worth quoting in full:

Artisans who work in large works & who need to further their profit by seeking things already prepared in nature, because she does not sell her wares to her children, and to also save the time they would use for grinding finely & for artificially preparing sands, seek the one of the s mine s , which is not too fatty, the one that is a kin of earth, not too lean & consequently without bond, but rather that which is pulled from the depths of the sand-bed in bricks & clods that show its natural compaction, which is quite difficult to break & which has a very small & delicate grain, & which is found soft when handling it between the fingers. And because the latter is only found near the rocks in mountainous areas or lean territories, & akin to the arene, it cannot be found in in the surroundings of all the bonnes villes where artisans willingly gather. And thus, if they do not have it close to their house, they prefer to have it come from afar, like from Lyon, Venice, Paris near the Sainct Chappelle & similar places, rather than prepare it. However, you can be certain that in all places you can render the sand from a mine [90v] good & proper for molding. For if it is coarse, pass it through a sieve, and if it is not yet fine enough, wash it, & when the water has rested a little, empty that which is still troubled in some separate vessel. The coarse will promptly fall down to the bottom of the first vessel, but the one which will have come from the troubled water, set apart, having settled, will be very fine. And then, if it does not have enough bond, grind it quite dry on porphyry, & you will render it impalpable, & which will have bond like chalk. Then, if it seems right to you, you will reheat it & again will grind it & mix it with salts, or linen, or burnt felt, or ashes of paper & similarly washed things.

Searching for the Sand

Intrigued by the mention of still identifiable locations around Toulouse, professor of art history at Université Toulouse-Jean Jaurès Pascal Julien went searching for the “Toulouse sand” in April 2015. Taking his cue from fol. 87r that notes the “usual good one” is “found in a vineyard near Puy David, but the one which is most excellent is the one from the Touch, near Sainct Michel & toward Blagnac, in a vineyard that is quite high up,” Julien began the search. Puy David is now Pech David, and “Pech” is an Occitan toponym which means a summit. The hill of Pech David (Fig. 19) is a high terrace of sandy clay alluvial deposits from the quaternary period which is cut through by the Garonne river (Fig. 20). The site was fairly easy to locate on the Pech David hillside, and the open space of the vineyard still existed. Julien collected sand from the cut of a recent road following the author-practitioner’s recommendation to collect it “from the depths of the earth” (fol. 84r) and to “choose the one which is as in clods & lumps, well deep in the earth, for usually the one that is found higher up is too much a kin of the earth, and the lower is a kin of the rock” (fol. 87v) (Fig. 21).

The second site for “the one which is most excellent is the one from the Touch, near Sainct Michel & toward Blagnac, in a vineyard that is quite high up” (Fig. 22). The Touch is a small river which flows into the Garonne at Saint-Michel de Blagnac, near a former Roman amphitheater, to the west of the city. This was formerly the location of a church and an old castle (Fig. 23).

Nothing, however, remains of the site now (Fig. 24); indeed, it is in the middle of a busy highway.

Not to be deterred, Julien took samples a few meters away on a bank on the other side of the river below the site of the old castle in a similar alluvial deposit (Fig. 25). Julien then sent about 3.5 kg. of each type of sand to the Making and Knowing Lab in April 2015, where Team members could experience for themselves that the author-practitioner's statement about the sand from near the Touch being “finer & a little fatter” (plus delie & un peu plus gras) described well its clayey, wet, and slippery feel that contrasted to the Puy David sand’s more lean and dry quality.

Making and Knowing Project Reconstructions with Toulouse Sand

Trial 1 (2015)

Making and Knowing Postdoctoral Scholars Jenny Boulboullé, Joel Klein, and then Project Manager Jef Palframan reconstructed entries about the preparation of Toulouse sand for molding and casting medals in 2015.3 They began by comparing the two samples of sand received from Pascal Julien, noting that the sand from Touch indeed felt finer and fatter. The sand from Puy David was drier, but when squeezed in the hand, it clung together (see below, Vid. 5). This is the “squeeze test,” as it was known in the Making and Knowing Lab, because the author-practitioner notes several times in different entries that the sand will be ready for molding when it “gives a good hold, nevertheless coming apart easily” (e.g., fol. 118v).4

In their first reconstruction, they worked with Puy David sand, relying mainly on the entry entitled simply “Sand” on fol. 81r:

One can find an excellent one on the little hill of Puy David near Thoulouse for lead, tin & copper. It does not want to be too much reheated because, drying out all at once, it loses its bond & burns at the first arrival of metal, which makes the work lumpy & not neat. It is better to reheat from a distance, rather than with a straw flame, which generates some some filth. Before filling in the frame, you can put in the finest sand you have in a very fine linen, & dry pounce the medal and then the moistened sand that you put on top, which has more body, will grip it.

They began by grinding the sand in a mortar (Fig. 26), grinding some until it was very fine (Fig. 27). They did not heat the sand before packing their mold frames because, as the passage warns, “It does not want to be too much reheated because, drying out all at once, it loses its bond & burns at the first arrival of metal, which makes the work lumpy & not neat.”

Continuing to follow the instructions on fol. 81r to “put in the finest sand you have in a very fine linen, & dry pounce the medal,” they placed the medal model for molding on the work space inside the frame mold, then pounced very finely ground brick powder through cheesecloth directly onto the medal. They ground some of the sand from Puy David in a mortar, mixed the sand with a small amount of apple cider vinegar (Fig. 28) and started packing the frame mold. The recipe specifies: “then the moistened sand that you put on top, which has more body, will grip it.” They observed that the moistened sand did indeed hold tight to the pounced brick powder. As they worked to pack the mold, they used both ground and unground Puy David sand. Because they were able to crush the sand quite finely with their fingers without grinding, they ground the sand in the mortar fairly briefly. Afterwards, they realized they should have passed the sand through a sieve, as almost all casting sand instructions in Ms. Fr. 640 note. The sand felt very grainy with some larger chunks that could mostly be crushed simply pressing with the fingers. When they lifted the frame mold from the surface of the workspace, the medal remained on the surface, thus they decided to make a one-sided cast in the open frame mold. After leaving the frame mold to dry overnight in the fume hood, they attempted casting, which did not succeed.

Trial 2 (2015)

Learning from their experience, they tried again. This time, Jenny and Joel first pounced a thin layer of powdered charcoal over the medal (as instructed elsewhere in the manuscript) (Fig. 29). Their next step showed the fruit of the close reading that reconstruction can engender: by this time, Jenny had noticed the ubiquity of the term “impalpable,” used several times in the manuscript, including in the Toulouse Sand entries, to denote the aimed-for texture of the sand for molding.5 On fol. 69r, at the start of the discussions of Toulouse sand, the author-practitioner first uses “impalpable” in attempting to describe with precision the characteristics of the sand in the sand bank:

And one ought not to take it from the surface, for fear that it be mixed with common earth, but three or 4 feet below & the closest to the tuf or stone. Since it comes out in large lumps one would say that it starts to take shape as a stone. But when it is wet, it comes apart easily. One needs to make sure that when breaking it up it is quite granular, & that when crushed between the fingers, it renders itself into very great subtlety, like impalpable, keeping nevertheless its sandy asperity, without muddying the fingers like clay.

In this passage, he first writes “impalpable” to describe the natural sand, but changes his mind and crosses it out to make clear that the sand actually retains a “sandy asperity,” thus is not quite impalpable. Yet, it is also not so fine nor so fat as to muddy the fingers on handling. The “asperity” of the sand becomes a way to test for the exact texture of impalpability, as when the author-practitioner instructs on fol. 68v, to grind a molding substance until “when putting it on your fingernail, you find it soft without any asperity.” Another instance is on fol. 84v, when he notes that in one of his artificial sands (made of “the bone of oxen feet”) that it was “thoroughly burned & pulverized & ground on porphyry, until it is not felt between your fingers,” which forms a good definition of “impalpable,” and is actually very close to the “unfeelable” contained in Cotgrave’s early seventeenth-century Dictionarie of the French and English Tongues, as well that in the Oxford English Dictionary (OED) today: “Incapable of being felt by the organs of touch; imperceptible to the touch; intangible. Said of things immaterial; also, of very fine powder, in which no grit is perceptible when it is rubbed between the fingers.” The first use of it in English cited in the OED, from 1509, refers to the celestial realm: “Though that aungell be invysyble, Inpalpable, and also celestiall (S. Hawes, Pastime of Pleasure (1845) xxiii. 106). This figurative use occurs more frequently in later centuries, according to the OED, and denotes a mental process: “Incapable of being (readily) grasped or apprehended by the mind; producing no definite mental impression; ‘intangible’.” The more usual use in the late sixteenth and early seventeenth centuries was in reference to powders, including by Hugh Plat in his Jewell House of Art and Nature (London, 1594), cited in the OED: “So subtiliated..as that it becam almost an impalpable powder” (p. 88).6 Other technical writers also use impalpability to denote the best consistency for molding sand, including Vannoccio Biringuccio in his 1540 Pirotechnia where he notes “The goodness of all these depends on three things; namely, on receiving the metall well, on being so fine as to be almost impalpable [impalpabile in Biringuccio’s original Italian], and on their being made with a magistry that renders them hard and strong when they are dry.”7 The pseudonymous Alessio Piemontese’s I Secreti also refers to the desired consistency of sands as impalpable.8

The author-practitioner is also concerned with impalpability, but notes that the impalpable sand he seeks still retains the “bond of earth”: “Next, they grind it dry on porphyry. And thus it becomes as if impalpable and not too dried out, it and it retains the body & the bond of the earth to which it is kin and is better than when it is so burnt” (fol. 87v). He is aiming for an artificial sand that is impalpable, but not too lean, or dry, even after being heated, and still retains “the body & bond of the earth.”9

The realization that “impalpable” was a technical term informed Jenny and Joel’s actions as they now ground the Puy David sand vigorously and thoroughly to obtain that desired quality of intangibility, of not being “felt between your fingers.”

In this trial, they followed up the laborious grinding by adding vinegar to both the fine, impalpable sand and the packing sand (Fig. 30) because the author-practitioner, after first recommending glair (egg white), which he noted “releases very neatly M very neatly in low relief, but not for figures in high relief,” he subsequently remarks that using vinegar for both the fine sand and the packing sand will release the model and the cast better:

since then, I have experimented lexper with moistening it only with very strong vinegar. It released a figure which I could not release previously. And I believe that, moistening the finest in a fashion as with glair, & the coarsest, for filling, with gushes of salt water or wine, that they do not join so well. But as they are of one nature & are moistened the same, they embrace each other & hold together one with the other (fol. 87v).

He regards the easier release as caused by unification of the fine and coarser sands into one nature when they are both moistened with vinegar, thus bonding (“they embrace each other & hold together”) tightly together instead of breaking apart when the mold is opened.

He completes this section by admonishing further that “For molding well, after having prepared your sands, mold in a day. Slowly reheat them the next day, then cast them on another.”

After mixing the sand together with the vinegar, Jenny and Joel pounced a thick layer of impalpable Puy David sand (Vid. 1) over the medal as fol. 81r instructed (in place of the brick powder they had used in their first trial), then they filled the mold frame with somewhat less finely ground packing sand, making sure to pass it through a sieve (Vid. 2) as they filled the casting flask and gently compacted it with a mallet (Fig. 31).

Their technique yielded a fine impression of the medal in the sand (Fig. 32, Fig. 33).

This time, they heated the mold before casting in order to make the metal run swiftly through the mold without cooling in order to produce a cast with crisp detail (Fig. 34). After pouring with a tin-lead alloy (Vid. 3), they opened the mold (Vid. 4) to reveal that it had held up well to the onrush of molten metal in casting (Fig. 35) and produced a fine cast medal (Fig. 36).

Trial 3 (2016)

In 2016 Joel Klein and Tianna Uchacz carried out a third reconstruction that compared the Puy David and Touch sands.10 They cast a bronze medal in Puy David sand and a tin-lead medal in the “fine and fatty” Touch sand. They made several changes to the 2015 process, as they relied on fol. 84r in addition to fol. 81r. First, they followed the author-practitioner’s observation on fol. 84r that “It wants to be, like all natural sand, well reheated before putting it to use. One pulverizes it, because it is in lumps, then one reheats it in a copper kettle or similar thing, until l it no longer smokes.” After heating the sand in an open metal pie plate on a hot plate on the highest setting for about four hours, the sand had turned a dark brown (Fig. 37). They then roasted the finely ground pouncing sand with a torch.

The medal used as a pattern for the mold was laid out in one half of the casting flask resting on a large baking sheet with a large trapezoidal piece of beeswax adjacent to the top side to serve as the eventual gate. Two approximately one-inch lengths of halved chopsticks were placed at the five- and seven-o’clock positions, branching off of the bottom of the medal to allow the air to escape when the mold fills with metal during casting (Fig. 38).

Finely-ground charcoal was then placed in cheesecloth to pounce a light layer of charcoal dust across the surface of the medal and the casting infrastructure. Extremely finely-ground impalpable Puy David sand was pounced in cheesecloth over the charcoal layer in greater quantity, until details of the objects beneath were no longer discernible (Fig. 39). The recently roasted Puy David sand was ground in an electric coffee grinder to ensure a uniform fineness, then it was hand blended in a glass bowl with splashes of live apple cider vinegar until it achieved a consistency whereby the wet sand could be squeezed into handful-sized clumps that could be made to crumble by pinching between the fingers and thumb (the “squeeze test”) (Vid. 5). This wet sand mixture was used to fill the remainder of the first half of the casting flask. It was pressed in and compacted with the flat surface of a rubber mallet and eventually leveled with the long handle of a wooden spoon. Excess wet sand was swept away and removed from the baking sheet (returning it to the bowl of wet sand). The flask and baking sheet were held together and inverted, then the baking sheet was put back beneath the now inverted first half of the flask.

A furnace for the bronze was readied (Fig. 40), the mold and the bronze were heated vigorously (Vid. 6), and the metal was poured into the mold (Vid. 7).

When the gate of the mold felt cool to the touch, it was opened (Vid. 8) to reveal the bronze cast (Fig. 41).

Joel then did a final trial with Touch sand and vinegar, noting that this sand had larger clumps and what appeared to be a higher clay content (Fig. 42). Certain clumps had a whitish clay that felt similar to bentonite clay (which is often used in the modern molding material referred to as “green sand”) (Fig. 43). The sand appeared dry, so he did not roast it.

When he added the vinegar to the sand, he noted that it frothed, as if undergoing an acid-base reaction (Fig. 44).

Using a small casting flask made from a canning jar lid, he made a mold from a small medal that had sharp features (Fig. 45), pouncing it first with charcoal (Fig. 46), then packing it with impalpable sand. The sand took the impression of the medal well (Fig. 47). He then removed the medal, allowed the sand to dry in the mold, and finally cast the mold with a 3:1 alloy of tin:lead.

Because he did not clamp the mold or use a weight on top, the cast produced abundant flashing (Fig. 48, Fig. 49), but the cast medal nonetheless contained sharp detail (Fig. 50).

Conclusion

The study of Toulouse sand yielded many insights into Ms. Fr. 640 and its author-practitioner. The realization that the manuscript was closely associated with Toulouse came especially in the sections that refer to the sand of Toulouse, and this link was strengthened when Pascal Julien went in search of the sand, finding it just where the author-practitioner had recommended. This intriguing discovery provided a physical demonstration of the text, showing the authenticity of the author-practitioner’s firsthand experiences of obtaining sand from the environs of Toulouse, and it highlighted a remarkable continuity of the historical landscape.

Despite the Making and Knowing Team’s relative lack of experience in the casting trials, the author-practitioner’s observations about the different characteristics of the two Toulouse sands tallied with Team members’ experiences with the sands in the Making and Knowing Lab. Additionally, the Making and Knowing Team’s molding and casting trials demonstrated proof of concept for the author-practitioner’s belief in the excellent potential of the natural sands of Toulouse for molding (Vid. 9).11

These reconstructions were especially fruitful in demonstrating how the iterative process of reconstruction can yield new insights into a text of practice. Such work requires reading the text closely, trying the process (often failing), then returning to closely read and study the text again, from which new insights—such as the technical meaning of “impalpable”—inform new reconstruction trials. This back-and-forth process between text and materials can continue many times, as the close reading and workshop trials mutually inform each other in the gradual process of interpreting a practical text.
