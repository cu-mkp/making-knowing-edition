Dragon's Blood

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/428x-aq29

Abstract

This essay explores the uses of a resinous material known as “dragon’s blood” in Ms. Fr. 640. Placing the relevant entries from the manuscript—including among others fols. 29v and 38v—in the context of sixteenth-century discussions of dragon’s blood in botany and medicine, it shows that its applications as a remedy and a colorant were informed by its material properties and symbolic correspondences between red, gold, and blood. An experimental exploration of its properties suggests possible explanations for its uses as a remedy and as an ingredient in colored varnishes.

An ingredient called sang de dragon or “dragon’s blood” appears in seven different recipes in Ms. Fr. 640 (e.g., fols. 29v, 38v). Among these recipes, the author-practitioner gives instructions to stop a bleeding nose by applying a powder of dragon’s blood.1 This application of dragon’s blood stands out in a manuscript that is otherwise largely focused on the production of materials and objects, but it is not alone, as close to forty other recipes in the manuscript give instructions for cures, preventatives, and cosmetic remedies.2 Because dragon’s blood appears in such a range of applications, and because the nosebleed cure seems to be as much about this fantastical-sounding ingredient as it is about nosebleeds, dragon’s blood as a material presents a good focus for an inquiry into medicine in this manuscript. By investigating the juxtaposition of dragon’s blood as a medicament, as a “strong dye,” and as a source of “beautiful violet” in Ms. Fr. 640, we can gain insight into the larger thought processes that led to such a collection of recipes. In its capacity as a colorant and a curative, dragon’s blood straddles the artificial boundaries between craft making and medicine, and between the realms of “art” and “science.” To explore the author-practitioner’s understanding and use of dragon’s blood, I give an overview of dragon’s blood as a medicine in the pre-modern western tradition, investigate what was known in the late sixteenth century about dragon’s blood and its origins, and experiment with the material itself.

Dragon’s Blood and Ms. Fr. 640

Dragon’s blood is the dried resin of any of a number of trees colloquially known as the “Dragon Tree”3 (Fig. 1). Since at least the first century CE this resin was available in Europe as a product of the species Dracaena draco, Dracaena cinnabari, and likely Daemonorops draco, initially from the island of Socotra, and then, as European voyages expanded toward east and west in the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, from the Canary Islands as well.4 The resin is a dark red color, typically with visible veins and color variation—perhaps this is what the author-practitioner intended to convey in his small pen-and-ink sketch in the margin of fol. 29v (Fig. 2). Dragon’s blood can feel chalky and waxy to the touch (Fig. 3). Throughout the medieval and early modern periods, dragon’s blood was employed as a colorant to make red, although it was not favored over vermilion, brazilwood, or cochineal.5

The seven appearances of dragon’s blood in Ms. Fr. 640 present an intriguing range of techniques, and they are presented here in order of their appearance on the text’s folios.6 The first is “Doublets” on fol. 7r, which briefly explains that dragon’s blood, when soaked in spirits, can be used as a glue, and, as the title of the recipe implies, this glue would be used to connect two halves of an imitation gem to make a “doublet.”7 The next appearance is on fol. 29v, where the material is fully introduced in a recipe entitled “Dragon’s blood.” This recipe explains how to prepare dragon’s blood for use—as in the “doublet” recipe, many applications require that it be soaked in alcohol—and how to select good quality samples for purchase. The copious notes on this recipe recommend applying it to gold. Next, on fol. 38v, the recipe “Against nosebleed and for dyeing” provides the only medicinal use of dragon’s blood, instructing the reader to apply ground dragon’s blood to a nosebleed, and then immediately suggests that the same stuff can also be used as a violet dye. On fol. 40v, the recipe entitled “Cross of the commanders of Malta” gives detailed instructions for producing a shiny, enamel-like material from a dragon’s blood solution and applying it to silver sheets, presumably in order to make the eponymous cross.8 On fol. 98r, “Varnish for lutes” gives a similar recipe for a varnish that, colored with dragon’s blood, can be used as a red-tinted coating for wood. Fol. 102v, “Painting on crystal or glass,” is the only explicit use of dragon’s blood as a colorant, but as in other recipes, it is used particularly for application on a material other than a canvas or panel, and it is used in combination with metal leaf. The final recipe to mention dragon’s blood (on fol. 165r) is, as on fol. 29v, entitled “Dragon’s blood,” but the instructions here are for imitating the effect of dragon’s blood on gold or silver by using lake pigment, and do not involve the material itself.

The recipes on fols. 29v and 38v particularly highlight the range of uses for dragon’s blood by introducing this material’s medicinal application and explaining important visual cues that signal its efficacy. Fol. 38v presents a very short recipe, which simply states that when someone suffers from a nosebleed, one should “pestle some sorrel or lapathum acutum of the sort that is red-veined, which is called dragon’s blood, and apply it to the forehead of the one who bleeds.” This recipe appears on a page that includes a number of recipes on dyeing. The context of these other recipes would suggest, then, that this medical application of dragon’s blood is secondary to the rest of the entry, which says: “This herb is a strong dye & makes beautiful violet.” It is possible that this capacity to stop bleeding is in some way related to the use of dragon’s blood as a dye, and this may also be a signal that the color of dragon’s blood, which is described here as both red and violet, is an indicator of its use. In contrast, the recipe on fol. 29v is much longer and more detailed. It comes at the bottom of a page after a series of recipes on gold and other metals, and its three marginal notes completely fill the open space around the main text. It details how to prepare dragon’s blood for use on gold (by dissolving it in eau-de-vie) and how it can be bought (in large chunks resembling pieces of cake). These instructions reinforce the importance of the red color and introduce the application of dragon’s blood on gold. The marginal notes reinforce these features: the first indicating again that the darker the color, the better the product, the second clarifying how to dissolve it in eau-de-vie, and the third explaining more fully how to apply it to gold.

All seven recipes, despite being scattered throughout the manuscript, signal the importance of the red color of dragon’s blood and its relation to gold. Dragon’s blood appears in association with gold or silver in four of these recipes (29v, 40v, 102v, and 165r). As described above, fol. 29v, which introduces dragon’s blood as a material, gives its only application as a varnish to gold; where fol. 165r gives instructions for imitating dragon’s blood, it does so only in the context of applying it to gold or silver. The importance of the color of dragon’s blood is emphasized or implied in every recipe. When the author-practitioner describes what a “well chosen tear” of dragon’s blood should look like, he says it should show off its “transparent red” (fol. 29v). Similarly, the effect created by dragon’s blood on fol. 40v is referred to as “rouge clair,” indicating the translucent red of the much valued vitreous enamel. On fol. 98r, dragon’s blood is added to varnish to “make it reddish,” where it must be translucent in order that the red varnish will not obscure the wood grain of the lute, and, on fol. 102v, it is painted on one side of glass or crystal to create a “quickly-done red,” and a leaf of topaz, gold, or silver is applied to the other side, which, again, the dragon’s blood paint must not entirely obscure. On fol. 7r, the dragon’s blood mastic must be translucent or it would spoil the effect of light passing through the false jewel. The recipe on fol. 38v does not directly imply that the translucent red of dragon’s blood is important to its function; rather, it presents the convergence of red substance and blood as a like-for-like means of healing.

Blood, Gold, and the Color Red

These features of dragon’s blood—its association with gold and its blood-like, translucent red appearance—point to a larger system of knowledge in which the author-practitioner is participating with his varied applications for the substance. Pamela Smith refers to this as “vernacular science” or “a web of correspondences among red, blood, and gold that can be teased out of artisanal recipes and practices.”9 Smith draws attention to the intersection of red color, blood, and gold in the production and writing of both vernacular and learned traditions in the early modern era to show the intersection between practice and theory in the production of knowledge about nature at this time. The manufacture of products in imitation of nature could itself be a way of learning about, and even harnessing, the properties of materials.10 Gold and blood, and by extension the color red, were associated with the maintenance of life and heat in living beings.11 In the manuscript, dragon’s blood is often used in recipes involving red and gold that are intended to create a plausible imitation of another product. The translucency of dragon’s blood is particularly important to this formula, as the ability to let light shine through livens up the manufactured jewels and painted surfaces for which the author-practitioner gives instructions.12

By extension, naturally occurring red substances came to be used in the treatment of bleeding. Smith points, for instance, to Albertus Magnus’s instruction to wear a piece of coral around the neck, which is “good against epilepsy and the action of menstruation.”13 This is consistent with a broader principle in early modern medicine, the “doctrine of signatures,” which was particularly emphasized by Paracelsus.14 This principle states that the cure must in some way resemble the ailment.15 Red substances, then, serve as likely remedies for bleeding. Ms. Fr. 640 reflects this idea when, by recommending dragon’s blood for a nosebleed, it equates dragon’s blood with another naturally derived red substance: “lapathum acutum of the sort that is red-veined” (fol. 38v).16 Not every red substance could stop a bleed within this theory, however. If that were the case, these carefully rendered instructions would not have to pick and choose between different red substances; they could simply suggest that the reader use any red herb. While red and transparency are certainly significant features for dragon’s blood, the fact that not all plant-based, transparent red substances can be used interchangeably suggests that there is another feature that makes dragon’s blood more appropriate for this application than other substances. This feature is not explicitly mentioned within the manuscript, and while it may be implied somewhere within the various dragon’s blood recipes, any suggestion is beyond our present knowledge and may instead come from the experiential knowledge of living with and using dragon’s blood.

A History of Dragon’s Blood in Medicine and Botany

Dragon’s blood has been used in many different eras as a remedy for bleeding without specific reference to the doctrine of signatures.17 Two particularly notable recommendations of dragon’s blood are in the Trotula and Avicenna’s Canon of Medicine.18 The Trotula is a twelfth-century compilation of three texts on women’s medicine, hygiene, and cosmetics, possibly authored by a woman known as Trota, written in Salerno, Italy. It includes six separate recipes involving dragon’s blood, which offer remedies for uterine ulcers, excessive menstruation, and loss of virginity.19 It is also used to “color the hair so that it is golden,” to tighten the vagina, and as a general cure for epilepsy and stomach ailments.20 In each of these cases, it appears in conjunction with a number of other plant and animal substances, particularly pomegranate and Armenian bole. These applications display some of the same features of dragon’s blood as in Ms. Fr. 640, such as the capacity to stop blood flow (in menstruation and presumably in uterine ulcers), and the ability to turn something golden (as in hair dye). Two recipes also indicate that dragon’s blood works as an astringent or in some way to constrict flesh, as when it is given to restore virginity and to tighten the vagina, and it is possible that this constrictive ability is what enables it to stop bleeding. Dragon’s blood is invoked somewhat less frequently in the Canon of Medicine, an eleventh-century Arabo-Persian medical textbook that was in use in western Europe through the eighteenth century. Its single appearance in this text is as part of a concoction placed on the cut site of the umbilical cord to prevent it from bleeding out.21 Here, dragon’s blood is accompanied by yellow spices—turmeric and cumin—and it is mentioned in no other kinds of applications, likely because this text was strictly medical. Again, the dragon’s blood is used to halt bleeding, and, specifically, to help the wound develop a seal.

Consistent with these medieval texts, port books and ledgers show that dragon’s blood was in increasingly high demand in England, and probably other parts of Europe, from the latter half of the sixteenth century into the eighteenth.22 However, these records do not reveal how dragon’s blood was being used when it arrived in Europe, whether as a pigment or a medicine.23 Nevertheless, it is worth contextualizing an apparently steady increase in the volume of imported dragon’s blood, which peaked in the last decade of the seventeenth century at over two thousand pounds per year, up from almost six hundred pounds per year in the last decade of the sixteenth century, and then fell back to just over seven hundred pounds per year in the middle of the eighteenth century. It is not clear whether this trajectory of the import of dragon’s blood reflects changes in its popularity and use or changes in its availability due to geopolitical circumstances.24

In addition to its medicinal uses, dragon’s blood was regularly employed during this time as a colorant applied to gold, in resins, and in paints, just as the manuscript suggests.25 Among surviving examples, a sixteenth-century German house altar was painted with a red background of dragon’s blood directly on glass (Fig. 4).26 This piece contains traces of both dragon’s blood from Daemonorops draco and cochineal, and, as in the recipe on fol. 102v, gold and silver foil on the back side of the panel.27 This combination of reds is attributed to the colorfastness of cochineal, which was more expensive and therefore diluted with the less effective but cheaper dragon’s blood.28 Because the paint was not mixed with drying oils or gums,29 it is possible that the dragon’s blood also served the function these materials would have, due to its tackiness as a resin. The general popularity of dragon’s blood in this time emphasizes the fact that this material was not used in one single way, that its material properties were useful in a variety of applications, and that this multipurpose quality was beneficial to its distributors as it broadened their clientele.30

In the sixteenth century, herbals—compendia of plants and their uses—continued to recommend dragon’s blood both as a pigment, and, due to its astringency, as a medicine.31 On fol. 35v, the author-practitioner writes “See Mathiol” for the use of a bitter herb for “preventing someone from eating a foodstuff,” one in a series of sleight of hand tricks and practical jokes contained in one section of the manuscript.32 He undoubtedly refers here to the Commentaires de M. Pierre André Matthiole medecin senois, sur les six livres de Pedacius Dioscoride Anazarbeen (Lyon, various editions), where the Arabic name for the bitter root, “iarus” is also found.33 Pietro Andrea Mattioli’s (1501–1577) commentary on the Materia medica of Dioscorides (40–90 CE) also explains that dragon’s blood was a strong constrictive and used to staunch blood flow, heal bone breaks, and lighten menstrual flow. He mentions that dragon’s blood was a strong colorant, similar to cinnabar.34 Other contemporary physicians also refer to dragon’s blood. For example, Carolus Clusius (1526–1609) recommended dragon’s blood for easing menstruation and curing gum bleeding in his 1576 book Rariorum aliquot stirpium per Hispaniam observatum historia; similarly, in 1586/87, Jacques Dalechamps (1513–1588) in Historia generalis plantarum explained that dragon’s blood is effective in halting bleeding due to its astringency.35

These three texts further attempt to explain the materiality of dragon’s blood, albeit at a variety of registers. Mattioli discusses dragon’s blood only briefly within a note on another astringent, a plant called “L’Achillee,” so he does not include any botanical information on the source of dragon’s blood.36 Instead, he reports the story told by Dioscorides and Pliny the Elder (23–79 CE) that dragon’s blood, also called “natural cinnabar” by Pliny, occurs when a dragon and elephant fight to the death and their blood mixes and solidifies.37 While it was widely known at this time that the pigment vermillion was a manufactured form of the naturally occurring mercury sulfide ore called cinnabar,38 authors repeatedly contended that true cinnabar and dragon’s blood were one and the same, following Pliny’s assertion.39 Dalechamps tackled this confusion, explaining that one form of cinnabar, sold in cakes or “tears” (called “Cinnabari lacrymam” by apothecaries), was in fact a form of the tree resin dragon’s blood, rather than the mineral cinnabar.40 Likewise, Clusius suffered no illusions about the origins of dragon’s blood, and was the first herbalist in Europe to report on the Dragon Tree (Dragon Arbre in French or Draconus Arborus in Latin) from life.41 Clusius is responsible for the first botanical illustration of Dracaena draco, which he painted in watercolor from the specimen that grew at the monastery of Santa Maria a Gratia in Lisbon in 1564 and copied as an woodcut in the 1576 book (Fig. 5).42 Clusius therefore asserted that dragon’s blood was extracted from the tree by means of incisions, from which the liquid resin bleeds out in tears.43 Subsequent drawings of the tree that appeared in texts such as Dalechamps’s were clearly copied from Clusius (Fig. 6), and only a few illustrations of the tree existed in Europe prior to this, such as the copper engraving by Martin Schongauer, Rest on the Flight into Egypt (1469–1472), in which the dragon tree appears prominently in the foreground on the left side of the image (Fig. 7),44 and Hieronymus Bosch’s Garden of Earthly Delights (ca. 1490–1510). Despite a lack of first-hand information on dragon’s blood, all three sixteenth-century herbals give its geographical origins as Africa, Socotra, and the Canary Islands, suggesting that all were familiar with the trade in dragon’s blood.45

Experiments with Dragon’s Blood

These historical accounts of dragon’s blood suggest that although there was some confusion surrounding the origins of the substance, common knowledge identified it as an astringent that could stop bleeding. Modern studies of dragon’s blood agree with this assessment: Gupta et al. found that a number of species of dragon tree are effective antiseptics, analgesics, and, in some cases, hemostatics, though, curiously, this last quality was not found in any of the Dracaena species that were the most popular sources of dragon’s blood in the medieval and early modern periods.46 Edward et al. claim that dragon’s blood from the Dracaena draco tree possesses astringent and antiseptic properties due to the presence of benzoic acid.47 However, their experiments found that benzoic acid was not present in the tears of dragon’s blood that were the result of collecting the resin in liquid form from the tree, and they postulate that benzoic acid could be activated within dragon’s blood when heated, as may occur when the raw form is compressed into cakes or rods.48 It is not clear, though, that this heating process was used to create the cakes to which Ms. Fr. 640 and the above-mentioned herbals refer. This study also found that there were much lower quantities of benzoic acid in Dracaena draco sourced from Socotra than in Dracaena cinnabari sourced from Socotra or elsewhere.49 However, this study did not test the effects of alcohol on any of the dragon’s blood sources, which Ms. Fr. 640 prescribes for most applications.

To explore how the qualities described by medical, botanical, and modern scientific sources determine the material effects of dragon’s blood, I conducted a set of experiments to test dragon’s blood as it is used in the manuscript. I was primarily interested in its ability to transfer color and its interactions with blood and flesh, since these seemed to be its most noteworthy features. As per the instructions in the “Dragon’s Blood” recipe on fol. 29v, I soaked samples of dragon’s blood in 40 proof liquor (Fig. 8) and then set aside other samples to be mixed with turpentine, as instructed on fols. 40v and 98r (Fig. 9), with tap water as a control (Fig. 10). I stained strips of undyed cotton with each solution to observe color transfer and note any other physical effects (Fig. 11). This experiment revealed alcohol to be the most successful color transfer medium by far, yielding shades of orange, deep red, and pink, depending on the degree of contact with the solution. The dragon’s blood, however, would only completely dissolve in the turpentine, which turned its color a murky brown and left a sticky residue on the cotton. Not only did the dragon’s blood not dissolve in the water, but it barely mixed with it—the powder remained floating on top, mostly dry. From this set of experiments I discovered the hydrophobic qualities of dragon’s blood and the tacky quality of the dissolved material, which my next experiment suggests is the reason it is capable of stopping blood flow.50

According to the recipe on fol. 38v, ground dragon’s blood could be applied to a bloody nose in order to stop the bleeding. To test the interaction of dragon’s blood with blood and flesh, I applied ground dragon’s blood to pieces of beef heart. To explore the range of interaction between the dragon’s blood and the meat, I also applied salt to one piece, both salt and dragon’s blood to another piece, and left a piece bare. I allowed these to sit for twenty minutes, giving the salt enough time to draw more blood out of the meat. I found that the meat with dragon’s blood alone was drier on its surface than the meat with nothing on it; the meat with dragon’s blood and salt was slightly wet; and the meat with just salt was sitting in a small puddle of liquid (Fig. 12). Although this experiment does not accurately mimic the biological processes of a nosebleed, it does suggest that the dragon’s blood is capable of forming a barrier that keeps fluid contained.51 Therefore, although the studies by Gupta et al. and Edward et al. suggest that the medicinal value of dragon’s blood derives from its antiseptic properties, in its most common application within the early modern medical literature, its ability to stop blood may simply be a function of its hydrophobic property as a resin.

Conclusion

From contemporary sources and modern experiments, it is apparent that the uses of dragon’s blood in Ms. Fr. 640 exploited its capacities as a material. While it is possibly a coincidence that this ingredient was effective as both a pigment and a hemostatic, further research into the use of dragon’s blood as a dye52 and as a paint colorant could reveal ways in which its astringency and hydrophobia may have been useful for its role as a pigment. However, as became clear in the course of my experiments, using dragon’s blood in multiple ways revealed new aspects of this material that were neither apparent from its appearance nor explicitly discussed within the early modern literature. These varied applications for dragon’s blood therefore represent a range of material properties that become clear through practice, and it may be that these properties fostered further experimentation on the part of the author-practitioner with this material.53

We should not underestimate the role of theory and belief either, for the author-practitioner’s interest in dragon’s blood may also have come from understandings beyond its purely material properties.54 Indeed, the interplay among the practices and techniques of artisans in the production of crafts, the theories of medicine contained in herbals, and deeper cultural beliefs—such as the association between red and gold—offer tantalizing glimpses into the systems of knowledge that underpin Ms. Fr. 640.55
