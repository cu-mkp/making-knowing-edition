Molding Fruits and Animals in Sugar

By , DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/sc91-ay82
← Back to Essays

Abstract

A single recipe in Ms. Fr. 640, on fol. 126r, gives instructions for making sugar casts of fresh fruits. Such cast sugar sculpture was a common sight at lavish banquets held in sixteenth-century European courts and cities. The author-practitioner provides practical instruction and tips for making this delicacy, but also frames his instructions within a set of properties that sugar exhibits as it changes from solid to liquid and back to solid. According to the author-practitioner, in its nature, sugar is by turns fatty, brittle, and sour. This essay explains the meaning of these properties within the author-practitioner’s material imaginary, and our reconstruction clarifies how this material imaginary provided a coherent supporting framework for the practical actions described in the recipe.

Sugar

In the early modern period, sugar, in its purest form, was expensive and considered a luxury.1 It was employed as a medicine,2 a preservative, a binding and thickening agent, and appeared as decorative (and sometimes edible) sculpture for banquets.3 Medieval monks used sugar in a glassy state to impart gloss to their illuminated manuscripts,4 and Cennino Cennini included sugar and honey in recipes for mordants5 and in gesso used as a substrate for gold.6

Molding fruits and animals in sugar

The full-page recipe on fol. 126r details steps for molding fruits in plaster, then casting them with molten sugar. The recipe begins by considering the properties of sugar and the transformations that occur as sugar changes from one state to another:

Sugar is fatty when runny and brittle & breakable when dry, and with it, one casts well round things & large muscles, but awkwardly things fine & delicate [...]. One needs also for it to be of easy release, for the sugar is sour & brittle.

Here, the author-practitioner notes that sugar is fatty (gras) when liquid, but “brittle and breakable when dry” (brusc & rompa{n}t esta{n}t sec). But he also notes that “sugar is sour & brittle” (aigre & brusc). Moreover, sugar is sticky with a tendency to adhere to the mold:

try well-clarified sugar. One needs to soak for one night or one day the plaster mold before casting the sugar, in order that it is very full of water & does not imbibe the syrup. One needs also for it to be of easy release...

His concern with stickiness is also the subject of a marginal comment:

If the sugar sticks, one needs to throw some wheat starch in the mold or rub it with an almond.

The author-practitioner also cautions his reader to use fresh fruit because the molding technique results in a perfect simulacrum of whatever is molded:

If you want to mold a bunch of grapes, one needs to take it, like any other fruit, in its true vigor, for if it is withered, it will ne come out that way. Take heed, therefore, to make your molds in the natural season of que all things.

He then turns to the difficulty of casting fruits of complex shape:

Finally, do not consider casting anything in sugar that does not release well and that cannot be neatly molded in two halves, to open it when you will need to [...]. The grapes that are usually wanted cast in sugar can be made artificially or with wax or earth, or even with some grapes attached with some melted wax on some slab & other full thing, so that they are very close together and release well, & only make up one half. Or, if you have some of those grapes called chauchés or sauvignons, which have the grapes very close together, encase half of them on in the clay slab and cast on the other half. And if some grape is not released, pluck it out. Note that neither in sugar, nor in metal, can a bunch which has light & separated grapes be cast properly, because the end of the bunch is so delicate, especially if the grapes are kept, that it could not sustain the large grapes. Thus, you will need to cast hollow, which you could not do if the bunch does not have the grapes close together & molds without having them scattered & spread apart.

He adds further notes on molding and on the process of boiling and casting the sugar in the left margin. These details give the impression of first-hand experience:

To cast pears & apples in sugar, one ought not to make any gates, but rather, fill one half of the mold and then join the two, and keep turning it until the sugar has set and is cold. One ought to mix nothing in the mold apart from plaster, reheated as you know.

***

One needs for the mold to have soaked one day and one night in cold water for, and to be humid, when you cast in sugar.

***

The sign that the syrup or the sugar melted in water is sufficiently cooked to cast fruits, is when it makes threads when shaking it. And if it passes that point, it is not good, for it will always be humid. If the sugar attaches to it, one needs to throw some wheat starch in the mold or rub it with an almond.

On fol. 31r, in “Fruit made from sugar,” the author-practitioner adds detail about decorating the sugar casts:

One does not paint them with a paintbrush like other s things with moistened color, because the sugar would melt. But one rubs them with color with the finger.

These are the only two entries in Ms. Fr. 640 that discuss the process of making sugar casts. They provide much information about the author-practitioner’s understanding of the nature and properties of sugar: it can melt into a liquid, it can make threads when heated, and it can be dry and brittle; it is both “fatty” (gras) and “sour and brittle” (aigre & brusc); and it is sticky. This entry also gives rich detail on the method of making molds and casting sugar. Our reconstruction confirmed the usefulness of the author-practitioner’s advice in this recipe, and allowed us to consider the nature of sugar within the author-practitioner’s material imaginary.

Reconstruction

Guided by the author-practitioner’s knowledge of the properties of sugar, we chose to cast a pear and a bunch of grapes, using fresh fruit (in “its true vigor” and “natural season”).7 We first pressed the fruit into clay (Fig. 1). With the pear, we heeded the author-practitioner’s advice not to “consider casting anything in sugar that does not release well and that cannot be neatly molded in two halves, to open it when you will need to” (Fig. 2).

The bunch of grapes was much more complicated, and the author-practitioner spends most of the second half of the entry on instructions for grapes:

The grapes that are usually wanted cast in sugar can be made artificially or with wax or earth, or even with some grapes attached with some melted wax on some slab & other full thing, so that they are very close together and release well, & only make up one half. Or, if you have some of those grapes called chauchés or sauvignons, which have the grapes very close together, encase half of them on in the clay slab and cast on the other half.

Our grapes were not closely-spaced so we brushed melted wax onto the bunch in order to facilitate an easy release from the mold (Fig. 3). The wax filled in voids that would create undercuts in the mold and make both the fresh grapes and their simulacra in sugar impossible to remove from the mold (Fig. 4). We prepared the bunch of grapes to “encase half of them on in the clay slab and cast on the other half” (Fig. 5). With our clay bases and mold walls complete (Fig. 6), we poured plaster (Fig. 7, Fig. 8).

We then prepared to cast the other sides of the molds, as the author-practitioner uses an unusual two-piece casting method here:

To cast pears & apples in sugar, one ought not to make any gates, but rather, fill one half of the mold and then join the two [...]. One ought to mix nothing in the mold apart from plaster, reheated as you know.

We removed the clay slab from the first side (Fig. 9) and built up the walls again to cast the second half of the mold in plaster (Fig. 10).

The pear was easy to remove from its plaster mold (Fig. 11), as was one side of grape mold (Fig. 12), but the other half of the grape mold (Fig. 13) required great effort—the grapes had to be plucked out individually (Fig. 14, Fig. 15), as the author-practitioner had foreseen: “if some grape is not released, pluck it out.” We used a propane torch to melt out the wax, which scorched the mold slightly (Fig. 16).

We then soaked our molds in water because, as the author-practitioner noted,

One needs to soak for one night or one day the plaster mold before casting the sugar, in order that it is very full of water & does not imbibe the syrup. One needs also for it to be of easy release, for the sugar is sour & brittle [Fig. 17].

We then prepared the sugar and mixture water. Like other contemporary texts, the recipe lacks specific instructions on the ratio of sugar to water. In Ouverture de Cuisine, written in France in 1604, Master Lancelot de Casteau explains to his master cook “to prepare sugar for casting images & fruits: mix melted sugar with rose water as much as you would like to have, & let it boil a long time until it becomes like syrup.”8 Other recipes are similar, instructing the reader to dissolve as much sugar as is reasonable, which suggests that the making of sugar syrup was widely known and practiced.

Although we had to guess at the ratio of sugar to water (Fig. 18), the recipe provided a visual sign for when the sugar is ready: “the sign that the syrup or the sugar melted in water is sufficiently cooked to cast fruits, is when it makes threads when shaking it” (“quand il faict des filets en le secouant”). This advice raised questions as to what shaking is and what the threads will look like. Other contemporary recipes also include such a visual sign: most often that the sugar should be boiled to a specific height in the pot.9 But our research into confectionery manuals illuminated the thread test. Food historian Ivan Day describes an eighteenth-century system of reading the sugar’s behavior as it changes temperatures, based on the length of the thread it creates. The clear syrup is tested by dipping the tip of one’s forefinger in the solution, pressing it onto the thumb, and then slowly separating the fingers. If the sugar is ready a short thread will form; if the sugar is boiled a little longer this thread will extend to about a quarter of a stretched forefinger and thumb, and the syrup is considered at “the great thread degree.”10 Fortunately for the safety of our fingers, another recipe for casting sugar advises testing the degree of the syrup with a wooden spoon. In Hugh Plat’s Delightes for ladies to adorne their persons, tables, closets, and distillatories with beauties, banquets, perfumes and waters, one boils the syrup “till by powring [pouring] some out of a spoone, it will run at the last as fine as a haire.”11 Using the spoon method to test the syrup, we discovered this to be the case after about an hour of boiling the sugar. When we removed the wooden spoon, the syrup did not drip from the spoon, but rather formed a long thread from the spoon into the pot (Fig. 19). This thread indicated the sugar syrup was ready to be cast.

We then poured the sugar into one of the two molds, both of which were still damp from being soaked in water all night. When casting, we followed the recipe’s instructions to “fill one half of the mold and then join the two, and keep turning it until the sugar has set and is cold” (Fig. 20, Fig. 21, Fig. 22). When the sugar cooled in the mold, it was relatively smooth and hard, so we decided to try to let the cast harden fully (Fig. 23).

While we were pouring the sugar into the second mold, the solution began to change suddenly and dramatically: the sugar began to bubble so furiously (Fig. 24) that, as we poured it into the plaster mold, it bubbled over the sides of the mold (Fig. 25), and continued to spread (Fig. 26). The syrup turned from clear to white and thickened into a paste. Almost immediately after we poured the second mold, the remaining sugar in the pan ceased to be liquid, becoming a crystallized solid mass (Fig. 27). We went back to the recipe text and read again the marginal note that “ if the sugar sticks, one needs to throw some wheat starch [amydon] in the mold or rub it with an almond.” Randle Cotgrave defines amydon as “Fine wheat flower steeped in water, then strained, and let stand untill it settle at the bottome; then drained of the water, and dried at the Sunne.”12

Our research turned up many modern-day recipes for making sugar candy that explained the process of melting sugar in water. We found that sugar is extremely hygroscopic, meaning it absorbs water very readily. When a solution is over-saturated with sugar, the solution is considered extremely unstable and has a high tendency to recrystallize.13 To remedy such recrystallization, confectionery recipes suggest adding a bit of starch.14 We thus hypothesized that the author-practitioner’s phrase “sugar sticks” in fact referred to sugar in the process of flash recrystallization, when the sugar clumps together into a mass. Unfortunately, we did not have any amydon at hand to throw into the mold.

Another modern source refers to early modern confectioners’ knowledge of recrystallization while making rock candy. According to Kate Hopkins, sixteenth-century English confectioners were aware that “after boiling sugar to the point where you can make pulled sugar, also known as rock, its chemistry will result in its reverting to its crystalline state. Candy in this state will become brittle and fall apart, becoming more akin to its natural, granular state of sugar. This process is called ‘graining.’”15 We speculated that this may be the cause of the sugar’s brittleness for “fine & delicate” forms that the author-practitioner noted.

After the casting, the molds were so firmly stuck together that we used a chisel to break apart the two halves. We were surprised to find that the sugar had not filled the pear mold. The bubbling up of the sugar must have misled us about the volume we had poured into the mold (Fig. 28). We could not remove the sugar from the pear mold, as it was stuck so firmly to the mold. The two halves of the grape mold came apart relatively easily, but the sugar stuck very firmly to the mold, and we had to break the mold (Fig. 29) to remove the sugar cast (Fig. 30, Fig. 31, Fig. 32).

This reconstruction showed us that the nature of sugar and its various qualities of—in the author-practitioner’s words—“fatty when runny and brittle & breakable when dry,” as well as of stickiness and its associated sourness, were components of a knowledge system that gave meaning to the qualitative assessments advised by the author-practitioner at the various stages of casting sugar. This system of knowledge—or “material imaginary”—underlay the sensory observation and the concrete practical actions performed by the author-practitioner in the course of the process.

The Material Imaginary of Sugar

In the author-practitioner’s telling, sugar is a transformative material with multiple properties. Sugar transforms from one state to another, and as it does so, it takes on new properties—the author-practitioner begins the entry on fol. 126r by noting that “sugar is fatty,” but then he rethinks this statement, and adds a note above the line of text (indicated by the superscript): “Sugar is fatty when runny and brittle & breakable when dry.” He thereby qualifies his statement to make clear that sugar is fatty when liquefied, which gives it particularly useful properties for casting round forms (such as “large muscles”). But when it dries and hardens (what we would call crystallization), it is brittle (“one casts well round things & large muscles, but awkwardly things fine & delicate”). Later in the recipe, however, the author-practitioner warns about yet another set of sugar’s characteristics when he notes that, “one needs also for [the mold] to be of easy release, for the sugar is sour & brittle.” The attribution of these quite different sets of properties to sugar merits further investigation. Although the terms fatty and dry may at first be unfamiliar to us, his statement makes sense when viewed in the light of other statements in Ms. Fr. 640 and other contemporaneous technical writings.16

Sugar as Fatty

In his article “The Qualities of Fat: Bodies, History, and Materiality,” Chris Forth describes conceptions of fat from antiquity through the early modern period. The terms “fat” and “lean” were used to describe qualities of soil starting in antiquity, with fat soil considered fertile, having the distinctive tactile qualities of oil or grease.17 We noted that the liquified sugar left our fingers feeling greasy.

Theophrastus, successor of Aristotle at the Lyceum, organized fat and lean soils into a taxonomic system, passed on to Roman writers, which provided a framework for how soils should be modified and farmed.18 Virgil described the tactile qualities of fat sand, as felt by the experienced farmer: “never does it crumble when worked in the hands, but like pitch grows sticky in the fingers when held.”19 The author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 notes several times the stickiness of sugar.

In addition to the unctuous, pliable, and adhering qualities discerned by the hand, fat soil was said to exhibit a tendency to swell. Forth discusses a test to determine if soil is fat and fertile described in the Roman author Columella’s De re rustica (On Agriculture): one removes a handful of soil and then reinserts it into the hole. “If there is an excess as by some sort of leavening, it will be a sure sign that the soil is fat; if it is insufficient, that it is poor; if it makes an even fill, that it is ordinary.”20 The author-practitioner refers to the tendency of fatty sand to puff up (soufler), creating bubbles (perhaps analogous to the “leavening” of fat earth) and porosity in metal castings. On fol. 85r, for example, fatty sand “makes things porous. Thus, soft lead does not come out so well,” and on fol. 86r “once fatty & even, it becomes porous & does not receive fine features.” Sugar, insofar as it is fatty, is likewise poor for receiving subtle impressions of metal, as the author-practitioner mentions in the recipe for sugar casting: it is good for casting “round things & large muscles, but [casts] awkwardly things fine & delicate” (fol. 126r). In addition, in our reconstruction, the sugar bubbled up in the mold—perhaps indicating to the author-practitioner a kind of leavening that tends to porosity.

Interestingly, the author-practitioner never mentions what we would consider as the overriding character of sugar—its sweetness—but this can be seen to be latent in the fattiness of sugar: we know that fatty means unctuous, dense, and malleable, and these qualities can create smooth, rounded surfaces. These large rounded forms (such as “large muscles”) could then withstand sugar’s brittle and breakable state when dry, whereas “fine & delicate” forms are fragile. Within the author-practitioner’s material imaginary, softness of form can also be conceived of as “sweet”: the author-practitioner speaks often about the painter’s technique of using shadowing to create the impression of three-dimensional relief in rounded surfaces, which is known as “adoulcir,” which can be translated as “softening” or “sweetening” (see, e.g., fol. 64r–v).

Sugar as Sour

For the author-practitioner, however, sugar is also sour (aigre) and brittle (brusc), which are quite different properties again. According to Randal Cotgrave, aigre is defined as “sharp, tart, biting, sower; also, brittle, or easily broken (with a hammer.)”21 Other mentions of aigre in the manuscript contrast it with doulx (or doux), which, according to Cotgrave, means “sweet, delicious, dantie, pleasing, soft, pliant, smooth, tractable, gentle, mild, meeke, lovelie, kind, courteous, loving.”22 As noted above, these meanings seem to align more closely with the quality of fatty than with that of sour/brittle.

A closer reading of other instances of aigre in the manuscript reveals, however, that the author-practitioner contrasts it with doulx in describing fusibility (how easily a metal is melted). On fol. 32v, he contrasts aigre and doulx metals, explaining that sour metals are easily melted, while sweet/soft metals are more difficult to found:

They [founders] buy rosette at xx lb a quintal, which is harder to melt than latten because it is softer [doulce]. For, the softer [doulx] the great metals are, the soft more difficult they are to melt. The tin for bells, which is fine tin is more and which is brittle [aigre], is easier to melt than lead, which is soft [doulx]. Latten made brittle [aigry] by the calamine is melted more quickly than red copper. The metal [a copper-tin alloy] which is the substance of bells, mixed with tin, and very brittle [aigre], is soon melted. The more silver is alloyed, the sooner it melts, that is why solder is made with it.

We would explain this by saying that the melting point of tin is lower than lead,23 but for the author-practitioner the determining factors are the sourness/brittleness of tin and the soft, fatty qualities of lead.

The author-practitioner also refers to sugar as easily melted. As noted above, in a recipe for coloring fruit cast from sugar on fol. 31r, he cautions, “one does not paint them with a paintbrush like other s things with moistened color, because the sugar would melt. But one rubs them with color with the finger.” His advice refers to the sugar after it has been cast, that is, after it has undergone its change of state from fatty to dry and brittle, and, in this entry, easily melted. This helps to clarify the author-practitioner’s reasoning: when sugar is fatty and liquid, it is sticky, but when it changes state by drying (crystallizing), it becomes sour, brittle, and easy to melt.

The Properties of Sugar in Medicine

These multiple properties of sugar are further illuminated by examining medicinal theories deriving from the medieval Islamic tradition.24 Sugar appears in a medieval Islamic text by Ibn al-Nafis, a physician considered the first to describe the pulmonary circulation of the blood. Born in 1213 in Damascus, al-Nafis wrote an encyclopedia titled Al-Shamil fi al-Tibb, in which he describes the medicinal effects of sugar on various pulmonary disorders. In the fourth section, describing sugar’s effects on the organs in the chest, he writes, “sugar has atoms with a ‘gentle’ quality, and as it dissolves easily in liquids, it moves easily into the organs of the chest and is highly effective on them.”25 Later, speaking of sugar’s effects on the digestive organs, he states, “however, sugar may also damage the liver with its ‘hot’ quality because sugar, as mentioned above, can take on a bitter quality.”26 Here, like the author-practitioner, al-Nafis presents sugar as composed of multiple, changing qualities. He explains the different qualities of sugar as pertaining to their elements—one atom of sugar is composed of elements of water, gentle hot earth, and air. These elements interact during the hardening of boiled sugar: “when the water element’s properties arise, but there is little water present, the properties of the air element overtake those of the water element. When boiled, sugar congeals, its water element properties naturally recede.”27

This account from the realm of medicine describing the multiple properties of sugar shows that the author-practitioner’s statements about sugar being fatty, sour, and brittle are not without meaning. His description of sugar expresses a material imaginary in which materials are classified along a spectrum from fat to lean and sweet to sour. The author-practitioner saw sugar as simultaneously possessing multiple properties that emerged and receded as sugar changed state through processes of boiling and hardening.

S’attaquer and Material Agency

The term the author-practitioner uses for the process of sugar sticking (or recrystallizing) is s’attaquer. This is interesting because the author-practitioner also uses attaquer to mean “attack,” as in the attack of a snake. Attaquer is defined by Cotgrave as “to assault, or set on; to incounter...any way to meddle with.”28 The Dictionnaire du Moyen Français notes that attaquer in the sixteenth century could be synonymous with attacher, and both could mean attack or attach, only becoming differentiated in the course of the century.29 According to Cotgrave, attacher means “to tye fasten, claspe, knit, annex...or clap upon a pot, wall, etc,”30 and s’attacher à means “to coape, deale, meddle, scuffle, grapple, quarrel, fight, brabble with.”31 Both these terms seem to indicate a very active, even aggressive agency. The flash recrystallization described by the author-practitioner as “le sucre s’attaque” (“the sugar sticks”), and the sugar’s subsequent, almost instantaneous, change of state from a liquid to a crystallized mass was so dramatic and irreversible that this aggressive term s’attaque (both attachment and attack) did not seem out of place to describe the phenomenon. The sugar seemed to be acting of its own volition.

To better understand this conception of materials as active, we can look to the author-practitioner’s contemporary, Bernard Palissy (ca. 1510–ca. 1589). In the Admirable Discourses (1580), Palissy includes a “Treatise on Metals and Alchemy,” in which he presents a dialogue between the characters “Theory” and “Practice.” In this dialogue Practice disproves Theory’s claim that “metals are dead and insensible bodies.”32 Practice tells Theory a “secret”—that all metals were created with the same life force as plants:

Just as I have told you that the seeds or matters of all vegetative things were created at the very beginning of the world along with the earth: also I have told you that all mineral matters (which you call inert bodies) were also created like the vegetative ones, and exert themselves to produce seeds to generate others. Also, the mineral ones are not so inert that they do not generate and produce from one degree to another, more excellent things, and to make you understand it better, the mineral substances are intermixed and hidden among the waters, in the womb of the earth, just as every human and brute creature is conceived as water in its formation: and being intermixed among the waters, there is some supreme substance which attracts others of its nature to form itself.33

Practice sees minerals as imbued with agency and a drive to reproduce, comparable to humans, brute creatures, and plants. This agency manifests in the attraction of like substances. Practice describes this force of attraction found after grinding a rock of fusible matter:

After having thus pulverized [the rock], I mixed it with clay, and a few days later when I wished to work with this clay, I found that the rock had begun to gather together, even though it was so thoroughly mixed with the clay that no one could have found a piece of it as large as the little motes that are seen in the sun’s rays when they come in to a room . . . that must make you believe that the materials of metals gather together and congeal admirably, according to the admirable order and power that God has ordained for them.34

Practice personifies the fusible rock as a material with its own agency, attracted to things of like form, and able to find those things and combine with them. In Palissy’s dialogue, Practice disproves the theory of metals as dead substances. The agency of materials emerges through practice, and the attraction of like minerals to form larger structures indicates a much larger purpose and life force imbued in natural materials.

The author-practitioner often describes materials as “demanding,” “wanting,” “desiring,” and “refusing,” but in the specific instance of sugar, perhaps Palissy’s attribution of changes in state to the action of elements at war is relevant: “fire is the destroyer of water, and wherever it enters it must expel the water, or if it does not, the water will kill it.”35 The dual nature of sugar—fat when liquid, and sour when dry—and its apparently willful actions, seem to align with this kind of violent material agency that causes sudden and significant changes of state.36

Affordances and Constraints of the Material

Recently, material agency has been described not as a life force, but, instead, within a framework of affordances and constraints. For example, Anne-Sophie Lehmann describes it as “the restriction or encouragement of certain actions that result from the specific properties of the material.”37 She points out that, in the act of making, action “is not simply exerted by the actor on the material,”38 but moves reciprocally back and forth between the two. We can see this, for example, in the author-practitioner’s observations about the properties of sugar which necessitate certain actions on the part of the practitioner enacting the process to transform sugar from one state to the next. Sugar is fatty, thus good for round things, but also brittle, hence the need to coat bunches of grapes with wax. In the same way, the sudden attaque of recrystallization by the sugar needed an immediate reaction from the practitioner—that of throwing starch into the mold.

As we performed and reflected on our actions in reconstructing the recipe, we came to see how, within the space of the recipe, the author-practitioner’s listing of the properties of sugar alongside instructions for actions constituted a description of this push and pull of reciprocal action between materials and maker. We also came to recognize that in our own reconstruction, the final product of sugar casts resulted from our interaction with materials that by no means passively accepted our efforts to transform them.
