Creating Original Patterns for Casting in Ms. Fr. 640

By , DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/hbnz-5z03
← Back to Essays

Abstract

Despite the quantity, detail, and breadth of entries about metalworking in Ms. Fr. 640, most instructions for casting presume that the object to be cast—usually medals, animals, or plants—already exists. The entries thus very rarely address the fabrication of the original pattern for making the mold. Medals are frequently reproduced or made in serial production in Ms. Fr. 640, but the actual procedure for making that first model is left mostly unmentioned. This essay investigates the few instances in the manuscript for creating patterns, including fols. 50r and 156r, and compares them to other sixteenth-century sources that treat molding and casting.

Despite the great number of detailed entries related to metalworking in Ms. Fr. 640 and the breadth of their subjects, most of the manuscript’s instructions for casting start in reproducing an already-existing object, rather than from the creation of a pattern or model. For the life casting of animals and plants, this is self-explanatory because the pattern is a natural thing, but for medals, which need the creation of a pattern carved or modeled from wood, wax, or other material, this is noteworthy, and may provide clues to the genesis and authorship of the manuscript. While the focus on practical advice in the manuscript may suggest that it was written in the context of a practicing artisan’s workshop, the metalworking descriptions most often involve replication, and very rarely address the making of an original object. While reproducibility or serial production is an important feature of medal making,1 in Ms. Fr. 640, whether the “original” was used for direct (single mold) or indirect (piece mold) casting,2 the actual procedure for making that first model is rarely mentioned. This essay investigates the few instances in the manuscript for making original patterns. Among the questions this essay seeks to address are: what materials were used to make these patterns, and what are the techniques for fabricating them? Were they carved, drawn, or modeled? How and with what tools? Our research into patterns in Ms. Fr. 640 also seeks to provide insight into the identity of the author-practitioner.3 What does the lack of instructions about how to make patterns for casting allow us to conclude about the author-practitioner’s occupation and his areas of expertise. Were the techniques of carving or creating models so widely known that they did not bear mentioning? Or did divisions of labor mean that the artisan who created an original pattern was not the same person who reproduced objects? What position would the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 have held within such a system of divided labor and knowledge?

Related Recipes from Ms. Fr. 640

The recipe “Wax for seal and imprint” (Cire a cachet et empraincte) on fol. 42r includes directions for making wax that is soft enough to take impressions, which instructs to keep wax in tepid water and knead it. The author-practitioner writes, “press [empraindras] it into whatever you want.” Since this recipe is about making seals, the wax itself will not be cast but instead will only take an impression, so this is not quite a description of how to make a pattern for casting. However, the recipe also suggests carving: “You can carve the figures [tailler les figures] & gild them, silver them, & paint them with colors in varnish.” Randle Cotgrave provides a contemporary definition of tailler as “to cut, slit, slice, hew, hack, slash, gash; nicke, snip, notch, indent; carve, [en]grave; also, to gueld, or spey; also to tax, impose taxes on, levie tributes of.”4 The term is related to intaglio processes, which Benvenuto Cellini alludes to in passages about engraving.5 On fol. 51r, Ms. Fr. 640 refers to Tailleurs en planches (Cutters of printing plates), but fol. 42r contains the only use of tailler in the sense of carving a model. The verb is more often used in the context of cutting stone or other materials.6

Although carving is not much discussed in Ms. Fr. 640, “modeling” or “roughing out” forms for other fabrication processes is. “For adorning beds, mirrors, and suchlike” on fol. 131r instructs, “Rough out some design [Esbaulche sur une ardoise bien unye quelque desseing] in half relief on a quite flat slate to adapt it to either round or flat things. And having cast it in mixed tin, quite thin, you can gild it with gold leaf & adapt it to whatever you want.” Cotgrave defines the verb esbaucher as “To rough-hew; grossely to frame, forme, square, or cut out of the whole peece; to begin rudely, any peece of work; also, to pruine a tree; and, in painting, to tricke.”7

“Cast of wax to represent an animal that one has not got” (Gect en cire pour representer lanimal quon na point) on fol. 139v describes a procedure for making a stiff white wax, sometimes called esbaucher wax. The title of the recipe appears promising for our research, since if “one has not got” the animal that one wishes to cast, then one’s recourse would be to carve a model for it. But the focus of the recipe is on the production of a modeling material that is “firm” (ferme), rather than on a discussion of the actual process of modeling or working that material. Nonetheless, the author-practitioner does make certain recommendations about casting that have to do with an ability to check one’s work, such as adding charcoal “The charcoal gives color and body to the wax, which would otherwise be transparent & the features would not be seen as well.” The note to the left of this recipe also begins, “This black sulfured wax is for modeling [pour esbaucher] round figures that are not for releasing and that one needs to burn in the mold à noyau before opening it, po like those which have arms & legs out front or entwined.” Like on fol. 131r, the word esbaucher suggests a technique of fabrication that goes beyond replication.

Esbaucher appears again on fol. 124v under the heading “Casting with gold,” and refers there to molds that have “cost a lot to model in wax” [couste beaucoup a esbaucher en cire].8 Similarly, fol. 109r contains a recipe titled “Modeling in wax” [Esbaucher en cire], which, again, is about making hard wax “more amiable.” While none of these recipes explicitly describes the process of modeling in wax once it has been made workable, one can conclude that the reason for making wax malleable is to work it into a pattern for casting.

Further evidence can be found in “Molding promptly and reducing a hollow form to a relief” [Mouler prompteme{n}t et reduire un cave en relief] on fol. 156r. It begins with the instruction: “You can imprint [empraindre] the relief of a medal in colored wax, & you will have a hollow form.”9 Again, the author-practitioner already has a pattern (the medal) as his starting point. Interestingly, empraindre is also used in the recipe on fol. 42r, discussed above in relation to tailler. In that instance the author-practitioner describes the preferred type of wax and a technique for making such a negative imprint of a metal relief in the wax. On fol. 156r, this step is not explicitly explained but is either assumed as common knowledge or, perhaps, as sufficiently described earlier in the manuscript. That further on in the instructions on fol. 42r, carving [tailler] this wax impression is mentioned may indicate alterations to the design being made, or flaws in the imprint being corrected.

“Molding” on fol. 50r is similar in that it does not discuss producing original patterns, but rather explains how to create the impression of an existing object for subsequent casting:

If you want to promptly mold something in half relief that comes to hand, fold some paper in five or six doubles, & place it on the medal & make sure the paper is folded on the back of the medal so that it is well secured. Next, take a stick, broad at one end & pointed at the other, well softened, and rub well on the paper, & with the point of the stick retrace the lines & proceed until you recognize that your imprint is well done. Then, at your convenience, slightly rub oil with a paintbrush lemp on the paper imprint and cast in tallow or wax or sulfur, & the paper, without burning, will render your portrait neat, which you will afterward be able to mold in plaster or tripoli, and then in lead & other metal.

Here the author-practitioner, as on fol. 42r, describes a process of lifting an impression from a medal that already exists, in this case, including careful registration of the paper on the pattern (“well-secured”). Even more significantly, this recipe also provides the procedure—albeit in an abbreviated manner—for moving from a model to a mold: the model is impressed into paper, which is then used as a negative mold for a casting material (tallow, wax, or sulfur); this casting material is poured into the paper as a positive, and then used to form a negative mold in plaster or tripoli, which results finally in a positive “in lead & other metal.” The terse last two sentences of this recipe provide as clear a set of step-by-step instructions for casting from a model as almost any other recipe in the manuscript.10 The author-practitioner’s unselfconscious use of patterns ready to hand may indicate widespread re-use of patterns that may not be as visible in the corpus of extant medals today.

Related Recipes from Other Sources

Ms. Fr. 640 does not appear to contain detailed information about creating original patterns, instead addressing the topic only fleetingly. Whether this is because the author-practitioner presumed this aspect of fabrication was well known, or because those instructions fell outside of the scope of the author-practitioner’s expertise or interests is a motivating question of this essay. A comparison of the few explicit descriptions of creating a model in Ms. Fr. 640 to contemporary authors is informative.

In a controverted passage in Benvenuto Cellini’s Autobiography describing the making of a pattern, Ms. Fr. 640 may even shed light on the process. Cellini writes, “During this man’s illness the Duke sent for me, and bade me take his portrait; this I did upon [innun] a circular piece of black stone about the size of a little trencher.”11 Cellini’s use of the conjunction innun is the source of controversy—does it mean “in” or “on”? Did Cellini carve the portrait in the black stone, or on the black stone, using it as a working surface to make a wax model for a seal such as in Fig. 1?12 As discussed above, fol. 131r instructs to “Rough out some design in half relief on a quite flat slate to adapt it to either round or flat things.”

By far the most informative of sixteenth-century sources on the making of original patterns is Cellini’s Treatises. Cellini writes about a number of materials and provides instructions for making patterns with them, including steel (for numismatic dies), black wax13 and white wax (in relation to which he describes the making of sand molds), and carving directly into silver.14

Cellini advises softening steel for engraving under fire then leaving it overnight covered in “a concoction made of earth, powdered glass, soot from the chimney, and bole of Armenia [with] a little horse-dung [and formed into] a paste with man’s urine.”15 Into this steel, using hard steel tools, one can engrave the ends of two tools, one for each side of the coin, using compasses to draw and measure the circumference of the coin.16

Regarding patterns in white wax, Cellini writes,

The first thing to be done is to make a model in white wax of the head, the reverse, and whatever there may be, to the exact size and relief of the final work, for we know this was how the ancients did it.

The white model in wax is made as follows: Take a little pure white wax, add it to half the quantity of well-ground white lead, & a little very clear turps…. [A negative] is made in the gesso just as the cardinals’ seals were, of which I erewhile told you.17 Then you take what are called the taselli [tools similar to those described above for steel dies]…. After you have softened them [the taselli] in the fire in the same way as I showed you above with the coins, you smooth and polish them very carefully with soft stones and mark out the size of your medal, the beading, the place for the inscription & so forth, with just such immovable compasses as you used before.18

The description of making a model in white wax given here is unsurprising, but the fact that Cellini includes this step in his narrative—and that he connects it to ancient practices—differentiates his text from Ms. Fr. 640. The other peculiarity in Cellini’s passage “About Medals,” is that it includes instructions for making tools, the taselli, evidently for reproducing the medals but also for fabricating monetary coins. This raises questions as to whether the distinction between coins [monete] and medals [medaglie] is simply functional. He also recommends that “you will do well to make wax impressions from time to time, while you are cutting, to see how you are getting on,” similar to his suggestion for periodically testing the steel dies by using tin.19

Vannoccio Biringuccio’s comments on reproducing medals also provide information about workshop practices that go unmentioned in Ms. Fr. 640 and in Cellini’s Treatises. In explaining the the making of sand molds, Biringuccio writes, “It [the sand and magistry discussed in the preceding text] also served me very well in that one, two, three, or four casts were made without having to mould it again, if the relief that was moulded was not very great.”20 Two significant implications are evident from this sentence: first, that in processes of sand casting, the same mold was indeed intended to produce several identical casts; and, second, that for medals, the number of times a mold could be used was apparently related to its size and/or the depth of its relief.

The pseudonymous Alessio Piemontese’s recipe “To imprint medalles in hoste [haste?] with Dragagant,” after explaining how to make a paste that would harden and could be used for casting, concludes with the suggestion, “And of this paste you maie make also other workes as you will, as Beades, stones, or other.”21 This suggests similar questions to those raised by Ms. Fr. 640, namely, why, if the author-practitioner suggests that other objects besides already-existing medals can be cast using this method, he chose not to expand his directions for making medals to encompass creating one’s own pattern.

Materials for Making Patterns

What little information Ms. Fr. 640 does provide on making original patterns adds to the documented research on the materials in which patterns were made. John R. Spencer follows George F. Hill’s series of articles on early modern medals, in assuming that the patterns for Italian Renaissance medals were uniformly cast from wax.22 Patricia Tuttle acknowledges that sulfur may have been used in the casting process but suggests that its use was as a material for making negative molds rather than for the positive form of a pattern.23 The addition of sulfur, especially, to the materials used for casting implies that a different set of tools for melting (since wax can be cast into sulfur but not vice versa) and almost certainly a different set of materials for molds might have been used. The melting point of beeswax, 144°F, is much lower than that of sulfur, at approximately 240°F (and that of tallow at around 113°F), which would make a sulfur mold suitable to use with molten wax or tallow. That pure wax was used as a pattern, and especially that medals were created using a lost-wax casting technique, appears to be an outdated conception of the process of making medals.24

Instead, scholarly opinion has shifted toward an understanding that sand molding was the primary method of fabrication. Partly because of the pressure necessary for impressing a pattern into sand, and the relative melting points and temperatures achieved during the steps of the casting process, a workable material harder than wax would have been ideal. On this point, Biringuccio gives plaster as an example: “It is also customary to make a plaster capable of being worked easily by hand in making medallion portraits… in bas-relief so that they can be moulded for making them in bronze if you wish.” He also gives several alternatives to plaster, including white wax mixed with white lead and tallow; plasters with gums, wax, “Greek pitch or ship’s tar;” soap; “sulphur and brick dust, or two parts of Grecian pitch and one of wax.”25 That all of these are viable materials for making a pattern that could produce, as Biringuccio writes, “multiple casts without having to mold it again” suggests that the scholarly assumption that medals began as carved wax models renders an incomplete image not only of the variety of substances used in fabrication but also of the ingenuity of the fabricators.

Conclusion

In general, processes of making patterns for casting are largely absent from Ms. Fr. 640. How to interpret that omission, however, is not readily apparent. The author-practitioner does seem to presume a fair amount of knowledge on the part of his audience, signaled by phrases such as “as you know” or “you know how to….” even when there are no entries in the previous folios describing these processes.26 The author-practitioner may have omitted instructions for fabricating a pattern because he assumed that part of the making process was unnecessary to describe, since any artist could go about it in whatever way he or she chose. Furthermore, the recipes collected in Ms. Fr. 640 mostly detail casting procedures that can be replicated with some precision. The act of carving materials is a particular learned skill that also depends on individual creativity, which the manuscript’s author-practitioner may have realized falls outside of the scope of the type of knowledge that he aimed to transmit, or that could be transmitted in a text.27

Finally, we return to the questions posed in the introduction to this essay. The most challenging of these is the identity of the author-practitioner of the manuscript. From the evidence gathered here, we propose that the author-practitioner’s focus was on reproducing medals and other objects and not creating entirely new objects to be cast. Our investigation does not give insight into the trade practiced by the author-practitioner. In this context, however, it is instructive to compare other late sixteenth- and early seventeenth-century individuals whom we know produced, wrote about, or collected medals (or possessed knowledge about them). On one end of such a spectrum lies the practical knowledge of an artisan, such as a founder or, slightly farther along, a goldsmith like Cellini, while on the other end we might place the more or less learned approach of the savant or amateur, for instance, a compiler of “secrets” or “recipes,” and, further along the continuum, a more “passive” collector and contemplator of medals.

The author-practitioner’s interest in and detailed descriptions of many things in fields other than casting and metalworking indicates wide-ranging interests, but the manuscript entries about mold making and metalworking may still provide important clues. His interest in and description of all parts of the casting process that deal with making molds suggests that he was not merely a founder. We know that Cellini was a practitioner who related his practical experience through texts and could point to products of his craft. And, we may also look to Hugh Plat as a counterexample, a compiler of instructions for various procedures who did not necessarily have any expertise himself.28

Another interesting example to consider is the Dutch polymath and nobleman Constantijn Huygens, who is known not only to have collected medals but also to have (re)produced them around 1628.29 While Huygens’s education and level in society remove him further from artisans than the less socially elite Hugh Plat, his hands-on experience blurs such divisions. As historian A. R. E de Heer notes, the benefit of Huygens’s medal-making adventures, according to his autobiography, was threefold: first, it demonstrated his talent, and, as such, was cause for praise and respect among his peers; second, he demonstrated that he was skilled enough that, if ever necessary, he could earn a living through manual labor; and, third, his practical understanding and knowledge of medals provided the foundation for proper judgment of such objects.30

Significantly, Huygens possessed a brief handwritten treatise concerning metal casting containing a similar structure and style of written recipes to Ms. Fr. 640 that explains the various processes of molding and casting medals.31 In a letter dated to 1629, Huygens’s friend Johan Brosterhuijsen mentions this treatise as a gift to Huygens, describing it as “the art of casting; it is a handwritten book, drawn from the memories of a great founder…” [… l’art de getter; c’est un livret escrit à la main, tiré des memoires d’un grand fondeur…].32 In the same letter Brosterhuijsen recommends Huygens learn the art of “casting herbs, etc. and gilding without gold” [mouler sur des herbes, etc. et de dorer sans or] from the “bag of secrets” [son sac de secrets] of the goldsmith Adriaen Rottermont—who does indeed appear to have been Huygens’s instructor.33

Huygens, then, poses an interesting example of a learned man who not only practiced the art of casting medals himself but also collected recipes of such processes, among many others. A closer look at his “recipe books” is necessary, but it seems safe to assume that Huygens did not write them himself, nor did he comment upon them. Within the spectrum mentioned above, however, the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 appears to have been closer to the practitioner’s end than Huygens, while perhaps closer to the scholar’s end than Cellini.

Although this essay cannot provide a definitive answer as to whether Ms. Fr. 640 was written by an author-practitioner who considered himself an artisan, our findings place him somewhere in between. The designation as “author-practitioner” seems appropriate, since he did have the skills, time, and interest to write this wide-ranging and intriguing manuscript, while also experimenting, or practicing, with his own recipes, as is evident from his notes and illustrations in the margins.34 Further research into founders’ and artists’ practices might indicate whether the tools and workshops that reproduced cast objects were the same ones producing patterns and, thus, what the significance of Ms. Fr. 640’s omission of this information might mean. A deeper understanding of the various communities interested in, experimenting with, and writing about such practical knowledge is needed in order to determine the intersections of practical or experiential knowledge with theoretical or learned knowledge. Research on how common the acquisition of practical skills in the arts was in elite education, as seen in Huygens, could also shed more light on the background and milieu of the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640.
