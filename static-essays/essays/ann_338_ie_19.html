Raymond Masse: Merchant-Goldsmith in the Environment of the Author-Practitioner

By
← Back to Essays

Abstract

Toulouse has long been an attractive city for goldsmiths. The author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640, who owned a goldsmith's forge (fol. 12v) and made multiple references to goldsmithing in the entries of his manuscript, was probably in contact with several masters from Toulouse. This profile resembles that of the merchant and goldsmith Raymond Masse. Active in Toulouse from 1585 to 1631, Masse’s activity, collaborations and relationships, both on a local and international level, bear many similarities with the concerns of the author-practitioner and shed light on several passages of the manuscript. As early as the fifteenth century, Toulouse began to attract goldsmiths, owing to its ardent Catholicism, its many religious sites, its celebrated relics, and the power of its clergy and lay brotherhoods.1 The town’s master craftsmen were particularly renowned in the Midi region around Toulouse.

One of them, Raymond Masse, was active from 1585 to 1631, and his business can be related to several references and concerns in Ms. Fr. 640. It is therefore tempting to speculate that Masse was in touch with the author-practitioner, who was interested in goldsmithing and used a goldsmith’s forge of his own (fol. 12v). In 1585, Masse became a master goldsmith, but he quickly turned to trade and undoubtedly enjoyed financial success, considering his connections, his businesses, and his influential clients.2 Numerous archived documents make it possible to determine the nature of his activities, as well as his travels and his social relations.

The Workshop

At the onset of the seventeenth century, two journeymen, Jean Coste and Dominique Guerguy, worked in his shop.3 Some of his fellow workers, such as Guerguy or Jean Masse, belonged to his family circle, while others, like Jean Coste, were from Lévignac, the master’s hometown.4

Raymond Masse also surrounded himself with foreign journeymen like Charles de Lindre from Antwerp.5 A dispute that occurred in 1607 sheds light on the different members of Masse’s shop, their origins, and the roles they played.6 It also shows how important it was for a goldsmith or a merchant-goldsmith to have keen expertise in gemstones, and thus explains why information on both goldsmithing and precious stones coexists in the documents.7 Guillaume Baraigne, a gem merchant from Cahors, had come to Toulouse to sell “a cabochon ruby set in a gold ring with black enamel” that he had bought from the archdeacon of Cahors. He offered to sell it to Raymond Masse and they agreed on the price of 100 gold écus. Masse promised to buy it on the sole condition that the stone be removed “from its setting” and assessed “without delay” by the members of his shop. One of them, Jean Caillou, was master goldsmith of the city.8 The three other men “living and working at the shop of the said Masse” were journeymen goldsmiths, “natives of Strasbourg in Germany”: Abraham Simon, Jacob Bauer, and Friedrich Feher. The latter removed the stone and all of them, masters and journeymen alike, assessed its quality. The deal fell through because “the said stone was not a ruby […] but only a doublet.”

Paris and Toulouse: A Few Links

Throughout his career, Raymond Masse was in touch with Parisian goldsmiths. He sometimes travelled to Paris, at least during the first decade of the seventeenth century, to meet with the Targier brothers, two powerful bourgeois merchants and business associates to whom he owed large sums of money.9

Being quite well-off, Raymond Masse helped his colleagues and regularly lent them money. In 1604, he assisted Richard Richaudeau, a Parisian goldsmith “suffering from some bodily ailment,” bedridden in the upper room of his home.10 It seems that, in Paris, Masse’s confidential agent was the goldsmith François Audry. He was sometimes charged with collecting the money owed to the Toulouse artisan.11 Both men also had business links. In 1610, Masse sent Audry “three cast gold ingots weighing [nearly] 22 ounces, with a bundle of letters.”12

These links were not unique to the Masse family: in the 1600s, the Toulouse goldsmith Pierre Lefèvre settled in Paris where his brother-in-law, Monsieur Faguier, was a surgeon.13 Lefèvre had previously taken on as “a servant to the said state of goldsmith” Baptiste de Fréchain, a journeyman from Paris. This illustrates the willingness to move that was inherent to apprenticeship, as well as the job opportunities offered by Toulouse.14

These points show that the references to the three Parisian masters on the first folio of Ms. Fr. 640 should perhaps not be surprising. The political, artistic, and literary links between the kingdom’s capital and Toulouse were both intensive and constant throughout the Renaissance.

The “Emeralds of Brissac” (fol. 2v)

Other of Raymond Masse’s business relations can shed light on some entries in Ms. Fr. 640. It is attested that Masse was surrounded with German and Flemish fellow workers, and that he travelled “to traffic in Spain and purchase merchandise for his work as a goldsmith” in 1593 with his brother François, also a goldsmith (doc. 1547–†1616). His business also led him to turn to Switzerland.

In November 1603, Antoine Jean, a “merchant lapidaire de Brissac en Suisse” (gem merchant from Brissac in Switzerland), appointed his servant Jean de Lisle as his proxy to recover:

each of the sums and parts of moneys which are, may, or will be owed to the said constituent in the land and kingdom of France by any persons whoever they may be, be it for the sale of merchandise, the loan money, or otherwise.15

Raymond Masse gave him one-hundred and sixty-two livres for the purchase of twenty-seven turquoises and sixty-five dozen “small white crystal square cut stones.”

Masse was not the only goldsmith in the city to be in touch with the Swiss gem merchant’s servant. In 1605, Pierre Lefèvre promised to pay eighty-one livres to Jean de Lisle, gem merchant from Geneva, “for the sale of certain stones used for goldsmithing.”16 The money was to be remitted to a merchant-goldsmith from Lyon, Marc Perachon, two months later. In 1606, the gem merchant Jean de Lisle, acting as Antoine Jean’s servant, delivered the sum of eleven écus owed by two goldsmiths from Agen, Jean Barthe and Pierre Probas, to the Toulouse goldsmith Daniel Thomas.17

These privileged commercial ties developed little by little from Paris to Switzerland via the central trade node of Lyon. All through the sixteenth century up to the early seventeenth century, this former capital of the Gauls sponsored fairs which brought merchants from France, Castille, Aragon, Germany, and all over Europe. These merchants, including goldsmiths, bought, sold, and settled their debts.18 The ties between Toulouse goldsmithing and Swiss gem merchants give meaning to the puzzling reference to the “Emeralds of Brissac [Breisach]” (fol. 2v) in Ms. Fr. 640.

The Blanques

One of Raymond Masse’s more lucrative activities was the blanque, a type of lottery invented by the Italians that, according to Étienne Pasquier, was the “game most suited that one could imagine to discreetly cheat the rabble,” usually being played during fairs.19 If the blanque appears, in Pasquier’s words, to be one of the “subtle means [of extorting] gold and silver out of France,” a topic already mentioned by Jean Prévost about sleight of hand, it was in reality controlled and monitored. Played in various ways, this game of chance consisted in selling hundreds or even thousands of tickets enclosed in a box, several days in a row. The losing tickets were white (blanc), hence the name of the lottery, while a small proportion of black ones entitled their purchasers to prizes such as jewelry, paintings, and woodwork, of various values, which were listed in a register and exhibited.

These lotteries spread throughout Europe in the sixteenth century.20 First created to fund public works, they also depended on private investors expecting profitable returns. They fostered the circulation of works of art, luxurious and simple, including gold plate, jewelry, precious stones, mirrors, paintings, and even weapons. These lotteries gave craftspeople like Raymond Masse the opportunity to sell their merchandise and offered ticket buyers the chance to win prestigious and desirable objects. Interestingly, Ms. Fr. 640 mentions most of these objects. Moreover, in the early seventeenth century, Pierre de Brun, a Flemish merchant with a royal license, organized such a lottery in Toulouse.21

The blanques held in Pézenas, near Montpellier, gave Masse the opportunity to team up with one of the more renowned painters of the city, Jacques Boulbène (doc. 1575–1576; 1586–†1605), who worked for the capitouls on numerous occasions in the late sixteenth century.22 In August 1601, Raymond Masse drew up a contract to share the gains linked to the sale of “certain merchandises of goldsmithing and painting” with the painters Pierre Delon and Jacques Boulbène, and the goldsmith Arnaud Cornayre.23 It is intriguing to compare the professional, political, and economic aspects of these lotteries with the entries in Ms. Fr. 640.

Another connection to the entries of Ms. Fr. 640 is a document dated August 31, 1609, which shows evidence of a direct link between Raymond Masse and the d’Ouvrier family and highlights the financial benefit that could be reaped from their relationship with the merchant-goldsmith.24 This contract, drawn up “in the home of monsieur Ouvrier, on the corner of the place du Salin,” is an acknowledgement of debt to Raymond Masse by two merchant-goldsmiths, Jean Bobelièvre, from Toulouse, and Maturin Rouaut, a native of Amboise. The two men owed 5,642 livres:

on the grounds and because of the sale of 23 pairs of gold pendants, 23 necklaces, also of gold, the pendants and necklaces being studded with diamonds, rubies, emeralds, and pearls, four glass cabinets adorned with silver and painted decorations, 12 dozen pearl buttons, two rock crystal mirrors adorned with gold, a gold box adorned with diamonds suitable for a portrait, and other goldwares.25

These goods were to be used as prizes “to be drawn at the blanque they hold with other goods in the shop of the said Ouvrier, which they hope to open tomorrow.”26

Masse’s Customers

The last facet of Raymond Masse’s career relevant to Ms. Fr. 640 concerns his social standing in Toulouse and the high status of his customers. A variety of documents reveal his ties with the social elite, his position of having valuable gold or silverware at his disposal, and his ability to borrow large sums of money. In 1604, Jean Antoine de Montpezat, baron of Ganac and gentilhomme ordinaire de la chambre du roi, acknowledged a debt of 2,500 livres to Raymond Masse, “for the sale and delivery of a necklace with brilliants and 125 diamonds, and a gold chain adorned with engraved and enameled pearls.”27 The merchant-goldsmith from Toulouse borrowed one-hundred and fifty livres from Pierre Cazalède, a magistrate at the local Parliament, to whom he left as security a magnificent piece of artwork:

a vessel with a large baroque pearl containing the story of Jason and Medea in the round, adorned with fifteen rubies and several small pearls with the Golden Fleece above, near the belhere (ring),28 all gold, with a drop-shaped pearl hanging down with its case.29

Masse’s reputation as a respectable citizen is quite obvious in the part he played in 1610 as a witness to the purchase of a commission of councilor, signed by the widow of Jean de Pins, Souveraine de Portal, and Jean de Senaulx, for 10,500 livres.30 In 1619, he sold “articles of gold artwork from his shop” to Adrien de Monluc, the grandson of Blaise, maréchal de France, (1,200 livres), and to Henry de Montmorencey-Damville, pair et amiral de France, and governor of Languedoc (1,800 livres).31

Lastly, Masse worked for the Béthune family. Philippe’s niece, Louise, married in 1620 to Alexandre de Lévis, baron of Mirepoix, placed an order with Raymond Masse in 1625, through Claude Valentin, the Duke of Sully’s secretary, for the making of “two imitation faceted sapphires, one white and the other green, to be set on picks and worn in the hair.”32

Raymond Masse’s shop, his businesses, and his connections illustrate the social role, the vitality, as well the scope and variety of activities that a merchant-goldsmith could aspire to in Toulouse at the end of the Renaissance. His field of expertise and his working connections intersect with many of the author-practitioner’s concerns.
