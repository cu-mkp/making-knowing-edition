Abstract

This essay reflects on the process of marking up Ms. Fr. 640. While it is tempting to think of encoding as a straightforward process, this essay argues that encoding Ms. Fr. 640 was more than a matter of simply reading a text, identifying categories, and implementing tags. Rather, it was a complex and messy process which required close reading, continuous reflection and alteration, collective expertise, and collaboration between humanists and computers. This essay highlights the flexible, volatile, and non-finite nature of markup, and it illustrates that encoding was not a linear process, but rather was characterized by moving back and forth between pages, constantly implementing new and correcting old markup in an attempt to adhere to new decisions and safeguard consistency. Finally, it shows how solutions to markup problems were frequently sought by considering a user’s perspective. We conclude that encoding Ms. Fr. 640 not only required knowledge but also produced it.

The Making and Knowing Project is a research and pedagogical initiative in the Center for Science and Society at Columbia University that explores the intersections between artistic making and scientific knowing. Today these realms are regarded as separate, yet in the earliest phases of the Scientific Revolution, nature was investigated primarily by skilled artisans by means of continuous and methodical experimentation in the making of objects – the time when “making” was “knowing.”

 — “About,” Making and Knowing Project website1

The authors of this article, two PhD students in the history of science, joined the Making and Knowing Project in the Summer of 2016 to participate in a Middle French Paleography Workshop held at Columbia University. Organized twice before, in the Summer of 2014 and 2015, this three-week workshop aimed to provide participants intensive training in the reading, transcription, translation, and XML encoding of Ms. Fr. 640. Not long after this workshop, the Making and Knowing team sent out an email requesting returning paleographers to indicate their preference for one of two tasks: translation or markup.2 As native Dutch and French speakers with no professional translation experience, neither of us felt sufficiently equipped to translate an early modern manuscript into English, and we thus both leaned toward markup. Moreover, we believed the task of encoding would be more straightforward, manageable, and demand less expertise of two ambitious but relatively inexperienced graduate students than that of translating. We would soon come to revise—and at times even regret—that thought. Our choice for markup was the beginning of a long-term and intense collaboration, devoted entirely to the encoding of the manuscript—that is, tagging words, phrases, and features of the manuscript’s text with a custom set of XML tags. This essay is a reflection on that collaboration, or, more specifically, on the complex, messy, and non-finite, but historically instructive and intellectually rewarding process of marking up Ms. Fr. 640.3 First, we will briefly discuss the foundations and conceptualisation of the markup for Ms. Fr. 640. Then we will explain the principle steps of encoding the manuscript, and finally we will reflect on the nature of that process and elaborate on some of the problems we encountered in it.

The Foundation and Conceptualization of Markup Categories for Ms. Fr. 640

When we joined the project in 2016, neither of us had any experience with XML and TEI markup.4 In fact, we had never even heard of it. Yet, while our markup journey only began in 2016, that of the project did not. Indeed, the foundations of the manuscript’s markup, upon which our encoding work would ultimately rely, had already been laid by the Making and Knowing team and former student paleographers working in 2014 and 2015.5 It is therefore necessary to briefly discuss those early stages of markup development before we reflect on our own encoding work.

The Foundations of Markup

While the main aim of the Summer 2016 workshop was to transcribe and translate the manuscript, one of its other goals was to “conceptualize and complete the markup.” “Conceptualize” here meant that we had to think about the “intellectual agenda” of the data that markup generates about the manuscript. In other words, what information about the text of Ms. Fr. 640 did we want markup to provide users of the Digital Critical Edition (DCE)? As we will show later, this required us to put ourselves in the shoes of potential users of the DCE and envision what specific information in Ms. Fr. 640 they might be interested in. In answering this question, a distinction was made between three types of information and its corresponding markup: structural, editorial, and semantic.

The transcribed and translated Google documents that we began working with in 2016 were already partially encoded with structural, editorial, and semantic markup developed in 2014 and 2015—hence the task to “complete” the markup. Editorial markup indicates the intervention of the editors (i.e., the Making and Knowing team or paleographers) to the text of the manuscript. Abbreviations offer a good example. The author-practitioner frequently wrote words like premierement or communement in an abbreviated form, by spelling out “premierem” and “communem” and supplying the “ent” by means of a supralinear tilde (Fig. 1). When transcribing these words, Project paleographers typed curly brackets around the implied abbreviations: premierem{ent}. In cases where the author-practitioner forgot to add the tilde, we “supplied” the missing abbreviation using a “sup” tag: premierem<sup>{ent}</sup>. Both the curly brackets and sup tag are forms of editorial markup.

The difference between the structural and semantic markup can be explained with part of a 2014 translation of the entry Pintiers (Pewterers) on fol. 28v:

<title id=”p028v_a1”>Pewterers</title>

<ab id=”p028v_b1a”>They think that the best pewter is in a great sow that has not been melted again since the mine, for masters melt it again on a grille weighing two or three pounds in order to cut it in bits and sell it by pieces. And they usually mingle it with some soldering pieces’ rests and ordinary pewter. And the best most shiny, as if polished for it is softer. Sometimes in their sows they find iron. Pieces, stones and similar things to dip in to some pitch. English pewter is so hard that minerons add some lead to soften it. The one coming from German coasts is softer. Usually the pewter masters wardens in good cities add six pounds of fine lead or eight sith a quintal of fine pewter . The others who work in mines add fifteen or 20 or the more they can and to cover the lead black color and its softness, they add some ice pewter 4 pounds with a quintal. </ab>

Structural markup provides information about the layout, organization, or structural logic of a text on a page—for example, what part of a text constitutes a title, paragraph, subheader, etc.—and how single blocks of text on a page relate to each other. In the example above, structural divisions are made with the help of tags—that is, complementary snippets of machine-readable code inserted on either side of the text in question. Notice, for instance, how the word “Pewterers” on the first line is surrounded by information contained between angle brackets (<title id=”p028v_a1”> and </title>), including the structural element “title.” This denotes that the text contained between the opening tag (<title id=”p028v_a1”>) and closing tag (</title>) is a title, as opposed to, for example, a paragraph. Similarly, the text block that follows the title is nested inside “ab” tags (i.e., “anonymous block”), which describes the text between the opening and closing ab tags as an undefined, but coherent block of text, thereby distinguishing it from other structural elements on the page, such as the title.

Structural markup differs from semantic markup in that the latter aims to provide information about the meaning of the text on a page. For instance, while the structural markup on fol. 28v informs the reader that “Pewterers” is a title, that same encoding does not tell us anything about its semantics, i.e., what “pewterers” signifies. Semantic markup is needed to convey that information. In 2014, the Making and Knowing team and workshop participants had conceptualized three semantic markup categories—materials, place names, and professions—and marked up all occurrences of these categories in the manuscript. As the entry on Pewterers illustrates, unlike the structural markup, these categories were not denoted by tags. Rather, they were indicated with simple formatting: materials were set in boldface, place names were underlined, and professions italicized. A reader familiar with how each category was formatted, could understand, for instance, that “lead,” “iron,” and “pitch,” on fol. 28v are materials. Although it was very easy for paleographers unfamiliar with XML markup to work with this type of formatting, it had obvious limitations. First, it only allowed for a limited number of categories (i.e., three), and therefore equally curbed the amount of semantic information that could be conveyed to users of the DCE. Second, it was very easy to accidentally remove the markup by formatting the text differently. So, in 2015, this method of encoding semantic elements was abandoned and replaced with the use of tags, matching the style of the structural markup. Similarly, the use of curly brackets to indicate abbreviations was substituted by “expansion” tags (e.g., premierem<exp>ent</exp>).

Coming back to the goal of the 2016 workshop, when we were tasked to “conceptualize and complete the markup,” this meant: first, to remove remnants of the old formatting; and second, to further develop semantic categories and continue to implement the new XML markup. The remainder of this paper will focus on the latter task of conceptualizing and implementing markup. We will reflect on our efforts to encode Ms. Fr. 640, identify some of the countless problems we encountered in the process, and highlight our key takeaways.

Conceptualizing Semantic Markup Categories

At the end of the 2016 workshop, we made a start on developing new semantic markup categories to extend beyond the three existing ones. Strictly speaking, our brainstorming sessions were geographically and intellectually situated, in that they took place within the walls of Columbia University in New York and as part of a discussion among participants in an academic workshop. However, in developing new semantic categories we pushed ourselves to think outside of those confines. Rather than conceptualizing what we, the Making and Knowing team and Text Workshop participants, wanted to learn about the manuscript, we framed this question in terms of “user stories.” That is, we asked ourselves, who would be interested in using the DCE, reading Ms. Fr. 640, and for what purposes? We can see, for example, that the user story of the historians of science interested in units of measurement has been translated into a “measurement” tag (<ms>), to be applied to words or phrases implying “measurement of any sort (temporal [including pater noster], volume, mass, length).” Similarly, to accommodate numismatists, we developed a coin and currency tag (<cn>).

Not all suggestions or ideas made it to the list. For example, there was no “agricultural” tag for cultural historians interested in word- and phrase-level references to rural food production and land use, though “cultivation” and “animal husbandry” would become one of the twenty-six categories by which the Project classified the entries of the manuscript by thematic content.8 Likewise, there was talk of a “fun tag,” to catch content like the method for training your dog described on fol. 131r, and a “WOW!” tag. In the end, neither of these categories made it on the list, although we, the authors of this essay, are still in favor of implementing them. Even after having worked on this project for several years, the manuscript never ceases to amaze us and in many ways a WOW! tag seems an appropriate response to its marvelous content. Just as important as the final set and meaning of these tags was when and how to use them. As will be discussed below, this was by no means fixed or straightforward.

The remaining question was how to implement these twenty-one categories or, in other words, how to mark up Ms. Fr. 640. In the next section, we will first lay out the principle steps of marking up the manuscript and will then reflect on our process.

Encoding Ms. Fr. 640

In the Summer of 2017, we convened daily for two weeks at the University Jean Jaurès in Toulouse with a group of eighteen people.9 By this time, all participants were returning (i.e., experienced) paleographers, translators, and encoders who had attended at least one of the three previous workshops. Based on preferences and competencies, the group was split up into two teams with each working exclusively on translation or markup. The latter was made up of five individuals, divided over two groups: the first consisted of Making and Knowing postdoctoral scholars Donna Bilak and Tianna Uchacz and graduate student Celine Camps; the second included PhD student Sebestian Kroupa and graduate student Margot Lyautey. In contrast to 2016, when we progressed through Ms. Fr. 640 sequentially, folio by folio, from 2017 onward, work proceeded thematically. The manuscript’s entries had been divided up into categories (Fig. 2), for instance, alchemy, casting, dyeing, painting, metal, weapons, etc.10 Each group was assigned to work on particular themes to cultivate specialization and facilitate the workflow.11 To keep track of our progress, the Project’s Assistant Director Naomi Rosenkranz had helpfully created a spreadsheet using Google Sheets, to track progress. Inserted dates and initials indicated an entry had been finished by one of the groups (Fig. 3).

The first step in encoding the manuscript was to decide which entries (or rather processes) to work on. This decision depended on the progress of the translation groups, as a folio would generally only be marked up once its translation had been finalized—i.e., signed off in the spreadsheet. Once an entry was ready for markup work, we would open the three corresponding transcription and translation documents in the Google Drive and go through the entire text from beginning to end to mark it up (Fig. 4, Fig. 5).

Like many components of the Making and Knowing Project, our working method was highly collective. We soon realised that we worked best in groups of two and ideally three people. This was in part because we had to check and mark up three versions of the manuscript simultaneously—the diplomatic transcription (TC), the normalized transcription (TCN), and the translation (TL)—to guarantee uniformity and consistency. While it was theoretically possible for one person to mark up all three versions, toggling between multiple documents was extremely strenuous and demanding on a single person’s eyes and mind, not to mention time consuming and inefficient, something we found out firsthand over a few days in the Summer 2018 Text Workshop.12 Therefore, each person would ideally only check and encode one document. When a group consisted of only two people, one person would work on the two French versions (TC and TCN) while the other looked at the English translation (TL).

In encoding the text and verifying and unifying existing markup, we mirrored the working method of the translation team: one person would read the text from the English translation out loud—also uttering the tags—and once a letter, word, sentence, or textblock had to be encoded or its markup corrected, we paused and conferred with each other. We then inserted and corrected the relevant tags. When we had completed the markup of an entry, we would double check our work, put our initials and the date in the spreadsheet, and move on to the next entry (always checking the progress of the translation team first).

Now that we have discussed the principle steps of the encoding process, the next sections will reflect in more detail on the nature of markup and the problems we encountered and tried to overcome in the process, drawing on examples and experiences across the various years.

Reflections on Encoding Ms. Fr. 640

As mentioned in the introduction to this essay, when we indicated our preference to concentrate on markup for the 2017 Text Workshop, we believed encoding the manuscript would be a fairly straightforward, unproblematic, and manageable process that would not demand much expertise. After all, a full tag set had been finalized in 2016, and it seemed a simple matter of implementing those tags. Nothing proved further from the truth. Encoding Ms. Fr. 640 was not a matter of simply reading the text, identifying categories, and implementing tags. Rather, as the following sections show, it was a highly complex and messy process which required close reading, continuous reflection and alteration, and collective expertise on a wide variety of subjects.

Close Reading and Collective Expertise

Fundamentally, markup takes the way that humanists read and interpret a text and makes it “readable” to a computer. So, a humanist marking up a text first needs to understand it. Take for example the sentence, “The surgeon reached for a scalpel.” If we were to mark this up according to our list of semantic categories, the encoded sentence would look like this: “The <pro>surgeon</pro> reached for a <tl>scalpel</tl>.” Notice how “surgeon” is nested in “pro” tags (“profession”) and “scalpel” in “tl” tags (“tool”). For anyone who does not know that “surgeon” is a profession or “scalpel” denotes a tool, it would be impossible to encode these words accordingly. In other words, encoding the manuscript was not just a mechanical but also an intellectual act. It required close reading and a level of knowledge about and understanding of a wide range of subjects that we neither possessed nor could have foreseen needing when we joined the Project. Most importantly, our ability to understand and mark up the text of Ms. Fr. 640 significantly depended on a collective of people and sources well beyond the manuscript or the Making and Knowing team itself.

Part of the difficulty in encoding the manuscript lay in the fact that many terms and processes described by the author-practitioner have become obscured over time and by modern historical terminology. For example, or mat on fol. 66r means “mordant gilding” in modern English, but this translation obscures the sense of the sixteenth-century term. Anachronistic translations and interpretations might have problematic consequences for markup. While a term may be classified as a material from a modern perspective, it may not have been in the sixteenth century. For instance, “coral” (fol. 3r) is nowadays understood as an animal, but was it also classified as such in the sixteenth century? We aimed, both in translation and markup, to bring the historical meaning of terms and processes (or actors’ categories) back into view by avoiding (as much as possible) to impose a modern understanding of categories (such as materials) onto the manuscript, except where those historical meanings might engender confusion in modern readers.13 This was easier said than done. While some entries were fairly straightforward, others were hard to understand, even for native French speakers or seasoned historians. More specifically, it was often difficult to grasp the meaning of terms or processes described by the author-practitioner. Our ignorance thus made it equally difficult to know if and how words should be tagged. For instance, we had to understand what azur d’esmail (fol. 11r) meant in order to determine if it should be marked up as a material or not.14

In some cases the manuscript itself provided more insight: while the meaning of a word was sometimes unclear in one entry, it was clarified or intelligible in another. For instance, the terms paillette (fol. 134v) and or de paillelle (fol. 149r) were translated as “spangle” and “spangle gold” respectively, due to the explanation on fol. 104v that pailleterie (spangling work) is the application of “scales of pure silver & of gilt silver.” As a result, only or de paillelle was marked up as a material, since paillette could apply to either silver or gold and seemed to denote, in this instance, more of a “format” for a material than a material proper. Reading multiple entries alongside each other could thus help unravel the meaning of certain terms and determine their appropriate markup. In other cases, we had to consult early modern dictionaries and reference works, use the glossary that was being developed and refined by the translation teams, gloss entries and terms with relevant academic literature, or mine other contemporaneous technical writings (e.g., Hugh Plat’s The Jewell House of Art and Nature from 1594) to make sense of a term or process.15

In addition to written sources, we frequently consulted other people, including the translation teams, who had often already been able to demystify a process or concept through their translation work. Certain entries, however, were so esoteric that we needed the help of specialists. A case in point are the recipes pertaining to weapons, which contained numerous technical terms and processes with which none of us were familiar. In the Summer of 2018, Jonathan Tavares and Pascal Brioist—two experts on arms and armor—joined our workshop. To help us all make sense of the chapter on the “Furbisher” (fols. 94r to 96r) or the entry on “Grey guards” (fol. 96r) Tavares and Brioist drew out and named the individual parts of a sword and harness (Fig. 6, Fig. 7). Through these drawings, we were able to understand the author-practitioner’s vocabulary and determine if a word should be marked up as a tool, material, or not at all. Similarly, Making and Knowing postdoctoral scholar Sophie Pitman, an expert on early modern dress and material culture, helped us distinguish when cotton denoted a plant or a specific type of cloth. Additionally, by consulting experts outside of the Making and Knowing team, we were able to determine that azur d’esmail may mean “smalt” (blue glass that was ground and used as a colorant in pigments) or azurite.16

To Tag or Not to Tag

In the previous section we illustrated how our ignorance of certain words and processes problematized the process of implementing markup. Yet even as we accumulated knowledge, the act of encoding did not necessarily become less complicated. For, as we worked our way through the manuscript, new markup problems arose. On numerous occasions, it was not the meaning of a word or process but rather the meaning of a markup category—i.e., its boundaries, possibilities, and limits—and how best to implement it became the subject of debate. This section will highlight the flexible, volatile, and non-finite nature of markup and its categories. In doing so, it will show that encoding the manuscript was not a linear process in which we moved in a straight line from fol. 1r to 170v. Rather it was characterized by moving back and forth between pages, constantly implementing new and correcting old markup in an attempt to adhere to new decisions and safeguard consistency. In other words, we will discuss some of the problems we encountered in working with markup categories and illustrate how solutions to them were frequently sought by approaching issues from a user’s perspective.

While understanding the meaning of a term certainly helped us to know how it could be marked up, this was not always sufficient. Even when we understood a term, and knew it could be marked up, the question frequently was: should it be? Materials, for example, often occurred with such frequency or their meaning was so vague that we decided to only mark them up in very specific cases: eau (water), for instance, would only be encoded as a material when qualified (e.g., eau de pluie (rain water)) or when used comparatively (e.g., “liquid like water”). Fol. 50r offers another good example: take the following clause: “If you want to promptly mold something in half relief that comes to hand, [...].”17 The word “hand” denotes a body part and, strictly speaking, could be marked up. However, because it is only used metaphorically and the phrase “that comes to hand” does not mean the actual use of one's hands, we chose not to tag it.

On other occasions, the reverse was the case. Though some words did not strictly fall under one of our markup categories, we believed they deserved to be marked up nevertheless. For example, although words like halene (breath), salive (saliva), and urine (urine) do not denote body parts, they do imply parts (or rather, fluids) of the body being used in making processes. We therefore decided that these words had to be marked up as body parts too. Similarly, when a material was implied in a word for a process, technique, or tool, the word was to be encoded as well: e.g., pot plombé (leaded pot) or huiler (oiling).

These examples also point to another question commonly raised in our encoding work: what is the scope of a tag, or how much do we mark up? This question was especially pertinent in the case of materials. For on numerous occasions the author-practitioner further defines and distinguishes between materials by adding a qualifier. On fol. 94v, for instance, he explains that the gold grips of sword guards are made with “thin yellow canvas or a different canvas yellowed with chalk.”18 While it would have been correct (and most easy) to mark up “canvas” (in both instances) and “chalk” as materials and proceed to the next sentence; the instructions do not call for just any canvas, but rather for “thin yellow canvas” or “canvas yellowed with chalk.” In other words, the author-practitioner has added qualifiers (“thin yellow”; and “yellowed with chalk”) to distinguish between various types of canvas. The question therefore was: should we expand the scope of the material tag to include qualifiers too? Because we considered it important to capture the distinctions made by the author-practitioner between various types of (the same) materials, we chose to mark up qualifiers as well. In accord with that decision, the TL markup of the example on fol. 94v resulted in: <m>thin yellow canvas</m> or a different <m>canvas yellowed with chalk</m>.

Though, as was the case with canvas, marking up qualifiers often only required us to add a few words more, the example of tuiles (“tiles”) on fol. 106v illustrates that our decision to expand the scope of a tag meant that it would sometimes span several lines. Take for instance the entry tuiles (“tiles”) on fol. 106v, which opens with the remark that: “Tiles with which one covers houses, the hardest & firmest, and purged of stones & coarse gravel, are used to mix in with the sand for molding.”19 Following our rule to mark up qualifiers, the material tag for this particular sentence starts with “tiles” (the qualified word) and runs all the way to “coarse gravel,” one line down, where the qualifier ends and the material tag closes: “<m>Tiles with which one covers houses, the hardest & firmest, and purged of stones & coarse gravel</m>, are used to mix in with the sand for molding.”

Examples like that of canvas or tiles were easy to encode given that the qualifier immediately preceded or succeeded the qualified word. But what do you do when a qualifier and the term to be qualified are separated by a long string of words, or, worse, when they are not even part of the same text block on a page? Directing our attention to another part of the entry tuiles on fol. 106v can help illustrate the problem here. In a marginal note, written on the right-hand side of the entry’s title, the author-practitioner remarks that: “Those of the houses that are not sandy seem to withstand fire better.”20 Notice that the author-practitioner here is qualifying a different type of tile: not “tiles with which one covers houses, the hardest & firmest, and purged of stones & coarse gravel”—described in the text block immediately below the entry title (and used in the previous example)—but rather tiles “of the houses that are not sandy.” Note especially, however, that the qualified word “tiles” has been substituted by the demonstrative pronoun “those”, thereby referring the reader back to the title of the entry (tuiles) and its accompanying textblock. In other words, the qualified word (tiles) and the qualifier (“of the houses that are not sandy seem to withstand fire better”) are not just separated from each other by a string of words. Rather, they are not even part of the same text block (the former being the title, the latter having been nested in a marginal note). So what should the scope of the material tag be here? Do you only mark up the qualified word (“tile”) or its substitute (“those”); only the qualifier (“of the houses that are not sandy.”); both the qualifier and the qualified word, or nothing at all? After much debate, we reasoned that because the word “tiles” had already been marked up as a material at least once before in this entry (namely in the title), fol. 106v would definitely come up when filtering the manuscript for occurances of tiles and any reader interested in this material would probably read the entire entry either way—thus equally noticing the marginal note. We therefore decided not to mark up the marginal note with an m-tag (except for the “material” not sandy).

As this example shows, questions of scope were not always easy to answer. They often had to be dealt with on a case-by-case basis and required lengthy group discussions (Fig. 8). To expedite the markup process and make it more efficient, we capped our discussion time at five minutes. If a markup issue could not be solved within that time, either because we could not reach an immediate agreement or the problem was too complex, we would make a note of it and move on.

Because the boundaries of markup categories were constantly in flux, rules had numerous exceptions to them, and new decisions were made on a daily basis, it was difficult to remember all markup rules. This was especially true from workshop to workshop: since we typically met for only two weeks each summer, by the time we reconvened, the decisions of previous years had almost been forgotten. An encoding protocol was therefore kept in the Project’s Google Drive that recorded all markup rules and decisions (Fig. 9).21 This protocol contained a handy “to tag or not to tag” table with a list of commonly encountered problems and current solutions in categories like materials, places, and body parts (Fig. 10, Fig. 11). Moreover, when new decisions were made during one of the workshops, these were also written on a whiteboard in our workspace, which was visible to all groups and prevented us from having to toggle between multiple documents while working (Fig. 12).

Finally, in many instances the question was not only should a term be tagged, but also, if yes, how best to do so. This latter question was mainly raised due to first, the difference in language between the transcriptions and the translation, and second, our aim to ensure a consistent, useful, and unified markup across all three versions (i.e., TC, TCN, TL). The remaining examples in this section will show that answers to these questions were frequently sought by approaching problems from a user’s perspective.

In terms of encoding, the 2017 Summer Text Workshop was devoted to completing the markup of all versions of the manuscript. By that time, the markup was the product of three previous workshops (held in 2014, 2015, and 2016), in which different people had worked on the entries of the manuscript with continuously changing markup procedures. The resulting markup was therefore in various stages and inconsistent. The aim from 2017 onward was to update the encoding and to ensure a consistent and unified markup of the manuscript. In other words, we strove to keep the structural, editorial, and semantic markup the same across all three versions. This was not always possible and in certain cases concessions had to be made.

The difference in language between the TC (and TCN) and the TL made it difficult to carry over all the markup from one version to the other. Take for instance expansions. We have already explained that we used expansion tags to indicate expanded abbreviations (e.g., in premierement or communement) in the TC and TCN. Markup protocol required us to maintain a consistent markup. Yet how do you carry over an expansion to the translation when the difference in language does not allow you to indicate in English which part of the source word is abbreviated? Specifically, how do you communicate to the English reader that the “ent” in premierement is abbreviated when they encounter Ms. Fr. 640 through the TL, whether first or exclusively? In such cases, we chose to deviate from our rule of consistency—expansion tags are not represented in the TL.

Deletions offer another good example. Protocol stipulated that deletions were to be maintained across all three versions. In many cases this could be done with ease. For instance, on fol. 4v the author-practitioner first wrote oignons, but then corrected himself and crossed it out. All it took to carry over the deletion to the TL was to translate the word oignons to “onions” and nest the latter in deletion tags: <del>onions</del>. Yet in other instances, adhering to consistency demanded creativity because of the nature of the deletion. Especially problematic were cases where only a string of letters, which could not be translated, were deleted. A case in point is a deletion on fol. 36v. In the first entry on this page, the author-practitioner originally wrote: “Il est vray que pour les rendre plus vendables les fondables y meslent plus de letton.”22 He then realized he had made a mistake and crossed out “ables” in fondables and replaced it with (i.e., added) “eurs,” thus changing fondables to fondeurs (Fig. 13). Our markup in the TC and TCN attempted to render the author-practitioner’s partial deletion and addition as well as the fact that the word fondeurs indicates a profession (Fig. 14). In accord with our markup protocol and aim to unify the three versions, all deletions had to be carried over from the TC to the TCN and the TL. However, the word “founders,” the English translation of fondeurs, does not include an “ables,” and the latter string of letters cannot be translated into English either. So the question was, how do we include a deletion tag here? The answer came from our asking what would be most beneficial to potential users of the DCE. We reasoned that a great number of users (perhaps especially those without French skills) might not read the translation alongside the transcription and, as a result, would be unaware of the deletion if not marked up in the TL. We therefore found it important to flag in the TL that the author-practitioner had corrected himself in the source text—in case users might be interested in this information. Concretely, our solution was to carry over the corrected letters, words, or strings of them, to the TL and, in cases where they could not be translated, nest them in “fr” (French) tags to indicate the difference in language (Fig. 15).

A paragraph in the entry “Pewterers” on fol. 28v, which contains various types of deletions, helpfully illustrates how we generally dealt with carrying over omitted words or strings of letters from the TC and TCN to the TL:

Usability rather than consistency also became the goal in marking up fol. 16r. In the entry “Founding of soft iron,” the author-practitioner explains that miners use deulx ou trois poignees (two or three handfuls) of wallwort “to make the iron run.” The English “handfuls” denotes a body part—the hand—and, following our markup protocol, should thus be marked up with a <bp> tag. Yet, the implied use of the hand is less obvious in the French poignees and tagging it as a body part could be considered illogical and potentially confusing to the reader. Should it therefore be tagged in the TC and TCN at all? While it would have been logical to omit the tag in the French, marking up only the English would lead to an inconsistency in tags. Yet, more important to our decision than a unified markup, were the implications our encoding practices might have for potential users of the DCE. Unless marked up, the entry “Founding of soft iron” on fol. 16r would not appear when filtering the transcription for mention of body parts, and users might not think of searching for the word poignees themselves. It was these assumptions about potential users and usability that ultimately made us decide to mark up poignees as a body part in the TC and TCN as well.

In sum, even though we had already developed our markup categories, their rules and the practice of implementation were not straightforward, their conceptual boundaries never fixed but rather constantly tested and redefined as we moved through and gained knowledge of the manuscript. Moreover, it was precisely because of the ever-changing, flexible, and non-finite nature of our markup categories that we constantly had to move back and forth between pages, either correcting existing or inserting new markup on previously completed folios, in accordance with the latest encoding protocols. In the next and final section, we will show that although we have focused primarily on the role of the human intellect in this paper, encoding always depended on a collaboration between humans and computers.

Encoding: A Collaboration Between Humanists and Computers

Although marking up Ms. Fr. 640 was primarily a human endeavor, computers and digital tools were nevertheless essential to our work. For instance, we already saw how the digital tracking spreadsheet made by Naomi Rosenkranz and the markup protocols made our efforts more efficient. Besides the fact that our markup work was done in the Google Drive, computers and digital tools also played a role in a different way.

Our markup ultimately had to be read by a computer, and any failure or error on our part when inserting tags would problematize this process. A commonly made mistake, for example, was to forget to close a tag. Let us explain this briefly: in order for a computer to read our markup language, each semantic category requires a tag that opens with the form <element name> and closes with the form </element name> (note the forward slash here). For instance, when we want a computer to interpret the word “fish” in the entry “Fish glue or isinglass (and mouth glue)” (fol. 7r) as an animal, it requires an opening <al> tag and a closing </al> tag. The proper markup would result in: <al>fish</al>. Yet the forward slash is easily forgotten (or confused with a backward slash) when inserting tags manually, thereby hampering a computer’s ability to understand the markup. Another frequent error was to confuse the order of multiple tags and create an asymmetry. For example, the word “fish glue” denotes both a material and an animal and has to be marked up as follows: <m><al>Fish</al> glue</m>. Note the order of the tags: the word “fish” is nested inside an opening and closing “al” tag, thus encoding it as an animal; the term “fish glue” is surrounded by opening and closing “m” tags, thus indicating it as a material. It looks straightforward, but mistakes are easily made. Compare, for instance, the order of the tags in the previous example with that in the following: <al><m>Fish</al> glue</m>. The elements are the same, but their order has changed significantly. The “m” tag (<m>) opens inside another element (the “al” tag), but it does not close before the outer element (i.e., <al>) does. In other words, these two tag sets overlap rather than nest. Nesting tags are permitted; overlapping are not. As a result, the markup in this latter example will cause errors when the computer tries to read it.

Though these mistakes are not hard to spot in isolated examples, they can be easily overlooked in a text as heavily marked up as that of the entry “For gilding with gold color and tinsel” on fol. 7r (Fig. 16). To reduce the risk of such errors and facilitate and expedite the process of encoding and identifying mistakes, Philip Cherian, an MSc student in physics and philosophy of science at the École Polytechnique and returning participant in the Text Workshops, developed two ingenious Google Docs add-ons (which he eventually consolidated into a single multipurpose tool), for the markup team: one for inserting tags (Vid. 1), and another for validating encoded documents (Vid. 2, Vid. 3). Video 1 illustrates how the first add-on enabled us to mechanically insert tags without having to type them out ourselves, reducing the risk of forgetting, for instance, a forward slash in the closing tag or of inserting the wrong element. Videos 2 and 3 show how we could validate our encoded documents—i.e., check for errors—by using the second add-on. This tool would not only notify us of mistakes (e.g., an imbalance in tags) and inform us about the type of error made (e.g., missing end tag), but also help us locate it on the page (e.g., on line nine). Notice also how this tool changes the format and color of the markup, making it easier to distinguish from the manuscript’s text.

Since markup mistakes would make the text impossible for a computer to process, validating our work was no mere formality, and we developed strategies to remind and incentivize ourselves to get the encoding right. If the markup team should ever have a mascot, it would have to be dancing Kermit, his hands flailing about upon seeing an error-free markup (Vid. 4). In fact, validating was so important that it not only became the group’s watchword—with both markup and translation teams being reminded in many ways to check their work before proceeding to the next entry or folio—but even became a song, aimed to keep the spirits high (Fig. 17, Fig. 18). Indeed, encoding Ms. Fr. 640 was a very demanding task, and by 2017 we got so immersed in it that, upon leaving the Text Workshop at the end of the day, we started mentally marking up the produce we encountered as we walked through the supermarkets of Toulouse, planning our dinners.

In sum, though largely a human affair, marking up Ms. Fr. 640 could not have been done without the help of computers and digital tools.

Conclusion

When we joined the Project in the summer of 2016 and indicated our preference for markup, we assumed the task of encoding Ms. Fr. 640 would be straightforward, manageable, and require minimal knowledge and expertise. This essay has demonstrated the opposite. Although much is left unsaid and this paper cannot do full justice to our work or that of others on whom we depended, we hope to have identified some of the most important characteristics and problems of encoding Ms. Fr. 640.

We have shown that marking up Ms. Fr. 640 was a complex and messy endeavor, which required close reading, continuous reflection and alteration, and collective expertise on a wide variety of subjects. We have equally highlighted the non-finite nature of markup. Indeed even now, as the Project has met its first major objective with the publication of Secrets of Craft and Nature in Renaissance France. A Digital Critical Edition and English Translation of BnF Ms. Fr. 640, and the markup of the manuscript is, in that sense, complete, the encoding can still be considered unfinished. There are always more tags to add or remove; conceptual boundaries to redefine, and new categories to be developed. In that way, markup truly is a never-ending process.

Above all, we hope to have shown that—contrary to what we initially believed—it took knowledge to mark up Ms. Fr. 640. Knowledge of the manuscript and its structure. Knowledge of the historical terms and processes contained within it and of the sources and experts who could help us obtain it. Knowledge of how computers and humanists read a text. And knowledge of how our work could benefit users. Most importantly, encoding Ms. Fr. 640 not only required knowledge, but through the very process of obtaining that knowledge, also produced it. In a sense, the resulting markup can be considered a mirror of the Project’s collective knowledge—both its acquisition and production. In other words—in the spirit of the Project and its mission as conveyed in this essay’s epigraph—ma<r>king was knowing.
