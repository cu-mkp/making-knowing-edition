Wax for Seals in Ms. Fr. 640

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/ycat-6k25
← Back to Essays

Abstract

Wax is a frequently used material in Ms. Fr. 640. Two entries on fols. 42r and 133r use wax for making “seals,” although the types of seals the author-practitioner had in mind are unclear. This essay explores wax seals for documents in early modern Europe. The reconstructions seek to evaluate the efficacy of the author-practitioner’s techniques for producing superior seals. The first reconstruction demonstrates that his methods for preparing and pressing wax for imprinting can yield a large seal that was as well formed as one made using an alternative technique involving pouring molten wax. The second reconstruction, which explores additives that give a greater hardness to wax, produced less conclusive results, in part because of uncertainty about how much lead white and how much pigment were to be added to a given quantity of wax.

One of the most widely-used materials in early modern Europe, wax also formed an important part of the author-practitioner’s work, and BnF Ms. Fr. 640 contains multiple references to it.1 In the sixteenth century, wax possessed material, spiritual, and intellectual meaning—it was the intermediary material par excellence, used for modeling, casting, and other processes; it was used for ex voto objects and for other sacramentals, such as the many candles employed in religious services; and it was viewed as related to the human mind and body, through analogies to the soul and its flesh-like malleability (indeed, made malleable by human hands) as well as its luminosity when used for portraits.2 The author-practitioner, however, is concerned solely with the material properties of wax in two entries on fols. 42r and 133r respectively. These refer to wax in relation to the making of seals (cachets), but the author-practitioner’s suggestions in the first of the two recipes (fol. 42r) regarding ways in which the wax might be further embellished after having been imprinted, raise questions about the types of “seals” he had in mind. This essay explores the kinds of objects that were referred to as “seals” in early modern Europe and considers which of these might have been of particular interest to the author-practitioner. The related reconstructions seek to evaluate the efficacy of the author-practitioner’s recipes and techniques for producing superior wax seals.

Seals in Early Modern Europe

Perhaps the most common use of the term “seal” was in reference to the imprint of a design and/or lettering in a piece of wax affixed to a document or commercial product as evidence of authenticity, origin, and quality. Seals were also used as a means of closing letters and other missives to ensure privacy, so the recipient could be sure that the document had not been opened or tampered with. In what follows, I will refer to the wax itself as a seal or a sealing, and to the instrument used to imprint the seal as a seal matrix.3 A seal matrix was often attached to a short haft to allow the user to exert greater downward pressure on the matrix and thus to make a clearer impression in the wax (Fig. 1). A small matrix might be set into a ring, in which case it was known as a signet (Fig. 2).4

The materials from which seal matrices were made varied, “but from the thirteenth century the preferred material was metal.”5 The matrix material needed to be durable and sufficiently inflexible to make an impression on melted, or at least softened, wax, but the specific material used for a particular matrix reflected both the resources of its owner and the use to which it would be put. The matrix in Fig. 1 was made of copper overlaid with gilding and champlevé enamel. The signet ring in the portrait in Fig. 2 seems to have been made of gold inset with an intaglio-cut stone. More modest examples might be made of bronze (Fig. 3).

The design and lettering on the face of a seal matrix were engraved into flat blanks. Some were custom-made according to the specifications of the owner, but others, intended for a less elite market, were engraved with generic ornamental designs and well-known mottos.6 Goldsmiths, as craftspeople accomplished in working with a variety of precious materials and in using engraving tools and techniques, were often called upon to fashion seal matrices for their wealthy clientele. Typically, craftspeople skilled in working with base metals, like pewterers and coppersmiths, supplied matrices for those segments of the market that could not afford more expensive materials. But, by the mid-thirteenth century in London, there was a group of artisans, known as “sealers,” specifically engaged in making seal matrices.7

Goldsmiths also produced bronze portrait medals, objects that were sometimes referred to as seals. Benvenuto Cellini (1500–1571) devotes several chapters in his treatise on goldsmithing to the techniques for casting and stamping medals. In describing the process of making cast medals for cardinals, Cellini refers to them as suggelli.8 It is unclear whether others used the word similarly.

In introducing the recipe on fol. 42r, the author-practitioner refers to “large wax seals” (les grands seaulx de cire). This is the only instance in the manuscript in which he uses seaulx rather than cachets, and its singularity might be taken to imply that he had something quite specific in mind, and perhaps something other than a documentary seal.9 Moreover, at the end of the recipe, he offers suggestions regarding ways in which the wax might be embellished after having been imprinted: carving, gilding, and painting the figures; transferring the imprinted wax to a painted glass support; or even inlaying the piece in some type of framework.10 Although some very important documentary seals may have been gilded, any change to the physical form of the design after it had been imprinted risked nullifying the authenticity of a documentary seal, thereby defeating its purpose.11 It seems unlikely, then, that these suggestions were intended to apply to ordinary documentary seals. Nevertheless, the very specificity of the suggestions points toward a particular objective, one with which the author-practitioner’s readers would presumably have been familiar.

Molded and modeled beeswax was used in myriad ways in early modern Europe, and the making of several types of wax objects other than seals undoubtedly involved one or more of the processes referred to in the author-practitioner’s suggestions in the entry on fol. 42r. Nevertheless, research reveals no other wax objects that were also referred to as “seals” and no such objects for which all of the techniques mentioned in the entries would have been applicable. Without better information about what types of objects might have been captured under the rubric of “seal,” it seemed prudent to assume that the terms cachet and sceau both referred to documentary seals. In view of this, the reconstructions described below were undertaken to attempt to determine whether the author-practitioner’s recipes would result in superior documentary wax seals.

Reconstruction of “large wax seals” from “Wax for seal and imprint” on fol. 42r

I undertook a reconstruction of the recipe on fol. 42r to test whether the author-practitioner’s instructions to knead wax and “to polish it” into a mold with a pestle could produce a better imprint, as he asserts, than could be made by pouring molten wax into a mold.12 The recipe divides the process of making “large wax seals” into three stages: preparation, imprinting, and embellishing. The steps in the preparatory phase—immersing the wax in warm water and kneading it by hand—are both designed to raise the temperature of the wax by a few degrees13 to improve its malleability prior to imprinting because it is quite inflexible at room temperature.14 For the middle stage, the author-practitioner describes a technique which he likens to “polishing” the underside of the wax and which seems designed to optimize the clarity of the imprint. The final stage of the recipe offers suggestions for enhancing and mounting the seal, which I did not follow.

The preparatory phase began with selecting and preparing the wax. I chose to use raw beeswax purchased from a local beekeeper rather than commercial wax pellets, since the former seemed a choice more in keeping with the spirit of the reconstruction.15 I then melted the beeswax and divided it into two parts, one to be used in the reconstruction of the author-practitioner’s process and the other to be used to test the author-practitioner’s assertion that his technique would result in a better imprint than using molten wax. Working with half of the cooled wax, I attempted to knead it to work out any internal inconsistencies which could result in flaws in the imprinted wax.16 However, kneading the room-temperature wax by hand was difficult; although it was relatively pliable around the edges where it was thinner and could be bent in half both lengthwise and crosswise, it was not malleable. Kneading it on a hard surface, using my knuckles and the heel of my hand, proved no more successful. After working on the wax for ten to fifteen minutes using only my hands to raise its temperature, I concluded that it would be necessary to immerse the wax in a pot of tepid water to achieve the requisite consistency. When the wax was removed from the water after about three minutes, it was still quite hard to manipulate, but it began to gradually soften. I continued to knead it for three to five minutes, shaping it roughly into a disk.

According to the recipe, the wax was now ready for imprinting. Not having an elaborate seal matrix to hand, my reconstruction was based on a humble substitute, a circular cookie mold about 9 cm in diameter. I centered the cookie mold over the wax disk and pressed down as hard as I could—the handle of the mold helped, but I resorted to using a hammer to increase the pressure. The manuscript instructions on fol. 42r then say, “put three or four pieces of paper on, & with a stick you even & round like a pestle, you will roll it as if you wanted to polish it.”17 I interpreted that this “polishing” action was intended to force the wax into the nooks and crannies of the mold, to ensure that the wax took the shape of the matrix, compensating, at least in part, for the fact that warm, malleable wax does not flow in the way that molten wax does. Accordingly, I inverted the matrix so that its open face was facing upward, and I covered the wax with several sheets of newsprint. As I moved the pestle over the paper, sometimes pushing the pestle down, sometimes forcing it forward in a plowing motion, the sheets of newsprint began to take on the shape of the underlying wax, which, in turn, responded to the pressure of the pestle.

When I was confident that the wax had conformed to the shape of the seal matrix, I eased the wax out of the matrix by pulling gently on the sheets of paper. The fol. 42v recipe says explicitly, “the paper … will help you lift it off the mold,” as the wax “will attach itself to the paper.”18 Small bits of the newsprint paper had, indeed, adhered to the bottom of the wax, but I did not attempt to remove them for fear of damaging the impression. The wax generally conformed to the shape of the matrix, but it did not penetrate to the outer rim of some of the “petals” of the flower-shaped stamp (Fig. 4).19

Against this first result, I tested the author-practitioner’s assertion that his technique would produce a better impression than “if you were to cast it molten.”20 I melted the reserve beeswax, the same amount used to create the disk above, and poured it into the same cookie mold matrix. When I unmolded this second imprint, I was surprised to find that it had the same defects as the first, namely, the wax had not reached the outer edges of some of the petals (Fig. 5). This may have been because the matrix was not absolutely level when the wax was being poured into it. But it seems more likely that my assumption that molten wax would flow unimpeded, an assumption based on the properties of other less viscous liquids, was incorrect.21

Beyond the scope of my reconstruction was the last stage of the recipe, which suggests embellishing the imprinted wax by carving, gilding, and painting the figures, transferring the imprinted wax to a painted glass support, and inlaying the piece in some type of framework. Nevertheless, the suggestion on fol. 42v to “carve the figures” informed my subsequent reconstruction.

Reconstruction of “Hard wax for imprinting seals” on fol. 133r

I undertook a two-part reconstruction of the recipe on fol. 133r, first, to test how the addition of lead white to wax at increasingly greater proportions might impact its pliability, color, and ability to take a crisp imprint, and, second, to see how these same qualities are impacted by the further addition of a pigment to thusly prepared wax.

The recipe found on fol. 133r, “Hard wax for imprinting seals,” like the recipe on fol. 42r, provides instructions regarding the preparation of wax for imprinting. In this case, the author-practitioner prescribes the addition of powdered lead white to make the wax firmer.22 The recipe also calls for “a drop of turpentine to bind it,” followed by the addition of pigment. Although the title of the recipe refers to imprinting seals, the last sentence says, “this is the wax goldsmiths use for modeling,” a juxtaposition that seems to imply that wax prepared according to this recipe might be used in contexts beyond imprinting seals.

The hardness of wax seems to have been important to a variety of craftspeople. Hugh Plat, the seventeenth-century English recorder of craft processes, endorsed the use of firm wax for imprinting seals. Speculating that “hard wax” was actually made largely from the gum Lacca, Plat noted that he had often made seals using gum Lacca. He notes, “I … do find the same to agree with hard wax, in the perfect taking of the impression of the seal.”23 Confirmation of the hardening effect of lead white on wax can be found in Jehan le Begue’s transcription of the third book credited to Eraclius (c. thirteenth century?). Recipe XXIV of Eraclius’s manuscript suggests applying a mixture of wax, finely ground tiles, and ground lead white to a wood panel that cannot by other means be made smooth enough for painting, offering the assurance that "if you hesitate about mixing white-lead with the wax, know that the more you mix with it, the harder it will be."24 Benvenuto Cellini describes using “black wax, a bit hardened” for modeling cardinals’ seals or medals. He subsequently recommends mixing wax and lead white in a 2:1 ratio to make white wax models for medals, but he does not specify whether the lead white is added for color, firmness, or both.25 It seems likely that harder wax would have provided crisper forms and that it would have been preferred in situations where there was some risk that the shaped wax could be damaged by subsequent processes.

But not all early modern authorities on the subject of modelling agreed that firmer wax was better wax. Vasari (1511–1547), in his discussion of the preparation of wax for making maquettes, indicates that the wax should be made softer:

To render it softer a little animal fat and turpentine and black pitch are put into the wax, and of these ingredients it is the fat that makes it more supple; the turpentine adds tenacity, and the pitch gives it the black colour and a certain consistency, so that after it has been worked and is left to stand it becomes hard.26

It seems clear that achieving the optimal consistency for a particular application hinges on the proportion of additives, often and especially lead white, in a given quantity of wax. Because the author-practitioner offers no proportions in the recipe on fol. 133r, I made three trials, producing sample mixtures with successively greater concentrations of lead white, which I estimate at two, five, and seven percent respectively. For each addition of lead white, I also added “a drop” of larch turpentine.27 After making each sample mixture, I poured off small amounts to be used to compare the changes in color and malleability manifested as more lead white was added. The raw beeswax had a slightly yellowish tinge, which the lead white mellowed to a creamy white. In terms of malleability, the raw beeswax was the most supple, and the first and second trial samples were marginally less so. The third trial sample could still be manipulated, but it offered a bit more resistance to attempts to fold it, and eventually it snapped in half. Thus, it appears that the addition of lead white does indeed increase the hardness of wax in proportion to the amount of lead white added (Fig. 6).

The second part of my reconstruction, which required the addition of pigment to the lead-turpentine-wax, again raised the question of how much to add. It seems that the author-practitioner assumed his readers would have known how much pigment was needed. Moreover, the correct proportion was probably dependent on the specific pigment itself and the desired intensity of color. In the recipe for black sulfured wax to be used for casting small animals (fol. 139v), the author-practitioner did provide a proportion, suggesting “half as much” ground charcoal be added to the wax to give it color, meaning to add half as much charcoal by weight as wax.28 Quite different guidance is provided by T. W. Cowan, a late-nineteenth-century expert on bees and beekeeping. In one of his volumes, Cowan offers numerous recipes for adding color, including a variety of red pigments, to wax. The suggested proportions range from 10:1 for alkanet root or safflower to 100:6 for dragon’s blood.29 In view of my decision to use red iron oxide and the striking difference between the proportions suggested by these two sources, it seemed worthwhile to make two trials with the pigment, one using a 2:1 ratio (Sample A) and a second using a 50:3 ratio (Sample B).30

For the pigment trials, I chose to use the wax mixture with the highest concentration of lead white; I reasoned that if the primary objective of the fol. 133r recipe was to make the wax harder, I should use the firmest sample. With the addition of the powdered red iron oxide, the Sample A (high pigment ratio) wax became the color of burnt umber. The color of the Sample B (low pigment ratio) wax was slightly less saturated but still quite dark. Efforts to try to manipulate both Sample A and Sample B at room temperature were unsuccessful: the blocks of cooled mixture quickly snapped in two, suggesting that the addition of the powdered pigment had increased the hardness of the wax. Interestingly, the core of Sample B revealed flecks of lead white, indicating that the additive had not been as thoroughly integrated into the mixture as I had thought (Fig. 7, Fig. 8).

To better evaluate whether wax to which lead white has been added is preferable to raw beeswax for imprinting seals and modelling, I devised additional tests. First, I repeated the kneading and “polishing” technique described on fol. 42r using the Sample B pigmented wax.31 At room temperature, the pigmented wax proved much more difficult to knead by hand than the raw beeswax; in fact, I could not knead it at all until it had been warmed in a pan of tepid water. After shaping it into a disk, I tried to imprint it with the same matrix I used before, but the impression was less than half as deep as the impression in the raw beeswax. To get a better impression, I returned the marginally-printed wax disk to the warm water for several minutes. After the second attempt at warming, the impression was equal in depth to the impression in the raw beeswax, with the pigmented wax generally conforming to the shape of the matrix (Fig. 9). The fact that the Sample B pigmented wax had to be warmed for a longer period of time was perhaps due to the higher density of the material; taking into account both the lead white and the iron oxide, I estimate that roughly ten percent of Sample B by volume was composed of powdered metallic compounds. From these trials, it is clear that imprinting seals with hardened wax is more laborious than with pure beeswax, however the hardening may result in other desirable qualities, such as durability, that I did not test.

As the author-practitioner says specifically that wax prepared in accordance with the fol. 133r recipe is “the wax goldsmiths use for modeling,” I undertook a comparative test involving modeling the wax with tools.32 To test the raw beeswax, I used the seal previously prepared by pouring molten wax into the cookie mold matrix. Using a short, flat blade, I started by cutting away the rim of excess wax that had formed when the molten wax overflowed the matrix. It was more difficult to remove this excess than I had anticipated, as the wax was quite thick and sticky. Once the excess had been removed, however, the wax responded readily to being modeled by a tool with a squared-off loop; I used this to clean up and bevel the trimmed edges. I then used a tool with a small oval loop to incise an outline on three of the petals and to carve out the center of the outlined area on one of those petals (Fig. 10).

The experience of modeling the pigmented wax was quite different. Expecting the harder red wax to offer more resistance, I started by experimenting with the block of Sample A wax, but it, too, yielded quite easily to the modeling tools. When I turned to the imprinted Sample B wax, I again used the small oval loop to incise lines in three of the petals. True to the author-practitioner’s prediction that “the charcoal gives color and body to the wax, which would otherwise be transparent & the features would not be seen as well” on fol. 139v, the addition of pigment made the marks of the modeling tools much more visible than in raw beeswax (compare Fig. 9 and Fig. 10).

Conclusion

Based on my reconstruction of the recipe for “large wax seals” in “Wax for seal and imprint” on fol. 42r, I was able to conclude that, with respect to the techniques described for preparing and imprinting the wax, the author-practitioner knew whereof he spoke: warming the wax produced an impression that was as good as one produced using melted wax. It seems likely that, with practice, an artisan would have been able to master the “polishing” technique, giving greater certainty that the wax would conform to the shape of the matrix completely.

The second reconstruction, of the entry “Hard wax for imprinting seals” on fol. 133r, that tested how wax could be hardened with lead white then colored with pigment, made clear the importance of adding color to any medium that is intended to be given shape and detail, either by molding, modeling, or carving. The pale semi-translucence of the raw beeswax reduced the visual impact of any surface shaping. The addition of color made surface details visible, probably because of the way pigment molecules reflect and absorb light. That transformation would have been critical, both to the working artisan and to the end-user of the artisan’s products.
