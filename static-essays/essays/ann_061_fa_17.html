What is Carton?

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/xwg0-2e10
← Back to Essays

Abstract

This essay focuses on the entry “Carton” on fol. 81r, which describes how paper can be pulped and molded in a cavity. This description at first appears similar to the process of using paper that later became known as papier-mâché. The research on the historical meaning and uses of carton in this essay, and its examination of references to carton in both Ms. Fr. 640 and contemporaneous French literature demonstrates that, while many uses of carton were similar to those of papier-mâché, carton was also employed in ways not associated with papier-mâché. By examining the multiple functions of carton—as a cheaper mold substitute, or as an element in composite decorative objects, for example—this essay sheds light on the capacious understanding of the material in early modern Europe.

Introduction

At first glance, the entry “Carton” on fol. 81r of BnF. Ms. Fr. 640 might seem to be about the material we now know as papier-mâché.

Carton

One ought to take paper from Florence, which is the finest, & pestle it & soak it several times & change the water every day, so that it does not smell bad, & when you have molded it in the hollow form, put a linen over it, & from the back, rub the back of the paper with a tooth, as if you wanted to burnish it, and it will mold very neatly. Then glue the cloth on the back with strong glue (fol. 81r).

Although extant remnants of papier-mâché sculptures, dolls, mirror frames, and ceiling ornaments dating from between the fifteenth and seventeenth centuries show its wide use in early modern Europe, it is difficult to ascertain to what end the author-practitioner intended to use his carton. This question is not illuminated by the reconstruction process because important details are missing from this entry, including the tools needed, as well as any description of the final product. Since the term “papier-mâché” only came into use in the late seventeenth century, this essay focuses historical research on the term carton. Two forms of analysis are conducted. The first starts from the author-practitioner’s use of the term carton in seven entries in the manuscript, and the second examines the term in wider contemporaneous French literature, including novels and correspondence. This analysis shows that in early modern Europe, the concept of carton was not limited to what later became known as papier-mâché, but also included uses as a cheaper mold alternative and as an element in larger, composite decorative objects such as tables.

Materials and Reconstruction of Carton

The entry on fol. 81r starts by specifying that paper from Florence is to be used for making carton, presumably because it is “the finest” (le plus fin). This immediately raises the question: what is meant by the “finest”? Does he mean the degree to which the rags were beaten to pulp in the initial paper-making process? An entry on “Paper” on fol. 12r also mentions that the “whitest & finest is the best” kind of paper. An emphasis on color and tactile quality are found in an eighteenth-century French language source, published as part of a series of texts describing arts and trades by the Académie royale des sciences in 1762. Joseph Jérôme Lefrançais de Lalande explains in Art du cartonnier that although scraps of paper could be collected from fan-makers and paper-makers, the cartonniers would separate the paper scraps, reserving the finest and whitest to make delicate white carton.1 Later, in discussing the multiple uses of carton, Lalande again emphasizes that

anyone can imitate the work of the cartonnier at a small scale and make himself a substance of the same nature as that of carton, nice and suitable for different kinds of works. He who wants only a small quantity of carton, nice and white, to make bas-reliefs, figures pressed in hollows or plaster molds, medals, etc., only needs to soak for a while some scraps of nice paper, grind them in a mortar until they are like gruel or a very fine cream; one applies a small quantity of this substance in a lightly oiled mold, so that the carton does not stick to it; one lets it dry or at least consolidate itself in the mold, and one obtains an exact impression.2

Lalande notes that there is “considerable commerce in such works,” thereby hinting at widespread use of carton to make such objects.3 Since his book primarily focuses on carton production on a large workshop scale, it is possible that his mention of white carton is linked to smaller objects created with fine paper.

In another contemporaneous source, albeit farther away in England, Helen Smith found that the “finest papers were beautifully smooth.”4 She finds evidence for the desirability of smooth paper in Edward Topsell’s Historie of four-footed beasts (1607), in which he wrote, “of the teeth of Oxen I know no other vse but scraping and making Paper smooth.” 5 The emphasis on fine paper is a justifiable concern when one takes into consideration the rags that were used in making early modern paper. Albert Proteaux, in his book Practical Guide for the Manufacture of Paper and Boards (1866), dates the beginning of preparing paper from rags to second half of the twelfth century.6 This is substantiated by findings from Thomas Collings and Derek Milner’s microscopic analysis of paper specimens manufactured between 1400 and 1800 in Europe. Collings and Milner found the specimens showed a mixture of hemp and flax fiber.7 Similar results were found from conservation projects on papier-mâché structures, which showed cellulose of cotton and hemp.8 Unless these rags were properly soaked and crushed, the paper could have a rough texture.

Once the finest paper from Florence is sourced, the author-practitioner’s next step is to “pestle it & soak it several times & change the water every day, so that it does not smell bad.” Although the author-practitioner does not mention the particularities of the water, clean water is generally considered an important aspect of papermaking and is often emphasized by paper mills.9 In a 1976 documentary on a paper mill making handmade paper from cotton, the narrator notes a chemical reaction between water and cotton, which forms a natural bond that holds the pulp together.10

My reconstruction of the crushing and soaking steps led to two important observations: the first pertained to the water’s role in cleaning the pulp. Having crushed and ground dry paper in a mortar and pestle as best I could, I soaked it in water and left it for a day.11 I then scooped out the soaked paper scraps and drained the water. I noticed that there were paper particles that did not stick with the larger lump and these were drained along with the water. I then crushed the leftover pulp and added new water. This cleaning procedure was conducted three times. I noticed that each time the water was drained, it became clearer (Fig. 1 and Fig. 2). A comparison between these two images shows that the water in which the pulp is soaked looks significantly different after the first and the second washes.

Second, I observed that the repetition of this process was not only for the sake of cleaning the pulp. While changing the water over a period of three days, I also conducted three rounds of “crushing.” The combination of extended crushing and soaking improved the smoothness of the pulp (Fig. 3 and Fig. 4). I realized that even though the author-practitioner did not specify the consistency required of crushed paper, his process of soaking it in new water not only prevented the paper from smelling bad, it also seemed to help dissolve the bonds between the fibers of the soaked paper, thereby creating a pulp of “fine” quality.

Once the paper is crushed and cleaned, the entry details how this finely-crushed pulp can be molded. Although the author-practitioner does not specify what kind of mold to use for this particular recipe, on fol. 12v in “Molding stucco promptly,” the author-practitioner gives instructions on making a mold out of ground brick and melted wax. He writes that this type of mold is suitable for casting “pestled paper.” It cannot be ascertained whether he would have used such a mold for the process on fol. 81r, as he simply notes there a hollow form. Studies on extant papier-mâché sculptures speculate about the use of plaster molds.12 I experimented with a wooden mold (Fig. 5) and a non-stick bundt pan (Fig. 6). In the next step, the author-practitioner advises putting a linen cloth over the pulp in this molded cavity and then rubbing it with a tooth. This process is reminiscent of burnishing, and its purpose is described as leading to a “very neat mold.” I chose Blick Belgian unbleached linen available in the Making and Knowing lab. Instead of a tooth, I used an agate burnisher (Fig. 7).

Although the rationale the author-practitioner provides for using the “tooth” was to create a “neat mold,” I noticed that “burnishing” the linen created pressure, causing excess water from the pulp to be released and soaked up by the linen (Fig. 8). This function of burnishing recalls a step in a seventeenth-century cartapesta (papier-mâché) recipe recorded by Filippo Baldinucci (1624–1697) in Vocabolario toscano dell'Arte del disegno (1681).13 He recommends using a sponge to remove the excess water from the pulp in the mold. Baldinucci’s recipe is considered one of the first technical explanations of cartapesta and is much more detailed than that of the author-practitioner. However, my reconstruction shows that, although the author-practitioner’s rationale for burnishing was to create a “neat mold,” this instruction has the added effect of removing excess water. Only the act of following his procedure makes this manifest.

The final step requires applying glue to the linen to adhere it to the cast. Object research reveals that swatches of linen or canvas were often used to reinforce papier-mâché structures, thereby mitigating their fragility.14 The author-practitioner only mentions using “strong glue” (colle forte), and I chose to use rabbit-skin glue (Fig. 9).15 Since he does not specify whether the glue should be applied to make the wet linen adhere to the wet pulp, or if both materials should dry first, I experimented with both processes. With the wet application, the glue initially did not seem to achieve adhesion. In two days, however, as both the linen and the papier-mâché dried, the glue worked by bringing the two surfaces together. With the dry linen and dry carton, an unexpected problem arose: the glue was absorbed by the carton. Therefore, I had to apply the glue predominantly to the linen surface to stick the two materials together. Figure 10 shows the final results (Fig. 10).

My reconstruction led me to two conflicting insights. On the one hand, this entry lacks much essential information, particularly concerning the tools required to make carton. There is mention of neither the implement with which to crush the paper (I used a mortar and pestle, as suggested by Baldinucci) nor the kind of mold to use (I tried both a wooden and metal mold). Either the author-practitioner assumes his reader knows the appropriate tools and will have them at hand, or he does not see such specificity as important for carrying out the process. On the other hand, this missing information is seemingly at odds with another feature of the entry: a consistent focus on the quality of materials and results. This focus begins with the author-practitioner’s call for Florentine paper. That he specifies Florentine paper appears to be significant, as France also produced paper by the late sixteenth century. This is evidenced by Richard Tottell’s 1585 complaint, when he attempted to establish a paper mill in England, that French paper merchants were buying all the rags and selling their paper at a loss.16 The call for “fine paper” from Florence thus marks a first emphasis on quality. Second, the water should be changed daily to prevent it from smelling bad, which presumably could indicate putrefaction. Third, the author-practitioner writes that rubbing the linen with a burnishing tool should allow it to mold “very neatly.” Lastly, he specifies using “strong” glue to support the mold when it is taken from the cavity. Taken together, these words such as “strong,” “neat,” and “fine” all show that he is concerned with controlling the carton’s capacity to mold cleanly and finely. To what end did the author-practitioner require such a high-quality carton?

Uses of Molded Paper in Early Modern Europe

Early modern molded paper is most widely preserved and studied today as a sculptural medium. From the fifteenth century onward, Italy appears to have been an important center for molded paper sculpture, and some of the leading workshops, including those of Donatello (ca. 1386–1466), Verrocchio (1435–1488), Sansovino (1486–1570), and Pietro Tacca (1577–1640), produced works in this comparatively humble medium. In the secondary literature, molded paper is uniformly called papier-mâché in English and French, or cartapesta in Italian, terms which the author-practitioner does not use. Art historians have speculated that in the face of a growing market for art objects and sculptures, papier-mâché could replace more expensive materials such as marble.17 Moreover, molded cartapesta sculptures could be produced serially and with relative economy; the molded paper and stucco Madonna and Child in Washington D.C.’s National Gallery, made in 1550 by sculptor Jacopo Sansovino, is one of numerous examples from the artist’s workshop.18 The use of papier-mâché in high-quality, complex works by celebrated sculptors militates against assumptions that the medium was used merely for minor or simple sculpture. Therefore, the use of carton in Ms. Fr. 640 cannot simply be taken as an indication of the author-practitioner’s interest in cheaper materials, whether for their own sake or for their ability to imitate more expensive ones.

Two factors recommend caution in concluding that the author-practitioner intended his molded paper for sculptural projects like those made in Italian workshops. First, extant pieces of cartapesta sculpture reveal that many of them are quite large and require additional support. A recent restoration of one example of a serially-produced crucifix by Pietro Tacca shows that the sculpture was cast in parts and is entirely supported by an internal wire armature.19 Similarly, Sansovino’s 120 x 98 cm Madonna and Child was mounted on a wooden support after being molded. The author-practitioner, by contrast, only describes a simple molding process with no mention of additional support beyond a mere canvas backing. Therefore, we might conclude the recipe was intended for smaller-scale objects. Second, some studies of cartapesta sculpture emphasize that glue was mixed with the pulp.20 Although the author-practitioner uses glue (colle forte), it is only to adhere the molded pulp to the linen. He does not incorporate it into the pulp mixture itself, and this raises questions about whether he intended to make larger-scale sculptures like those of his Italian contemporaries. It is worth noting here that Lalande’s Art du cartonnier also notes that crushing sized paper (i.e., paper with glue or “size” applied) to make carton for molding is advantageous because its glue would later help to give body to the paste.21

English-language primary sources and scholarship on papier-mâché also show its wide use in architectural ornaments, especially from the eighteenth century and later.22 In his article, “History, Technology and Conservation of Architectural Papier-Mâché,” Jonathan Thornton writes that molded papier-mâché reliefs were used on ceilings and walls to imitate plasterwork while being cheaper and lighter alternatives.23 In 1766, a bookseller and printer by the name of Henry Webley made a sales announcement aimed at “every individual concerned with building.”24 One of the books he listed for sale was entitled Forty-eight designs for corners, with the note that this book was useful for papier-mâché workers.

The use of paper pulp to mold domestic decorations is evidenced as early as 1594 by Hugh Plat (1552–1608) in The Jewell House of Art and Nature, conteining divers rare and profitable Inventions, together with sundry new Experiments in the Art of Husbandry, Distillation and Moulding. Plat, an English recipe collector of artisanal techniques, mentions several uses of paper. Of greatest relevance, in chapter 142, “The Art of Molding and Casting,” in a subsection entitled “special uses of this art,” Plat notes that one “may cast many rare and excellent patterns... with the pap of common paper, being well wrought and laboured by a workman.”25 He recommends such casts “to garnish beds, tables, court-cupboards, the jawms [jambs] and mantle trees of chimneys, and other stately furnitures of chambers or galleries,” noting that one may,“with smal cost, cast off whole borders for chambers or galleries.”26

Given the smaller dimensions and relatively simpler structures of architectural molding, it is possible that the author-practitioner might have aimed to use his carton to make such pieces. The linen support he recommends could be used to make the ornament more stable as it was embedded in or hung on different surfaces.

Most research on molded paper and papier-mâché architectural decoration is based on English sources and examples, so how did French sources use the word “papier-mâché” and “carton”? A search in ArtFl Frantext, a database of French texts, shows that in the eighteenth century, papier-mâché was specifically used in reference to figurines.27 The first use of the term in the database is recorded in a French novel Le Télémaque Travesti: Comprenant Les Treize Derniers Livres retrouvés et réimprimés pour la première fois (1736) by Pierre Carlet de Chamblain de Marivaux (1688–1763).28 The novel includes a dialogue between two characters called Omenée and Phocion,

my word, you look like papier-mâché, at the price of what you were then. The reason is this: that you have had troubles; but what fine wretchedness of wrinkles and staring eyes, when the heart is steadfast and good, and one has learnt that all men are scoundrels.29

By referring to a thin physical appearance as resembling papier-mâché, this passage suggests three things. First, that papier-mâché was common enough by 1736 to make it a recognizable point of reference. Second, it appears that papier-mâché could refer to sculptural or other designs made in the shape of human figures. Third, papier-mâché was known for its light color, thus making it a metaphoric stand-in for individuals of pale complexion. Other literary sources support this latter use. For instance, in 1784, Jacques-Henri Bernandin De Saint-Pierre’s (1737–1814) Études De La Nature (Studies of Nature) contains the passage,

Children compelled to such regimes as those of most of our doctors and apothecaries all have a face like papier-mâché, and when they grow up, they have their fathers’ pale complexions and ill-humoured temperaments.30

Here again, papier-mâché is associated with the appearance of those who are thin and sickly. By the late nineteenth century, “to have a papier mâché face” (“avoir une figure de papier mâché”) had become an idiom.31 These two instances show that early uses of papier-mâché, at least in literature, related mainly to its specific shape as figurines/dolls and that paleness was an important feature of papier-mâché’s characterization.

These literary insights are interesting, however, the earliest instances of the word “papier-mâché” date from the late seventeenth century.32 The word is absent from Randle Cotgrave’s 1611 Dictionarie of the French and English Tongues, a work which includes many other terms relevant to artisanal production. Up until now, this essay has followed other secondary sources in equating “papier-mâché” with the author-practitioner’s “carton.” Our definition today of papier-mâché as “three-dimensional objects composed of paper fiber, whether layered up in sheet form or cast as a beaten pulp,”33 accords well with the author-practitioner’s process for making carton, which involves paper beaten into a pulp and then molded. Despite this overlap, the term carton must also be situated within the author-practitioner’s temporal and geographical context of late sixteenth-century France.

What’s in a Word? Etymology and Terminology

Cotgrave’s translation of the French term carton provides the following description: “thicke paper on where painters draw sometimes and that whereof some fannes are made, also a square piece thereof pastboard; (also a carter, or carre-man).”34 Both the entry on fol. 81r and Cotgrave’s definition imply that thickness of paper is an important feature of carton. Yet Cotgrave emphasizes a certain solidity or rigidity (“thicke paper” on which painters could draw) absent from the author-practitioner’s entries. So, although fol. 81r explains that carton is formed by molding the pulp in a hollow form, it is possible that the use of the term carton carries a more expansive definition than implied by the more recent term “papier-mâché.” In other words, we must ask: how was the term carton understood by the author-practitioner? In the following section I address this question in two ways: first I analyze the author-practitioner’s use of the word carton in six other entries in the manuscript, and second, I examine its use in wider contemporaneous French literature.

Writing and Painting Surface

A comparison of these six entries from Ms. Fr. 640 shows the multiple uses of carton. The first use of carton is consistent with Cotgrave’s definition, i.e., as a thick paper surface on which to write, draw, or paint. This is evident in three entries: fol. 57v “Painter,” fol. 68r “Fanciful tables,” and fol. 142v “Molding grasshoppers and things too thin.” The use of carton as a painting surface finds resonance in a 1689 play L'intrigue des filous by French playwright and poet Claude de L’Estoile (1602-1652),

Must you rail so against your fortune,

For a little carton smeared with paint?

Or perhaps Florinde is ugly in crimson?35

In spite of its mocking tone, this passage supports the conclusion gleaned from fol. 81r—that carton was used as a surface to draw or paint on, likely due to its thickness, rigidity, and perhaps even portability. Further, on fol. 57v, the language of “images made of carton” that could be coated with resin to resist wet weather implies that carton was portable and could be taken or used outside. This suggests that carton could be an appropriate support for painted images used for outdoor public festivals or royal entries, for instance.

A Composite Material in Furniture

While functioning as a painting surface, the carton in the entry on fol. 68r, “Fanciful tables,” is also part of a larger decorative object. Interestingly, Louis le Comte (1655–1728), a Jesuit who went to China, included a letter in Nouveaux mémoires sur l'état présent de la Chine (1696) that shows the use of carton in making Chinese lacquer tables: a

For tables and ordinary chairs, one is content with applying two or three coats of varnish, which leaves it so transparent that one can see the grain of the wood through it... But those who wish to make something finished paste onto the wood a kind of carton made of paper, flax, chalk, and some other well-beaten materials, in which the varnish incorporates itself. With this they make a perfectly uniform and solid ground, on which the varnish is applied little by little in thin coats, which one leaves to dry one after the other.36

In this passage, Le Comte describes carton made from paper beaten together with flax, chalk and other unspecified materials, which is then applied to the structure. Finally, varnish layers are added on top. As in “Fanciful Tables on fol. 68r, carton is incorporated into a seemingly more durable object made from other materials such as wood.

Molding and Imprinting

“Founders of small works of tin” (fol. 80v), “Excellent sand for lead, tin, and copper” (fol. 86v-87r), and “Molding grasshoppers and things too thin” (fol. 142v) all show carton used as a cheaper supplementary workshop material in molding. Although carton is not part of the final product in these entries, it is used as part of an intermediary step in the making process.37 For instance, in the entry “Founders of small works of tin,” carton is used to take imprints from stone reliefs, whereas in “Excellent sand for lead, tin, and copper,” a temporary carton mixture ensures that cast objects fit well within the mold, and in “Molding grasshoppers and things too thin,” the raised letters painted onto the carton form a relief from which an impression can be taken.

Material Affordances

While these entries show the varied uses of carton, they also illustrate the author-practitioner’s familiarity with both the constraints and affordances of the material. First, he is aware that paper interacts with the surrounding environment through humidity and moisture. This raises for him concerns of durability and conservation. On fol. 57v, in the entry “Painter,” he specifically cautions that if images on carton are not soaked in resin, the carton will turn limp in wet weather. Since carton is made by manipulating paper’s ability to absorb water, the author-practitioner is sensitive to the effect that humidity and dryness can have on the shape and integrity of the image painted on carton. Similarly, on fol. 142v “Molding grasshoppers and things too thin,” he writes that moisture of sand and clay plate will undo glue writings on carton, again bringing attention to the impact of moisture on the material. This understanding of how carton’s shape is affected by the moisture of both the external environment and adjacent materials in larger making processes leads the author-practitioner to suggest alternative materials to mitigate the impact. For example, instead of using glue to write on carton, he recommends cinnabar mixed with oil in a marginal note on 142v. This recalls Tim Ingold’s statement that “when human beings make things, they work within a world rather than acting upon a material world from outside.”38

If carton’s vulnerability to moisture can be understood as a constraining factor that concerned the author-practitioner, carton’s versatility in its paste form shows why it was used as a supporting material in his other entries. I refer to the author-practitioner’s concern to control the thickness of carton—a kind of manipulation the material specifically affords. In “Founders of small works of tin” (fol. 80v), he specifies that carton as “thick as one finger” can be used to take impressions of stone reliefs. In “Excellent sand for lead, tin, and copper ” (fol. 87v), when he cautions to ensure that cast objects line up directly with their molds, he suggests using carton to adjust for any discrepancy. He advises experimenting on the depth of the mold first with carton, and he ultimately writes, “of whatever thickness you please.” These instances show the degree of flexibility and versatility that carton could provide. In other words, carton’s ability to be formed into varying thicknesses and its malleability makes it a useful and versatile workshop material. Therefore, while carton’s vulnerability to water damage limits its use, the flexibility it allows in terms of thickness, malleability, and versatility makes it an important part of the processes of artisanal making.

Conclusion

This essay initially sought to understand how the author-practitioner intended to use his carton recipe on fol. 81r. Due to the striking similarity between his description of carton and our understanding of papier-mâché, it was assumed that these were synonymous. After conducting a reconstruction and a literature review centered on papier-mâché and its uses, a more interesting question emerged. What did the word carton mean to the author-practitioner? Was there more to this word than our initial translation of it as “papier-mâché” implied, especially given that the latter term only emerged in the late seventeenth century? By studying the author-practitioner’s own use of the term carton as well as its use in contemporaneous French writings, this essay shows that the word carton could designate molded so-called papier-mâché objects, but the definition of carton was clearly more expansive than this. It designated a versatile, paper-based material with uses in casting, furniture making, painting, and draftsmanship, among others. Thus, the author-practitioner’s description and use of carton shows his experience of paper’s material properties, and alerts us to a broader understanding of the term, one that is not always conveyed by historians’ use of the later term “papier-mâché.”

Vannoccio Biringuccio gives a purportedly first-hand description of Giambattista Pelori’s method for making reproductive molds for sculptures: the process begins by coating the sculpture to be reproduced in mashed paper, “just as masks are made.” This initial layer is then coated with several layers of a paste composed of flour and hide scrapings, then the whole is covered with linen scraps. After drying, this layer, it is cut off the statue, forming a mold form. This mold is rendered impermeable with Grecian pitch and wax, and is then ready for plaster to be poured into it to create multiple reproductions. Biringuccio notes that “these moulds were made with little expense; they were strong and unbreakable, light and easily carried to suitable places; surely in my opinion, he had a fine discovery.” Vannoccio Biringuccio, Pirotechnia, 1540, ed. and trans.
