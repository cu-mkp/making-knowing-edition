Abstract

Ms. Fr. 640 contains an entry on fol. 43v for “Varied and Transmuted Wine.” This recipe contains no wine, but transmutes red “wine” into white. Is this entry a test for detecting adulteration in wines? Or is it a deceptive trick?

Wine in Ms. Fr. 640

The recipe for “varied and transmuted wine” on fol. 43v describes a means to “make wine as claret colored as you like,” then change its color to white. What is the purpose of this “varied and transmuted” wine, that “can be drunk without danger”?

Wine is a frequent ingredient in Ms. Fr. 640 entries, and is used in varnishes, in pigments, as a binder in sand molds, in making vinegar, ubiquitously in medicinal cures, “for fortifying a harried horse” (fol. 71r), as an alternative to soapy water or urine for cleaning panel paintings (fol. 60v), and for many other uses. Besides “varied and transmuted wine,” there is one other recipe for producing an imitation of wine: “A drink having a taste of wine,” on fol. 47v, which specifies to “Take tartar from large vessels & soak it in water & it will have the taste of wine.” On fol. 33v, a practical joke “For making blood or wine issue from someone’s forehead or from a wall,” recommends using “wine or liquid rosette of Brazilwood or black cherry juice” to imitate blood.

Reconstruction

The recipe on fol. 43v calls for brazilwood (bresil), clear water, and a drop of lemon or orange juice:

Varied and transmuted wine

Grate brazilwood very finely, put it to soak one or two hours in clear water, then take this tinted water & add to it some clear water & you will make e wine as claret colored as you like. If you please, put a drop of lemon or orange juice in it & it will immediately turn white. It can be drunk without danger.

The brazilwood available in the Making and Knowing Lab had been donated to the project by the conservation lab of the Metropolitan Museum in New York (which had received it from B. Barette in January 2000). This brazilwood was already in a very finely grated state, and we did not grind it further (Fig. 1, Fig. 2).1 We soaked 40 ml (by volume) of the brazilwood in 200 ml of tap water for two hours (Fig. 3). The filtrate was odorless and had a clear, dark orange color (Fig. 4 Fig.5).

We divided the dark orange liquid into three beakers, filled to 55 ml each (Fig. 6). We tested one with orange juice, one with lemon juice, and the last one we diluted with water. We added the lemon and orange juice drop by drop, until we noticed a change in color in each beaker. The total amount of citrus juice added to these beakers did not exceed 2 ml. The sample tested with lemon juice rapidly turned a light-yellow color, while the sample tested with orange juice turned a clearer and lighter shade of orange (Fig. 7). We then added tap water to the last beaker and noticed a very slight change in color once we had added about 15 ml of water, the orange tint had gotten lighter (Fig. 8). We then proceeded to add more lemon and orange juice to each beaker respectively until no further color changes were visible, and both appeared to be similar hues of yellow (Fig. 9). It was only upon the addition of 350 ml of tap water that the colored water changed to a red-pink tint (Fig. 10). We gradually added the water in units of 10 ml until no further changes in color could be discerned. The color stabilized at 550 ml.

We then took two 40 ml samples of the red-tinted water (which in these smaller amounts appeared an even softer pink color) to which we added orange and lemon juice. The addition of a few drops of orange and lemon juice to two different samples produced a further change in color to white (Fig. 11). After adding three to four drops of lemon juice to the first sample, we noticed a rapid change in color, and the sample turned a very light yellow, the color of white wine. We then added the same number of drops of orange juice to the second sample, which also became a light-yellow/white wine color, but much more slowly. The colors did not change or deepen with the addition of more lemon or orange juice.

“Can be drunk without danger”

Although we did not taste the “wine” we produced, we realized that the author-practitioner’s statement that his “varied and transmuted” wine “can be drunk without danger” might provide a clue to the recipe’s purpose. Adulterated wines are a frequent problem in wine making. Ágoston Haraszthy’s 1862 Grape culture, wines and winemaking devotes 200 pages to the problem of adulterated wines, and includes tests for adulteration. Ure’s Dictionary of Arts, Manufactures and Mines (1878) includes methods specifically to determine if red wines are adulterated or diluted with brazilwood. Both these books emphasize that wines adulterated with brazilwood could be drunk without danger.2 Could fol. 43v form a test for detecting adulterated wine? Prohibitions against the adulteration of wines date to at least the fifteenth century in France and Germany,3 and the adulteration of beer and wine became a crime punishable by death in Scotland at about the same time.4 Our cursory search for sixteenth-century descriptions of tests for wine adulteration was unsuccessful.

Instead, we performed a simple test: we added different amounts (10, 20, and 30 ml) of the same mixture of brazilwood and water we had used for our original experiment to 40 ml of red wine in three beakers. While the red wine gave off a more fruity aroma upon the addition of the colored brazilwood water, we observed no significant color changes upon adding the lemon juice (Fig. 12). More testing is needed to draw definitive conclusions from this experiment, however, the addition of brazilwood and water to wine is already visible to an observant naked eye, and does not require the lemon juice test to be perceived. While the addition of brazilwood, logwood, cochineal, and other red pigments to red wine appears to have been a problem in the seventeenth through nineteenth centuries, we concluded that it was unlikely the author-practitioner’s aim was to adulterate, or to detect adulteration in, wine; there is nothing in the language of the recipe on fol. 43v to suggest that the “wine” made from brazilwood was intended to deceive or to stand in for the real thing.

Practical Jokes

Instead, the recipe on fol. 43v is more closely connected to a section in Ms. Fr. 640 on sleight of hand tricks that runs from fols. 33r–43v. These tricks and jokes are often accompanied by illustrations, and contain interesting directions and meta-commentary on the tricks, for example, fol. 33r states that “The conjuror ought not to amuse himself by watching what he is doing, but look at the spectators and give them plenty of good hocus-pocus words so that they look at his face & not at his hands.” Similarly, on fol. 34v, the entry “For melting or transmuting a jewel put inside a box” suggests that while executing the trick, and “making the requisite gestures,” the performer will chant the nonsense magic formula “inhonorificabilitidinitadtudinibus.”

Why did the author-practitioner include these tricks and jokes in his practical text?5 Most texts that collected jokes and jests in the fifteenth to eighteenth centuries (published in English under the name of “jestbooks”) did not include instructions for conjuring tricks, but rather, songs and letters, wordplay, riddles, banter, dinner-table conversations, and pithy sayings. Popular trickster tales that recount the exploits of figures such as Scoggin or Robin Goodfellow,6 while not meant to be “recipes” in the fashion of Ms. Fr. 640, do provide specific instances of trickery or artifice.

Another possible explanation is suggested by Paula Findlen who sees jokes as integral to the investigation of nature in the early modern period,7 meaning that the author-practitioner might be seeking to demonstrate his “ability to match nature’s complexity with his own artifice”8 in such tricks.

Conclusion

The recipe “Varied and transmuted wine” is clearly meant to surprise, if not to deceive. The Making and Knowing lab was surprised and charmed by this simple trick, which, we imagine, was the author-practitioner’s precise aim (Vid. 1). Like so many other entries in Ms. Fr. 640, then, its goal was to create an impressive visual effect through artifice—an aim of all “art,” and one that is not so distant from sleight of hand.
