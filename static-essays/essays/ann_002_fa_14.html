Molding Hollow on One Side and in Relief on the Other

By , DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/spcq-m286
← Back to Essays

Abstract

This essay explains and reconstructs the method of casting medals as described on fols. 83v and 92r. This type of medal, which is cast with a convex relief on the obverse and an exact concave negative of the relief on the reverse, was widespread throughout Europe by the late sixteenth century. Modern scholars frequently refer to these medals as “without reverse,” “hollow cast,” “incuse-reverse cast,” or “uniface.” Little is known about the methods of their production. Ms. Fr. 640 presents at least one entry describing a previously unknown method for casting this type of medal. This essay sheds new light on their facture, and discusses their broader significance within Ms. Fr. 640 and in other contemporary sources.

On fol. 92r of BnF Ms. Fr. 640, the recipe “Molding hollow on one side and in relief on the other” gives advice on how to cast a thin medal with a convex relief on the obverse and an exact concave negative of the relief image on the reverse (Fig. 1, Fig. 2, Fig. 3, Fig. 4). Modern scholars frequently refer to these medals as “without reverse,” “hollow cast,” “incuse-reverse cast,” or “uniface.”1 The diverse places such medals originated suggest that the manufacture of these objects was a well-established practice throughout Europe by the late sixteenth century, however, little is known about their purpose, reception, and the methods by which they were produced. They are sometimes held to be incomplete because they often lack mottoes and emblems on the reverse, or they are thought to have had more practical uses than simply as decorative or collectible objects, and are sometimes even seen as potential molds, whereby the reverse could be used as a matrix.2 While Ms. Fr. 640 offers little insight into the purpose or display of these medals, it provides a unique opportunity to explore the production methods of this type of object. Where the few early modern sources treating medal casting seem hardly to mention these variants—perhaps one of the reasons that such medals have often been regarded as nineteenth-century electrotypes—the manuscript offers a number of recipes concerning their manufacture, illuminating previously undocumented aspects of their fabrication.3

The purpose of this essay is twofold: first, to explain the method of what we will henceforth call the “hollow-relief” medal casting as described on fol. 92r, and, second, to examine it both within the context of other medal casting processes described in Ms. Fr. 640, and in other contemporary sources. Ms. Fr. 640 contains several recipes that can be connected with the hollow-relief process on fol. 92r, either in the technique, the outcome of the process, or the terminology used.4 That said, the terminology used in Ms. Fr. 640 to indicate or describe hollow-relief medals and their manufacture is not wholly consistent, and it overlaps with related processes such as making hollow molds and casting with a core. Our investigation of the broader context of the technique described on fol. 92r included material evidence found in extant medals, as well as a search for other early modern written accounts of this process. Although neither our experiments nor our research on the context of this recipe have been exhaustive, interesting findings and conclusions can still be drawn.

The Recipe

As in many entries in Ms. Fr. 640, the one on 92r goes into detail regarding certain aspects of the process it describes, without explaining the entire method from beginning to end:

Molding hollow on one side and in relief on the other

And for this effect, one casts a medal of fine tin, which is harder to melt. And since it is neat, one molds with it. And one leaves it in one half of the frame & presses it a little so that it holds better. And next, you will cast in your frame some solder described above, or another more meltable than fine tin. And thus, the second medal will melt & mold itself on the first one without spoiling it. But to make sure this is done right, mix some lamp black with water &, with a paintbrush, give a light coat of this to the medal, which remains in the frame, & leave to dry. Thus it will not melt.

But if you have a medal of copper or silver, you can leave it in the cast if you want to cast with lead or tin. But it is necessary that it be a little hot, for the cold would make the tin contract.

Tin wants to be cast quite hot to come out neat.

Soft tin, which is the best for the cast, is the one that, once cast in grille, is burnished & shiny & polished like a mirror, & appears to have been burnished. And does not have holes like the one that is brittle & that is not shiny as if burnished (fol. 92r).

The focus here is on making sure that the original model, i.e., the medal that is to be reproduced, is suitable for the process. The author-practitioner touches upon its material, use, and treatment, and suggests starting with a medal made from pure tin (estain fin)5 that is neat, or, in other words, containing sharp enough detail to be used as a pattern. The metal used for casting is called souldure (solder), as “described above” in “Mixture easy to melt” (also fol. 92r), that is of a lower melting point than pure tin. In a second paragraph he notes that “if you have a medal of copper or silver, you can leave it in the cast, if you want to cast with lead or tin.” The author-practitioner’s point in discussing these different types of metals is that, not only must the original medal create a sharp impression (i.e., “neat”) when used as the pattern, but that it must also be made of a metal with a higher melting point than the metal used for casting.6

With regard to the method of casting, the recipe provides the following clues: “And one leaves it in one half of the frame, & presses it a little so that it holds better. And next, you will cast in your frame some solder described above, or another more meltable than fine tin. And thus, the second medal will melt & mold itself on the first one without spoiling it.” Further on, he reiterates, “you can leave it in the cast.” Thus, the original medal is not only used to imprint the mold, but it actually becomes part of the mold. This explains the importance of the different melting points of the original medal and the metal used for casting.

Another important factor to the caster was the protection of the original medal. If the medal was deemed worthy of reproducing, it can be assumed that it would be preferable not to damage it during the reproduction process. Keeping it intact would also offer the possibility for continuous reproduction. The higher melting point of the metal used for the original prevents it from melting during the process. And the author-practitioner offers more advice for further protection: “mix some lamp black with water, &, with a paintbrush, give a light coat of this to the medal, which remains in the frame, & leave to dry. Thus it will not melt.” The layer of lamp black appears to function not only as a separator but also as a protective layer for the surface of the original medal, which was to be left in the mold during the casting.

Experiments

After reading this recipe closely, it became clear to us that the original medal was to be used as a part of the mold, as explained above, but it was not at all clear what exactly the technique was. We hypothesized that it would only make sense to leave the medal in the upper, or male half of the flask, after making a negative imprint with it in the bottom, or female half. Pressing the obverse (the front) of the original medal all the way into the female half and pressing the reverse of the original firmly into the male half would ensure sufficient space between them for the molten metal to enter. Variations on how deeply the medal would be pressed into the sand on either side would allow for results of various thicknesses.

Patricia Tuttle proposes a model for the manufacture of hollow-relief medals in which the original medal is first used to make an impression in the sand in the female flask and is then taken out (Fig. 5, Fig. 6). After drying this female half of the mold (Fig. 7), the male flask is filled by fitting it over the female flask (Fig. 8). After the application of a separating agent between the male and female halves, such as fine ash, it is filled with sand, thus creating a positive imprint of the same medal. Again, this half is also left to dry. To create a thin cavity between the two halves into which the molten metal could be poured, Tuttle suggests the use of a shim whose thickness would be that of the desired medal (Fig. 9). This shim would be fastened to the outer edges of the male half, and filled with the molding sand surrounding the positive imprint (Fig. 10). After cutting the pouring channel and vent(s), the two halves would be joined and the medal could be cast.7

We decided to experiment with both methods: the Tuttle method and the one in Ms. Fr. 640 on fol. 92r. We wished to test Tuttle’s proposed method to see if it actually worked and what difficulties might arise during the process. Second, we wanted to see if our hypothetical method, based on fol. 92r—in which we would leave the original in the male half of the mold—would be workable and what disadvantages or advantages this method might have (Vid. 1).

For all experiments we used the following materials: a brass medal as the pattern, wooden casting flasks, sand, ground charcoal dust as a separating agent, a metal ruler to level the flasks filled with sand, a knife to cut gates into the sand, tin, a cast-iron melting ladle in which to melt the tin, a propane torch to melt the tin, and clamps to hold together the two halves of the mold. The most difficult of these materials to obtain was the sand. Without significant casting experience, how were we to decide which sand to use? The Delft clay used in a previous experiment seemed inauthentic because it contained oil as its binder,8 and so we searched for a so-called “green sand,” bound with water, that was used for casting by foundries. We wanted to use a modern sand rather than make our own based on a recipe from Ms. Fr. 640, because we hoped that this would remove some variables in the experiments (the focus of which was to be on the process rather than on the materials) and would provide a stable and effective medium for casting. We decided upon a mixture of green sand containing 10% clay (bentonite) mixed with 4% water (Fig. 11, Fig. 12).9

Our choice of sand, however, turned out to negatively affect our casts. It was crumbly and very brittle, even more so after we dried it, with many loose grains of sand (Fig. 13, Fig. 14). Possible improvements might include altering the ratio of sand-clay-water, pushing the sand through a sieve or cloth to ensure the use of only the finest grains, and mixing the mixture longer or more thoroughly.10 Or, as other recipes from Ms. Fr. 640 suggest, perhaps we had let the molds dry too much or too quickly:

One can find an excellent one on the little hill of Puy David near Thoulouse for lead, tin, & copper. It does not want to be too much reheated because, drying out all at once, it loses its bond & burns at the first arrival of metal, which makes the work lumpy & not neat.11

Or, perhaps we had used the wrong sand altogether for casting tin. The author-practitioner makes clear that the right sand is of utmost importance:

I believe that the secret to cast well lies in finding a sand that receives the metal well, one for lead, the other for another, for each one has its particular one. Let it be molded slowly & carefully, and leave it for a few days to become compact by itself, if you have the time for this. And next, reheat it very well, not all at once nor over with a large fire, but little by little, otherwise it crumbles & always has some fault (fol. 86r).

Patricia Tuttle’s Proposed Method

Despite the careful text explaining her proposed method and its accompanying illustrations, getting a good grasp of Tuttle’s method with all its steps proved challenging. Although we understood the basic idea, we failed to see exactly how Tuttle proposed to make the shim, so we came up with our own solution:12 We used a thin sheet of wood with the shape of the medal cut out (Fig. 15, Fig. 16), instead of Tuttle’s thin third layer of sand. This functioned similarly to her proposal, since both methods involved producing one side of the mold (male) from the negative imprint in the other half (female).13 Making our wooden shim to fit perfectly was the most challenging part of this experiment and proved relatively time-consuming and labor-intensive (Fig. 17, Fig. 18). A shim filled with sand, as proposed by Tuttle, may be easier to achieve, although the challenge there might be to leave the edges of the positive imprint uncovered while leveling this thin third layer of sand.14 We concluded that the method might work if executed with more practice. More experience with the casting process and the use of a different type of sand would most likely have resulted in a more desirable outcome (Fig. 19, Fig. 20). We attribute the coarseness of our medal cast to our inexperience, as well as to the unsuitable, crumbly sand we prepared, rather than to Tuttle’s suggested method. More experimentation using different sands and different shims is necessary for a definitive conclusion.

The Method of Ms. Fr. 640, fol. 92r

We had made the molds for our first experiment with our hypothesized method before conducting the experiments with the Tuttle method, and so we used the same unsuitable sand. Nevertheless, following the method outlined in Ms. Fr. 640 worked relatively well. We first pressed the medal into the sand-filled female half until its flat reverse was level with the sand (Fig. 21). Following this, we could immediately move on to making the male half of the flask, without having to wait for the female half to dry. To ensure perfect alignment, we put the sand-filled male flask on top of the female flask (with a layer of lamp black in between as a separator), while the latter still contained the original medal (Fig. 22). We then kept the two halves together (Fig. 23) and turned them upside-down, before removing the female half (now on top) with a little tap, which left the original medal in position on the male half, which was aligned with its impression on the female half (Fig. 24). Now the original medal just needed to be firmly pressed into the sand of the male half and remain there during the rest of the casting process (Fig. 25, Fig. 26). See Vid. 1.

The obverse of our cast medal during this first experiment with the method in Ms. Fr. 640 was, as in the Tuttle experiment, coarse (Fig. 27, Fig. 28). The reverse, on the other hand, was smooth and highly detailed: a perfect negative of the brass original that had formed the pattern (Fig. 29). We concluded that using the original medal as part of the mold could result in a highly detailed reverse. The method, as we envisioned it based on fol. 92r, worked. The coarseness of the obverse seems to be a direct result of the coarseness of the sand. Before we poured the metal, we also noted that the sand had crumbled on the edges of the imprints in both halves of the flask. This most likely caused the molten tin to extend beyond the edge of the original medal, enclosing it and making it difficult to separate.

Because of the disappointing quality of the sand, we decided to do another experiment with the manuscript’s method using the green casting sand known as Delft clay (a “sand” with an oil binder). We made smaller flasks out of mason jar caps (Fig. 30, Fig. 31) and poured from above, directly into the sand, with the medal horizontal instead of almost vertical (Fig. 32). We also chose to push the medal only halfway into the male flask, in order to experiment with the thickness of the cast medal. Although at first the molten tin did not seem to have entered the mold at all, the result was surprisingly good: a tin medal with a clear image on obverse and reverse. The main drawback was that the tin failed to flow into the entire cavity (Fig. 33, Fig. 34, Fig. 35, Fig. 36, Fig. 37). This may be improved through alterations such as tilting the mold at an angle as the metal is poured, heating the tin to a higher temperature before pouring, or slightly increasing the diameter of the negative imprint—which would also require more filing to be done afterwards (Fig. 38, Fig. 39). Compared to the Tuttle method, the manuscript’s method has the advantage of being less time-consuming and labor-intensive: both halves of the mold can be made simultaneously, and the additional step of making and drying a shim can be skipped altogether.15

Michaela Groeneveld and Marianne Nuij, students in the University of Amsterdam Metals Conservation programme, also experimented with this process, and, after many trials, were able to produce a successful hollow-relief medal (Fig. 40).16

Related Recipes and Terms

Another recipe from Ms. Fr. 640 describes a similar method for making medals, which includes leaving the original in the frame mold during the entire casting process. The entry on fol. 83v is is similarly entitled, “Molding in relief on one part and hollow on the other” (“Mouler de relief dune part et creux de laultre”):

It is necessary to cast two pieces of copper, latten or similar metal, very neatly, and then when you have molded the hollow form [le cave] in the sand, leave the figure of metal that you have molded in the frame, without moving it from its place. And cast, & if there is not enough thickness press a little & push the figure down in the frame....When you mold, always leave your medal in one of the frames, is without stirring it.

Interestingly, this recipe uses two different words for “hollow:” creux and cave. While the former is here used for the concave reverse side of the final product (the medal), the latter is used to indicate the cavity, or negative impression made into one half of the mold. Although not entirely consistent, it seems that this distinction can be made more generally. In all of the five recipes where we found the use of creux in Ms. Fr. 640, the word indicates the hollow part of a cast object, i.e., either the hollow reverse of a medal or the hollow or cavity on the inside of a more complex object, such as an animal.17 Cave, on the other hand, is used to denote either object or mold. That is, it is used to refer to the hollow part of a piece of equipment used in the process, not the end product. This research helped Making and Knowing editorial team members finally translate cave as “hollow form.”18

Another technique described in Ms. Fr. 640 to create hollow-relief medals is found on fols. 130v, “For molding thin,” and on 153r–153v, “Giving the thickness that you wish to a medal,” which both use a “paste.” On fol. 130v, a lasagne de paste is applied onto the impression of the original in the hollow mold:

Next, make a small lasagna of paste of such thickness as you would like, and having anointed with butter your the hollow of your first hollow mold, adapt the paste to it and then make your second mold on top.

Although this entry does not refer to medals (or any type of object in particular), this method could certainly be used to that end. The entry on fol. 153r–v uses an original medal to make the female (hollow) mold half, but rather than pressing that medal into the male half, it uses a paste (as in fol. 130v) to create a space between the two parts of the mold, on top of which the male half of the mold is made.19 Then, after separating the two halves to remove the paste before joining them again, the hollow-relief medal can be cast, and will have the same thickness as the paste.20 Some of the steps are spelled out clearly, such as the thickness of the paste; the author-practitioner even includes a small illustration specifying the tool to make and use for rolling out the paste evenly to the desired thickness (Fig. 41). But, many of these steps are not spelled out literally but are implied. Our conjecture here is based on the knowledge we gained from comparing recipes and from our hands-on experience.21

However, the concave (or “hollow”) reverse of the products resulting from these paste methods are unlikely to have displayed the same level of detail as the relief on the obverse, depending on the qualities of the paste and the amount of pressure that could be exerted pressing the paste into the cave without affecting the mold. Patricia Tuttle’s observation that some of the medals she examined displayed traces of a cloth fabric:

others must have had wax pressed into a mold using a piece of cloth, possibly as a separator, which left behind its fabric pattern in the metal surface. These molds were probably made of plaster, reinforced plaster, or sulfur, as the sand molds discussed above would be too delicate to withstand the pressure of pressing semi-hard wax against their surface.22

The recipe on fol. 153r–153v does indeed mention that the mold material “sets,” thus indicating a plaster mix, and recommends strengthening it with “crocum or well-ground iron scales to firm it up.” The fabric pattern may be the result of other processes, however, as suggested by fol. 153r–v, where the author-practitioner writes, “press on it [the paste] with some cotton & your figure will imprint itself in the paste.”

This paste method corresponds almost exactly to a technique described by Benvenuto Cellini, in “On Cardinals’ Seals.”23 He explains that he started with a relief modeled in wax on a polished black stone, of which he made a negative cast in plaster. He proceeds to describe how to press this plaster form into one half of a sand mold. “This done,” he continues,

dry well that portion of the mould where the figures come …, then have ready a little dough [pasta di pane crudo] in the form of a cake similar in shape & thickness to what your silver or metal seal is finally to be, and put this over the figures formed by the gesso and which will appear in relief, having previously smoked over the mould with a little candle-smoke. This done, take the second box, fill it with the same moist earth and when dried set it upon the first. Mind in so doing that you do not disturb the part already dried where the figures are.… Then open the mould, after taking out the dough-cake, make the mouths and the two vent holes, beginning at the bottom and going up as high as the mouth or ingress hole. When both parts are dry, smoke them over with a little candle-smoke and let them cool, have your silver well molten and then pour it in.24

In addition to this contemporary Italian source, a slightly later Dutch source exists: a manuscript sent to the diplomat Constantijn Huygens by Willem Dedel (dated 1629) contains some recipes for casting medals.25 One of these recipes is titled “To cast thickly a medal that is too thin” (Om een medaille die te dunne is dick te gieten). The technique described here is similar to that of the paste method, but instead of using a paste of flour, the author-practitioner suggests using (several layers of) paper.26 This same treatise contains a recipe “To cast hollow a medal in relief that has a flat back” [Om een verheven medaelle die achter plat [is], hol te gieten].27 This recipe has similarities to both the method described on fol. 92r and the paste method on fol. 153r–v, but it mentions neither leaving in the original medal, nor applying a paste. How the thin cavity between the two mold halves is achieved is unclear, but perhaps this is related to the extra step of breaking the first female half of the mold and remaking it.28

Conclusion

To our original question of whether Ms. Fr. 640 provides a practicable method for the casting of hollow-relief medals, the answer is a definite yes. In fact, it suggests multiple techniques toward this end. The multiplicity of techniques and materials used to create medals of this sort, as well as for a variety of other types of medals both in Ms. Fr. 640 and in other contemporary sources, suggests that early modern craftsmen used whatever materials were available: original patterns, dough, paper, sand, and plaster, all of them serving similar ends. Artisans throughout Europe are likely to have known about (and experimented with) the various qualities and properties of the materials and substances available to them, and deduced how to employ them for their purposes.

Given that the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 provides various methods to obtain similar results, and, given the difference in reverses and their level of detail in early modern hollow-relief medals examined by Tuttle, it seems likely that there was not one way of doing things, although there may have been personal preferences. As Cellini recommends when discussing two methods for making a cardinal’s seal: “Do not, however, fail to try both, because it is good for you to learn them, & you will find them very helpful to you in many ways in other branches of the goldsmith’s art.”29

Patricia Tuttle noted common features of many hollow-relief medals: First, the reverse, she says, is a highly detailed negative of the obverse. This could definitely be explained by the Ms. Fr. 640 method on fol. 92r, in which the original medal remains a part of the mold. Second, there is often evidence of filing on extant medals, usually around the perimeter, that may suggest increasing the diameter of the imprint on the female half (which would also help the molten metal to spread through the entire impression). Third, she comments that the reverse is generally coarse as compared to the obverse. This, we think, would indicate the use of a different method than that explained on fol. 92r, where the use of the original as part of the male mold actually seemed to result in the opposite: namely, the reverse being sharper than the obverse.30 Following Tuttle, it may be interesting to investigate to what degree a separating agent might affect this.

The goal of making a thin and even casting is intended to decrease the likelihood of casting flaws. On this point, on fol. 102v, “Casting,” the author-practitioner notes that

Tin comes out better being thin & fine rather than thick, because being thick & in great heat, it retracts. Therefore, if you want to mold a thick piece in tin, mold it only on one side & with a cavity on one side, if it is possible, in that way you will have it more neat, and then you will be able to solder two halves together.

This technical advantage, as Tuttle also suggests, may be one compelling reason for the appeal, manufacture, and purpose of such hollow-relief cast medals.31

Finally, to better understand the techniques used to make hollow-relief medals, more experimentation is necessary, as well as more research in early modern sources, searching both for related recipes and for vocabulary used. Perhaps most fruitful, however, would be to examine extant medals for traces of their facture in light of the new information presented in this essay.

In 2016 and 2017, subsequent to completion of this essay, the Making and Knowing Team examined hollow-relief medals from the Scher Collection in the Frick Collection. We observed on the sixteenth- and seventeenth-century medals the traces of a remarkable variety of techniques (also observed by Patricia Tuttle in her research). No firm conclusions about the exact techniques used could be drawn without scientific analysis (such as photogrammetry), but these traces indicated that the materials and techniques in Ms. Fr. 640 for creating hollow-relief medals seem to have been employed in the production of extant medals.32

After careful study, however, we concluded this was not the case. The author-practitioner fills the female half of the box mold with sand, covering the medal (although in a note he says that he first filled the mold with sand and then pressed the medal into it). Then, turning this half of the box mold around so that the medal faces upwards, he draws a line on the back of the medal (near the edge [bord]) that continues onto the sand, to indicate where to make the pouring gate [pour denoter la place pour faire le gect]. Without removing the medal from the female half, he then adds a layer of charcoal onto the still uncovered side [contour] of the medal, adds the male half of the flask on top of the female half, and fills it with sand. Only after both halves of the mold are made does he remove the medal. Thus, this would make an exact replica of the original medal, rather than a hollow-relief version.↩︎
