Within the Workshop Walls and Beyond: Environments of Artisanal Experiment and Inquiry

Tianna Helena Uchacz

This essay begins from a premise: the places we live and work—what we keep in them, what we do in them, and how we describe them—can tell us something important about our professional lives, communities, and modes of understanding the world.1 This may seem an unusual point of entry into BnF Ms. Fr. 640 and the world of its author-practitioner, who rarely describes the spaces he works in or moves through; his focus is almost exclusively on activities of making. Nevertheless, his making, as well as the sourcing of the materials and knowledge that supports it, is always a situated practice. Spaces, places, and their material and conceptual contours support the author-practitioner’s activities. This essay attempts to reconstruct something of the author-practitioner’s workshop, his itineraries in and beyond Toulouse, and his assumptions about environments of creative labor.2

The Workshop

Reading Ms. Fr. 640 can conjure up the image of a curious, experimental, and at times intently focused man iterating and improvising in a workshop arrayed with all manner of tools, materials, and works in progress. The manuscript’s recipes, observations, and advice relate to a wide range of craft practices from metalworking, painting, and glassmaking to dyeing, cultivation, and medicine, while its descriptions of technical processes are unusually specific and sometimes even first-hand. As the text jumps from topic to topic, third-person description to first-person narration, and general observation to minutely detailed instruction, a reader can easily conflate the author-practitioner’s interests with his expertise.3 This section of the essay will try to disentangle the tools and materials that the author-practitioner used himself—and thus that constituted his workshop—from the plethora of substances, goods, and equipment invoked across the manuscript’s folios.

To be clear: the author-practitioner makes little explicit mention of his own working space, its layout, or the extent of its inventory. Of note, however, is a very short passage that is richly suggestive. On fol. 12v, the author-practitioner describes his experience calcining the surface of a carnelian stone: “I experimented,” he writes “[...] on the fire of my goldsmith’s forge [ma forge dorfevre].”4 A forge was a major installation.5 It would have dominated a workshop space and circumscribed a set of activities that could take place therein, including the use of smaller furnaces set inside it. It was also a fitting piece of equipment for an artisan preoccupied with the transformation of materials using fire, from the making of varnish, imitation gemstones, and the preservation of small animals through desiccation on the one hand to mold making and fine metal casting on the other. Indeed, the ability to mold and cast small works was a fundamental branch of the goldsmith’s art, discussed by both the sculptor Benvenuto Cellini (1500–1571) in I Trattati dell’Oreficeria (1568) and the metallurgist Vannoccio Biringuccio (ca. 1480–ca. 1539) in the Pirotechnia (1540).6 Indeed, when the hundreds of casting entries in Ms. Fr. 640 are considered alongside the sizable corpus of recipes for counterfeiting, polishing, and inlaying gemstones, the author-practitioner’s brief phrase, “my goldsmith’s forge” seems to hold promise for our understanding of the author-practitioner’s training and work.

Possessing a goldsmith’s forge, however, was not necessarily the same thing as being a goldsmith, and there are problems with identifying the author-practitioner as a goldsmith tout court. His range of interests was broad, and while he may not have had first-hand experience of all the craft processes he compiled, his expertise was not confined to the goldsmith’s craft. Moreover, he himself complicates matters on fol. 121v when he writes “if the goldsmiths knew [knew] the substance to make silver run in their works, they would buy much of it,” implying a certain distance between his own practice and that of goldsmiths in general. Moreover, as Michèle Bimbenet-Privat notes, the author-practitioner makes no reference in the manuscript to the many legal obligations that accompanied this highly regulated trade.7 What was, then, his relationship to the trade? If not a goldsmith, might we better consider him a practitioner of “the small art of casting,” as the metallurgist Biringuccio called the work of mold-making in frames using powders and sands?8 Or was the author-practitioner a different kind of artisan altogether? Was he, like his Toulouse contemporary Raymond Masse, trained as a goldsmith but working with other craftspeople and prominent patrons, perhaps unbounded by guild regulations and even economic need?9 As we will see next, a comparison between the author-practitioner’s workshop contents and those of other goldsmiths suggests that the situation is, indeed, rather complicated.

The Author-Practitioner’s Materials and Tools

In order to distinguish the author-practitioner’s own tools and materials from the several hundred he invokes across the manuscript—and to thereby reconstruct his working conditions with greater certainty—we can look to his use of first-person pronouns.10 In general, the entries of Ms. Fr. 640 were written using a combination of third-person descriptions (e.g., ilz font, aulcuns prennent), impersonal constructions (e.g., il fault), imperatives (e.g., faictes bouillir, broye le), or statements directed at either indefinite subjects (on y mesles, on faict) or undefined addressees (tu pourras, tu y mectras).11 We find first-person pronouns, whether personal, reflexive, or possessive, used somewhat rarely—they appear in only seventy-five of the nearly one thousand entries in the manuscript. These seventy-five predominantly address techniques of casting (forty-five entries) but also discuss gemstones (eight), painting (six), and medicine (two).12 A third of the entries that feature first-person pronouns seem to relate either the author-practitioner’s own observations about the practices of founders, gunpowder makers, painters, goldsmiths, master life-casters, and peddlers (e.g., ainsy l’ay je veu employer, j'ay veu que, en ma presence, etc.) or his speculations about potential improvements to processes he is otherwise describing impersonally (e.g., je croy(s), je cuide, j’ay opinion, a mon advis, etc.).

Most helpfully, forty-four entries appear to relate his personal, hands-on experience with specific tools and materials.13 Even here, his use of first-person pronouns is often restricted to short passages embedded within larger impersonally constructed entries; as a result, we cannot take for granted that the author-practitioner had first-hand experience of all materials, tools, or processes mentioned in those full entries.14 The most conservative interpretation, drawing only on those passages in which first-person pronouns describe first-hand experience, provides us with the following glimpse of his inventory.

The author-practitioner’s personal material inventory reflects his overwhelming first-person engagement with casting and the more specialized tasks of mold-making, lifecasting, and metal and alloy preparation. Thus, for mold-making, he uses various kinds of clay earths, sands, minerals, and salts15 as well as finely pestled materials,16 calcined materials,17 and smokes.18 His experiments, especially his lifecasting, bring him into contact with plants and animals of greater and lesser specificity.19 He uses luting materials,20 waters and solvents,21 binders and fats,22 various resinous products,23 and wax. He also makes use of mineral pigments,24 semi-precious stones,25 and tripoli to polish.

Finally, there are the metals. He uses gold leaf and silver of various kinds26 as well as varieties of copper.27 He reports using mercury, tin, and lead, often noting the purity and fineness of the last two. Yet he was most interested in alloys, whether those he acquired ready-made28 or the lead-tin alloy he was continually and carefully adjusting for the purposes of finely detailed casting and lifecasting in particular.

In addition to housing a goldsmith’s forge, the author-practitioner’s workshop appears to have accommodated other furnaces (c.f., “mon fourneau,” on fol. 86v), including a four à vent, which he uses on fol. 132v. His own fire-based work relied largely on charcoal as a fuel.29 Perhaps Etienne Delaune’s 1576 engraving of a goldsmith’s workshop, in which a young man attends a furnace constructed with firebricks set within a larger forge can give a clue to how these various heating elements might have coexisted in the same space (Fig. 1).30 Moreover, he reports having adjusted his furnace with tiles when making imitation rubies, raising it “two tiles all around” (fol. 100v) from the charcoal fire so as to create greater heat and covering the crucible itself with a tile “in order that heat reverberates better there” (fol. 101r). The author-practitioner also reports having used casting equipment, such as crucibles, iron caskets and trivets, mold frames, and even a press for molds. In fact, the author-practitioner even illustrates a press for large molds on fol. 160r (Fig. 2). While it is unclear if his own press was of this kind, he notes that presses could be heavy to move near the forge for casting. Such repositioning might have been easier with the help of an assistant. Though the author-practitioner advises using the help of a garson (workshop boy) elsewhere in the manuscript (fols. 123v, 135v), it is unclear if he took this advice himself. Additionally, the workshop would have housed at least one large working surface or more. The author-practitioner mentions constructing a mold on a very clean and smooth table (fol. 84v), a requisite for his precision work. We can begin to imagine a workshop space large enough to accommodate a forge, various furnaces, a somewhat sizable press that could be moved about, and perhaps room for an assistant to work alongside the author-practitioner at a large table.

The workshop also included a range of tools, some acquired on spec from other craftspeople but others bespoke or made by his own hand to accommodate particular needs. Accordingly, the author-practitioner possessed some tools in multiple varieties and materials, such as his grinding surfaces,31 pestles and mortars,32 tools for sifting,33 vessels,34 brushes,35 and candles.36 He also reports using a lead wheel for polishing, a diamond point, glassworkers’ solder, iron wire, a silver spoon, linen cloths, napkins, a sponge, knives, (tallow) candles, and even a birdcage.

The preceding is but a very conservative list of materials and tools that can be securely linked to the author-practitioner through his first-person reports of use, which are comparatively few. Most certainly, the workshop also housed a much larger range of items. Indeed, as stocked and equipped as his workshop appears to have been, the extent of this inventory pales in comparison with the hundreds of additional materials and tools mentioned across the manuscript.37 The full list shows the author-practitioner had enough familiarity to describe the use of a range of additional specialized materials (e.g., storax for a gout cure, bread crusts to rid linseed oil of grease, lye of quicklime and litharge for a black dye, and gold leaf to make rubies) and tools (e.g., plumb line, compass, and ruler for drawing in perspective, looms for weaving horsehair sieves, a mustard mill for grinding earth).38 Surely, he possessed far more than can be ascribed to him based on his own pronoun use.

The Material Culture of the French Goldsmith

Having established the baseline contents of the author-practitioner’s workshop (and admitted that he will have handled a far greater range of items than his explicit reporting implies), we can draw comparisons with the materials and tools used by early modern French goldsmiths as documented in contemporary inventories and visual sources. From the second half of the sixteenth century, artists began to depict craft practices and artisanal workspaces with greater frequency. Capitalizing on the healthy market for technical literature and books of secrets that had been in vogue for a half century, these artists offered buyers a pictorial counterpart, and they developed conventions of representation that would come to define the burgeoning genre and its task of articulating the cultural inflections of artisanal work.39

A wealth of information is contained in Étienne Delaune’s 1576 engraving of a goldsmith’s workshop (Fig. 1), and its companion piece, which may illustrate the silversmithing end of the same workshop (Fig. 3). Delaune (1518/19–1583) was an esteemed goldsmith and designer who worked for a time at the royal mint in Paris, produced works for Henri II and the French court, and is today best known for his many surviving prints and drawings for metalwork ornament.40 Diane Lee Carroll has discussed Delaune’s set of engravings of the goldsmith’s workshop in detail, identifying the many specialized materials and tools, depicted with special care, as well as the activities of the ten men shown at work.41 She notes the presence of coils of wire, crucibles, bellows, a flux dispenser, hammers, mallets, stakes, anvils, punches, mortars and pestles, nippers, cutters, shears, pliers, clamps, vises, tweezers, drawing plates, gravers, dividers, rasps, brushes, and candles. This list is only marginally different from the items recorded in the probate inventories of contemporary Parisian goldsmiths, which have been extensively studied by Michèle Bimbenet-Privat.42 She notes a similar range of basic tools for the activities of holding, cutting, pounding, piercing, metal drawing, and measuring, though fewer related to heating processes and more related to finishing techniques, such as goldsmiths pickle (bouture; cf., bouteure in Ms. Fr. 640), burnishing stones, pumice, and tripoli.43 Studies of goldsmiths’ tools in New France reveal remarkable similarities.44

All of the above-mentioned tools from the Delaune engravings and the Parisian inventories make an appearance in Ms. Fr. 640, albeit with slight variations here and there.45 On the other hand, a few items in Delaune’s engraving go entirely undiscussed in the pages of the manuscript, including a wire-drawing bench, pitch bowls to support punching and chasing, drills, balances, and calipers.46 Additionally, some of the core activities of the goldsmith are mentioned only in passing in Ms. Fr. 640, notably raising (see fol. 32r). These differences are inconclusive, since neither Delaune’s depiction nor Ms. Fr. 640 aims to be an exhaustive treatment of the goldsmith’s art. More conclusive, perhaps, is the author-practitioner’s lack of a tell-tale tool. Bimbenet-Privat has put forth the bigorne, a double horn anvil, as the most emblematic tool of goldsmiths—those working in Paris often possessed these anvils in multiples. This tool is illustrated in the lower right of Delaune’s goldsmith’s shop, set into a cylindrical stump, and it is nearly ubiquitous among the inventories of goldsmiths working in New France.47 While the author-practitioner mentions an anvil (enclume) four times in the manuscript, there is no mention of its more specialized variety, the bigorne.48 If he were a goldsmith, then he had a sub-specialty that took him away from some of the more recognizable activities of the trade.

Unusual points of contact between Ms. Fr. 640, Delaune’s engravings, and probate inventories are more illuminating, perhaps, and the author-practitioner’s focus on making sand molds for casting is worth underscoring. As Carroll notes, this technique was relatively undocumented at this time, though it would have been known by pewterers.49 While Biringuccio and Cellini had related the technique in some detail in the Pirotechnia (1540) and I Trattati dell’Oreficeria (1568) respectively, Delaune contributes to this discourse with the earliest known depictions of casting flasks.50 These hang from pegs at the very left edge of the goldsmith’s print and from pegs in the upper right corner of the silversmith’s print (Fig. 4, Fig. 5). The author-practitioner’s preoccupation with making sand molds and lifecasting placed him within a rarified circle of skilled, erudite, and self-promoting artisans experienced in the arts of fire.

If it is difficult to characterize the author-practitioner as a goldsmith tout court, this may reflect the great freedom afforded to this category of artisan, who could not only raise, chase, and set an object with gems but could perform the functions of other professionals—they could gild and cast even though there were professional gilders and founders.51 In Provence, goldsmiths were unregulated by guild statutes until the early sixteenth century; a 1498 probate inventory of a goldsmith from Draguignan suggests how wide the range of materials and tools might be in an independent workshop.52 In addition to owning a large collection of tools of the various kinds discussed above—including five bigornes and a wire-drawing bench—this artisan’s workshop contained finished objects, gold and silver coin for melting, various measures of colored cloth, a pound and a half of coral, numerous unmounted gemstones53 as well as stones explicitly labeled as imitations.54 If the professional autonomy of this goldsmith afforded him an ampler inventory than his guild-bound brethren elsewhere in France, then we might infer that the author-practitioner was also an independent artisan.55 The entries of Ms. Fr. 640 suggest he enjoyed a greater professional freedom still, with his interest not only in fine metalwork and imitation gemstones but in alchemical secrets, weapons and military strategy, optical effects, and experimental transformations of natural materials. Moreover, it seems likely that he acted on these interests, not simply collecting recipes and the know-how of other artisans but experimenting in his workspace with tools and materials well beyond those appropriate to the goldsmith’s trade.

The Workshop Unbounded

In the first half of this essay, we considered the space of the author-practitioner’s workshop. By isolating those passages in which the author-practitioner gave first-hand accounts of his making experiences (distinct from his first-hand witnessing or opinions), I offered a conservative survey of the equipment, tools, and materials in his workspace. I used his passing mention that he owned a goldsmith’s forge to assess the material culture of his workshop against what is known of contemporary French goldsmiths more generally. In spite of any specialized training the author-practitioner may have had, he does not fit the description of a goldsmith in any conventional, guild-regulated, and commercial sense of the term.

In the second half of this essay, we will pull back from the narrow investigation of the working space proper. The manuscript offers ample evidence that the author-practitioner’s broader interests, alongside his drive toward experiment and virtuosity in casting, required access to a series of spaces, ever-widening in scope. These spaces extended from the domestic and its close environs to the urban landscape, the countryside, and the world beyond. Collectively, these spaces stand as an extended supply chain of materials and expertise that support the activities of the workshop. It is to these concentric circles of space and place, and to the itineraries linking them, that we now turn.

The Home and Estate

In the sixteenth century, it was common for an artisan’s workshop to be integrated into the home. Accordingly, various rooms and areas in and around the estate constitute an intimate part of the author-practitioner’s working environment, field of inquiry, and intended destination for crafted works.

Among the domestic spaces that appear in the manuscript we find cellars and deep wells used to keep casting powders humid and items cool; draft-proof rooms and roof drains used to help preserve fruit and nuts long term; ovens from of which one takes freshly baked bread to serve as a quick and ready molding material or to make room for small animals to dry; fireplaces of big kitchens to harvest the best greasy bister, a black soot pigment; and pantries where one finds the fattest flies most suitable for casting from life.56 There is also implied access to more intimate spaces: private chambers with ornate furniture, such as beds encrusted with counterfeit jasper and stucco ornament or bedecked with valences decorated with stamped tin sheets. Additionally, those entries that treat the decoration of grottos (fols. 110r and 118r) suggest an implied familiarity with the fashionable estates of the social elite. The intersection of workshop, home, household, and estate reveal the interweaving stories of professional and daily life that invest these spaces with meaning.57

The author-practitioner can hardly refer to discrete places within the estate without bringing them into animation as spaces for larger, coordinated activity.58 For instance, in the entry “Keeping fruit for the entire year” on fol. 50r, we note the need for “a room where there is not a drop of air & of wind.” Despite the need for stillness, this room bears witness to various comings and goings. One must carefully place there, upside down on an even table, the glass ampuls one has already boiled (i.e., sterilized), which will eventually receive the fruit, as well as their wax stoppers, made to measure; these have been presumably prepared elsewhere beforehand (in the kitchen, perhaps?). One also returns to the draft-free room to dexterously pack and stopper the ampuls with fruit after a summer’s jaunt outdoors: we read about the need to carefully hand pick seasonal fruits—cherries, plums, or whatever fruit one wants—on a warm, dry day. Storage instructions for the bottled fruit invoke further spaces and seasonal conditions around the estate: the filled ampules are kept in a vat of water in the cellar through the summer, but—anticipating that the vat of water might freeze in the winter—one moves the bottles into a weighted basket lowered into a deep well, where the water will “be like river water” (i.e., moving). In this entry, the unfolding stories of activity temporarily transform the draft-free room, the kitchen, the orchard, the cellar, and the well into spaces of artisanal production.

The City

This animation of places continues as we reconstruct the movements of the author-practitioner between his workshop and the city, which allowed him to observe, record, and reimagine the work of other artisans for his own ends. Mentions of the tiler’s oven, the potters’, furbisher’s, and glassworker’s furnace, the blacksmith’s forge, the goldsmiths’ bench, the pewterer’s wheel, fuller’s earth, currier’s water, gunpowderers burn salve, etc., all evoke the stories of workshops, skilled craftspeople, and their practices, as well as the author-practitioner’s privileged access to all three. Artisanal workshops were found in nearly all neighborhoods of sixteenth-century Toulouse, with craft workers often clustered by profession, as they were in many early modern cities. Archival records allow us to trace something of the author-practitioner’s movements in Toulouse, especially along the right bank in the six districts (capitoulats) of the city (cité) proper (fig. 6).59

The author-practitioner’s references across the manuscript’s recipes to flours, perhaps ground in the city’s mills, place him along the Garonne, perhaps at the famous Bazacle mills to the north of the city or at those along the edge of the island of Tounis. This island, which was absorbed into the fabric of the city in the eighteenth century and is today the city’s edge on the river, was home to dyers and starchers, and, at its north end to slaughterers, butchers, and curriers who were supported by neighboring abattoirs; this was but one of the various places in the city where butchers clustered or an abattoir (affachoir) could be found. It is in this neighborhood, perhaps, that the author-practitioner sourced horn for his counterfeit jasper or the ox foot bones he calcined as an ingredient in his molds.

Crossing the pont de Tounis onto the mainland and into the Dalbade district, the author-practitioner might have encountered gold beaters and orpailleurs—panners for gold—who lived on the petite rue de la Dalbade, from whom he could have sourced the gold leaf and spangle gold that appear in the pages of Ms. Fr. 640. On the nearby rue des polinaires, one wonders whether he encountered any of the remaining polishers and burnishers of metals for whom the street once took its name. Elsewhere in the Dalbade district, he might have encountered the chair caners of the rue du coq-d’Inde, the carpenters and wood merchants on the then rue des Fustières (the southern half of today’s rue des Paradoux), or the various bladeworkers (espasiers, taillandiers, esperonniers, couteliers, and razoriers) of the rue des Couteliers.

Walking east and crossing the rue de Pharaon into the district of Saint-Barthélemy, the author-practitioner could have observed on the rue des Filatiers the practices of the city’s fashion workers (tailleurs, boutonniers, chaussetiers, couturiers, gantiers, perruquiers, and brodeurs). On the rue de Chapeliers, then known as the rue de Ugnères, he might have sourced some of the oils and suet for his workshop from the sellers of fats (ugnères), whose shops were being slowly replaced by those of the hatters, from whom the street would come to take its new name. Around the Carmelite convent, place des Carmes, were numerous small merchants and artisans; from the author-practitioner’s time, goldsmiths began to take up shop in this neighborhood. Just as eclectic was the rue de Fleurs, where he could have seen the work of locksmiths, candle makers, and button makers, among others.

In the Pont-Vieux district, he could have seen cauldron makers, crossbow makers, and doublet makers along the rue Peyrolières, or spoken to lantern makers in their guild house on the eponymous rue Lanternières. Some of the city’s many drapers were to be found in the rue de la Bourse and the rue Cujas.

In the Daurade district, along today’s rue des Blanchers were found the leather bleachers, tanners, and chamoiseurs as well as many of the city’s numerous fishermen, from whom, perhaps, he might have sourced his cuttlefish bones or materials for fish glue—or perhaps he got these from the fish hall in the Pont-Vieux district. Of especial interest must have been the rue d’Argentières, today the rue Gambetta, where he could have found his artisanal kinsmen, the silversmiths and goldsmiths who prepared the prizes of lifecast flowers for the city’s jeux floraux.

The author-practitioner may have been privileged enough to enter the arsenal, enclosed within the city hall complex in the Saint-Étienne district, on the site of today’s Square Charles-de-Gaulle.60

Nevertheless, in the sixteenth century, it must have been the eclectic Pierre district that captivated the author-practitioner the most. There, in what is today the rue des Tourneurs, he could have met artisans as varied as makers of arms, makers of wooden troughs (grazaliers), turners, and doublet-makers. Oil merchants and oil bearers were to be found in the rue Fourbastard, and in today’s rue des Arts, part of which was known in the sixteenth century as the rue Estagnères-Vielles, he would have encountered the tinsmiths and tin founders, in whose work he surely took a great interest.

As a larger-scale analog to his own experiments in small-scale casting, the author-practitioner’s intense interest in the work of the city’s founders is instructive: he weaves in and out of various workshops and sites of production, observing, documenting, and performing something akin to an ethnography of craft practice. He records in detail the practices of various kinds of founders (artillery founders, founders of large casts for statues, bell founders, founders of small works of tin), noting their alloys, their mold materials, and their heating practices. He even sketches the birds-eye view of a founder’s “furnace for melting a large quantity of metal” on fol. 48v (fig. 7), schematically mapping out the infrastructure required for large-scale casting. His engagement with founders is also personal in nature—he discusses by name one family of founders active at the Toulouse arsenal, the Poncet, observing that they use a different alloy than is commonly used for the cannons of France, and he even records the superstitions of founders (casting is unlucky on Monday, Wednesday, Friday, and Sunday). A marginal inscription on 49r notes that the Poncet “cast with solder that pewterers glassworkers use.” His correction here suggests his familiarity with pewterers and glassworkers, setting up an implied itinerary among workshops in the city.61

The founders and allied artisans of Toulouse were rich sources for the author-practitioner, and he used his observations of their practices to his own ends, hypothesizing, experimenting, and refining his way toward ever-better mold-making materials and techniques. On 80v, in the entry “Founders of small works of tin,” he describes their work in the third person, yet he makes three annotations in the left margin that reveal his thought process as he shifts towards his own workshop practice. The first marginal note gives advice to an unnamed or impersonal “you:” “Make sure the pegs of your frame enter easily so that in opening the frame will easily open without shifting anything, and that your frames fit well together, & the table is quite even.” The next two are experimental exhortations: “Try to etch with distilled vinegar.” “Try calcined oyster shells. They are said to be excellent for molding.” In the entry “Method of casting in bronze” on fol. 82v, as he describes the bronze founders’ process, he cautions that the high heat makes the crucible prone to breaking, but, he writes, “you can lute it as you know founders do,” an assumed knowledge for an assumed reader—who may be none other than the author-practitioner himself. Across these two entries, just a few folios apart, we see the author-practitioner moving back and forth between a description of the tin founder’s work and a reflection on his own, between their materials and his equipment, between the bronze foundry and the workspace of his manuscript’s addressee. We are left with an implied itinerary in and around Toulouse that helps us to map his professional project.

The author-practitioner’s interaction with founders, particularly the Poncet, may suggest his participation, however peripheral, in an epistemic “trading zone.” These sites of innovation and knowledge exchange among scholars and practitioners were often facilitated by those with political power and economic means. Pamela O. Long has argued that early modern arsenals as well as cities themselves were paradigmatic trading zones, where textual and technical experts commingled with political elites to further a common enterprise.62 It is significant that, in this period, Toulouse was host to a royal arsenal enclosed within the city hall complex. A vibrant and localized knowledge exchange among scholars from Toulouse’s university, metalworkers, and civic and royal representatives clearly took place within the arsenal, yet conversations must have, at times, continued outside its walls. The series of entries in Ms. Fr. 640 on cannon caliber, mortars, and siege strategy record surprisingly up-to-date information, and they may have been informed by the author-practitioner’s interactions with the Poncet, with others working in the arsenal, or with close associates of either.63

These entries notwithstanding, there is little to suggest that the author-practitioner had access to the arsenal himself. Rather, his trading zone, the one in which he was an active contributor, was the city of Toulouse writ large. As we have seen, he moves in and out of various complementary spaces of making and knowing. He interacts with artisans across the city’s neighborhoods, and he uses the information he has gathered to innovate in his own workshop. Though we have no record of the craft insights that he shared with his artisan interlocutors, he most certainly brought something to the table. After all, on fol. 162r, he acknowledges the transactional nature of knowledge exchange and his desire to both contribute and profit. In the entry “For the workshop,” which seems to include prefatory material for the eventual publication of his manuscript, he writes: “I, from a desire to learn, am exposing what little is in my workshop to receive, through a common commerce of letters, much rarer secrets.”

The Countryside and Beyond

As we have seen, Ms. Fr. 640 documents the author-practitioner’s intense experimentation in his own workshop as well as his exploration of the workspaces of artisans across Toulouse. The manuscript also reveals much about the author-practitioner’s efforts to source materials in the countryside and from regional and global trade networks. These materials range from the generic to the specific, and the sites whence he sources them range correspondingly from the implicitly unremarkable to the highly particular and from the local to the global.

The manuscript abounds with references to natural and pastoral environments, including hills, mountains, mines, quarries, rock caves, gravel banks, sandy paths, the countryside, forests, meadows, fields, vineyards, gardens, rivers, riverbanks, and the sea. Such generic environments are often invoked as places to find generic materials, as in the case of rivers, where one can source river water, river sand, and river pebbles, all of which appear in the manuscript. Such generic environments could also denote a metaphorical “anyspace.” In the entry “Fountains” on 80r, the author-practitioner draws a bent-around cornet in the margin, a device meant to work as a perpetual fountain. He notes that such a device can be inserted into “some rock,” without bothering to tell us anything more about its type, its finish, or its placement. Above, he draws a charming mountainous landscape spanning the width of the folio, with a pipe zigzagging from the mountain’s lower left to its upper right (fig. 8). The mountains in this drawing, used to illustrate how “to make water rise higher than its source,” are conceptual overkill; the hydraulic principle in question presumably would hold at smaller scales more common in fountain construction, and the entry itself discusses the principle in isolation from any specific environment. Nonetheless, the author-practitioner uses a slice of nature, here a mountain, to stand indexically for anywhere that has a change in elevation.

In rare cases, the author-practitioner is extremely specific about a particular mine or a particular vineyard. When he is, he means to emphasize that the location in question yields not merely a usable material but the ideal resource for a given process of making—something worthwhile seeking out from a particular palace. For instance, in “Sand from Thoulouse” on fol. 87r, the author-practitioner notes: “The usual bo good one is the one which is found in a vineyard near Puy David, but the one which is most excellent is the one from the Touch, near Sainct Michel & toward Blagnac, in a vineyard that is quite high up.” When he is relatively specific in his description of the source or site, he is practicing a form of quality control over his workshop materials.64

At the broadest level, the author-practitioner’s entries tell the story of expanding global trade networks, with his references, for example, to cochineal, pipes for smoking, and the “medicine of Orientals.” In these references, materials, tools, and recipes travel great distances before reaching the author-practitioner, who in turn accommodates them in his workshop, the pages of his manuscript, and his material imaginary. References to non-local materials tell spatial stories of a pan-European community of professional artisans that produce, trade, and use specialized and high-quality items, such as “paper from Florence,” “chalk from Champaigne,” “glass from Lorraine,” “lead from Germany,” “tripoli of Brittany,” “Cardona salt,” and “Flanders blue.” Moreover, this community of artisans also practice regionally specific techniques like “the work of the Flemish,” “the work done in Algiers,” and “the work of Colchis,” which the author-practitioner documents with interest. It is unclear from his words whether many of these more distant places have lived meaning for the author-practitioner or whether they exist only as part of a mental map that helps him roughly distinguish near from far, his practice from that of others, exempla with which to vie, and his world from worlds beyond. In such attempts to distinguish, we can discern the metageographies by which the author-practitioner ordered his knowledge of the world.65

Nature as Storehouse and Workshop

As the author-practitioner explores the countryside around Toulouse, sourcing materials for his artisanal experiments, we see him conceiving of the space of nature as an environment and nature itself as an agent that provide him with workshop supplies. For instance, in his singular quest to find the ideal sands for mold-making, the author-practitioner sources various material samples to be calcined and pulverized in his workshop—some of which he would need to purchase but most of which he could collect himself in and outside of the city. The natural materials he eventually calcines include alabaster, slate, white pebbles, black pebbles, pumice stone, horse bones, ox bones, human bones, cuttlefish bones, river shells, oyster shells, sand, walnut wood, and vines. In his forge and furnaces, he heats these materials to high temperatures until they transform into a more friable substance or turn to ash. He then grinds them finely, until they are impalpable, and either adds a binder to simply cast with these experimental powders or uses them as additives to improve the casting qualities of natural sands.66 In essence, the natural world, its fields, riverbanks, and forests were stockrooms for goods.

The countryside around Toulouse functions as a storehouse of materials when it comes to natural sands themselves, a critical ingredient in the author-practitioner’s artisanal making. The author-practitioner takes care describing the various qualities of sands, and he has a sophisticated vocabulary of technical descriptors, such as fatty, lean, the ability to take body, etc., that helps him convey the desirable characteristics of sands and earths. Fortunately, as he notes in a rare first-person statement, “the sand near my area is very excellent” (fol. 69v).67 He offers more specifics about where to find that sand as the entries go on:

One can find an excellent one on the little hill of Puy David near Thoulouse for lead, tin & copper. (81r)

The sand from Thoulouse, which is taken, from the depths of the earth, from the small hill of Puy David, is excellent in itself, but to make it withstand several castings, I mix it with pulverized sandever & moistened, which hardens it, & takes body & bond with it, & makes it withstand more than five as many castings as you wish. (84r)

The usual bo good one is the one which is found in a vineyard near Puy David, but the one which is most excellent is the one is from the Touch, near Sainct Michel & toward Blagnac, in a vineyard that is quite high up. (87r)

As these passages report, although the Puy David sand is good on its own for casting in some metals and can be given greater longevity with certain additives, it is surpassed in quality by that found in the vineyard near the river Touch.68 The author-practitioner makes explicit the advantage of seeking out such good sand a few folios later:

Artisans who work on big works & to save time, do not need to grind & seek the curiosities of artificial sands, benefit from seeking some ready-made by nature, which has the finest grain possible, & for small works, they only pass it through a sieve. (88v)

Artisans who work in big works, & who need to further their profit, by seeking things already prepared in nature, because she does not sell her wares to her children, and to also save the time they would use for grinding finely & for artificially preparing sands, seek the one of the s mines, which is not too fat, the one that is a kin of earth, not too lean & consequently without bond, but rather which is pulled from the depths of the sand-bed [...]. And because the latter is only found near the rocks in mountainous areas, or lean territories & akin to the arene, it cannot be found in in the surroundings of all the bonnes villes, where artisans willingly gather themselves. And thus, if they do not have it close to their house, they prefer to have it come from afar, like from Lyon, Venise, Parys near the Sainct Chappelle & similar places, rather than prepare it. However, you can be certain that in all places you can render the sand from a mine good & proper for molding. (90r)

These two excerpts impart something more than a space animated through narratives of activity. They hint at something like a metageography that structures the author-practitioner’s understanding of craft, commerce, and nature. It is useful here to draw on political geographer John Agnew’s notion of the “sense of place,” which he defines as an “identification with place as a unique community, landscape, and moral order.”69 We have already seen how the author-practitioner’s concept of Toulouse and its surrounds is wrapped up with his artisanal enterprise. The community, as the author-practitioner experiences it, is rich with practical expertise, which he exploits as best he can through a lively exchange of knowledge; presumably Toulouse ranks among the bonnes villes where artisans of all stripes “willingly gather themselves.” Moreover, the urban and exurban landscapes are abundant with materials, whether the good sands and other such things found in the countryside or the specialized commodities that could be imported into the cosmopolitan city from preferred centers such as Lyon, Venice, or Paris. Together, the community and landscape of Tououse offered a highly particular space in which to source, observe, imitate, experiment, produce, and exchange.

Nevertheless, the values that underpin the author-practitioner’s sense of place—that is, the moral order—emerge most clearly when he discusses nature. For him, nature is not an abstract and extended space abundant with materials and goods; the concept of nature as a place is a later development in the French language.70 Rather, nature is a force of creation.71 It is a nurturing mother who gives freely so that “her children” might economize time, labor, and costs. As Rebecca Zorach has shown, in the sixteenth century, France was conceptualized as Cybele/Natura/Artemis of Ephesus—a female body with endless milk flowing from her (many) breasts (fig. 9). By extension, France and nature were understood as limitless sources for the nourishment of the people and the production of wealth.72 The author-practitioner participates in this discourse, whether knowingly or not. He personifies nature both as the artisan-merchant, who makes and sells her wares, and as mother, the inexhaustibly giving female body. He thus emphasizes labor as a principle of material transformation and profit on the one hand and appropriates the value of that labor as the righteous entitlement of a child from its mother. As a result, the natural environment functions not only as a storehouse of generic materials but as the locus of an active mechanism, nature herself, that renders sand “good & proper for molding” by her processes. (After all, the materials that an artisan finds available “already prepared” or “ready-made by nature” were still made nonetheless.) Put otherwise, for the author-practitioner, the materials found in the Toulousain countryside are not raw as we might be accustomed to think of them today but in various states of process—in preparation by a workshop assistant who is also a mother figure. She dutifully gives to her artisan children without asking anything in return so that they might “further their profit” through her invisible labor, “to save time” “rather than prepare” artificial sands themselves.

The hill of Puy David, the vineyard of Touch, and the Toulousain countryside are thus extensions of the workshop not simply in a loose sense as sites where the author-practitioner gathers supplies for his craft but more strictly as spaces where nature does the grunt work of preparing materials for more specialized use by her master-artisan son for the benefit of the “family” business. In this interpretation, the author-practitioner’s sense of place evinces a moral order governed by Christian virtue, here of selfless maternal charity, and a proto-capitalist commodification of time, labor, and knowledge for use in “common commerce”—of letters as well as goods. It is a sense of place that elides the norms of patriarchal Christianity, domestic divisions of labor, and guild-regulated hierarchy while it subtly foreshadows centuries of extractive capitalism to come.
