Introduction

Ms. Fr. 640 is remarkably informative about a rich variety of making processes, often including details not mentioned in any other known textual source. Over a third of the manuscript is devoted to mold making and metal casting, and, among these long and detailed entries are the accounts of many first hand trials of lifecasting.1 The process of lifecasting was not uncommon in the sixteenth century, and inventories list lifecast animals and plants, with many examples still extant today (Fig. 1, Fig. 2, Fig. 3, Fig. 4). Such objects emerged out of an intense interest in this period in the imitation of nature by art. Imitating the artifice of nature by the artifice of the human hand is the aim of numerous processes in Ms. Fr. 640, as it was in many other artefacts of art and architecture in early modern Europe.

The process of molding and casting from life produced a lifelike metal simulacrum of plants and animals by forming a plaster mold from the body of the live plant or recently-killed animal. While other printed sources give the outlines of this process, Ms. Fr. 640 provides observations on the habits of animals used for casting, including instructions for catching, feeding, keeping, and killing them, along with extremely detailed first hand experimentation in molding and casting them. The author-practitioner treats lifecasting in dense passages from fol. 106v right through to the end of the manuscript, under headings such as “Catching lizards and snakes” (fol. 107r), “Snakes for molding” (fol. 109r), “Killing snakes for molding” (fol. 109v), “Crab and Crayfish” (fol.111v), “Making the arrangement and disposition of the animal” (fol. 112v), “Tempering the sand and molding the first cast” (fol. 113r), “Molding bouquets, flowers, plants” (fol. 116r), “Flower in the mouth of a snake” (fol. 122r), “Spider’s web” (fol. 124r), “Molding turtles” (fol. 125v), “Spider molded on a leaf” (fol. 129v), “Stag beetle” (fol. 130v), “Vine leaf and small frog” (fol. 135r), “Molding grasshoppers and things too thin” (fol. 142v), “Toad” (fol. 143r), “Carnations” (fol. 143v), “Molding the foot of the bittern, or of the eagle, or of other birds for the foot of saltcellars or vases” (fol. 148r), “Molding a fly” (fol. 149v), “Bat” (fol. 149v), “Molding a rat” (fol. 152r), “Molding a rose” (fol. 155r), “Molding a bird” (fol. 157v), “Crayfish” (fol. 163v), “Fly wing” (fol. 165v), and many more.

The author-practitioner’s intense engagement with lifecasting in the manuscript starts with three entries about catching and keeping nightingales that are interspersed with grafting and gardening instructions on fol. 105v. On fol. 106r, under the heading “For casting,” he begins to assemble materials for molding and casting, along with comments on their properties and where they might be sourced. For seven extremely packed pages, he discusses materials: clay on which to pose the animals, necessary ingredients for the molding “sand” (sable)—sal ammoniac, bismuth, feather alum, crocum ferri (rusted metal filings), plaster, tiles (to be pulverized)—and for other materials needed in the process such as wax for making the gate and the sprues, and eau-de-vie (used as a release agent), as well as sourcing the animals themselves (Fig. 5).

These entries contain many unique observations, such that on casting spiders (Fig. 6) on a leaf in a two-piece mold:

The very big ones usually have hairy feet, which are vexing to mold if you do not lay them flat, or burn them with a candle, or stiffen them & lay them flat with some, anointing them with wheat oil. Kill them in vinegar & urine, like snakes &, or in eau-de-vie, and then shape them on a well-made vine leaf or other thing. Next, you can give them back their capricious hair with bourr the sieved fine hair of cloth waste, having anointed them with a with fish glue or similar. Their true color is of vinegar & verdet, & then fumigate them in various places with sulfur. Having made your tourt clay slab, place on it your vine leaf, and the dead spider in the middle, & pierce with a point of iron or latten wire the middle of the spider’s body & the leaf together. Next, place diagonally small points of latten wire around the vine leaf to secure it well. Then fix, with a little melted wax and the point of a hot iron wire, the end of the legs, adapting them with the end of small pincers. Do the same thing with the end of the small cornicles of the spider. Once the wax is cool, scrape the excess with the point of a penknife in order that the end of the legs stay neat. Next, place the circle of earth around & cast your tempered sand, like for other things. In this manner, you will need to burn the vine leaf in the mold, otherwise not. And to cast more easily, let the animal die fully, in order that, when struggling, it does not mix up its legs. Having made your first cast, uncover the reverse of the leaf and make the second cast. To make the gate for the spider on the leaf and to prevent the sand from covering the spider from underneath the belly, furnish the end of the tail & the bottom of the body with a little wax, melted & applied with the hot iron wire, as you know. In this way, when the leaf burns & the wax is melted, there will remain two little holes in the leaf which will be the gate of the spider (fol. 129v).

It is characteristic of these entries that they mix observations about animal behavior with comments on the behavior of molding materials and metals. Over the sixty folios following fol. 105, the author-practitioner penned multiple entries on the entire process of casting from life: catching, keeping, feeding, and killing animals, arranging the animal on a clay base, then pouring plaster molds around the animals and plants, burning out or otherwise removing these organic models from the mold, cleaning the molds of the ashes, binding the multi-piece molds together, pouring metal into the void left by the burned-out or removed creature, detaching the mold from the cast object, removing the mold infrastructure and metal flashing (where the molten metal leaked out of the sides of the mold), and making any necessary repairs where the cast failed. Although the author-practitioner is quite comprehensive in his account of lifecasting, he is by no means methodical about his exposition. The entries on lifecasting are interspersed with many, many other processes, and even in the entries themselves, he loops back multiple times to repeat and modify slightly the processes, apparently writing down additional information as it occurred to him, and filling the margins with comments and notes. He does, however, seem to be attempting to cover all parts of the process of lifecasting in the compass of these almost seventy folios from 106v through 170r.

It is clear that lifecasting was a major preoccupation (perhaps the major preoccupation) of the author-practitioner, and these folios contain the longest and most densely written entries, with numerous marginal additions, much recounting of first hand experience, and many remarkable sketches, making them among the most complicated but rewarding for the modern reader to decipher.

Casting from Life

Casting from life goes back to antiquity, and is mentioned by the fourteenth-century painter Cennino Cennini in his Libro dell’ Arte. Instructions for casting from life were collected, printed, and reprinted into the eighteenth century.2 A contemporaneous example, Hugh Plat (1552–1608), a brewer’s son and irrepressible collector of recipes, recorded processes for casting plants and animals in The Jewell House of Art and Nature conteining divers rare and profitable Inventions, together with sundry new experimentes in the Art of Husbandry, Distillation, and Molding (1594), roughly contemporaneous with Ms. Fr. 640. In the fourth book, entitled “The Art of molding, or casting of any live bird, or little beast, hearbe, or flower, or of any patterne of mettall, wax, &c. into gold, silver, plaister, &c.,” contains detailed instructions for casting a branch of rosemary as a lost-pattern cast; two-piece molds “if you woulde save your patternes”; casting in glue and wax molds that are flexible and capable of being removed from undercut work; sulphur molds; breadcrumb molds; box molds and core molds; molding a man’s face and hands; killing insects, frogs, and toads in order to mold them; and papier-maché molding for the ornamentation of mantelpieces “and other stately furnitures of chambers or galleries.”3

From the number of printed sources and lifecast objects in sixteenth-century collections, it is clear that this was a widely known and much desired technique for at least three centuries. This textual and material evidence points to the popularity of lifecasts among collectors in the sixteenth and early seventeenth centuries. The inventories of the Kunstkammern of the Wittelsbachs in Bavaria and of the Habsburgs contain of hundreds of lifecasts in silver, tin, lead, plaster, and other media, and the eagerness with which they were sought can be detected in the anxious letters between Wenzel Jamnitzer and Archduke Ferdinand of Austria (1564–1602) between 1556 and 1562 about a commission to build a fountain with animals cast from life around the base.4 Another example of this interest can be found in a letter from an official at the court of Grand Duke Francesco I de’ Medici (1541–87) to Duke Wilhelm V of Bavaria (1548–1626) that details a “Pergkhwerckhstuckh” (a Handstein combining goldsmith’s work with natural mineral specimens), that the Grand Duke was sending to Wilhelm. It included lifecast lead patterns for plants that were to be attached to the work. A postscript to the letter states that the live frogs, snakes, plants, and other animals that the duke desired could not be obtained due to the inclement weather.5

Lifecasts were produced in great numbers throughout Europe in the sixteenth century, particularly in Italy and in the southern German lands, especially Nuremberg, where lifecasting was a well-known technique by the first half of the sixteenth century, reaching a high point in the work of the Nuremberg master goldsmith Wenzel Jamnitzer (1508–85).

Casting from Life in Ms. Fr. 640

Vernacular Natural History: Catching, Keeping, and Killing Animals

One of the most distinctive aspects of Ms. Fr. 640’s entries on lifecasting is their detail about the catching and keeping of animals to be used as the patterns for lifecasting. These entries include observations on the habits, and likes and dislikes of animals, thus providing a fascinating window into the engagement with and understanding of nature in the sixteenth century.

Take a stick and attach a string at the end which has a knot eyelet slip eyelet at the end. And there being two or three to distract the lizard by whistling, approach the eyelet toward its neck, and when its head [ l ] is inside, pull. The lizard is more tedious to catch by hand than the snake & bites without letting go & grips like pincers (fol. 107r).

And if he were to bite living flesh it would au not let go, & there would be no better remedy than to promptly cut off his head (fol. 111v).

In these instructions for capturing reptiles, many natural historical observations are recorded, such as “the small lizards that one takes in the summer...grey on the back & green on the belly...have rougher scales than the... [larger] lizards & show better,” and to take lizards in the spring “as they have changed skin, they are more beautiful [for molding]” (fol. 111v).

Snakes can be caught by hand, provided that it is covered by a thick woollen cloth, for the teeth of the snake stay in the cloth & cannot pierce like they would with a linen. The dangerous ones are recognized by their blue & asses aseuresazure eyes. They hardly ever bite in water, which crayfish catchers experience (fol. 107r).

When you take [snakes] for molding, if it is possible, do not remove their teeth if you want to keep them. For, having had their teeth removed, they get sore gums & mouth, & can no longer eat. You can keep them in a barrel full of bran or even better of earth, in a cool place, or in a glass bottle. And give them any live frog or other little live animal, for they do not eat anything dead. Also, I have noticed that when they want to bite or eat something, they do not bite with a direct attack but with sinuous turns & from the side, as do Satan & his disciples. It has a small head but a very long body. The entryway to sin seems small & inconsequential, but the consequences of it are very great. It abstains from eating seven or eight days, once it has devoured some frog. It can swallow three or four of them, one after the other, and what it devoured is neither corrupted nor consumed in one go in its stomach, but p some part little by little, that is to say bones and everything. And the remains are found as fresh as when it had devoured them, such that sometimes, when one presses & torments it, it renders up what it has engulfed, parts of which are found totally consumed & others as fresh as if it were alive. It can keep a frog engulfed for two to three hours & renders it up completely alive. When it is big, one needs to mold it hollow, & if you want to make it have an open mouth, put some cotton inside, & then on the cotton some melted wax (fol. 109r–v).

At another point, the author-practitioner mentions that it is best to mold the animals quickly, as these innately “corrupt animals [animaulx infects deulx mesmes] stink within one day” (fol. 114v).6 Such remarks suggest that reptiles, especially snakes, called up moral and sensory feelings of disgust, decay, and spiritual evil. At the same time, reptiles and amphibians were liminal animals, living in the two elemental zones of water and earth, which appears to have led to an association with the liminal state of birth, and birthing amulets and practices involving frogs and lizards. Lizards were often associated with transformation, even transmutation, as indicated in the author-practitioner’s observation that “Leadsmiths say that making a lizard die in the melted tin it des makes the tinning become very golden. Or else putting in sal ammoniac” (fol. 98r).7

Catching crayfish

Attach to the tip of a stick some liver or lung of a cow or sheep, & with a small string you make a basket hang from the tip of the said stick. Put in water where crayfish eat, & they will quickly come to accumulate on the flesh. Extract gently from the water, & those who want to go back into the water will fall into the basket (fol. 160v).

Keeping Animals

Instructions for keeping snakes are not quite as detailed as for nightingales, but are discussed multiple times. Snakes should be kept “in a barrel full of bran or even better of earth, in a cool place, or in a glass bottle” (fol. 109r). The snake should be protected from harm in order to keep it eating: “When it has eaten something, by tormenting it, it renders it up. And if, after having eaten something, it is thus pressed underfoot, this pains it greatly & hurts it. If it is wounded, it will not eat willingly.” Snakes should be fed live frogs, rather than dead ones. It was important to have the animal eat because “in three [days], it is almost as thin as it will be afterward if it does not eat. Molding it very soon after it is caught is best, before it becomes thin. One ought to put it to death only a quarter of an hour before you want to mold” (fol. 110r).

And, having put it in a glass bottle d with bran, or in a barrel full half full of fresh & humid earth, if you want to store them or keep them a long time, give them live frogs & not dead ones to eat. For if you keep them without feeding them, they wither & grow thin & have long wrinkled aulx skins. Thus the best would be to mold it as soon as after possible apre after it has been taken. But before, put it to death in this way. Place it in a clean bottle or vessel so that it would collect no dust. And put inside such a quantity of good vinegar & urine that it can be covered. And shake it & torment it therein until it is dead, which will be in an hour or a half. But if you are in a hurry to cast, make him take through the mouth some good eau-de-vie or else mix a little with the vinegar. And soon it will be dead, without any lesion which deforms it. For large animals, for whom one would need a lot of eau-de-vie, one uses vinegar and er urine. But for small ones, only eau-de-vie, which is done sooner. When your animal is dead, take it out & clean it carefully of its old skin, if it sheds, or of the filth, or of the lice, like lizards have, and then arrange it thus. Now, put enough in a glass, & soak & continually keep in it the head of the animal, or the whole of it, if it is not big (fol. 112r–v).

A marginal note about snakes on fol. 112r underscores the necessity of making certain the animal is dead: “to know if it is quite dead, take it out of the vinegar and take it by the tail. If it moves it, do not mold it for it still has feeling, & when piercing it, it would draw back & spoil the mold.”

Posing Animals

Published instructions for lifecasting, such as Plat’s, agree in general terms with the instructions in Ms. Fr. 640, but the manuscript provides invaluable technical details, including the precise method of posing the animals on the clay base—including information-rich marginal drawings of this hard-to-describe process (Fig. 7, Fig. 8)—and the design of the channels through which the metal flows into the mold (Fig. 9, Fig. 10). Sixteenth-century objects extant in collections today show that the techniques recounted in Ms. Fr. 640 were employed. For example, the author-practitioner describes the process of passing pins through the animals’ feet to the clay beneath, either by threading wire through these holes or leaving the pins in place, in order to hold the animal in place on the clay slab (Fig. 11). The pins are then removed from the other side of the mold when the plaster for the second half of the two-piece mold is poured (fols. 112v–114r). These pins leave a very small void in the plaster that fills with metal during casting, and which must be clipped off the metal cast. One of the most impressive sixteenth-century examples of lifecasting, almost certainly by Wenzel Jamnitzer, shows the traces of this technique.

The lizard (Fig. 12) and toad (Fig. 13, Fig. 14) mounted on the writing box in the Kunsthistorisches Museum reveal tiny protrusions of metal on their feet, traces of the pins that once held the animal to the clay base.

Smaller and lighter objects such as flies could be attached with wax to a large leaf (Fig. 15) or plant, which was held in place by its gate as the plaster was poured in. The most delicate, thinnest objects, such as grasshopper wings, the feet of flies, and flower petals needed to be thickened to give them substance for casting, and Ms. Fr. 640 contains instructions for applying butter or wheat oil to these delicate parts (Fig. 16, Fig. 17, Fig. 18).8

According to the author-practitioner, wheat and olive oil not only stiffened and dried the hair, but also laid flat the hair of rats and the feathers of birds and kept them from sticking to the mold material (fol. 141r). The wings of the cicada on the lid of the Vienna writing box show evidence of such thickening treatment: their thickened edges of silver have been filed off after casting (Fig. 19, Fig. 20).

The author-practitioner also gives suggestions (fol. 122r) for animals entwined with each other (Fig. 21, Fig. 22), as well as for placing a flower in the mouth of a poisonous snake: best is a plant that acts as an antidote to the poison of the animal (fol. 122v).

A ewer and basin set covered with lifecast creatures in the Dubrovnik Cathedral Treasury contains several lizards and snakes holding plants in their mouths (Fig. 23, Fig. 24).

Casting Infrastructure

The system of gates and vents illustrated in the manuscript is also evident in traces left on extant objects. For example, the author illustrates the preparation of a lizard (fol. 124v) with a gate for the metal leading into the reptile’s tail (Fig. 25) and a crayfish (fol. 141v–142r) with a gate through its thin, bony tail (Fig. 26, Fig. 27).

Lifecast lizards in Nuremberg and Basel show evidence of this procedure (Fig. 28, Fig. 29),9 as does a brass crayfish in the Munich Bayerisches Nationalmuseum (Fig. 30, Fig. 31).10

Ms. Fr. 640 contains much advice about constructing the gate through which the metal flows into the mold, including different forms of gates for different objects (fols. 122v, 124v-125r, 129v, 133r, 158v, 164r) and for different types of metals (fol. 154v), as well as admonitions to score the plaster of the gate in order to prevent the metal from running “furiously” (fol. 122v, 164v).

The lifelikeness of the metal animals and plants in all their minute detail was prized in the sixteenth century. The arrangement of gates and vents illustrated in the manuscript ensured that the most delicate features of the animal were captured in all their detail (Fig. 32, Fig. 33, Fig. 34, Fig. 35).

The tiny details of lizard and toad feet (fols. 133r, 143v) and crayfish legs (fol. 142r) could be successfully captured in casting by creating a void beneath the feet into which the metal flowed. The ball of metal that formed at the end of each toe could then be clipped off after casting (Fig. 36).11 Capturing the finest details was of utmost importance because these were intended as Kunstkammer objects, meant to be handled and examined closely. A lizard or crayfish cast through a gate at its thickest point, the underside of its belly, as would be common today (and is advised in other descriptions of lifecasting),12 would leave a large area of afterwork on the object’s belly, invisible to today’s museum visitors viewing it in a display behind glass, but unsuitable for a Kunstkammer object.

Making the mold

From molding lines on the objects, it is clear that the manuscript’s description of molding the animals in two-piece molds was common practice. Two-piece molds enabled removal of the animal from the mold to reuse it as a pattern,13 or in order to use the mold more than once,14 or to make possible removal of the ashes of the creature from of the mold after burnout (e.g., fols. 159v and 164v). Lizards cast from life in the Germanisches Nationalmuseum show molding lines along their bellies (Fig. 37), as do snakes in the Kunsthistorisches Museum (Fig. 38, Fig. 39).

Even the most unlikely creatures, such as stag beetles, were cast in two-piece molds. The rhinoceros and stag beetles atop the writing box in the Kunsthistorisches Museum both show molding lines (Fig. 40, Fig. 41), and Tonny Beentjes was able to create a very successful reconstruction of the rhinoceros beetle in silver using a two-piece mold (Fig. 42, Fig. 43).

In general, lizards, snakes, and large insects were cast in two-piece molds (Fig. 44, Fig. 45), while plants, flowers, and small insects — flies and beetles — were cast in one-piece, lost-pattern molds (Fig. 46, Fig. 47).

BnF Ms. Fr. 640 gives instructions for molding all large animals (including snakes, lizards, stag beetles, and rats) in two-piece molds. Birds, and particularly their feet (Fig. 48), had to be cast in piece molds. On fol. 116v, the author-practitioner notes that some people make a plaster piece-mold of a snake in a straight position, into which they then cast wax. They then twist the wax snake into whatever position they desire: “And others mold lengthwise a natural snake, without it being curled, with common plaster, reheated as said pu , en noyau & in two half molds, as said, then they cast it in wax. In that way they have snakes that they can twist as they like, and then they cast them in metal, as is said” (fol. 116v).15

The manuscript also contains instructions for making casts of amphibians, in particular toads and turtles, by a process of indirect casting. Indirect casting involves making a wax inter-model in a plaster mold for which an animal formed the original pattern, filling this wax model with a core of refractory (heat-resistant) material held in place by wires or pins, and then investing (covering) the wax pattern and its core with plaster. The wax is then melted out and metal is cast into the void space left by the wax. The surface of the piece resulting from this indirect cast will always be slightly less sharply detailed than the solid-cast animal, but this process has the advantage of producing an object that uses less metal, is lighter, and cools more evenly, resulting in fewer casting flaws. Because the description of this process in the manuscript is not nearly as detailed as, for example, the positioning of the animals on the clay base, it is unclear whether the author believed this process was self-evident, or whether he was not practiced in it. Moreover, the author provides a rather unusual version of indirect casting and frequently makes the point that although larger animals—toads, rats, birds, and turtles—should be cast hollow so they are not too weighty, it is a very time-consuming process, mentioning that in the time it takes to mold one turtle hollow, dozens of flower molds could be made (fols. 150v–151r).

For producing hollow casts of turtles, the author specifies that the animal should be molded in a two-piece or multipiece mold as usual, and then burned out (fol. 144r–v, 146v–147r, 148v, 150v–151v). The ashes should be cleaned out and then the mold reassembled. While it is still open, a metal wire should be threaded through the hollow center of the mold. At this point, a hole is made in the hardened plaster in the middle of the belly side of the mold and a raised lip formed around it. The mold is then reassembled and clamped tightly. A mixture of half tallow and half wax is slushed in through the hole in the mold. When the excess wax is poured out and hardened, a liquid core is poured into the mold through the same hole. This core is to be composed of the same material as the original mold (made of plaster, brick, iron filings, and feather alum). The raised lip of the hole is sheared off, the mold luted with the original plaster mixture, and the wax gently melted out through the hole in the mold. The mold is now ready to be cast with a mixture of lead and tin. Without the illustrations, it would be hard to make sense of this process (Fig. 49).

The author-practitioner offers another tantalizingly fragmentary method for indirect casts of small snakes and lizards that involves coating the mold with butter, then lining the inside of the mold with a “lasagne” of paste (fol. 130v). Plat’s Jewell House explains the process more clearly: line the insides of a mold with a thin paste composed of “tough” flour and water and made into a uniform thickness with a rolling pin; brush the paste with a thin coating of melted bacon grease; place wires through the mold lengthwise and crosswise, then pour the plaster into this egg-shaped cavity (which forms the core). Finally, take apart the two parts of the mold and remove the paste; hang the core in the mold again, using the crossed wires to hold it in place; and then lute the entire mold, anneal it, and pour the metal into it.16

Burning Out the Organic Matter

Although the author-practitioner mentions that fresh animals could be removed from their two-piece mold, it seems he mostly burned them out, noting that some were harder to burn out than others. Bird feet, for example, were impossible to burn, thus needed to be molded in a piece mold in order to remove them for the metal pour (fol. 157v). Crayfish were “awkward” to burn in the mold (fol. 142r). Plants molded in a one-piece mold always necessitated burning out, but the process of removing the ashes could be difficult. The process of burning out also melted out the wax gate, sprues, and vents to create a void through which the metal could enter the mold.

After your molds have their gate, vents & clamps, as is said, make at the end of the forge in, or in some clean place, a surrounding of bricks, and fill it with large half-lit charcoals. And place your molds on top, and leave the charcoals thus to kindle by themselves for a half hour, so that they heat little by little and not all at once. Finally, as they will begin to become white, beat little by little the thus half-lit large charcoals on top & blow with your little bellows until they are warmed. And thus, little by little, invigorate the fire, without haste, until your molds are quite red on the outside & the inside. If it is for casting some plant or some animal which does not release well, & if, for this reason, one needs to burn it in the mold, & when you look through the gate, & through it, you see the molds are very red & inflamed inside, this will be your signal that they are reheated enough. But if you do not see this signal, increase & continue the fire until it is the case. If there is nothing in the mold that needs burning, it will be enough that they are dried well on the same fire, if it is for casting tin. They reheat better in a reverberatory furnace. All the molds of flowers are reheated two times, first for burning the plants, flowers, and animal parts which are left inside. Next, one needs to clean them & remove the ashes. Second, reheat them & render them red for casting. At the beginning, reheat with some charcoals, gently lit, and put your noyau [i.e., plaster] molds flat on the charcoals surrounded by bricks (fol. 115v–116r).

Heating the Mold and Metal

Once the various parts of the mold were cleaned of all ashes and organic matter, they were then bound together with wire then “luted” (or, “invested”) with a jacket of “founders’ earth,” a mixture of clay, horse manure, and hair (fol. 142v). The parts of the mold were often painted with plaster (fol. 165v), especially at the joints of the molds, and held together with a press when metal was poured into them (Fig. 50).17

The details of the plant and animal surface textures could only be captured if the metal flowed into all of the smallest channels of the mold. To achieve this, the author-practitioner recounts much first hand experience of casting in tin and lead, metals with low melting points, and less frequently in silver and gold, although he provides detailed instructions for heating silver and gold on fol. 128r–v. He provides very clear, generally consistent directions for alloys: “For hollow things like turtles, half fine lead and half tin. For flowers, almost all tin, namely a lb of fine tin and two ℥ of fine lead. For lizards that are as thick as one or two fingers, almost all lead, namely a lb of fine lead and 4 ℥ of fine tin.” (fol. 150r).18 And, in another example:

Because tin wants to be cast very thinly, if your medal, plant, or other thing for molding is thin & fine, do it so that there is more tin, much more than lead, namely less than the fourth part lead for three parts tin. And still, one puts lead only to form an alloy. On the contrary, if you want to mold something strong & thick, put a lot more lead in than tin. And in one & the other you can put a little looking-glass tin, but only a little, with a little rosin, when you want to cast.

He then adds, “since then, when molding with fine and new lead, I put into one lb two ounces of fine tin. And when molding with fine tin, I put in two ℥ of fine lead for one lb. I made plants & snakes just like nature” (fol. 110v).

Although more lifecast objects in silver and in bronze have survived from the sixteenth century than in other metals, lifecasts in lead and tin are attested to in the inventories of the Wittelsbach and Habsburg Kunstkammern.19 Lead and tin produce fine impressions, and many medals were cast in these softer metals also, as well as in bronze or brass.20 The manuscript also emphasizes repeatedly that molds and metals must be heated “red hot” in order to fill all the small parts of the mold, however it also warns against overheating lead: “I cast tin almost red, and lead the same, which, however, had not remained in the fire for too long, for it becomes brittle and calcines” (fol. 110v).21

It appears that heating mold and metal to a high temperature to cast was a common practice, as is indicated by the dendritic surface texture of a sixteenth-century silver table bell in the British Museum. This shows that the molten metal cooled slowly, indicating that this delicate object was cast with very hot metal into a mold heated to a very high temperature.22

Finishing and Afterworking

In a long entry on “Repairing cast things,” the author-practitioner discusses chiseling off flashing, re-incising lines (e.g., in a turtle’s shell), and other elements of repair. In the crayfish entry, he notes, “Repair with penknives, files, little chaples [a type of chisel], or burins, &c” (fol. 142r). Delicate elements, such as the horns of the crayfish, that might not have cast successfully can be substituted “with whitened latten wire” and soldered on (fol. 141v).

He also gives instructions for painting the metal casts with oil paint, emphasizing the goal of lifelikeness: “To paint it, one does the middle of the back mixed with vermilion mixed with a little lake, and the sides & the belly & underneath the legs is with a flesh color made of vermilion, white of ceruse, a little yellow ocher. As in this & all other things, have always the natural one in front of you to imitate it” (fol. 141v). He advises comparing the color of the natural plant with the pigment by painting out directly on the leaf or flower:

For marigold flowers, lightly ground minium for some, & for others which are more yellowish, a bit of massicot with it. For green, the verdigris has depth & is too dark. If it is a yellowish green, you can mix with the verdigris a little yellow ocher & stil de grain yellow. If the green is dark, put with it charcoal of peach tree pits, which makes a v greenish black, in the same way that black of ox foot bone looks bluish. And thus, by judgment & discretion, put the color on the natural flower or leaf to see whether it comes close. But layer it transparently, so as not to cover the lines of the work (fol. 158v).

For painting on metal, he advises an oil binder, though, “because oil takes a long time to dry,” if you are in a hurry, use “not-too-thick gum” or “color with glair beaten with peelings of the fig tree....layer it thinly” (fol. 129r). Sixteenth-century lifecasts show the use of oil paint (Fig. 51, Fig. 52, Fig. 53).23

Casting from Life in the Making and Knowing Project

The Making and Knowing Project team carried out lifecasting experiments with students and expert maker, Andrew Lacey, in Spring 2015, and again with the core team in 2017.24 They based their work on the initial experiments of Pamela Smith and Tonny Beentjes, carried out between 2007 and 2011, in part with students in the Metalwork Program of the MA in Conservation, University of Amsterdam (Fig. 54). After locating and examining nearly all identifiable lifecast objects in European museums, Smith and Beentjes deciphered, interpreted, and attempted to follow the various entries in the manuscript for lifecasting insects, reptiles, and plants. The processes for lifecasting a branch of rosemary and a crayfish follow below.

Rosemary

The author-practitioner deals with the lifecasting of plants most thoroughly on fols. 117r and 145v–146r. Two methods are detailed in these entries, both using one-piece molds. In one, the plant lies horizontally, fixed into the encircling clay walls by its stem, and, in the other, it stands vertically, held up by threads. In “A means of molding flowers and plants” (fol. 117r), the reader is directed to “choose them in their full verdure & vivacity, & them mou not pick them, if possible, until the time one will want to mold them, so that they do not wilt. Or, if you need to carry them from afar, soak their stems in a bottle of water or, better still, wine.” The instructions continue with directions to reinforce the stem with iron wire, form a wax gate, and two vent channels formed with sticks, then to lay the plant halfway down into the encircling clay, with the wax gate pressed into the clay. The process is clearly illustrated in the marginal sketches (Fig. 55).25 The plant is painted with eau-de-vie, then the thin plaster “sand” is quickly cast into the mold, allowing “two good fingers” thickness of plaster above and below the plant. Once the plaster has set, the mold is heated to burn out the plant, sticks, and wax gate. Because it is a one-piece mold, it can sometimes be difficult to clean the mold entirely of the ashes, and the entry “For making the ashes of flowers and plants leave molds” on the following page (fol. 117v) deals with this problem. In “Molding plants and flowers” on fols. 145v–146r, the instructions are repeated in greater detail (Fig. 56), with fuller instructions for wetting the plant with eau-de-vie before casting, for the ingredients of the plaster mixture, for removing ashes from the mold before casting, for heating the mold before casting different alloys (tin-lead, silver, and gold), and for removing the cast from the mold (with the point of a knife and a sharp iron wire, or by soaking in water), and removing flashing with a penknife. This entry also refers to the author-practitioner’s first hand experience: “I have molded thus a marigold with its leaves” and “the alloy with which I cast a large branch of marigold, which came out neatly like the natural with its flower, its buds, and its leaves, was of one lb of fine tin mixed with two ounces of lead.”

Both entries for lifecasting of plants and flowers note that a thread passed through the plant and the clay walls can help hold the plant firmly in the mold as the plaster is poured in so that the plant is neither crushed, nor rises up to the top of the mold with the liquid plaster: “When you mold them, if they are not strong enough to hold themselves upright, pass a thread through the mold to keep them from rising up” (fol. 145v). This can also be prevented by casting in “the sand little by little,” and blowing on the liquid “in order that it settles everywhere,” and preventing bubbles in the plaster. During group lifecasting under the direction of expert practitioner Andrew Lacy in 2015, art history student Sofia Gans used the instructions (with some variations) in fols. 117r and 145v–146r to lifecast a rosemary branch (Fig. 57, Fig. 58, Fig. 59, Fig. 60, Fig. 61, Fig. 62, Fig. 63, Fig. 64, Fig. 65, Fig. 66, Fig. 67, Fig. 68, Fig. 69, Fig. 70, Fig. 71, Fig. 72).26

Crayfish

The entry on the lifecasting of crayfish is an extremely densely written section running from fol. 141r through 142r. The author-practitioner includes much first hand experience in this entry, and he views the posing and molding of crayfish as a model for beetles, turtles, and crabs, citing the fine points of the crayfish techniques extensively in the instructions for other types of animals. Like so many of the lifecasting entries, he begins that on crayfish with observations that combine natural historical observation with molding advice:

The crayfish is one of the most fanciful to mold, but also provides an example for casting many other very difficult things. Males are distinguished by the eggs that females carry and by the four legs little white leglets at the back that males ap have inside their tail, after the eight main legs. It is only a good thing to dry them out a little, because the lumps only come out hard rougher & more beautiful, for everything that is of shell does not diminish. It is true that if you let them dry too much, these little inside leglets diminish & become more slender & do not have as much body. If also they are too dry, the flesh separates from the scales. Take heed, therefore, to use moderation (fol. 141r).

The long entry goes on to provide a comprehensive guide to posing, molding, and burning out the crayfish, along with tips on how to repair and paint the cast. Missing from this account is the actual metal casting, however many other entries treat the casting of metals into lifecast molds. Two helpful illustrations indicate how to position the crayfish and to create the gates (fols. 142v and 141r) (see figs. 26–27).

Traces of Lifecasting in and around Toulouse

Although extant examples of lifecasting are common in northern Europe, French examples are very rare. Louis XIV’s need for bullion in the seventeenth century led to a campaign to melt down almost all earlier works in silver and gold. An exception was Wenzel Jamnitzer’s large basin that survived in the royal collections (Fig. 160, Fig. 161).

Toulouse was the site of the literary contest known as the Jeux Floraux (Floral Games), held since 1323, at which lifelike gold and silver flowers, including the marigold, wild rose, and violet, were awarded to the winners. In the late sixteenth century, two Toulouse goldsmiths were responsible for creating these flowers: Jérôme Véret in 1576–1582 and 1584–1589, and Arnaud Cornayre in 1583. Sources are not explicit about whether these flowers were lifecast at this time, but it would appear likely.29

Lifelike lizards cast on bell waists exist in various locales in France (Fig. 162, Fig. 163), although further research is needed to determine whether these were cast from life. Professor of Art History Pascal Julien discovered nearly a dozen such bells in the Landes area of Aquitaine, which all date to the eighteenth century.

He also located bells with lizards in the villages of Provence and Languedoc, including a bell in Congénies cast in 1759 by a founder named Poutingon, which features a large lizard (Fig. 164, Fig. 165).
