Abstract

Ms. Fr. 640 contains several entries for making metals “run.” Fluidly running metals are essential in casting. These entries, including “Making gold run for casting” on fol. 106r, are among the most carefully observed and most laboriously written sections in Ms. Fr. 640. Like lifecasting and gem making entries in other parts of the manuscript, they appear to have been at the heart of the author-practitioner’s interests and his firsthand experimentation.

Making Metals Run

Ms. Fr. 640, fol. 106r, contains an entry for “Making gold run for casting,” which describes how to make and employ a substance that the author-practitioner says will maintain the heat of gold during casting so that the gold will run fluidly into all parts of a mold and the cast will “come out neatly.” This is especially important to the author-practitioner because he sees pure gold as very “dry” (sec): “When gold is very fine, it is so dry that it can hardly endure a hammer [i.e., it is very brittle and will crack on being hammered], but this mixture softens [adoulcist] it like lead.” Today we would call this substance a flux, which we see as cleaning the surface of the metal from oxides and causing it to run more fluidly through the mold, as defined in a twentieth-century manual:

A flux is a substance applied to the surface of molten metal primarily to prevent oxygen in the air from coming in contact with the hot metal, to cause oxidation. In addition, the flux dissolves oxides, which may form while the metal is being heated […]. A flux in powder form is normally used for metals during the melting for a casting operation […]. The powder fluxes may contain finely divided charcoal or other ingredients mixed with borax and boric acid to give added protection by producing what is described as a reducing flux. The presence of the charcoal not only helps to prevent the formation of oxides on the metal surface, but also tends to reduce to free metal any oxides that may have been formed. Fluxes in powder form may contain also a very small percentage (1 to 2%) of finely divided silica flour which aids in holding the molten flux in position on the surface of the hot metal.1

The author-practitioner conceived of his substance to make gold run as cleaning, or “clarifying” the gold, but he saw the more important function of the substance as heating and softening (or, literally, “sweetening”) the metal. This comes through clearly in the entry on fol. 106r because this recipe appears to reflect two campaigns of writing by the author-practitioner: He first titled the recipe simply “Gold,” and it dealt with how to remedy gold’s characteristic of cooling down too quickly (making it unable to fill all parts of a mold). While a mixture he calls “sublimate” works well to make gold run, it goes up in smoke, so he gives an alternative substance that keeps gold warm. It contains sal ammoniac (ammonium chloride), verdet (verdigris), borax, and saltpeter (potassium nitrate):

Gold

Because gold cools down very quickly, one ought to give it a mixture when melted q it is well melted which maintains the heat. Sublimate softens it nicely. But, because it goes up in smoke, it does not continue to help. Therefore, mix this composition when you want to cast: sal ammoniac, the best verdet that you can find, a little borax & saltpeter qui li . And this, by keeping it warm, it will come out neatly.

Then, in a different ink, and probably at a later time, the author-practitioner added further information to the title to make it more specific, “Making gold run for casting,” and, apparently at the same time, provided key information about the effects of the ingredients—noting that saltpeter clarifies and heats the molten gold, and commenting that the verdigris is the most important part of the recipe, with the result that gold runs “like lead”:

Saltpeter clarifies it & p heats it. But the most important thing in this mixture is the verdet, which has to be good. This mixture softens the gold qui so much that it becomes treatable like lead, even fine gold,”

In the margin, he then summed up the effect that the substance will have on gold: “When gold is very fine [fort fin], it is so dry [sec] that it can hardly endure a hammer, but this mixture softens [ladoulcist] it like lead.” He returns to emphasize this information about gold in its purest state in “Fine gold,” on fol. 124v:

There is a property of gold which, even though it is very fine & unalloyed & has been passed through aquafortis & antimony, is nevertheless so brittle that it hardly withstands the hammer. And the composition of verdet, described above to make gold run, makes it as soft as lead.

Writing, Observing, Experimenting

The author-practitioner’s intensive engagement with this recipe, indicated by his successive writing campaigns during which he added further information after what may have been his firsthand trials with the process, can be found in several other recipes that involve making metals “run,” including gold, silver, copper, latten, and tin. Indeed, the entries on making silver run on fols. 120v and 123r–v, as well as the additional entries for making gold run on fols. 120r, 124r–v, 128r–v, and 134v are some of the most closely observed and laboriously described accounts of processes in the entire manuscript, surpassed perhaps only by the lifecasting entries. These entries take up entire folios, and the numerous deletions and interlinear additions of further observations make it clear that the author-practitioner labored over describing the process. Moreover, the language in many of them indicates that he returned to them after firsthand trial.

Among the recipes for making metals run, fol. 123r–v is a particularly comprehensive example. It lists the ingredients in classic recipe fashion, with measurements for each substance, careful step-by-step procedures, including even the necessary ingredients for luting the flask (i.e., coating it with earth to protect it from breakage) in which the substance will be heated, as well as timing for the entire process. This entry also includes information about the hazards of the substances, including a means to protect oneself from this dangerous substance:

Make each time a good quantity of this substance, so as not to do it often, because the fumes, which are dangerous, could hurt you. And before working at it, take in the morning good buttered toast, and hold the said butter, or zedoary, or gold coins, in your mouth, and ada cover your face with a cloth from the eyes down.

He even suggests an alternative to these personal protective measures: “Or else [...] have had your drugs pestled by some rustic,” and “have the fire managed by a shop boy familiar with charcoal.”

Unusually in Ms. Fr. 640, the author-practitioner exhibits an attempt at secrecy in this carefully described recipe on fol. 123r–v: he writes the title as, “A means di far correr lotnegra” (Moyen di far correr lotnegra), including a phrase in Italian, “di far correr lotnetgra,” with “silver” (argento) written backwards, which all translates as “A means to make silver run.” This rather transparent code is found only a couple of times in the manuscript. This entry is also unusual because, just as the author-practitioner does not usually specify that processes should be kept secret, he also does not frequently mention financial aspects of processes, yet he does both in this recipe for the substance to make silver run:

Goldsmiths who have knowledge of this substance sell a denier of it for 5 sous to other goldsmiths, & especially to those who work in plate and large wares, for at the end of their works, they can solder over the first solder by means of this mixture [...]. This material should not be divulged, lest it be abused.”

On fol. 121v, in another entry on “Silver for casting,” he mentions again that “If the goldsmiths knew the substance to make silver run in their works, they would buy much of it. Some buy it five sous for a denier.” The repeated interest in the commercial potential of a material is missing from much of the rest of the manuscript.

Another recipe for making metals run, “Casting with gold and silver,” on fol. 128r–v, is similarly detailed, laboriously recorded, and refers back to the recipe for “making silver run” on fol. 123r–v. Another long and detailed entry follows immediately after “Casting with gold and silver” on fol. 128v for “Whitening of cast silver.” This fascinating entry describes in great detail the procedure of “an excellent German” in lifecasting a lizard, and his secret for whitening silver. In both these densely-written, very long entries that flow over multiple folios, there appears to be a combination of both firsthand experimentation and his recording of practices he has witnessed. On fol. 128v, a marginal notes admonishes, “do not let yourself forget to put, before all things, a little borax in the melted silver, for even though goldsmiths do not put any in, nevertheless it is good, and I have seen it practiced well.”

The laboriousness of the author-practitioner’s efforts in setting down in writing these processes for making precious metals run, and the mix of language contained in them indicating both firsthand and observed experience, make it difficult to know whether the author-practitioner is speaking as a goldsmith—these substances are certainly of prime interest to a goldsmith—or as an interested observer. These recipes certainly give the impression of expert, professional, and even commercial interest in this area. The author-practitioner’s expertise in other knowledge domains of goldsmithing is also demonstrated in his casting and gem-making recipes.2

Whatever the case of his expertise may be, the author-practitioner is quite consistent in the substance that he recommends for making gold run. Besides the entry on fol. 106r, there are two other recipes, on fols. 120r and 134v, that refer specifically to a mixture of borax, sal ammoniac, saltpeter and verdigris to heat gold and keep it hot while casting. On fol. 120r—another densely worked-over page—he includes specific information about German practices in the margins, and mentions, like on fol. 106r, that sublimate heats and cleans “gold because by its exhalations, it draws everything out as it goes up in smoke,” but only the verdigris mixture conserves its heat and “makes it run”:

Sand for casting in gold

You could cast gold well in common sand of goldsmiths, should you throw therein some substance that makes it run. Before the invention of crocum, one would cast flowers in silver but not at all in gold. It has not been forty years that one knows this in Germany.

***

Sublimate is commonly employed by goldsmiths for gold. Some add sulfur, but they & others are wrong, for sulfur sours, even as it heats. And the sublimate is agitated, boils, and bubbles. It is very good to clean gold because by its exhalations, it draws everything out as it goes up in smoke. But to warm gold & conserve its heat, there is only the couleur, which is verdet, sal ammoniac, saltpeter & borax. This makes it run, & you can throw in a branch of wormseed.

One wonders how he knows this particular detail about substances used by Germans.3

On fol. 134v, in the entry “For making ☀ [gold] run,” the author-practitioner provides more specific information about the amounts of these ingredients that should be used:

Verdigris & sal ammoniac, as much of one as the other, borax & saltpeter, as much of one as the other. But the s borax & sel armo saltpeter together must only weigh half of the others, that is to say, as much together as the verdigris. Grind them finely into powder on marble, then grind them again with some good strong vinegar until it is fine, like a couleur and verdet ground in oil. Next, leave it to dry on its own, if you have time, or in the fire lent of the forge, & make sure it is well dry.

Our cursory search for similar recipes in contemporary sources showed that goldsmiths had used fluxes4 for centuries: Benvenuto Cellini mentions adding charcoal to lead, silver, and copper when making niello to purify the mixture.5 He also mentions using a combination of borax and verdigris for soldering silver. Borax is mentioned by several makers during the early modern period (including the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640), as are sal ammoniac and saltpeter,6 but our preliminary research did not turn up any other sources that mentioned using all four materials mixed together. Much more research is necessary, however, to determine this point with any certainty.

Reconstruction

Materials

We “grew” our own verdigris, using a recipe derived from the fifteenth-century collection of painting recipes by Jehan le Begue,7 leaving new copper plates standing above vinegar for several weeks (Fig. 1).8 A thin layer of vibrant green “efflorescence” appeared on the copper after about twelve days (Fig. 2), and we harvested the verdigris after thirty-five days (Fig. 3). It fell cleanly off in a powder when we simply bent the sheets (Fig. 4). Three sheets of approximately 6x8 inches and one sheet of approximately 8x14 inches yielded about 8 ml (by volume) of verdigris powder (Fig. 5), which we then ground in a mortar and pestle to ensure there were no larger clumps (Fig. 6).

Process

To make the substance, we combined a ½ teaspoon of verdigris with a ¼ teaspoon of borax, saltpeter, and sal ammoniac, respectively (Fig. 7).9 We guessed at these amounts because no detail was given in the recipe except “a little borax & saltpeter,” and, at the time of reconstructing this process, we had not noticed the instructions of ratios on fol. 134v. We chose to add more verdigris than the other ingredients because the recipe specifically states that, “the most important thing in this mixture is the verdet, which has to be good.”

Because the mixture is said to soften “the gold so much that it becomes treatable like lead, even fine gold,” we used 22 carat gold.10 In our understanding, gold that is purer has a higher melting point, but, for the author-practitioner, pure gold was “dry,” thus making it hard to melt until it was made soft and sweet.11

We used a propane torch to heat gold in a ceramic crucible.12 As the pieces of gold began melting, they drew together into a molten globule. However, this globule was somewhat flat and oxides were visible on its surface, giving it a soiled appearance. Once our expert maker, Dr. Boyd, felt that the gold and the crucible were hot enough, we added our verdigris mixture, with a dramatic result: the flame around the crucible flashed greenish yellow, and a pungent, acidic odor arose from the crucible. The substance appeared momentarily to sizzle on the surface of the gold and the crucible, and then the oxide on the surface of the gold disappeared, and the gold drew together into more of a round shape.

In order to test how well the metal flowed, we molded two different shapes: a small cross shape with bulbous ends through which the metal could flow easily, and a large unembellished ring that we thought would prove more challenging for the flow. The addition of the verdigris substance did visibly “clarify” the gold, and the pour into our first mold, the small cross, worked perfectly on the first try (Fig. 8). Our second mold, the ring, took three tries for the gold to flow completely through it, but eventually we were also able to get a successful cast of the shape (Fig. 9).13 While these trials do not prove the efficacy of this substance, it performed so similarly to the flux in use by the dental office of our expert maker that Dr. Boyd jokingly asked if it were available for purchase. According to him, “it worked exactly the same” as his usual flux, presumably aside from the flash of green and the acidic smell.
