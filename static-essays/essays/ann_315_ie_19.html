A Fragmentary Goldsmithing Manuscript from Seventeenth-Century Toulouse

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/7hbr-by50
← Back to Essays

Abstract

The Archives municipales of Toulouse hold an unpublished fragment of an anonymous seventeenth-century manuscript that compiles goldsmiths’ statutes, recipes from a goldsmithing workshop, and knowledge on stones and minerals. As part of the Mint’s collection, it raises questions related primarily to its source and use. Through analysis of the manuscript’s eight folios, this essay compares it with Ms. Fr. 640. In contrast to Ms. Fr. 640’s many recipes, this document shows a more heterogeneous character, however, some of its recipes, as well as its lively interest in gemstones, display surprising similarities with Ms. Fr. 640.

The Archives municipales of Toulouse hold an unpublished document linked to the Mint’s collection that appears to shed light on the context in which BnF Ms. Fr. 640 was written.1 This fragment of an anonymous manuscript in which the beginning and end are missing consists of four sheets folded in half, creating eight consecutive folios relating to goldsmithing. Each of the four sheets is numbered on the first recto, the second recto of each sheet has no number. The first sheet of the fragment is numbered 17, which means that the first thirty-two leaves are missing. This essay focuses on the context of this manuscript, on the sources used to write it, and on a better understanding of its origin and purpose. Comparisons with Ms. Fr. 640 give insight into the purpose and use of both documents.

Composition and Dating

An examination of the paper, the writing, and the constitution of this document allows us to place it in its context. The fragment includes different subjects related to goldsmithing. The first folio and the recto of the second folio contain the last articles of goldsmiths’ statutes (Fig. 1). Then, fourteen recipes, spread over three to four folios, deal with the production of gilding and etching, and the use of gold, silver, copper, and mastic. Then, four to five more folios are devoted to knowledge about twenty-one stones (Fig. 2). Finally, the fragment contains the first paragraphs of more general knowledge about minerals, of which only four are preserved (Fig. 3). All the themes follow one another, without the author having used a new folio to start a new theme.

The watermark of the paper used, which shows a hand, allows us to date this manuscript to the sixteenth century or the first half of the seventeenth century. No exact correspondence was found with the hundreds of watermarks listed in the studies of Briquet, Gaudriault and Nicolaï.2 The hand is one of the printers’ watermarks with the most numerous variations (hand only, blessing, with or without scalloped cuff, surmounted by a star, flower, crown or cross). It was used in France over a very long period, particularly in the southwest from the Poitou to the Pyrénées. Such paper with the hand watermark dominated this area in the sixteenth century until 1620, when it was gradually replaced by the fleur-de-lis.3 Moreover, examination of the writing, all in a single hand, allows us to confirm the dating of the document to the first half of the seventeenth century.4

The subjects treated in the fragment indicate that it belonged to a manuscript intended for goldsmiths, as it contains a diversity of themes which, as mentioned in several printed sources, were necessary for this profession. Treatises on goldsmithing were relatively rare in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries. The most famous is that of Benvenuto Cellini, printed in 1568, which did not have a French translation until the nineteenth century.5 The contents of Cellini’s Italian treatise shows the diverse knowledge a book on goldsmithing could contain. Cellini first discusses stones, then he details processes, such as niello production, enameling, and carving. After that, he mentions the striking of medals and the making of vases, then gives several recipes for gilding and making etchings. The Toulouse manuscript fragment and Cellini’s treatise correspond mainly in the presence of these types of recipes, as well as knowledge about gemstones.

More general works also inform us about the knowledge that goldsmiths had to acquire. In his 1598 Miroir universel des arts et des sciences, Fioravanti explained, in a chapter on “The art of the goldsmith:”

These are the things that belong to the workers of this art: first of all to know how to melt gold, silver & other metals; then, knowing how to refine them in their own place, how to divide the gold from silver, how to form & cast all kinds of raised & relief works; soldering & coloring gold & making white silver, having full knowledge of all stones & knowing how to set them, both in gold, as well as in other metal, knowing how to gild silver, bronze & other metals; knowing how to draw, & working with a chisel, both in relief, as well as in hollow. They need to know many other things that I don’t really know about.6

A manual on goldsmithing should thus contain this range of subjects, some of which are found in the manuscript fragment.

While most of the knowledge in this fragment, then, is intended for goldsmiths, the last section on minerals and its location in the Mint’s collection, seems to indicate a more complex purpose.

Origin and Purpose

Several elements give an idea of the origin and purpose of the manuscript: the brevity of the recipes and the limited information about the production processes, compared to that contained in printed books, suggest that these recipes were written or dictated by a goldsmith and that they were intended for a professional. The difference from published books on goldsmithing is also observed in the discussion of the statutes at the start of the fragment. The joint presence of recipes and goldsmiths’ statutes is not reflected in the treatises printed in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries.7 This significant difference seems to indicate that this text was probably not intended for publication.

The examination of these statutes makes it possible to better situate the context in which the manuscript was written. These regulations contain information that is found in most of the statutes of goldsmiths in sixteenth- and seventeenth-century France, and is common to several cities. For example, they specify that it is prohibited to use fake stones, work in a secret place, and open a shop on Sundays and during holidays. On the other hand, several entries show that they are the statutes of the city of Paris, not those of Toulouse, for example, “No apprentice from Paris can be received to make a masterpiece to be received in said city if he has not served his master for eight full years;”8 and “All the receptions of master goldsmiths are held in the Mint of the city of Paris.”9 In Toulouse, the apprenticeship of goldsmiths was set at six years in the statutes of 1537, 1550, and 1617. The fragment states the term of apprenticeship at eight years, as specified in the Parisian statutes of 1612.10 The Prévôt of Paris reaffirmed this term in 1633, because some Parisian goldsmiths were known to take apprentices under the title of servant and keep them in apprenticeship for less than eight years.

Other statutes in the fragment similarly confirm its context in goldsmiths’ ordinances: they describe a liberty taken by goldsmiths of melting coins in order to use them in their works, which had led to a shortage of coins in trade and an increase in the exchange rate. This statement might refer to an edict of Louis XIII of 1636 which reported “the excessive manufacture of a quantity of goldsmith’s works of extraordinary & excessive weight.”11 Such descriptions of excesses on the part of goldsmiths could thus bring the manuscript closer to the 1630s.

The discourse on stones, which follows the recipes, is explicitly addressed to goldsmiths and lapidaries, while that on minerals is addressed to the Général des mines (General of Mines)12 and, more generally, to all those in charge of mining. Some descriptions of minerals and some articles of the statutes, however, make several references to the Mint and the minting of coins. The author points out the utility of minerals in the manufacture of coins. At the beginning of his discourse on minerals, he explains: “The number of mines is not fully known. Each one is only in part related. Without the knowledge of the others, they remain useless, and it would be impossible to come to the perfect knowledge of metals and other lesser things that help and aid in the perfection of all coins.”13 Similarly, the author notes about the magnet stone: “One cannot take the plan of a place, much less find the waters, nor the hidden treasures without it, nor can the refiners make the separations of the washings of the coins, or the goldsmiths after their sorting in the wooden shell, [for] all the metal is mixed with the iron scrapings which come from the chisel or the burin or from elsewhere.”14 The Mint is also mentioned in the statutes concerning the liberties taken by goldsmiths. Taking all this together, it would seem the purpose of the manuscript is oriented toward minting coins.

The city of Toulouse had a privilege to mint coins, and the Mint, located on Place du Salin, was placed under royal authority, and thus under the jurisdiction of the Court of the Mint of Paris.15 Conflicts between the Royal Court of the Mint and the capitouls of Toulouse are regularly reported in documents. For example, the statutes of the goldsmiths of Toulouse of 1536, sent to Paris for approval by the generals of the Paris Mint, saw the authority of the capitouls disappear to the advantage of that of the Court of the Mint,16 leaving only the Generals of the Mints able to apply these statutes to the members of the Toulouse goldsmiths’ guild. After having brought the case before the King’s Council and having reminded the Council that control of all trades was conferred on them, the capitouls were once more given authority in the 1550 statutes, with the officers of the Paris Mint no longer mentioned.17

Other conflicts arose during the period of the Catholic League, in the late 1580s and early 1590s, when Toulouse sided with the League against the royal power. A decision was taken to abolish the Toulouse Mint, which was transferred to Pamiers, which, however, issued no coin.18 The Toulouse Mint then acted against the royal authority by making coins bearing the effigy of then King Henry III and issuing them illegally. After several further events that led to conflicts, Henri IV published his peace edict in January 1596 and the Toulouse Mint was then placed under the authority of the King and the orders of the Paris Court of the Mint.19

New conflicts between Toulouse and the Paris Mint arose in the first decades of the seventeenth century, and led to the drafting of new statutes in 1617.20 Further articles were written in the following decades but unfortunately none of the statutes established between 1617 and 1689 are preserved.21 Similarly, no document making it possible to understand the relationship between the Mint and the goldsmiths of Toulouse is extant for the first half of the seventeenth century. Previous conflicts often show a requirement imposed on the goldsmiths of the Toulouse Mint to act in greater concert with the rules of the Paris Court of the Mint. If this manuscript fragment did indeed belong to the Toulouse Mint, the presence of the Parisian statutes at the beginning of the manuscript could thus testify to a repeated reminder about royal authority.

If this manuscript is associated with the Mint, the discourse on minerals can also be explained. From the fifteenth century, the Mint administration also directed the exploitation of the mines in the Toulouse region. This double administration, maintained until the seventeenth century, was due to the needs of the Mint to obtain supplies of metals.22 In Toulouse, the General of Mines was thus in charge of transporting the metal to the Mint, as shown by the example of the architect Dominique Bertin.23 In the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, several individuals belonging to different Mints in France possessed official positions related to mining. François Garrault, who published a book on the Mines d’argent trouvées en France in 1579, was General of the King’s Mines, committed to the Stewardship of the Kingdom’s Gold, Silver and Lead Mines and General of the Mint Court.24 In his book, he explains that the role of the General of Mines exercised “oversight of the work of all mines, & power to have all others searched that will be found in some parts of the country.”25 In 1600, Jean Malus, Master of the Mint of Bordeaux, was also sent by Henry IV to the Pyrenées to explore the mines. He then produced a manuscript on the Recherches des mines des Pyrénées which was later printed by Jean Du Puy in 1601.26 Later, in 1641, Sébastien Guichard was both an Essayeur at the Grenoble Mint and a General Contractor of Mines in the Dauphiné region.27

A connection of the Toulouse Mint appears to explain the diversity of the topics covered (statutes of goldsmiths, goldsmith recipes, discourses on stones and minerals) in the manuscript fragment. Among these subjects, the presence of recipes about goldsmithing and the discussion of stones echoes Ms. Fr. 640.

Comparison with Ms. Fr. 640

Comparison between the recipes in the fragment and those on gold and silver work in Ms. Fr. 640 provide a better understanding of the purpose and objectives of these two documents. The fourteen recipes are one of only two complete parts of the fragment. Unlike Ms. Fr. 640, they are very few in number and only reflect the work of the goldsmith, in contrast to the many professions mentioned in Ms. Fr. 640, including, among others, painters, dyers, and founders. The recipes in the fragment would seem to be the most common in the field of goldsmithing. They are almost exclusively intended for gold work, including gilding on silver, copper, and iron. The fragment also contains information on etching metal, as well as polishing metal, and applying gums of different colors to color the metal objects. The titles of some recipes refer directly to works of goldsmithing, such as gold rings, church crosses, and other “molded works.”28

Similarities and differences can be observed between the recipes of the two documents. On the one hand, the instructions given for some recipes indicate similar ingredients and processes, with only a few differences. For example, the ingredients of a recipe for making a “good mixture to color gold” (“bonne saulce”) (fol. 104r), which specifies as much sulfur as small gravel, with the addition of salt, are the same as in the fragment, the only difference being the addition of terra merita by the author-practitioner.29 In the recipe for making “couleur” (fol. 135r), both manuscripts use as much verdigris as sal ammoniac, and twice as much saltpeter.30 On the other hand, other recipes are very different. In the recipe for making etching solution (fol. 75r), the author-practitioner uses vinegar, verdigris, copperas and solle, while the anonymous author of the fragment uses rock salt, alum de plume, glass alum, verdigris, and salt.31 Similarly, in the recipe for gilding on iron or tin (fol. 76v), the author-practitioner advises to “wash that which one wants to gild in wine lees, and then wash it again with white wine,” whereas the author of the fragment advises to wash it in fountain or river water that is boiled.32 In addition, other recipes are absent from Ms. Fr. 640, such as those for “gilding silver and copper,” “drawing gold gilding from all kinds of silver work,” and “making good royal copper.”33

Beyond the differences in the wording of the recipes, certain details contained in the titles of the fragment refer to the statutes, an attention to norms that does not characterize Ms. Fr. 640. For example, the first recipe of the fragment is entitled “To gild on silver and copper when a goldsmith has the power and permission to do so.”34 In contrast, Ms. Fr. 640 provides recipes for counterfeiting jasper, coral, emeralds, rubies, sapphires, and diamonds (fols. 3r, 10r, 100v, 138v).

Another section that has been entirely preserved in the Toulouse fragment is a discourse on stones. Following a long introduction on the creation of mineral elements, based on sacred texts, including the book of Genesis and Saint Augustine’s writings, and texts of Antiquity, including the Pimander attributed to Hermes Trismegistus, twenty-one stones are clearly described according to their appearance and origin, properties, and virtues. The order in which the stones and theoretical content are presented is similar to books by scholars such as Anselme Boèce de Boodt,35 Johann Jakob Wecker,36 and Étienne Binet.37 On the contrary, several entries in Ms. Fr. 640 concern some of the same stones but have a more technical or practical bent. They give instructions, for example, to polish stones, set, or imitate them (fols. 2v–3r, 8v, 12v, 38r, 100v–101v, 138v). In addition, Ms. Fr. 640 and the fragment treat different stones, such as jasper and amber, which are mentioned in Ms. Fr. 640, but are completely absent from the Toulouse fragment. The Toulouse fragment thus displays a theoretical perspective on stones, which is also true for the fragmentary section on minerals, while the rest of the document has a legislative and technical character. This is quite different from Ms. Fr. 640.

The section on minerals, which only deals with mercury, vitriol, saltpeter, and sulfur, also shows the theoretical orientation: it begins with a presentation on the genesis of minerals, then explains the characteristics and properties of each mineral. Similar to some chemistry texts, such as those by Jean Béguin and Pierre-Jean Fabre,38 it does not, however, contain the techniques to produce ointments and dyes which are found in these texts. The fragment is reminiscent of seventeenth-century treatises on alchemy, in particular because of the discussion of the mythological figure of Demogorgon, which became established at the end of the sixteenth century as a symbol of the universal spirit of the world, the essence of the elements, and the celestial Mercury.39 The author of Ms. Fr. 640, for his part, does not devote any entries to a theoretical treatment of minerals. Whatever “theory” there is in Ms. Fr. 640, for example, about the genesis of stones, must be extracted from resolutely practical instructions and comments.40 Thus a principal difference between Ms. Fr. 640 and the Toulouse fragment is between their contrasting theoretical and practical orientations.

The structure and the synthesis of the knowledge reflected in the Toulouse fragment suggest that it was written for a practical purpose within the guild. The discourse on stones is sometimes adapted to the goldsmith’s profession, as when the author explains how to recognize counterfeit rubies. Although the fragment states that it was forbidden for goldsmiths to use imitation gems, archival documents from Toulouse show that goldsmiths sometimes used them. For example, in 1625, Philippe de Béthune’s niece, Louise, commissioned Raymond Masse to make two false sapphires.41 The author of the fragment has consciously ordered his treatise—in contrast to Ms. Fr. 640—pointing out that there are a large number of gemstones but that he chooses to talk only about those that are commonly used in Europe. He then invites the curious to “a more accurate search.”42 Similarly, he justifies the brevity of his discourse, which is nevertheless relatively developed, by pointing out that, “To clarify this formation more precisely, a broader explanation is needed, but since it is only a question of showing where the minerals originated, it suffices to give a simple illumination of them.”43 He explains that his motivation is to train the reader, perhaps an apprentice, by providing concise knowledge about stones and minerals, without linking them to alchemical recipes.

Conclusion

This manuscript fragment, which compiles legal texts, recipes, knowledge about stones and minerals, and which was probably accompanied by further similar subjects, therefore appears to be in the form of a practical manual. Perhaps destined for the Mint, it is mainly addressed to goldsmiths but also to lapidaries and the General of Mines.

The paper, the script, and its composition link it to the Mint of Toulouse, and make it possible to propose a dating of this fragmentary manuscript around the 1630s. The concision of the recipes suggests that it is not a manuscript intended for printing but rather a manual potentially useful for a workshop. It illustrates the kind of written knowledge regarded as necessary to a guild workshop, including the rules and statutes of the profession, manufacturing techniques, and a more theoretical knowledge about materials. In comparison, Ms. Fr. 640, would not seem to be connected to a guild workshop, but rather to form a set of notes for direct employment of practical techniques, some of which overlapped with those of the Toulouse fragment, but which originated in a different context and was intended for a different purpose.
