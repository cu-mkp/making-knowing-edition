Sand Molds of Ox Bone, Wine, and Elm Root

By , DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/sz36-aj97
← Back to Essays

Abstract

BnF Ms. Fr. 640 features several recipes for making “sand” molds. They usually involve the combination of a dry element with a wet binding agent, often referred to as “magistra.” This essay examines the sand casting technique described on fol. 84v that involves the use of a mixture of calcined and pulverized ox foot bone with elm root boiled in wine. While ox foot bone is referenced in the manuscript as an ideal medium for sand casting, the particular properties of elm root boiled in wine as a binder are less clear. In our reconstruction of the recipe entitled “Sand,” we sought not only to shed light on the ingredients and procedures involved in the preparation of the elm root and wine binder but also to consider the nature and processes by which knowledge is produced in the Making and Knowing laboratory.

BnF Ms. Fr. 640 features various recipes for making “sand” molds, typically involving the combination of a dry element and a wet binding agent, the latter often referred to as a “magistra.” While nearly all sand molding recipes feature this basic combination, the substances that can fulfill the role of “dry” or “wet” elements are diverse. For instance, magistra can refer to anything from egg whites (fol. 69r: “in big works [of sand casting] it is necessary to moisten the sands with magistra or glair of egg”) to saltwater (fol. 84v: entry on “Eau magistra”), to wine—as is the case in an entry on fol. 84v, simply entitled “Sand,” which this essay treats. This account of the author-practitioner’s first hand experience involves two components, calcined and pulverized ox foot bone and wine boiled with elm root:

I tried the bone of oxen feet, thoroughly burned & pulverized & ground on porphyry, until it is not felt between your fingers. It molds on its own very neatly. But because on its own it is very arid & lean, it wants to be well moistened & dampened with wine boiled with elm root.

Ox foot bone ash makes up the dry component of the sand in this recipe. This substance is referenced at various other points in the manuscript as an ideal medium for sand casting: for instance, on fol. 67v, in a recipe entitled “Bones of the foot of oxen for sand” (“Os de pied de beuf pour sable”), the author-practitioner informs us that “once having been burned well twice & pulverized, they mold very neatly in sand & need not be reheated, but simply heated with the flame of straw.” Ox foot bones also feature in a recipe for sand on fol. 89r, which involves calcining and pulverizing them, combining them with rock salt, and leaving them to dampen “in the serain of the night or in the moisture of the cellar.”1

The wet component of the recipe on fol. 84v is a “wine boiled with elm root.” Elm root is mentioned throughout the manuscript in connection with making a binder for sand molds; the only reference to elm roots that is not connected with this practice is on fol. 71r, where the author-practitioner discusses colors of various woods for inlay work: “The elm, in its knotty root, has beautiful streaks diversified with grey and black, and the root of the maple, but one needs to choose well the grain of the wood.”

The elm roots boiled in wine formed the puzzle for our reconstruction, for the author-practitioner seems to assume boiling elm roots in wine to be an unproblematic procedure, one that his readers would probably be familiar with. Other recipes in Ms. Fr. 640 containing elm roots help to clarify the procedure. On fol. 69v, the author-practitioner states that “lean” (maigres) sands can be moistened by the addition of “boiled wine with elm tree roots & similar things” (more on the concept of “lean” and “fatty” sands below). Another recipe for “Magistra,” on fol. 87v, states that “Founders take the roots of a young elm when it is in sap & boil it in wine, or better yet vinegar, and keep it all year long in a barrel.” It thus seemed reasonable that the liquid to which the recipe on fol. 84v refers would be produced by boiling elm roots in wine, but we were unsure what properties boiling elm roots in wine would create and why this would make a good binder. This was a question because other recipes in the manuscript call for wine alone (“good pure wine,” for example, on fol. 69v) as a binding agent. What qualities might the elm root impart to the wine? Given that the author-practitioner specifically mentions that founders boil elm roots in wine when the elm is “in sap,” what effect does this have? These are some of the questions we had when we began our reconstruction of the recipe. As we planned and carried out our experiments, we also came to realize that we were relying heavily upon a variety of sources for gaining experience and knowledge, and we found ourselves asking many questions about the nature of experiential knowledge and about the processes of knowledge production both in our own lab and in the early modern workshop.

Elm Roots

The first step in creating the elm root decoction was to source elm roots, which proved a particular challenge in our desire to replicate the recipes as authentically as possible. First, the harvesting of the elm roots for this recipe is seasonally contingent. The “Magistra” entry on fol. 87v mentions “roots of a young elm when it is in sap.” Sap flow occurs during spring, as a result of changes in root pressure stimulated by temperature variations. As we were conducting these experiments in April, it seemed we were perfectly timed to collect fresh elm roots to boil in wine.

Initially, the elm we settled on for our recipe was Ulmus americana, which Pamela Smith located in the New York Botanical Garden and arranged to be harvested. However, as its name suggests, this species is native to Northeastern America, and therefore would not have been the type of elm to which the author-practitioner refers. However, Deanna F. Curtis, Curator of Woody Plants at the New York Botanical Garden, suggested via email that an elm hybrid cultivar known as “Pioneer” (binomial Ulmus x hollandica) might be more suitable for our purposes, as the Pioneer cultivar is formed by the crossing of two European species, Ulmus glabra (or Wych Elm) and Ulmus minor (the Smooth-leaved Elm), and thus more closely approximated the European elms from which the author-practitioner would have sourced his roots than did the Ulmus americana. We decided, therefore, that in the interests of authenticity, it would be better to use the Pioneer hybrid for our experiments.

We also obtained a modern species of European elm roots from the horticultural gardens of Wave Hill. Visually, these roots contrasted greatly with those of the Pioneer elm: while the Wave Hill roots were thick and sturdy, almost resembling branches (Fig. 1), the roots of the Pioneer hybrid were very fine and delicate (Fig. 2).

Boiling Elm Roots in Wine

We experimented first with boiling the Wave Hill roots, sourced by Wayne Morris, Assistant Director of Horticulture at Tree Man, a tree service company. Cutting them down into a suitable size was a rather intimidating process: the roots were much larger than we expected. Using a handsaw, we cut three of the root pieces into fairly large segments, each around 3–4 inches in length. In retrospect, we thought we might have achieved different results by cutting them into smaller pieces, in order to increase the surface area of the roots, thereby increasing the likelihood of sap being exposed to and interacting with the wine. We were initially skeptical about whether the tree was “in sap,” apparently the defining quality of the elm roots collected by founders, but when we began to handle them they felt fresh and bendable, and our fingers picked up a sticky residue from the cutting process.

Once we felt that we had a sufficient quantity of cut-up roots, we measured out 500 ml of our chosen wine (a cheap Cabernet Sauvignon—the manuscript, after all, says that vinegar is an even “better” medium than wine in which to boil the elm roots) into a metal pan, added 250 g of elm roots, and placed the pan, uncovered, on a hotplate to boil. No quantities are noted in any of the recipes in which an elm root and wine decoction is mentioned, thus we extrapolated. We were especially interested to see what the consistency of the binder would be after boiling, as in Fall 2014, an experiment was done to produce the same binder but using slippery elm inner bark powder rather than fresh elm roots. The experimenters reported that boiling one cup of wine with two teaspoons of elm bark powder resulted in a very viscous liquid with a mucilaginous texture similar to that of albumen.2 Our results were very different from the expectations that had been set by this 2014 trial. The wine in our experiment reduced to a fifth (around 100 ml) in about 20–30 minutes of boiling on the highest setting of the hot plate. Although the mixture certainly seemed more viscous, it was hard to tell if this was a quality imparted by the elm roots per se, or if this was simply the result of the wine’s reduction—and while the mixture could be described as viscous, it was certainly not of jelly or mucilaginous consistency. Nevertheless, we strained the elm root-wine liquid through a sieve into a jug, and then stored the mixture in a glass container in the lab refrigerator.

Boiling the Pioneer elm roots was a less successful endeavor (at least as measured by our expectations that a viscous liquid was needed). After washing the roots thoroughly, we cut them into small pieces and placed them in a pan with the same amount of wine as used before (500 ml)—and, again, the boiling process lasted for around 20–30 minutes. However, the mixture that resulted from this experiment did not have even the viscosity of the first liquid. Perhaps this was due to the nature of these small, new elm roots: when handling them, we found them to be quite brittle and twig-like, and certainly not anywhere near as fresh and “sappy” as the Wave Hill roots. We considered that perhaps a longer boiling time was required to allow the heat to break down the elm roots, but boiling the Pioneer roots in two more trials, once for two hours and once for 6½ hours, seemed to produce no increased viscosity in the resultant liquid.3

Taxonomies and Material Properties

Our lack of success with the Pioneer elm roots led us to consider a larger question about the difference in taxonomic ordering between the early modern period and the present day. Certain characteristics were selected for in the hybridization of the Pioneer elm, and our question was whether those were the same as the qualities valued by the author-practitioner. For the author-practitioner, the valuable properties or “virtues” of the elm seemed to center on the sappiness of the roots, whereas we suspected that the values selected for in modern-day hybridization would likely be morphological traits such as leaf size and shape. The Pioneer elm, then, as a hybrid of the European Ulmus glabra and Ulmus minor, might have been produced with the aim of recreating the appearance of a European elm rather than for the utilitarian properties prized by the author-practitioner. Pamela Smith contacted garden historian and Curator of the John Bartram Garden in Philadelphia, Joel T. Fry, with this question, to which he gave a particularly illuminating response: While sixteenth-century herbalists, artists, and craftspeople were interested in plants that “produced useful chemicals or compounds,” Fry stated, “modern, intentional hybridization of trees is mostly for some limited number of benefits. Something like dwarf size, a certain color, no fruit, no thorns, fast growth, or ease of propagation by cuttings. In the case of the ‘Pioneer’ elm it looks like fast growth, and perhaps some resistance to Dutch elm disease were what they were looking for. Certainly, gooey sap from the roots would be one of the last things they were looking for.”4 Given that tree hybridization now seeks to produce specimens that are fast-growing and profitable, according to Fry, it seems very likely that a property like root sappiness could have been lost in the process.

Reconstructing Sand

We decided that, in order to create our sand, we would use the liquid produced from boiling the Wave Hill roots in our first experiment, and the liquid produced from the trial when the Pioneer hybrid was boiled for 20–30 minutes. As these two experiments had been conducted under identical conditions, using the same equipment and the same wine, we thought this would minimize the potential of additional confounding factors, while still allowing us to compare the “authentic” elm product with the “less authentic.”

For the dry component of our sand, we had two options: a commercial bone ash and a non-commercial bone ash produced by calcining bovine foot bones in the lab kiln in Fall 2014. We decided that we would produce an “authentic” sand (made by combining the non-commercial bone ash with the Pioneer elm root liquid) and an “inauthentic” sand (a mixture of the commercial bone ash and the Wave Hill root decoction), so as to compare a sand that would be as close as possible to that used by the author-practitioner with a sand that made use of modern amenities and short-cuts (i.e., the commercial bone ash) and non-contemporary materials (the elm species that would not have been present in early modern Europe).

The two experiments we performed—one which we perceived as faithful as possible to the materials with which our author-practitioner would have practiced his craft, and one which we saw as cleaving slightly less closely to historical context—can be situated in the distinction that Hasok Chang makes (developing on the work of Dietmar Höttecke) between “historical” replication of experiments, and “physical” replication. Historical replication, Chang explains, seeks to match as closely as possible the instruments and substances that would have been employed in past recipes. Physical replication, by contrast, uses “any convenient instruments and procedures that will help one create the phenomena of interest, and faithfulness to the details of the experiment is of secondary interest.”5 The “philosophical challenge” of the latter, Chang asserts, is “not in the verification of the exactness of repetition, but in the characterization of the phenomena to be replicated.”6 Chang’s division of experimentation of this sort into two distinct categories, neither of which is superior or inferior to the other, was useful, in that it helped us to look beyond a restrictive binary of “authentic” and “inauthentic” experimentation, and instead see both our experiments as seeking to replicate the activities of the author-practitioner in different, but equally valuable, ways.

Although the process of making sand molds is detailed in various other recipes in the manuscript, the particular recipe for sand made from ox foot bone, elm root, and wine does not specify any particular method for combining the wet and dry elements, nor does it give any sense of the consistency that the sand should possess. We therefore relied on two sources in the mixing of our sand: texts, including not only Ms. Fr. 540 but other contemporary works detailing artisanal practices; and our past experience in making sand molds. We had previously worked on making a “Sand” from pine tree ash and wine on fol. 93r, the process of which was very similar to what we expected from this recipe. We therefore had a good sense of our procedure and a set of ingredient ratios on which we could base our new experiment.7

Our first questions were: how wet should the sand be? What kind of texture and consistency should the sand possess? Contemporary technical literature assisted us in understanding the desired consistency of the sand. In the eighth book of Vannoccio Biringuccio’s metallurgical treatise De la pirotechnia (1540), in a chapter entitled “Various methods of Making Powders in Which to Cast Bronzes in the Small Art of Casting,” Biringuccio stipulates that sand for this type of casting should “stick together” if you squeeze it in the palm:

A loam is made from these and mixed by beating with wool-cloth cuttings, spent washed ashes, and horse dung. This is made into cakes and dried. These are then put to bake in a furnace or in some other way and, in fact, one baked very well. Then it is pounded and sifted with a fine sieve, as it is ground in a potter’s color mill, or by hand on porphyry with water, to the fineness that the craftsman desires, or as fine as he can make it. When it has been ground, it is again drained free from water and dried out with fire. Then as much magistery of salt is taken as will moisten it. It is dried and pounded again and passed through a sieve. When it has been made as desired in this way it is moistened again before use with water, urine, or vinegar just enough so that it holds together when it is squeezed in the fist. Then, when it has been brought to this point, it is molded as you will hear.8

To determine the consistency of our bone ash without any added moisture, we tried squeezing it in our fists according to Biringuccio’s method. Although both types of bone ash actually did stick together a little (indeed, much more than we had expected), it was clear that neither would function as a sand without the addition of the binder.

In our previous experiments with making sand molds with pine ash and wine, we found that very little wet binder was needed to moisten the dry part of the sand: in fact, we only used 20 ml of wine to one cup of ash. Since the recipe for the sand made from bone ash, elm root, and wine was so similar to the one we had made earlier in the semester, we decided to begin this experiment using the same ratio of ingredients. We decided to make up the “inauthentic” sand first, composed of the commercial bone ash and the elm root and wine liquid derived from the Wave Hill roots. As there was only half a cup of commercial bone ash available to us, we measured out 10 ml of the elm root and wine liquid, which we mixed little by little into the bone ash.

We began mixing the two ingredients by hand in a plastic mixing bowl, adding the liquid periodically in very tiny volumes (a few milliliters per addition) and working it into the bone ash as if we were kneading a very dry dough. Very quickly it became apparent that the mixture was coming together; however, we decided that after adding the 10 ml of liquid we had measured out, even more was needed to fully bind the sand. We therefore portioned out another 10 ml of the liquid, and after working this into the bone ash, we felt that we had a sand with a good enough texture in which to mold—the sand was moist enough to stick together when squeezed in the fist, but it did not feel wet in any way (Fig. 3). The addition of the red elm root liquid to the white commercial bone ash produced sand of a beautiful to shell-pink color, as Fig. 3 shows. This figure also illustrates how the mixture clumped to our satisfaction according to the Making and Knowing lab’s “squeeze test,” which we were taught to use with all types of sand molding.9

Our more authentic ingredients, however, did not combine in the same way. One cup of non-commercial bone ash required the addition of 70 ml of liquid in order to produce a suitable sand for casting, nearly twice the amount of liquid binder required for the commercial bone ash (and over three times as much as required for the pine ash in our previous sand casting work). As we were working the sand, we found that it felt much coarser than the commercial bone ash; in fact, it felt similar to beach sand. This was presumably because the calcination process had not been fully completed, and some impurities remained in the bone ash. But why would we need to add so much more liquid than our previous sand had required?

The answer to this may lie in the distinction between “lean” and “fatty” sands that the manuscript articulates. In this material imaginary, “lean” typically refers to sands that are dry and crumbly, while “fatty” designates sands that clump together easily (for example, on fol. 84v, the same page on which the recipe for sand from ox foot, elm root, and wine appears, the author claims that “a fatty sand which is rendered very smooth, makes it [the cast] porous”). Most relevant, though, is the passage on fol. 69v, which states that

It is necessary that lean sands be more moistened than others, namely with magistra or good pure wine or boiled wine with elm tree roots & similar things. But very fine sands, like burned linen, which is fatty & soft on its own, want to be applied dry.

Adopting the author-practitioner’s terminology, we can characterize the very fine commercial bone ash as “fatty & soft” and the non-commercial bone ash as “lean” and therefore in need of more moistening than a “fatty” sand.10

Molding and Casting

Once our sands were clumping properly, we set about the process of molding. We used small mason jar lids as our frame molds, since we had so little of each type of bone ash. Our first attempt was to mold a simple shape (a key) in the commercial bone ash. Although it imprinted well, it was very difficult to get the key back out of the mold without completely disturbing the impression. We therefore switched to two different objects which were rounded and much deeper than the key: a round, knobbled earring, and a medallion with the raised impression of a bird. The bird was pressed into the sand made from commercial bone ash and the earring into the sand made from the lab-produced bone ash. The models were easy to remove from their respective molds (Fig. 4, Fig. 5).

Our metal, a 50/50 alloy of lead and tin, was then heated in a crucible.11 We tested the heat of the metal with a piece of paper, choosing to skim and pour the alloy when the paper turned a deep brown upon being immersed in the molten metal.12 We poured the alloy, then let it sit to cool for approximately fifteen minutes. We did not use a separator on either mold.13

We were frankly astonished by the clarity of both casts when we broke them out of the molds (very easily—clearly no release agent required). Both had picked up a large amount of detail, and the sand made from commercial bone ash in particular had produced an exceptionally smooth surface on our cast bird medallion. The cast of the earring had noticeable stippling on its surface, which we assumed resulted from the relative coarseness of the sand made from lab-made bone ash. That said, even the detail of that coarseness was fine and rendered in tiny, perfect detail (Fig. 6, Fig. 7). Both types of bone ash seemed to produce a finer cast than the calcined alabaster experiments going on in the lab at the same time, which had also produced a cast of the bird medallion.14

Producing Knowledge in the Making and Knowing Laboratory

One of our key questions in undertaking these experiments was to think through the production of knowledge in the Making and Knowing lab.15 The production of knowledge (and also of expertise and authority) is often equated with a hierarchical organization of authority, such as in Cennino Cennini’s Il libro dell’arte:

I, Cennino, the son of Andrea Cennini of Colle di Val d’Elsa, I was trained in this profession for twelve years by my master, Agnolo di Taddeo of Florence; he learned this profession from Taddeo, his father; and his father was christened under Giotto, and was his follower for four-and-twenty years; and that Giotto changed the profession of painting from Greek back into Latin, and brought it up to date; and he had more finished craftsmanship than anyone has had since….16

More recently, the production of knowledge is viewed as resting on the authority of experts in implementing the “procedures of science.”17 In what follows we make an attempt at understanding and articulating some alternate forms and sources of knowledge production in our workshop.

As we worked on our reconstruction experiments, we came to see how heavily we relied on knowledge and materials that had been produced by the preceding cohort of student-experimenters, which made it possible for us to scaffold our experiments on a wealth of collective experience and knowledge:

  1. Our lab-produced bovine foot bone ash was produced by the previous cohort of Making and Knowing Project students working on a recipe on fol. 89r.18 This product of their work served us as a materialization of the knowledge they had gained while carrying out their experiments, which we could effortlessly incorporate into our own processes. This was also the case with the other molding and casting paraphernalia that collected in the lab over two semesters: the commercial sands, the oft reused model medals for testing sands, the improvised canning jar lid molds reused many times, and the measuring implements.

  2. More important even than this materialized knowledge contained in tools and equipment were the bodily gestures that came to form units of practice and knowledge in the lab: the “squeeze test” and the “paper test,” both of which had emerged by themselves out of the collective experimentation, and which went on to become routine techniques in the lab.

  3. Our knowledge of how to make molds from sand and how to cast models in those molds came in large part from the skillbuilding done earlier in the semester, in which we experimented with creating a model and then casting it in a sand made from a recipe in Ms. Fr. 640. These skillbuilding exercises had been adapted and streamlined from the previous cohort’s experiences (and from some spectacular failures) with making models, molding them in various sands, and casting them.19 Our skillbuilding with sand made from pine ash and wine (fol. 93r) thus built on the previous cohort’s experiments, but also provided us with our own necessary bodily experience that fleshed out (so to speak) the textual and verbal instructions of our sources, of our peers and teachers, and, of course, of the author-practitioner.

  4. The 50/50 lead-tin alloy of our casts, first used in the skillbuilding exercises in cuttlefish casting, led by spring 2015 expert maker Andrew Lacey, led to the accumulation of a large supply of 50/50 lead-tin ingots in easily meltable units. This alloy was then used in recipes that did not specify casting alloys (including in our own experiment) and even sometimes in recipes that specified a different alloy. This is an example of how the weight of collective practice, lab habits, accumulated experience, and, perhaps most important here, accumulated materials in the workshop led to certain modes of (sometimes unexamined) practice.

  5. The Making and Knowing students and lab also relied upon many experts, for example, the expert maker Andrew Lacey, and, in our reconstruction, the combined expertise of staff from the New York Botanical Gardens who were able to pinpoint the likely genetic makeup of a period-appropriate species of tree, harvest the roots, and preserve them in order to transport them to us. In addition, Joel T. Fry’s deep knowledge of historical plant cultivation, and the knowledge and ability of Professor Smith to make these connections for us, made possible our questions about taxonomic classification and valuable material properties. Such connections helped us to determine the type of modern elm to substitute for an elm that would have been used in sixteenth-century France, and thus provided us with the “correct” roots to use.20

The point we want to make with the list above is that the activities of a busy workshop, while relying in part upon the authority of outside experts—the New York Botanical Garden, the Curator of the John Bartram Garden, and expert makers—take place in a space where knowledge is not just handed down from above or obtained from outside experts, but where techniques and knowledge emerge through the collective work of the community that is active in that space. Production of knowledge in this setting is thus distributed, collective, and emergent. In many respects, then, our reconstruction could be considered largely the work of others, a view in sharp contrast to the conception of individual creativity and originality that structures so much of the rhetoric about the production of knowledge in our own day.

  12. This test is detailed on fol. 145r, where the author-practitioner describes how to dip paper into molten tin or lead in order to test that it is not heated to too high a temperature: “One ought not to cast either tin or lead too hot in it, for it would burn the bone & come out lumpy. And to know when it is at a good heat, dip a little piece of twisted paper into it. If it blackens it without lighting, it is at a good heat. But if it burns it & catches fire there, it is too hot.”↩︎
