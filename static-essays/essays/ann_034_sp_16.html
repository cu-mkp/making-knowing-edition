Rouge Clair: Glass or Paint?

By , DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/nb5d-a140
← Back to Essays

Abstract

This essay explores a material called rouge clair, which is discussed in three separate entries in Ms. Fr. 640 on fols. 6r, 40v, and 124v. The term usually refers to a vitreous substance used for enameling, but the author-practitioner’s instructions complicate this definition: they include its use as a “cold enamel” that could be painted on metal without the use of heat. By reconstructing these entries and testing the application of the rouge clair produced, we conclude that for the author-practitioner, rouge clair did not necessarily refer to a specific substance (vitreous enamel), but rather to an optical effect that could be produced by different materials.

Introduction

Three recipes in Ms. Fr. 640 refer to rouge clair, a term that today means a vitreous red enamel applied over gold. On the evidence of these three entries, this essay will show that in the early modern period this term could refer to either true vitreous red enamel or a substance with similar optical qualities.1 This essay compares the rouge clair entries on fols. 40v and 6r that refer to a red paint-like substance with a third recipe on fol. 124v for “rouge clair” (to which the author-practitioner apparently later added “esmail” [enamel]) which calls for heat processing of a red glass. The difference between these three recipes underscores the variety of meanings that esmail could have in the sixteenth century.2

Rouge Clair and Gold

The entry on fol. 124v, although entitled “Rouge clair enamel,” seems to be mostly about gold. This should not surprise us because, as Susie Nash notes, the term rouge cler during the late fourteenth and early fifteenth centuries referred to “the costly red translucent enamel that was a relatively new and much prized technique, and which could only be applied to gold.”3 In the sixteenth century, Benvenuto Cellini described rouge clair in his Treatises on Goldsmithing and Sculpture as a technique of goldsmiths consisting of a red enamel known for its unsurpassed beauty and clarity.4 While the linking of rouge clair and gold on fol. 124v is unsurprising, it is significant for the purposes of this essay that the author-practitioner advises against the use of pure gold “which is opposed” to the rouge clair which “remains yellowish [iaulnastre],” and “renders the enamel a dead color.” Instead, “alloyed gold is more appropriate for it, such as that of écu & pistolet [coins made of gold alloy].” Through this discussion of the gold base for the enamel, the optical properties of rouge clair emerge. The author-practitioner notes that “one puts it back on the fire to give it a reddish color [rougeastre], to make the enamels beautiful. Otherwise, they are matte.” The author-practitioner’s addition of “matte” (mat) here in opposition to “beautiful” (beaulx) makes clear that a beautiful rouge clair should be a lively red translucent substance. While the entry on fol. 124v provides no clue to ingredients of the rouge clair, it probably refers to a vitreous red enamel colored by copper (smalto roggio), an extant example of which is found in a fourteenth-century Royal Gold Cup made in France, now in the collection of the British Museum (Fig. 1). The author-practitioner, however, continues to focus on gold, adding that some rouge clair “can be found that have grains of gold inside, and it is also the opinion of good goldsmiths that the good one is made with gold.”

Rouge Clair as Paint

In contrast to fol. 124v, the recipes on fols. 40v and fol. 6r do not involve heat, nor a vitrification process, but instead are similar to paint and paint glaze (paint diluted with additional oil or resin). On fol. 40v, the entry for “Cross of the commanders of Malta” observes that the “beautiful rouge clair which makes the field of the white enamel cross” is created from

fine tear of dragon’s blood tempered with eau-de-vie or else Indian laque platte, which in my opinion is made in Flanders, tempered with clear turpentine & tear of mastic & laid down on a silver leaf, not the kind which the painters use, but a thicker kind, which is burnished by those who make gemstone foils Av or by goldsmiths, & that gives it this beautiful brilliance.

Here the author practitioner makes clear that this color should have a “beautiful brilliance” supplied by the reflective surface of the thick silver.

The full-page entry on fol. 6r “For laying down and seating burnished gold and giving red or green or blue,” which, like the entry on fol. 124v is mostly about gold rather than the colors to be painted on it, specifies

if you want to lay in rouge clair & glaze with it de , grind Venice laque platte on marble with walnut or linseed oil. Once ground, mix turpentine or spike lavender varnish & apply on the gold with the paintbrush. Brazilwood & laque ronde die.

In avoiding the pigments brazilwood and laque ronde, this entry repeats the warning to avoid materials that will create a “dead color” elaborated on fol. 124v, thus making clear the aim of these entries for a substance of a deep red, “lively” color. Moreover, the metal surfaces (silver for dragon’s blood and burnished gold for Venice laque platte) will provide similarly brilliant reflective surfaces that shine through the translucency of the “rouge clair.”

Taken together, then, these three recipes complicate our understanding of enamels generally, and of rouge clair specifically, as only fol. 124v involves heat and vitrification, something that today we would expect to be part of any process for producing rouge clair. Given that the other two entries are for paint-like materials, what does this term “rouge clair” mean to the author-practitioner? This essay addresses this question first by examining other related entries in Ms. Fr. 640, and second by reconstructing the recipes on fols. 6r and 40v. This reconstruction provides a better understanding of the optical effects of resin- and oil-based paints on metal surfaces. In addition to illuminating the author-practicioner’s understanding of rouge clair, this essay contributes to more general questions about the author-practicioner’s understanding of esmail that are further explored in Amy Chang’s essay, “What is esmail?”5

Rouge Clair Recipes in Ms. Fr. 640

The three entries for rouge clair are spread across the manuscript, and the entries that appear around them differ significantly. The recipe on fol. 40v, “Cross of the commanders of Malta” that calls for laying in dragon’s blood-based paint on silver, is preceded by recipes on fol. 39v that include “Enamel,” “Colors for illumination on glass,” and “Tracing some history on glass,” and on fol. 40r for “Buttons of vermeilles” (red glass buttons). The entries on the following folio 41r–v include casting and molding recipes, as well as “Glassworkers’ black,” and “Colors and gilding on glass.” While there are many less clearly related entries on these folios, including for grottos, ducks, aquafortis, and vinegar, this sampling of entries around “Cross of the commanders of Malta” suggests that the author-practitioner placed this particular recipe for “beautiful rouge clair which makes the field of the white enamel cross” in proximity to entries for painting on glass, perhaps thereby conceptualizing it as related to both painting and glass processes. Indeed, the method of reverse painting on glass described on fols. 39v–40r is explicitly connected to the vitreous enamel technique basse-taille.6 Similarly, oil- and resin-based paints (often referred to as “cold enamels”) were applied to cast metal objects to add greater naturalism, enhancing the object with translucent color that allowed the metal underneath to be seen, perhaps in imitation of applying rouge clair on an underlayer of metal leaf.7

The recipe on fol. 6r, “For laying down and seating burnished gold and giving red or green or blue,” which uses a Venice lake-based paint in oil, is located between entries on mirrors on fol. 5r, and, on fol. 6v, for embellishing cages with thin glass (esmail) canes of various colors. Although mirrors may seem unrelated to rouge clair, the author-practitioner is interested in mirrors for the optical effects they produce: “an endless number of pretty conceits which seem magic.” His entries on mirrors and on rouge clair laid down on metal backings may both thus relate to his interest in optical effects. More obviously relevant to rouge clair are the vitreous enamels to which he alludes in the description on fol. 6v of a process to bend thin enamel canes “by the heat of a lamp.” This process, now known as lampworking, is a technique for which the author-practitioner notes “glass button makers also avail themselves of the said furnace.”8

The focus on glass and on optical effects in these various entries surrounding fols. 6r and 40v may indicate that the author-practitioner sees a similarity or relationship between the concept of rouge clair and the glass and metal entries. As we shall see, that relationship is in the nature of the optical effects they produce.

Red enamel (designated as esmail rather than rouge clair) also appears in the manuscript on fols. 3r and 39r. The recipe on fol. 3r mentions it in relation to “Counterfeit coral,” where the author-practitioner includes a note that “the coral made of gules red enamel endures the file and polishing.” The red enamel mentioned here, esmail de gueule, known in English as “gules red enamel,”9 differs from rouge clair in that it is not translucent but opaque. An entry entitled “Goldsmith” on fol. 39r observes that, “when goldsmiths have thus assembled their silver filings with saltpeter, a red enamel [esmail rouge] vitrifies at the bottom of the crucible.” He then adds, “I do not know if the copper mixed in with the silver is the cause,” making a note to himself to “Try for enamel.” Here again, he seems to be referring to red glass colored by copper (smalto roggio). While these entries mentioning red enamels use some of the same materials as the rouge clair recipes, they can be either translucent or opaque. This comparison thus underscores the characteristic translucence that the author-practitioner sees as typifying rouge clair.

Reconstruction

Pigments

The pigments the author-practitioner calls for are ambiguous: the recipe on fol. 6r calls for laque platte de venise, which we identified as red lake pigment sold at the time in a small flat cake rather than a round form (laque platte vs. laque ronde).10 Ms. Fr. 640 mentions that Florence lake (laque de florence) is better than Flanders lake because the best dyes are made in Florence (see fol. 57v), thus it seems that the author-practitioner refers to specific lakes by their place of production, and thus laque de venise is lake from Venice, and Florence lake is superior to that from Venice. In the late fifteenth and early sixteenth century, Venice also produced dyes, and was famous for its high-quality red lakes.11 Because the author-practitioner states that “brazilwood and laque ronde die,” we decided not to use brazilwood, however, we still did not know what the colorant for Venice lakes was. We noted that the author-practitioner was concerned here, like on fol. 124v, about producing a “dead color.” Thus, after observing the cochineal samples in the lab, and the relatively pale pink color they yielded as lakes, we decided that madder lake would yield the liveliest and deepest red—presumably the effect the author-practitioner was pursuing—so we used madder as the pigment.12

Dragon’s blood on fol. 40v presented similar problems, as dragon’s blood was used as the common name for the resins of several plants including: Daemonorops draco, Dracaena draco, Dracaena cinnabari, and a few species of the Croton genus.13 In order to decide which variety to use, we looked at the home-range of these species and the locations and traditions of use. We immediately eliminated any varieties of the Croton genus (which are the product known as sangre de drago in the New World) because the genus has anticoagulant properties.14 The manuscript mentions dragon’s blood several times and among the uses identified that it was a coagulant. Based on this information, and the fact that the sangre de drago of the New World would appear to be a less likely source for the author-practitioner than those traded from North Africa and the Levant,15 we ruled out this variety.

We were then left with dragon’s blood of the genera Daemonorops and Dracaena. The author-practitioner discusses the properties of “the good kind of dragon’s blood” on fol. 29v, where he states that it “can be found in large pieces like cake [...] & once broken it shows on its edges scales, transparent as ro rouge clair enamel, it is also lumpy in some parts like small rubies.”16 He also notes in the margin that “the darker dragon’s blood is the best & has more of a tint.” This description gave us clues both to the type of dragon’s blood we should use, and also to the author-practitioner’s conception of the desired product of the recipes for “rouge clair” on fols. 6r and 40, which should have a dark, deep red hue, like rubies. Daemonorops comes from the Southeast Asian rattan- or cane-palm and is the most common source for commercially harvested dragon’s blood today.17 While this species could have been traded into France (as were other pigments, such as indigo), when dried it takes on an orange cast, and the color it yields is not as deep red as a ruby. In contrast, both of the Dracaena species yield a deep red color, and the Dracaena genus has been noted for having medicinal properties as a coagulant18 and as an antiseptic,19 also properties that the author-practitioner elsewhere ascribes to dragon’s blood.

Based on this process of elimination, we decided upon Dracaena cinnabari.20 Besides its long history of use as a dye and medicine in the Mediterranean basin, ancient sources record its use to stain horn to resemble tortoiseshell, which seems to resemble one of the author-practitioner’s suggested recipes for dying horn.21 European voyagers to the Canary Islands22 in the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries harvested Dracaena draco (as well as in Morocco where it also grew) to the point of extinction. Dracaena cinnabari, which is now restricted to the island of Socotra off the Yemeni Peninsula, was once prevalent around the Mediterranean coast, making it likely the most common and accessible species.23

Tools

Having decided on our colorants, we turned to the tools for applying the rouge clair onto the metal surfaces. We experimented with brushes, our gloved fingers, and palette knives. The brush was inappropriate, because it not only created brush lines but also retained a large amount of pigment. Similarly, our fingers did not work very well as the application was uneven and left clear traces when we lifted our fingers from the surface. Overall, the palette knife proved most effective because it provided control over the application and did not absorb pigment.

Optical Effects

Our reconstructions of the two recipes on fols. 6r and 40v for rouge clair confirmed the hints about the author-practitioner’s conception of “rouge clair” that had emerged from our textual analysis: both recipes yielded deep-hued, lively red—ruby-like—translucent glassy surfaces through which their metal substrates reflected brilliantly. Although these two recipes use different materials—one is oil-based (fol. 6r), and the other is resin-based (fol. 40v)—and our reconstructions resulted in two materially different rouge clairs, they had almost identical color and translucency when freshly made (Fig. 2, Fig. 3). Over time, however, the resin-based rouge clair came to more closely resemble the structural properties of vitreous enamel, in that it formed a thick hard surface, while the oil-based rouge clair remained comparatively thin and fragile.

The rouge clair enamels also took different amounts of time to dry. The oil-based rouge clair took more than a month to dry, while the resin-based one took only a week to harden completely. Despite their differences, both rouge clair enamels could be conceptualized as creating an optical effect that imitated glass by the mixture of pigments and binding materials.

The Creation of Optical Effects – Materials

In order to explore which of the ingredients created the glassy optical effect, we made a control trial of dragon’s blood soaked in eau-de-vie, which was not mixed with turpentine and mastic. In this trial, undertaken in order to isolate the effect of turpentine and mastic, the resulting substance did not form a glass-like surface (Fig. 4).

Further experimentation indicated that it was in fact the mastic that determined the depth, liveliness, and translucency of the color. This is corroborated by Mary Merrifield’s statement that

gum is used for illuminating and giving lustre and brilliancy to the colours which are heightened and rendered gay by the gum; just as varnish gives a beautiful lustre to oil paintings, serving as gauze and talc to defend them from the dust; and as crystal, to give lustre and light to that which seems gloomy and eclipsed.24

The reconstruction also demonstrated how different proportions of colorants, binding materials, and backings could change the depth of the red hue (Fig. 5). In order to produce deep, lively, translucent ruby red, the proportion of binder that gave the vitreous effect could be of greater importance than the colorant. For instance, too much dragon’s blood and not enough mastic created an extremely dark crimson color (Fig. 6).

The Creation of Optical Effects – Ground Layers

The ground layers—gold, silver, and gesso—also played a significant role in creating the desired optical effects as well. Benvenuto Cellini said that red enamels pair better with gold,25 and the entry on fol. 124v discusses gold with vitreous rouge clair, but the resin-based rouge clair on fol. 40v, specifies using silver leaf, but “not the kind which the painters use, but a thicker kind, which is burnished by those who make gemstone foils or by goldsmiths, & that gives it this beautiful brilliance.” Despite distinguishing between silver and gold grounds, the author-practitioner does not compare or describe the difference that gold and silver will make in terms of different optical effects. To better understand this, we tested both recipes on gold and silver, as well as on plain gessoed panel. Our reconstructions showed that the red of the “cold” rouge clair recipes appeared bright, lively, and glassy over both gold and silver leaf when freshly made (see Figs. 2 and 3). However, after drying, the appearance changed. When freshly laid on, the paint on the gold leaf resulted in a slightly more vivid red than the silver leaf, but after drying, the dragon’s blood on silver became darker (Fig. 7), while the color applied directly to the gesso board looked dull and no longer had a glass-like appearance after drying. The gold or silver base seemed to be especially necessary to the oil-based rouge clair in order to preserve its liveliness (Fig. 8). Through our reconstructions, we concluded that cold rouge clair had a more vivid color and vitreous visual effect on gold. The silver surface also worked well, although it seemed to be especially well-suited to a deeper colored red (which the resin-based dragon’s blood rouge clair became over time). The gessoed board did not provide the same reflective surface of the two metal leafs, and thus did not produce the intended optical effect.

Silver may have been used in Ms. Fr. 640 as a more affordable substitute for gold,26 however we observed that the application on silver yielded a slightly darker red. Perhaps the author-practitioner recommended silver on fol. 40v for the same reason that in the vitreous rouge clair recipe on fol. 124v he employed alloyed gold because the use of pure gold rendered the enamel a yellowish and “dead color.” His concern with the weaker optical effect of this dead color can also be found in the instructions on fol. 6r not to use brazilwood and laque ronde because they “die.” The author-practitioner’s worry that this red might die appears to allude to a material imaginary, also underlying other entries in Ms. Fr. 640, that associated red substances with blood and life. In this imaginary, current in sixteenth-century medicine and metalworking, red and blood were also associated with gold. That rouge clair was applied on gold probably enhanced not only its color but also its meaning as a material.27

Conclusion

Paint with similar properties to both the oil-based and resin-based rouge clairs of Ms. Fr. 640 has been found on the sixteenth-century table ornament by Wenzel Jamnitzer, now in the collection of the Rijksmuseum, Amsterdam (Fig. 9). Oil-based “cold enamel” occurs inside one of the domed caps for the shields, and some of the orange-red paint identified on this piece contains colophony, a type of resin.28 Study shows that colophony occurs not only in the color red but also in black and yellow.29 It is possible that such resin-based paints on metal were common in sixteenth-century Europe; perhaps the rouge clair recipes in Ms. Fr. 640 provided a basis for other resin-based paint colors.

In sum, both the author-practitioner’s comments in Ms. Fr. 640 and the results of our reconstructions led to the conclusion that the rouge clair recipes sought to create specific optical effects, and that, for the author-practitioner, rouge clair did not necessarily denote a type of red vitreous enamel or the materials that went into producing it. Instead, he meant by it a saturated, lively, and translucent ruby red that possessed optical properties characteristic of glass and gemstones. Compared to the oil-based rouge clair (fol. 6r), the resin-based substance described on fol. 40v forms a hard glass-like material, which has been identified on objects from the period, and was used as an alternative to vitreous enamels.30 Our reconstruction therefore supplemented our understanding of resin-based paint on metal, a use that might have advantages in certain circumstances over both vitreous enamel and oil-based paints, due to the resin-based paint’s properties of quick drying, durability, and ability to create a plausible imitation of glass.
