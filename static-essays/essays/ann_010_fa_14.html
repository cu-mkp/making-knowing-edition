Casting in Frames

Abstract

The making of portrait medals and the proliferation of “books of secrets” in sixteenth-century Europe form an important historical context for “Casting in frames” on fol. 118v. This entry contains detailed instructions for making “sand” for molding and casting a portrait medal. This essay reconstructs the making and casting of a sand mold (with productive failures), and compares Ms. Fr. 640 to contemporaneous texts containing similar techniques, such as Vannoccio Biringuccio's Pirotechnia (1540) and the Secreti del Reverendo Donno Alessio Piemontese (first printed 1555).

The recipe “Casting in frames” on fol. 118v appears after a dense grouping of recipes that detail various forms of casting, such as “en noyau” casting (casting with a core) and the casting of flowers. The frame casting recipe lays out how to produce “sand” (sable) and how to heat the mold, devoting little attention to the casting of metal itself. As this essay will show, this emphasis can be explained through the process of reconstruction in conjunction with examination of relevant textual sources. Further, this recipe should be understood through its presumed aim of producing portrait medals, whose popularity in late sixteenth-century France aligns with the dating of the manuscript to the late sixteenth century.

Study and Reconstruction of the Recipe

The author-practitioner begins the entry by describing the production of sand made by grinding cores from previously used molds, which are composed of plaster, brick, and feather alum.1 It is unsurprising that sand would be reused, as this is still common practice today in metal foundries, where sand casting is the main technique used for the production of industrial machine parts.2 In our reconstructions, the only previously used cores available lacked feather alum, which was mentioned as necessary for plaster molds by the author-practitioner.3 This ingredient, “alun de plume,” could refer to asbestos or other minerals such as feldspar or gypsum; in modern terms, it refers to the mineral Halotrichite, which is made of soft, parallel strands of a white color, which has the appearance of a feather.4 Whatever it was, “alun de plume” appears to have been important as a binding agent, but for which no substitute was introduced for the previous molds (Fig. 1). This omission made it possible for us to pass the sand through a sieve, which the feather alum rendered impossible for the author-practitioner (Fig. 2). Given the time needed to break down the old molds, even using a sieve, one can only imagine the time needed to grind these molds to a “very soft” consistency without one, as the manuscript directs. In the recipe, the author-practitioner then outlines the preparation of sal ammoniac water (a solution of ammonium chloride and water) using two portions of sal ammoniac the size of walnuts (noix), a process similar to that described in an earlier recipe on fol. 111v, which calls for portions of sal ammoniac the size of chestnuts. Following the manuscript, this solution was mixed with eau-de-vie (interpreted as brandy) and the sand we had produced through the grinding and sieving process (Fig. 3). We then pressed the mixture into the female half of the mold over the medal (Fig. 4). The recipe calls for a finished medal as a pattern, but we used molded plaster models, which we thought a reasonable substitute. The author-practitioner does not stipulate the process of packing the sand into the mold; we found this required a great deal of time, as the medal was placed facing upwards as small quantities of sand were slowly layered on top of each other with additions of sand or water to adjust the mixture as needed.

After the molding of the medal, the author-practitioner describes marking the medal’s side and reverse, as well as the sand, a step whose function was not immediately clear to us, so we omitted it. Prior to filling the male side with sand, the recipe calls for dusting the entire side of the female mold with charcoal (“charbon pulverisé”), just as the medal itself had been dusted before insertion into the female side (Fig. 5). In our reconstruction, this step proved very effective when later separating the male and female molds. After dusting with charcoal, the author-practitioner warns specifically not to disturb the medal in order to remove it from the mold. The function of marking the location of the medal earlier then became clear, as the author-practitioner explains that he supported the area of the box mold where the medal was (presumably the point marked) and then struck the back of the mold with his hand. Evidently, on rereading his text, the author-practitioner rethought the word “struck” (“frappé”), adding a note in the margin that he had not struck it, “but rather pressed it only with the strength of my hands” to avoid distorting it (Fig. 6).

A major concern of the author-practitioner was the heating and drying of the frame molds. Had the medal not separated after striking the molds, the author-practitioner explains that he would have heated them. During reconstructions we were far less patient, opting not to heat the mold to remove the plaster model. Rather, our technique for removing the plaster model was to insert a knife into the channel made for pouring, creating a wedge that could lift the model out (Fig. 7). Our impatience would prove more problematic when we did not heed the author-practitioner’s next step: heating the molds by resting them on two iron trivets with the imprinted side facing upwards. The author-practitioner’s insistence on this point can be found in the small drawing in the right margin and a corresponding marginal note emphasizing the proper spacing of the trivets (Fig. 8). While the recipe ends here, not explaining how in fact to pour the medal, there are two further marginal notes that explain what is meant by heating the mold (Fig. 9). As a result, our decision to let the molds sit in a laboratory fume hood overnight and warm for five to ten minutes in a fairly low oven was inadequate preparation for casting, as the moisture in our molds caused imperfections in the coloration and details of the medals during casting (Fig. 10, Fig. 11). The author-practitioner’s second marginal note, which emphasizes the need to “redden the box mold” for gold and silver, proved especially prescient: when pouring silver into one box mold during reconstruction, steam and a spray of silver erupted from the the mold, as the moisture retained in the sand turned explosively to steam (Fig. 12). The resulting silver medal is a perfect snapshot of what occurred inside the mold as the steam forced the molten silver out of the mold and up through the gate (Fig. 13).

An understanding of the properties of the sand and the need to heat the mold can be gained by consulting fol. 161r, “Preparation of sand for frames.” This recipe outlines a nearly identical process of breaking up previously used cores with a stick to create sand, soaking the sand in sal ammoniac, baking the sand in a furnace until it reddens, and grinding it up again. This entry on fol. 161r gives insight into the value of using sand from previously used cores, as it explains that through the repetition of this process, the sand will reach a state such that it does not separate from the box mold. The author-practitioner also stresses the importance of heating the mold adequately so that the sal ammoniac can calcine properly. The presence of Latin terms in the recipe on fol. 161r, “gip de lateribus” (a type of mortar?) and “alumen jameni” (a type of alum?), suggests a textual source for this recipe, which could not be identified based on these phrases.

Portrait Medals in the Sixteenth Century

The recipe for making sand molds on fol. 118v is assuredly linked to the longstanding tradition of portrait medals, which were frequently made using this process. The earliest surviving Renaissance portrait medals are two lost medals that entered the collection of Jean de France, Duc de Berry (1340–1416), by 1413, although there is no way to know whether the originals were cast or produced with a repoussé technique, as copies of both types are extant (Fig. 14, Fig. 15).5 Such objects complicate the widespread scholarly consensus that cast portrait medals were developed in Italy.6 The mass production of pilgrimage badges (made by pouring a tin-lead alloy into molds), as well as the production of medals for numerous French monarchs, affirm the presence of artisans in fifteenth-century France capable of casting medals.7 Nonetheless, the most extensive early written sources of portrait medal casting are of Italian origin: Cennino Cennini furnishes a brief recipe for the making of medals using plaster and either a wax or clay mold, and Leon Battista Alberti was known to model portraits in relief in wax that were subsequently cast in bronze.8 The individual credited with the invention of the portrait medal genre itself is Pisanello (Antonio di Puccio Pisano, c.1395–c.1455), who was installed at the Este court in Ferrara.9 Bringing together humanist learning with erudite patrons concerned with the propagation of their fame, the Este court offered the ideal environment for the birth of the portrait medal, which shows a portrait on one side and an emblem or impresa on the reverse (Fig. 16).10 As Ulrich Pfisterer has shown, portrait medals grew in popularity in Italy over the course of the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, functioning as “social currency” that patrons could exchange in order to establish and consolidate bonds of friendship and alliance.11 The level of erudition needed to decode the messages implicit in emblems and imprese delineated membership within tightly controlled social circles of learned individuals.12

Knowledge of the production of medals certainly traveled from Italy to France with Italian artisans, the best known of which are Francesco Laurana in the fifteenth century and Benvenuto Cellini in the sixteenth century. While portrait medals were produced in France by goldsmiths beginning in the fifteenth century, Mark Jones has demonstrated that in France, centers for medal making developed around Lyon—the center of French trade with the Italian peninsula—and reached their apogee of manufacture between the second half of the sixteenth century and the early seventeenth century.13

The appearance, function, and understanding of medals in France differed somewhat from that in Italy. In the sixteenth century, French medals deemphasized erudite pairings of emblems and individual portraits in favor of standard images of French monarchs, and the function of such objects seems to be more closely centered on exchange with the royal court as a means of currying favor.14 Mounting interest in portrait medals in mid-sixteenth-century France is epitomized by Guillaume Rouillé’s La premiere partie du promptuaire des médailles des plus renommées personnes (1553), which was dedicated to Marguerite of France, sister of the Duc de Berry, and features engravings of coins (some invented by Rouillé himself) with subjects ranging from Adam to Charles V to Suleiman, Emperor of the Ottoman Empire (Fig. 17).15 Rouillé writes in a prefatory note to readers that such portraits recalled antique tradition, preserving together for posterity text and an image of each subject’s face, “the most beautiful and most honest part of man.”16

Portrait medals were also linked to the academies that developed in late sixteenth-century France. The statutes of the first royally sanctioned French academy (L’Académie de poésie et de musique) prescribe the making of “medallions” to regulate the admission of members, as Frances Yates has discussed.17 While medals with portraits of academicians survive, they do not bear members’ devises on the reverse, as the statutes stipulate for the medallions (Fig. 18).18 By the time the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 wrote his manuscript, therefore, there was a well established demand for medals in France.

Comparison of Ms. Fr. 640 with Contemporary Texts

While the sand molding and casting recipe on fol. 118v is presented in a first-person narrative that implies first hand experience, earlier published sources offer precedents for many of the techniques described by the author-practitioner.19 Numerous books printed in sixteenth-century Italy detail methods for sand molding and casting that closely align with the recipe in Ms. Fr. 640. In his Pirotechnia (1540), Vannoccio Biringuccio outlines the process of using a wax or clay model for the invention of one’s design, which can be preserved by casting this model in plaster of Paris, a technique that allows for the reuse of both the original and plaster models.20 Biringuccio offers multiple recipes for how to mold such a model in a wooden casting frame that has been filled with sand, a process that closely mirrors the technique in Ms. Fr. 640.21 Similarly, Benvenuto Cellini discusses the casting of medals using a model made of wax in his Due trattati, published in Florence in 1568.22 In comparison with the information presented in such treatises, the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 is far more detailed in explaining the merits of the various ingredients and the techniques whereby the readers can test whether their use of the ingredients matches his own. For example, the author-practitioner explains that the water with two walnut-sized pieces of sal ammoniac should be “moderately salty” (fol. 118v). By presenting his text as a recipe, the author-practitioner seems to aligns himself with the tradition of Renaissance books of secrets. William Eamon explains that the use of a “recipe” to record technical information was a common trait of books of secrets, distinguishing them from the “descriptive-historical” method that characterized authors like Vanocchio Biringuccio.23

The casting of portrait medals might be viewed as part of a broader early modern interest in the transformation of materials. Along with knowledge of the heavens and understanding of the body, the ability to manipulate materials provided a key impetus for interest in the acquisition of knowledge about nature. Yet metallurgy and casting remained a mostly embodied endeavor during this time. Because expertise in metalworking frequently garnered the support and patronage of the nobility and royalty, the practitioner often saw it in his best interest to keep this knowledge to himself and mine it for its potential social and political expediency. Biringuccio’s Pirotechnia, however, discussed the technical details of mining gold ore openly within the framework of knowledge acquisition, dismissing such craft secrecy as a duplicitous method of suggesting expertise and technique where it did not exist. From Biringuccio’s characterization of this phenomenon, though, it is evident that secrecy was indeed a common trope in such works.24 For example, Bernard Palissy, the most well-known of French life-casting artisans, remained secretive concerning the specifics of his casts, the techniques for which can only be extrapolated from manuscripts such as our own.25 The classification of knowledge as “secret” could be rhetorical at times, but it also conveyed a concern about sharing knowledge that possessed potential social or economic value in a society without intellectual property protection.26 The designation of “secret” and “mystery” was frequently applied to the techniques and skills of artisans and craftspeople, sometimes collectively termed arcana artis. Such language made suggestive connections to a larger class esoteric knowledge that also could depend upon embodied techniques and/or expertise.27

Portrait medal casting recipes thereby fit into a larger corpus of books of secrets, the most famous of which was Secreti del Reverendo Donno Alessio Piemontese, first published in Venice in 1555 (Fig. 19).28 Translations made the text popular beyond the Italian peninsula: the first French translation of the text appeared in Antwerp only two years later, and within a decade the text had been published more than twenty times in Italian, French, Latin, Dutch, English and German.29 Before the dawn of the seventeenth century, thirty editions of the Secreti were published in French alone.30 The Secreti are divided into six books, which were arranged somewhat thematically and include recipes for remedies, perfumes, preserved fruits and vegetables, beauty secrets, dyes and inks, and metals. While books of secrets generally display numerous medicinal remedies and medical knowledge, Ms. Fr. 640 records little of note in this regard; it betrays a comparative lack of focus on healing procedures when juxtaposed with Piemontese’s Secreti.

In 1567, Girolamo Ruscelli—best known as an important editor and literary figure in Venice31—claimed authorship of Piemontese’s text in the prefatory letter of the posthumously published Secreti nuovi di meravigliosa virtù del signor Ieronimo Ruscelli (Fig. 20).32 Presumably written prior to Ruscelli’s arrival in Venice in 1548, this rare edition offers new recipes to the original Secreti, asserting that the text is a product of experiments undertaken and of knowledge gained by Ruscelli and twenty-seven fellow members of the “Accademia Segreta” in Naples, although no other sources related to the academy are known.33 As William Eamon and Françoise Paheau have shown in their study of the Secreti nuovi, the text contains a far higher number of medical recipes than the original Secreti (1,024 in comparison to 108).34 It seems possible that the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 was aware of the Secreti nuovi because he wrote a Latinized version of Ruscelli’s name on on fol. 1r, among a list of other classical and contemporary authors who may have informed his writing (Fig. 21).

Why, however, did the author-practitioner write Ruscelli’s name in the manuscript if his own text bears greater overall similarity to the Secreti—ascribed to Alessio Piemontese—than to the Secreti nuovi? It may be that Ruscelli’s presumed authorship of the Secreti was commonplace knowledge by the time of the manuscript’s writing. Yet recent studies suggest a different story. At the conclusion of their careful examination of the publication history of the Secreti, Massimiliano Celaschi and Antonello Gregori argue that while Ruscelli’s peers in Venice (where the text was first published) were clearly aware that the name Piemontese was synonymous with Ruscelli, the continued reprinting of the Secreti across Europe under the name Piemontese would have obscured this fact.35 The author-practitioner may have seen an edition of the Secreti, the major representative of a genre to which his own manuscript seems to have aspired; however, a comparison with the original Italian edition shows some generally shared aims but no specific recipes that the author-practitioner borrowed.36

A comparison of the recipe for sand casting in Ms. Fr. 640 to the Secreti and Biringuccio’s Pirotechnia reveals many parallels between the techniques described in the two processes as well as a few differences, the most significant of which will be highlighted here.37 The first has to do with the means of releasing the medal from the mold: Biringuccio recommends greasing the medal in animal fat and applying charcoal dust to it.38 Ruscelli notably does not recommend applying charcoal at all.39 The author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640, by comparison, stresses instead that any grease or oil on the medal should be removed, and charcoal should be applied at numerous stages in the mold-making process. Like Ms. Fr. 640, the Secreti and Pirotechnia both place significant emphasis on drying the molds over a fire but devote no time in this section to the actual pouring of the medal.40

While the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 included over forty entries that include “sand” in the title, many of them indicating the ideal properties of sand, as well as where and how to procure it, they discuss an extremely diverse variety of “sands.” Biringuccio and Ruscelli, by comparison, offer very detailed steps for preparing general categories of sands, each using different types of clays and powders that can be employed in any combination. Following the recipe for “The true and most perfect practice of molding medals,” the Secreti includes a list of seven different types of sand (“Terra prima da gittarvi i metalli fusi,” “Terra seconda,” “Terra terza,” etc.) (Fig. 22).41 Throughout their texts, the desired qualities of such sands remain the same: “the goodness and perfection of each sand in which to cast fused metals consists in the following: that they are very soft, as if impalpable, because the designs are imprinted very clearly.”42 Such information was carried through in later translations of the text.43 The author-practitioner, too, was very concerned with the properties of casting sands, and used the same standard of impalpability in his recipes. “Impalpable” thus appears to have been a widespread technical term. The author-practitioner did not place his great variety of sands into categories as did the other texts, but they shared the same concerns for identifying the ideal function for each variety of sand.
