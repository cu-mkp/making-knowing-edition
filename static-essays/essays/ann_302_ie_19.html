The Body and the Senses in Ms. Fr. 640: Towards a "Material Sensorium"

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/027y-d055
← Back to Essays

Abstract

This essay explores the “material sensorium” of the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640, that is to say, his discussions of the senses and their place in his project of making and knowing. The manuscript presents a unique opportunity to explore the senses as a site of bidirectional exchange between the human body and the materials with which it works.

Introduction

The body and embodied sensory experience have long been the subject of historical inquiry. In the history of art and science, bodily knowledge of gestures and materials has come to the forefront, particularly in studies of artisanal ways of knowing. To early modern artists and artisans, the encounter with and understanding of the material world was a distinctly physical and sensory one, resulting in a kind of knowledge that was intimately linked to the body and hard to put into words.1 In the history of medicine, understandings and experiences of the body have been reconstructed from both sides of the medical encounter. Following the impulse of Barbara Duden’s questions about the construction of the modern body, scholars have shown how physicians and, more importantly, patients, understood bodies in terms of inspection, palpation, auscultation, and “fluxes” felt within the body.2 Although focusing on the same object—the body and bodily sensation as both object and tool of knowledge-making—approaches from the history of craft knowledge and the history of medicine have tended to remain within their respective areas of scholarship.

BnF Ms. Fr. 640 represents a rare opportunity to bring questions about embodied knowledge and about the body in health and disease into conversation with one another. While even scholastic physicians clearly used their senses to assess diseases and remedies, few early modern sources describe sensory experience in such detail as the author-practitioner does. From the sound of the local bell named Cardaillac, which Toulousains claimed was the most beautiful in all of France (fol. 20v), to the scratchy feeling of walking in “boots of hay” (fol. 54v) to the “pain of g[out]” (fol. 7v) or the smell of bread freshly drawn from the oven, where they are to be replaced with the drying bodies of animals (fol. 130r), the quotidian and material-focused perspectives of the manuscript readily provide a rich imaginary of sensory perception.3 Furthermore, in the context of complex technical processes, the author-practitioner explicitly discusses the body and senses as epistemic tools.

I focus on the non-visual senses, partly for the sake of brevity, partly because the visual is usually given much more attention than the other senses, and partly because the latter can be said to have a clearer physical component and thus provide a more fertile ground for exploring embodied sensation.4 Based on references to touch, taste, smell, and hearing in the manuscript, I explore what we might call the author-practitioner’s “material sensorium”—the set of sensory tools and how they resulted in knowledge and expertise of materials and processes. I then show how these interactions between body and materials change the body not only in terms of accrued embodied knowledge but also with respect to the fabric of the body and the practitioner’s health. Finally, I argue that the interaction cuts both ways: touch and exhalations may harm materials and affect processes just as the human body may be harmed by them. While this essay is an initial exploration and not a definitive assessment, it brings together key examples of the author-practitioner’s sense of the senses and opens up questions about the relationship between the body and the workshop.

Touch

Unsurprisingly for someone who clearly worked with his hands a lot, touching plays an important and intriguing part in the author-practitioner’s practice and writing. Touching something is perhaps the most direct way to experience it, but while the tactile experience is highly immediate, it can also be fooled. Stick a coin to your forehead with saliva. Tell your friend that you will do the same to them, and challenge them to shake it off without using their hands. Instead, palm the coin and plant your well-licked thumb on your friend’s forehead, et voilà: “thinking he has the piece on his forehead due to the coolness of the saliva, he will shake his ears & will get nowhere” (fol. 33v). Just like the sense of sight, that of touch can easily be misled by sleight-of-hand tricks, and all the more convincingly for the immediacy of the experience.

To the well-trained touch of a craftsman, especially one who melted and cast metal, this immediacy could be immensely useful. In the absence of reliable objective means of measuring temperature, the subjective experience of touch was a ready guide. The water used to wet a particular kind of molding sand “should be of such heat that you cannot hold your finger there without feeling strong heat” (fol. 137v).5 The temperature of the mold itself, too, was crucial, to ensure that it would not cause the molten metal to bubble or prematurely congeal during the cast. In an entry on casting tin, for example, the author-practitioner notes that the mold should only be moderately hot, “such that you can hold it in your hand” (fol. 115v), whereas in another, on casting lead and tin, he writes that “your mold must not be cold, but of such a heat that you can handle it without harm, or that you can keep your finger in the gate without burning yourself” (fol. 131v). While bodies were different, and the well-callused finger of a seasoned metalworker surely embodied a different sense of what constituted a bearable temperature, the author-practitioner relied on the sense of touch as a guide to fairly specific temperature ranges such as the “lukewarm heat” required for a mold into which lead and tin is cast (fol. 116v).

In choosing and preparing the right sand for making molds for casting—one of the author-practitioner’s preoccupations—another aspect of touch comes into play: its ability to detect subtle differences in consistency. “Mineral sand,” for example, should “break apart between your hands & have very small & delicate grains & of the same nature” (fol. 88v). Such fine, homogeneous sands could be achieved by artifice, but, as the author-practitioner points out, many artisans prefer their sand ready made by nature herself. In that case, one should

seek the one of the mine, which is not too fatty, the one that is a kin of earth, not too lean & consequently without bond, but rather that which is pulled from the depths of the sand-bed in bricks & clods that show its natural compaction, which is quite difficult to break & which has a very small & delicate grain, & which is found soft when handling it between the fingers. (fol. 90r)

The success of a detailed cast relied to a great extent on such specifics of particle size and bond of the sand, and the sense of touch was instrumental in assessing these and communicating them to others in writing.

The author-practitioner uses the very limits of haptic sensation as just such a material indicator. Another sand for casting is made with “the bone of oxen feet, thoroughly burned & pulverized & ground on porphyry, until it is not felt between your fingers” (fol. 84v). Here, the reader is told to stop grinding not when they can feel a particular sensation but when they can no longer feel the material at all with their fingers. As it turns out, this criterion of eluding the sense of touch was an important technical concept for early modern artisans. Postdoc Jenny Boulboullé and students Raymond Carlson and Jordan Katz have shown that the term impalpable, that is to say “un-feelable” or “impalpable,” is central to the way the author-practitioner experiences and thinks about different kinds of materials used for casting molds. Furthermore, they found that he is not the only one: the use of the term “impalpable” is used in published works on metallurgy, such as the well-known book De la pirotechnia (1540), by the sixteenth-century Italian founder and metallurgist Vannoccio Biringuccio (ca. 1480–ca. 1539), and De’ secreti (1555), a famous book of secrets by Alessio Piemontese, likely a pseudonym of polymath Girolamo Ruscelli (1518–1566). In his emphasis on the haptic sensation of a material being impalpable, then, the author-practitioner speaks to a sensory terminology widely shared by expert makers.

Taste and Smell

While touching could yield important information about structures and surfaces, materials’ more intrinsic, internal properties were more amenable to tasting and smelling. To make sal ammoniac water of the correct concentration, the author-practitioner notes that “the size of two chestnuts of pulverized sal ammoniac suffices in a pot of water,” but since such measurements could vary, he adds, “to the tongue, you find the water moderately salty, for too much is not good” (fol. 111v). While “moderately salty” is perhaps just as contingent as “two chestnuts” and “a pot,” the author-practitioner clearly thought it a useful indication of what should be aimed for.

Perhaps more usually, the sense of taste was used in judgments of quality rather than quantity. Early modern Toulouse was a centre of the woad trade, so it is not surprising that the author-practitioner should record local expertise in the thorough assessment of the prized dyestuff: “The goodness of the pastel woad is known when, put in the mouth, it gives a taste as of vinegar” (fol. 39r). This was just one part of the rigorous assessment, which also involved detailed visual examination and a dyeing test, but it is striking that a quick bite of a fermented woad ball was seen as a definite clue to its quality.6

It also points to another aspect of the author-practitioner’s sensorium: the use of material referents to describe sensation. He clearly assumes the taste of vinegar to be so widely familiar as to give most readers a good idea of what they should look for in a woad cake. Similarly, Emma Le Pouésard suggests in her essay that bread functions as a common material referent in Ms. Fr. 640, providing widely intelligible points of comparison for texture, consistency, and oven temperature.7 Although the author-practitioner occasionally includes more specialist referents, such as “the smell of tin” that identifies a certain type of mercury, these comparisons suggest an effort to make the sensory experience of his workshop available in his writing by drawing on a shared material sensorium of everyday life in an early modern city (fol. 92v).

In addition to the artisan’s sense of taste, that of the buyer also had to be taken into account. Kitchen pots should be made in such as way as “not to give a bad taste to the food,” and while some producers were tempted to “mix in more latten to make them yellower, to make them more vendable,” the author-practitioner warns that “the latten by itself, & at a mere touch, is stinking & of bad odor” (fol. 36v).

Here taste and smell overlap in their off-putting effect, but smell also stands on its own as a sensory tool in the manuscript. A strong smell was often a sign that things had gone wrong, as in the case of the malodorous kitchen pots, or, for example, when papier-mâché had been left to soak for too long and had turned rotten (fol. 81r). However, smells could also help identify the materials needed for a recipe. The ingredient list for another alloy, for example, includes the intriguingly specific “congealed mercury with the smell of tin” (fol. 92v). A material’s smell could also give clues as to its properties and likely behavior in a given process. Returning to his favourite topic, the making and use of different casting sands, the author-practitioner notes that “white sand smells like sulfur when reheated, and I believe it would melt. And since the substance has been cast in it, it acquires in the mold a luster as if it were leaded or vitrified. I believe that glassmakers could use it” (fol. 99r). In addition to his observation of a vitreous glaze on the cast object, it is the sulfurous smell which suggests to the author-practitioner that this particular kind of sand is prone to melt and could even be used for making glass. This is particularly interesting in the light of sulfur’s frequent appearance in the manuscript as a material that can be easily melted and used to cast small objects, and even as a sort of material metonymy for transformation and experimentation.8

Hearing

The sense of hearing becomes itself the subject of a short entry. Under the heading “Hearing from afar,” the author-practitioner records another miscellaneous trick for daily life which are scattered here and there throughout the manuscript: “make a small hole in the earth, & place your ear fully there, at night or at a silent time, & you will easily hear the noise” (fol. 125r). In addition to such playful uses of hearing, specific noises could serve as helpful indicators in the workshop. Before casting metal into a mold made from cuttlefish bone, the author-practitioner writes, one has to make sure that it is completely dry: “you will recognize that they are dry enough when, after having brought the inside & the imprint of these to the fire, they cry & crackle once brought near the ear” (fol. 145r). If one was prepared to listen carefully, the materials themselves could tell when they were ready to be worked upon. A similar notion emerges in Paracelsus’s (1493/4–1541) more explicitly articulated approach to embodied and sensory knowledge of the material world. In his philosophical apologia of experience as a powerful and legitimate way of knowing, he claims that rather than studying texts, it would be more profitable to “overhear” what nature herself had to say.9

Burns, Fumes, and Toad Piss: Sensory Experience and the Body

Sensory experience does not leave the body unchanged. It is clear that the author-practitioner, through repeated touching, tasting, and listening, acquired a nuanced and sensitive material sensorium, calibrated to make fine-grained distinctions. It is also clear, however, that the sensory experience of the workshop could affect the artisan’s body in a distinctly negative manner.

Working with high heat and using one’s finger to feel temperatures by touch carried obvious risks, as is attested by an entry titled “Against burn,” recommending crushed onions soaked in verjus, or black soap applied to the wound (fol. 20v). A much longer entry—“Against burns, excellent”—gives a detailed and fascinating description of how to make a burn salve, including holy water and the recitation of prayers. To underline the efficacy of the remedy, the author-practitioner concludes, “a gunpowder maker, who had almost completely burnt himself & showed no sign of the burn, taught me this” (fol. 103r). Touching things thus resulted not only in haptic knowledge of materials but also in expertise in dealing with the negative effects of that direct contact on the body.

Lifecasting animals brought with it not only the dangers of metalwork but also those of catching and killing the creatures in the first place. Toads, lizards, and snakes were popular as lifecast objects due to their symbolic association with nature’s cycles of putrefaction and generation, but the very attributes which likely earned them this reputation could be a challenge for the artisan. Speaking of toads, the author-practitioner writes: “if by chance you want to mold this vile animal, & if it were to piss in your hand, it would burn your hand & itch, as if you had handled nettles. But the most powerful remedy for this is to put your hand into fresh earth & to cover it as if you wanted to bury it” (fol. 109v). Snakes, on the other hand, could bite if not caught with great care, and the author-practitioner describes the precautions he observed from “the one who taught me to mold them” and, as with the toad, provides a remedy of damp earth to extract the venom (fol. 110r).

Fumes and smells, too, could be harmful to the body as well as revealing material properties. In an entry on casting with a copper alloy called latten, the author-practitioner discusses the addition of calamine, a zinc ore, to the mixture, including the warning: “if you cast with recent calamine, keep away from the fumes, for they are pernicious” (fol. 136v). Elsewhere, describing a flux for silver which contains a range of toxic ingredients, he advises that one should only attempt this procedure with adequate personal protection, that is, “keeping your face covered, over the nose & the mouth, from the eyes down, to avoid the exhalations of arsenic, sublimate, and orpiment” (fol. 123r).

This was evidently a common enough problem for someone working with metals, and it is explicitly addressed in a short entry titled “Antidote against the fumes of metals.” It reads, “in the morning, take a piece of toast with butter, & neither antimony nor any other vapor will be able to harm you. Or put half a pig’s bladder in front of the face” (fol. 79v). One could either shield oneself from the fumes, the author-practitioner suggests, or counteract their deleterious action within the body with an “antidote” of buttered toast. This tells us much about the way he conceived of the body’s place in and interaction with the workshop environment. While there is a sense of an inside and an outside, the two could interpenetrate, and fumes could reach within the body and disturb its healthful workings.

Other fumes, of course, could do the opposite and be conducive to health. The “medicine of the orientals against all maladies,” for example, consists in inhaling the smoke of dried rosemary from a pipe and holding it inside one’s mouth and nose to do its healthful work (fol. 77r). On the very last page of the manuscript, we find a medical recipe “for preserving,” in Latin this time, which also relies on fumes: a mixture of vinegar and juniper berries is poured onto hot bricks, and “the vapor shall be received through the mouth & nostrils” (fol. 170v). This vapor served to preserve the body when venturing into “infected air”—many diseases including plague were thought to be caused by bad air—but could also work outside the body, to remove such unhealthy miasmas from clothes or homes.

Deleterious interactions between body and materials were not, however, unidirectional. When changing the brine for preserving olives, for example, “one ought not to touch [it] with the hands, for this makes them spoil” (fol. 93r). Likewise, one should avoid touching fruits to be preserved, “for where the hand will touch, it will rot, and it is even good not to breathe on them” (fol. 121r). Indeed, bad breath could even disrupt glass painting: “if the glassworker who is working has a stench from his nose or his mouth, & he breathes on the glass, the color will not take on it” (fol. 37v). As hot metals, poison, and disease could adversely affect the human body through touch and smell, so could the body’s unhealthful touch or exhalations spoil the works of nature and of man.

Conclusion

This exploration of the author-practitioner’s “material sensorium” shows that the knowledge he collected in his manuscript was deeply informed by physical, sensory experience, and that he attempted to capture this expertise in writing, for example by describing key sensory cues—the temperature of metals and molds, the salinity of brine—which could spell success or failure. It is clear that he developed a complex sensory repertoire allowing him to make fine-grained distinctions, for example between consistencies of sand, right up to the point of impalpability, where sensation ceases altogether. This bodily interaction with and receptivity to materials often came at a cost: even as sensory experience informed and shaped embodied knowledge, it also changed the body in other ways, burning its exterior or disturbing its internal balance with toxic fumes, while the right kinds of fumes could cure disease. Conversely, the touch or exhalations of the body could interfere with the material world, and the artisan had to be just as cautious not to spoil his work by the touch or exhalation of his body as he had to be in protecting his body from harm. It is clear, then, that the author-practitioner, like many early modern people, conceived of the body as permeable and in constant exchange with the environment—an exchange which was as likely to be valuable and fruitful as it was to harm.
