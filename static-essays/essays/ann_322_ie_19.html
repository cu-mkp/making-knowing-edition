Methodologies for Making and Knowing: Reconstructions for Historical Research

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/s7f5-5h76
← Back to Essays

Abstract

This essay summarizes some recent historiography that has featured historical reconstructions, and discusses the Making and Knowing Project’s particular methodologies for reconstructing the practices, techniques, and technologies documented in Ms. Fr. 640. Reconstruction has been successfully employed by historians of science, art historians, conservators, experimental archaeologists, and a variety of other groups who each used a particular methodology for addressing questions about history. In the graduate seminars of the Making and Knowing Laboratory, students were guided through several skill-building activities before devising a research project of their own. Research in the laboratory emphasized a careful understanding of historical materials, a focus on the process rather than the outcome of reconstructions, and a synergistic relationship between textual research and laboratory work. I argue that our experimental method can best be understood as exploratory experimentation. Likewise, while the methods developed were specific to reconstruction of entries in Ms. Fr. 640, there are many other applications across a broad spectrum of historical research and pedagogy.

Introduction

In recent decades a diverse group of historians has turned their attention to the reconstruction or reworking of historical experiments, recipes, techniques, and technologies as a means of doing historical research. These research methods are not entirely novel, and have in part been borrowed and extrapolated from scientists, archaeologists, as well as conservation and museum scholars. Within archaeology, the field of “experimental archaeology” is focused on the reconstruction of ancient structures and artifacts to replicate or approximate their use in earlier cultures and generate and test hypotheses about their feasibility.1 Likewise, art conservators and art historians have long reconstructed earlier techniques and materials to understand processes of making art, and also how to preserve and restore objects.2 Far from replacing more traditional textual research, these material methods are generally thought to be supplementary, but they still have occasionally been controversial among historians. Still, following the so-called “material turn” and the more recent success of a number of research programs in technical art history and the history of science, there is less controversy about the legitimacy of using such reworkings and reproductions in historiography.3 Even so, using reconstructions as a means of doing historical research is still relatively new territory, and practitioners thus must tailor research methods to particular fields and questions. This was certainly the case on the Making and Knowing Project. This essay summarizes some recent historiography that has featured reconstructions and then discusses the Making and Knowing Project’s methodologies for carrying out research on the recipes of BnF Ms. Fr. 640.

Historical Reconstructions in the History of Science and Art History

The use of reconstruction is not unprecedented in modern historical research, and, in addition to informing the work of museum scholars, archaeologists, art historians, and art conservators, similar techniques have been used by historians of science since at least the mid-twentieth century.4 Some scholars have used the terms “reworking,” “remaking,” or “re-enacting” to describe similar approaches, and Sven Dupré has helpfully grouped these under what he calls “performative methods.”5 Disentangling the particular connotations of each of these terms is beyond the scope of this essay and I thus use “reconstruction” as that term has been commonly used in the contexts that are the subject matter here—the history of science and art history.

Early work utilizing reconstructions was situated primarily within the history of physics and focused on reconstructions of Galileo Galilei’s mechanics and Isaac Newton’s optical experiments.6 In recent years, significant research programs in the history of science that have employed reconstructions are Otto Sibum’s Experimental History of Science, Lawrence M. Principe’s and William R. Newman’s New Historiography of Alchemy, and Hasok Chang’s reconstructions of historical experiments for novel uses in science education and to “complement” contemporary science.

Sibum reconstructed James Prescott Joule’s paddle wheel experiments on the mechanical value of heat and concluded that Joule’s visual representations and descriptions in laboratory notebooks were insufficient to accurately carry out the experiment.7 Far from simply providing information about the internal workings of a famous experiment in the history of science, Sibum argued on the basis of his reconstruction that Joule could not have worked alone (as Joule had claimed), and that his tacit knowledge and manual expertise in measuring precise temperatures came from working in his father’s brewery. Sibum recently explained the importance of using this methodology, writing, “Only through the historiographical approach, called experimental history of science, could I get access to hitherto unrecognized dimensions of past practice.”8

Principe and Newman’s diverse lines of research using experimental reconstructions have been a foundational component in reshaping the field of the history of alchemy, namely by demonstrating that many of the seemingly implausible, bizarre, or esoteric descriptions in alchemical texts—often hidden by Decknamen or code names—were actually about physical phenomena that could be reproduced in a laboratory. Principe has reworked alchemical experiments from diverse alchemists including Robert Boyle (1627–1691), George Starkey (1628–1665), and the pseudonymous Basil Valentine (fl. 1600), while Newman has focused on reconstructions of experiments by Isaac Newton as a part of The Chymistry of Isaac Newton.9 This work has not only shown that early modern alchemy involved a high degree of technical difficulty and a sophisticated understanding of chemical transformations on the part of some of the most luminous intellectuals of the early modern era, but that it was also not unrelated to other areas of the scientific enterprise such as optics or mechanics. This research has entirely deflated the view commonly held in the twentieth century that alchemy was predominantly a psychological phenomenon based largely on a textual and esoteric tradition instead of material practice in a laboratory.10

These several projects have uncovered important historical details that were concealed in written accounts. Joule’s texts had the effect of obfuscating the nature of his collaborative scientific practice, likely to preserve his image as a lone genius, while many alchemical texts used obscure language to conceal secrets from the uninitiated, while at the same time revealing information to other adepti who understood the alchemical code, as it were. Reconstruction research has also uncovered details about practices that were likely considered too commonplace to mention or that were too difficult to put into words on a page. It has thus helped to dispel notions that certain scientific theories arose from a generally theoretical milieu, disconnected from the messiness of practice, and has likewise shown the importance of artisanal and craft traditions in the rise of early-modern science.11

Hasok Chang has suggested novel ways that reconstructions of historical experiments can be deployed not only to change science education, but as an important complement to contemporary scientific research.12 Stimulated by the work of diverse chemists from the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries who studied temperature and the phenomenon of boiling, he carried out a series of experiments that show the variability of the boiling point of water, challenging the “myth” commonly told to students, “that pure water always boils at 100°C (212°F), under normal atmospheric pressure.”13 Chang suggests that the reconstruction of historical experiments is not only important for science education and for dispelling other such myths about the nature of knowledge, but asks complementary questions that are generally excluded from current science. He argues that this complementarity provides an important role for the history and philosophy of science, which, informed by reconstructions, “can recover useful ideas and facts lost in the record of past science; address foundational questions concerning present science; and explore alternative conceptual systems and lines of experimental inquiry for future science.”14

While the use of reconstructions in historical research has gained a certain prominence in recent years, this should not give the impression that scholars who engage in such reconstructions have promised results beyond what they can deliver, or that they have suggested their work ought to displace other more traditional methods. Indeed, these historians have generally been transparent and circumspect about what reconstruction can do. As Sibum has concluded, the reconstruction of historical experiments is “a complementary technique within the conventional tool kit of the historian.”15 These historians have been equally forthcoming about the difficulties and pitfalls inherent in this research and the need for training that enables scholars to ask fruitful historical questions and that allows them to conduct meaningful work in the laboratory.

Comparing these several research programs in the history of science illustrates well that there is no single method for carrying out a reconstruction. Just as the different disciplines that constitute science today employ diverse methods for exploring the natural world, so too different reconstructions and questions about history require different tools and techniques. Within the history of science, some reconstructions have required careful attention to the replication of an experimental process that simulates as closely as possible the apparatus, performative gestures, and environmental conditions of the original, whereas other reconstructions take the approach of limiting variables or abstracting particular phenomena from a process in order to test a hypothesis.

Art historians and conservators have likewise fruitfully employed the methodologies of reconstruction to investigate a large variety of historical materials and techniques, and such reconstructions have been an important part of the rise of the new subspecialty Technical Art History.16 One especially thoughtful and influential reconstruction methodology that has been tailored to the particular questions and needs of a group of technical art historians and conservators is the HART (Historically Accurate Reconstruction Techniques) methodology.17 This approach can be applied to a range of cultural artifacts and begins with analysis of an object and the textual study of contemporary documents that discuss the object at hand (e.g., early modern recipes for making pigments). It then calls for chemical analysis of extant works of art to identify materials used, and then proceeds to reconstruct the materials and processes using—as much as possible—substances and tools that would have been available to historical actors. Since the HART method was formulated, authors have continued to adapt and fine-tune its methods.18 Likewise, beyond the Making and Knowing Project, methods from Technical Art History have been integral to other large-scale projects such as the ERC-funded ARTECHNE Project, which explores the transmission of techniques in the visual and decorative arts.19

In all reconstruction research, while there are broad similarities that cut across disciplinary boundaries, the particular methodology that one chooses will be determined largely by the nature of the questions that one is asking about history. Beyond the aforementioned areas of the history of science and art history, reconstruction and performative methods have been deployed to address a gamut of historical questions on notable subjects including the history of fashion, early music, and culinary recipes.20

Making and Knowing Methodology

The Making and Knowing Project at Columbia University drew on the aforementioned projects and scholars to develop a methodology for reconstructing the recipes in Ms. Fr. 640.21 The research on the project was carried out almost entirely by graduate students participating in the Craft and Science seminars held in the Making and Knowing Laboratory, but involved close collaboration with professors, postdoctoral researchers, paleographers, museum professionals, conservators, and expert makers. It is important to note also that this project was aimed at pedagogical as well as research ends, and entirely disentangling these two goals would be practically impossible. This also had an impact on our methodology insofar as the semester-long course only allowed for limited time to repeat and refine experiments, often resulting in a basic “proof of concept” rather than robust experimental data.

In brief, the reconstruction component of Craft and Science seminars began with various skill-building activities related to the theme of a particular year (i.e, Metalworking and Moldmaking, Colormaking, Practical Knowledge, Ephemeral Art, and Printmaking) that challenged students to begin thinking about how one formulates a research question that can be addressed by a reconstruction, and then how one ought to proceed in the laboratory to answer that question. Each seminar developed a “Template for Reconstruction” that addressed such issues as: the importance of historical context and reading widely to determine a recipe’s “genealogy;” whether a given recipe is complete or has omitted information necessary for reconstruction; how individual words in the recipe might have meanings different from modern language; how to address issues of scale, units of measurement, and the common problem that measures are sometimes omitted from early-modern recipes; and perhaps most important, how to determine what materials and equipment were used and how these could be replicated with as much historical care and sensitivity (and as little anachronism) as possible in a reconstruction.

After skill-building and training in how to keep field notes documenting their work in the laboratory, students proposed a research project that required some manner of reconstruction of one or several recipes in Ms. Fr. 640. Students generally found that this was one of the most difficult aspects of the course, and formulating a historical question that could be addressed with laboratory research and that was feasible within the space of half a semester was no simple task. Likewise, many students found that their research questions evolved as they became more familiar with historical materials, as their work in the laboratory informed their reading of the manuscript, or as their techniques and abilities improved.

Each student’s research was unique, and the projects spanned an enormous thematic breadth, partially as a result of the sheer diversity of the recipes in Ms. Fr. 640, but over the course of the project several motifs emerged that were helpful in formulating research questions and carrying out historically interesting reconstructions.

First, we found it important to emphasize—and continually remind ourselves and the students—that focusing on the process rather than the outcome of a reconstruction would yield more insights about history. It was very easy—at least, speaking personally—in the course of reconstructing a recipe, especially one that promised an aesthetically beautiful final product, to lose sight of the true end goal which was to understand the process. This methodological tenet is instantiated in most of the objects produced in our laboratory which are purposefully unfinished and show, for instance, the gates and sprues used to cast a flower from life (Fig. 1, Fig. 2).

Second, our work on this project benefited from the nature of our collaborative practice. Students gained insights into materials and techniques from colleagues, expert makers, the student paleographers who transcribed and translated the manuscript in the summer Text Workshops, and other contributing scholars (including participants of the annual Working Group Meetings) whose advice and experience was integral to the success of research projects (Fig. 3, Fig. 4). This type of collaborative practice is normal for the sciences but is largely absent from historical research, which tends to solitary textual research, and while this is not to say that one could not perform reconstructions alone, much is to be gained from collective work.

Another theme that was continually reinforced over the course of the project was the synergistic relationship between textual research and work in the laboratory. Naturally, taking the approach of reading very widely in both primary and secondary sources led to the most interesting research questions, but what was perhaps more surprising was the manner in which reconstructions led to different readings of a recipe or even just one word in a recipe. Simply by being forced to take the time to think about each material and each step of a process forces one to read passages more carefully, and often the experience of reconstruction provided special insights into an author’s particular usage of a word. Students in one of the Moldmaking and Metalworking seminars found this to be the case with the author-practitioner’s use of “s’attaque” in several recipes, where a modern translation might give the idea of a substance attacking other materials or tools, but their experience in a reconstruction with sticky, unyielding substances led them to question this interpretation and reread early modern dictionaries, which suggested the possibility that the connotation of the word was instead something more like attach.22 Such re-readings were commonplace in the laboratory.

Similarly, because many of the materials and techniques described in Ms. Fr. 640 are unfamiliar today, we found that even our most basic material explorations yielded interesting and novel information. For instance, Stephanie Pope and Caroline Marris carried out a reconstruction of “powder for hourglasses” which called for the peculiar technique of mixing molten lead with common salt, and they ultimately found that this process yielded small, heavy beads of a shape and size ideal for an hourglass.23 This experiment demonstrated that the author-practitioner’s recipe was feasible, but it also opened an important piece of early modern material culture to sensory and empirical understanding. The experimenters were able to explore how the hourglass sand behaved and the sensory qualities it produced: what it felt like, how it rolled and flowed, and its general heft. The experience of making this material also opened up numerous questions about domestic production, timekeeping practices for artisans and households, and the cultural significance of hourglasses in early modern Europe.

The methods in this experiment are representative of much of the research on the Making and Knowing Project, and, when considering what its mode of knowledge production is, can perhaps be best understood as a form of exploratory experimentation. Modern philosophers of science have suggested that this type of experimentation is not strictly driven by the testing of hypotheses, and instead seeks out empirical regularities when dealing with questions where there are no or few well-developed theories.24 Such experiments investigate phenomena without first limiting the outcomes of the experiment in accordance with extant theories. Descriptions of this manner of investigation, however, are certainly not limited to modern philosophers; Robert Boyle, for instance, described a method for “indagatory” or “exploratory” experiments that is strikingly similar. Boyle noted that experimenters sometimes encountered unfamiliar materials and the best course of action to understand the said materials was to perform various experiments “to discover what quality or other attribute may be found in the subject, of which we have but a suspicion not knowledge.”25 Boyle also described some of his exploratory experiments with his air pump as being carried out in a “haphazard manner [...] in order to determine more accurately the effects it was capable of producing.”26 Making sense of the unfamiliar materials and techniques in Ms. Fr. 640 required such forms of material exploration, and among the diverse conceptual outputs of our students, a common refrain was that an experiment had demonstrated proof of concept—that the author-practitioner’s description of a given technology or process was feasible.27

Our methodology in the laboratory was limited by concerns about safety, the availability of materials and apparatus, and the rather short amount of time that students could devote to a research project in the space of a semester. Likewise, because most students had limited experience working in a laboratory setting, the range of potential projects was somewhat restricted. And, unlike other projects in Technical Art History or Experimental History of Science, we lacked much of the modern analytical apparatus that could have been used to produce empirical data regarding the processes and products we produced in the laboratory.

Even so, the Making and Knowing Project provides an important case study demonstrating that those without extensive laboratory training can produce historical knowledge, or at least raise questions about material culture that could not have been produced through strictly textual scholarship. The methodology that evolved in our laboratory was specific to the reconstruction of the recipes in Ms. Fr. 640, but many of the general guidelines we developed and the lessons we learned are applicable across a broad spectrum of experimental historical research and should prove useful to other scholars looking to add tools to their methodological and pedagogical tool kits.
