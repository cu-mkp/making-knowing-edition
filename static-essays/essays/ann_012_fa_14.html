Fat, Lean, Sweet, Sour: Sand of Ox Bone and Rock Salt

Abstract

One of many recipes in BnF Ms. Fr. 640 devoted to making sand molds for metal casting, “Sand of burnt ox bones and rock salt” on fol. 89r contains only two components: “burnt ox bone” and rock salt. Most sand mold recipes contain a dry and a wet component, but this one specifies two dry components. This essay discusses sands and binders, solves the puzzle of how two dry molding components create a successful bond in the sand, and concludes by discussing the experiential and conceptual categories of fat and lean in the author-practitioner’s material imaginary.

This recipe on fol. 89r “Sand of burnt ox bones and rock salt” describes a “sand” (sable) to be used for making molds. This sand has only two components: “burnt ox bone” and rock salt, which are to be pulverized and mixed, then “moistened [...] in paper, folded in a wet napkin, which is previously made in the serain of the night or in the moisture of the cellar.” The resulting sand, according to the author-practitioner, is good for casting tin or lead. Many recipes in BnF Ms. Fr. 640 are devoted to making sand for molds suitable for casting metals. Most of these recipes contain a dry and a wet component. The dry component is the “sand,” and the wet component is the binder that holds the sand together. For example, on fol. 84v, the recipe includes burnt bone of ox foot and a broth of elm root.1 And on fol. 118v, the sand is a powder of plaster, brick, and feather alum, and the wet binder is sal ammoniac, water, and wine.2 The recipe on fol. 89r, however, contains two dry components.

Other sixteenth-century authors also list various materials used as sand. According to Biringuccio, sand is usually composed of powders “made of crushed brick, tripoli, vine ashes, tiles, and glazed drain pipes, or burned emery, calcined tin, straw, and of burned paper and horse dung as well as of young ram’s horn ashes and many other things,” and the binder is usually a “magistry of salt.”3 Good sand should be finely ground and take the metal well, and the binder should make the sand strong and hold together when the mold is dried.4 Based on a comparison of the recipe on fol. 89r with other recipes in Ms. Fr. 640 and in Biringuccio’s Pirotechnia, the sand detailed on fol. 89r seems unusual because of the absence of an obvious wet binder other than the “moisture of the cellar.” This recipe raised other questions as well: What is powder of ox bone, and how is it made? Can the powder of ox bone and rock salt bind together without a liquid binder? If not, what serves as the binder? How fine must the powder be, and how is that achieved? Additionally, the author-practitioner notes that “The bone of the foot of oxen is always so lean on its own that, without being mixed with one or two parts of some fatty sand & one that has bond, such as tripoli, salts, felt, ashes & similar things, it would not release & would not mold neatly, for it crumbles.” What is the meaning of “fatty sand?” These questions impelled our reconstruction of this recipe.

This essay first treats the two components in this recipe—ox bone and rock salt—and their preparation methods, then it turns to the question of what serves as the binder in this recipe, and it concludes with a discussion of the experiential and conceptual categories of fat and lean. As will become clear, our reconstruction process provided answers to the questions raised by this seemingly implausible sand, and it gave many insights into the author-practitioner’s material imaginary.5

Burnt Bones of the Foot of Oxen

Fol. 89r does not give any information about the production of the powder of burnt ox bones, but clues appear in other recipes in Ms. Fr. 640, for example, on fol. 67v, a recipe, “Bones of the foot of oxen for sand,” mentions the bone is “burned well twice & pulverized,” and on fol. 69r–v, the recipe for “Sand” again mentions “Sand from oxen feet, burned twice & finely ground.” These statements informed our conclusion that the powder of ox bone is produced by calcining and grinding ox bones into bone ash.

Bone ash seems to have been a common material for sand casting.6 In addition to the recipe on fol. 89r, the author-practitioner also mentions the use of bone ash on fols. 67v, 69r–v, and 84v–85r. The author-practitioner seems to regard bone ash as an ideal material for casting because he claims that it enables easy removal of the cast objects from the mold: on fol. 67v, he states that “bones of foot of oxen,” form “the neatest sand that can be found for copper.” In “Sand of burnt ox bones and rock salt” on fol. 89r, he notes that

I have not found anything else which releases more neatly than this one. It wants to be rather humid. And if you want to cast very thin, make sure it is very hot. It came out very neatly in soft tin, like the principal [i.e., the pattern], and withstood several casts. For tin, I believe that it is not necessary to seek a better one, nor for fine lead either, which comes out almost as neatly as tin.

Our reconstruction confirmed that bone ash and rock salt molds do take tin well and can be removed cleanly, while, in contrast, cast sulfur objects cannot be removed cleanly from molds made according to fol. 89r.

The source of bone ash, according to fols. 67v and 89r, should be the “bone of the foot of oxen.” This refers to the digital bones including proximal phalanx, middle phalanx, distal phalanx (coffin bone), proximal sesamoid bones, and distal sesamoid (navicular) bone (Fig. 1). Contemporary sources point to other kinds of bone used for bone ash: Biringuccio, for example, mentions ash made of young ram’s horn, leg bones of horses, donkeys, and mules.7 In a recipe for sand stretching from fol. 69r through 70v, the author-practitioner states on fol. 70r, that “sheep foot bones are even better than those of oxen foot,” and “human bones are the best for casting once calcined.” Because the author-practitioner chose ox foot bone so often, we sought out the same, however, we could not obtain ox bones, and instead settled for bovine foot bone and calf leg bone for our reconstruction, which we purchased from a kosher butcher shop.8 On comparing the bone in the foot and the leg, we found that the foot bone is much denser and harder than the leg bone, so we hypothesized that the foot bone would be a better source of bone ash (Fig. 2, Fig. 3).

Burning Ox Bones

The process for calcination is not described in the manuscript: the author-practitioner does not give any information about the temperature and time needed for this process in those recipes that mention burnt bones. Other recipes from the same period are similarly opaque about the process. For example, fourteenth-century painter Cennino Cennini describes the production of bone ash from chicken bones for panel paintings: “put them into the fire; and when you see that they have turned whiter than ashes, draw them out, and grind them well on the porphyry.” 9 Biringuccio mentions using a furnace to calcine the bone and then pound and sift the bone ash, but he does not provide any information about time or temperature. The absence of descriptions of calcination suggests that the knowledge about this process was common in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries. Cennini uses burnt and pulverized bone ash for preparing a ground layer for panel paintings,10 and Biringuccio uses bone ash to make crucibles, the vessels employed in refining metals.11 A 1678 publication of The Royal Pharmacopœa by Moyse Charas describes calcination as occurring by means of an “ordinary” fire,12 and it records a recipe for a styptic ointment that contains calcined bone ash as one of the ingredients.13 The wide usage of bone ash in all kinds of crafts and medicine indicates that calcination of bones was a common practice.

We found that the production process of bone ash in the bone china industry served as a point of reference. Here, bones are calcined at up to 1250°C to produce commercial bone ash for bone china manufacture,14 with the main component of the resulting bone ash being tri-calcium phosphate in the form of hydroxyapatite Ca5(OH)(PO4)3.15 According to the scientists Sergio Galeano and Mari Luz Gracia-Lorenzo, at over 650°C (1202°F) the organic components are completely removed. The bones turn black at 400°C (752°F) and then turn gray between 450°C (842°F) and 600°C (1112°F). At 650°C (1202°F), the bones become white.16 Based on Cennini’s description about the color of bone ash, we concluded that 650°C represented the lowest temperature that would result in calcination.

We prepared the cow’s foot bones for calcination by boiling them for 104 minutes (Fig. 4). We removed the remaining skin, cartilage, and other soft tissues from the bone using knives and brushes. Then we put the cleaned bone in the oven to dry at 200°F (93°C) for 1.5 hours and 300°F (149°C) for five hours. For the sake of comparison, we also prepared the leg bones in the same manner. However, the heads of the leg bones became spongy and soft after boiling, and turned gray after being dried in the oven. The foot bone turned white and hard after being dried in the oven, and its pieces rang like porcelain when tapped (Vid. 1). Since the foot bone was cleaner and denser than the leg bone, we consider the foot bone—as stipulated in the manuscript—to be a better source of bone ash.

We calcined the foot bone in an electronic ceramic kiln (Paragon Dragon kiln) (Fig. 5). We increased the temperature at a rate of 1100°F (593°C) per hour to 400°F (204°C), then held the temperature at 400°F (204°C) for 30 minutes, after which we increased the temperature at a rate of 1100°F (593°C) per hour to 1500°F (816°C) and held it there for 60 minutes. At around 692 °F (367°C), much smoke issued from the kiln, changing color from white to black, and then from black to gray and to white. At around 800°F (427°C) a gust of smoke again issued from the kiln, but by 834°F (427°C) the smoke had almost disappeared. This suggests that the organic components in the bone started to burn above 800°F. Upon opening the kiln, we found the bones had become extremely white, with only some gray parts remaining. These white bones were easy to grind in the mortar. When we ground the bone, we found some bones had a black layer inside of them, although their surface was completely white. This suggests that the bone was not completely calcined and still had some organic components. Overall, the ground bone ash felt like fine sand and was a grayish color. For comparison, we also purchased commercial bone ash, which is calcined using modern industrial methods, with the result that it is extremely white and fine, and looks and feels like flour.

The physical characteristics of the bone ash that we made in the kiln—fine and powdery—correspond to the author-practitioner’s description of the powder of ox bone. The author-practitioner pointed out that the ash of ox foot bones is dry and crumbles if not mixed with fatty sand. On fol. 84v, the author-practitioner mentions that ox foot bone “on its own it is very arid & lean, it wants to be well moistened & dampened with wine boiled with elm root.” We can thereby deduce that the ox foot bone ash is too dry a substance to bind together by itself.

Rock Salt

Rock salt is the mineral form of sodium chloride (NaCl). It is mined in mountains, for example, one major salt mine is located in Berchtesgaden in Rheinberg, Bavaria. Biringuccio states that it is “made by Nature in the form of stone,”17 and he viewed Hungary as an abundant source of rock salt.18 Although the color of rock salt varies in each mine, the main component, sodium chloride, is the same. Because it was easily available, we chose Himalayan salt from the Khewra Salt Mine in Pakistan for our experiment (Fig. 6). The chemical composition of Himalayan salt includes 95–96% sodium chloride and iron oxide, which gives the salt a pink color.

What is the Binder?

“Sand of burnt ox bones and rock salt” on fol. 89r stands in contrast to other recipes for sand casting because no liquid binder or magistra19 is mentioned. Instead the author-practitioner instructs the reader to place the sand made from bone ash and rock salt “moistened [...] in paper, folded in a wet napkin, which is previously made in the serain of the night or in the moisture of the cellar.” On fol. 88v, the author-practitioner uses a similar method to moisten sand containing rock salt: “put them on a marble in a cellar. In one night, they had been moistened enough by themselves without dampening them further.” Thus we can see that in recipes which use ground rock salt as part of the sand, the author-practitioner advises moistening it in the air or paper instead of directly adding liquid into the sand. Insight into this technique comes on fol. 88v, where the author-practitioner states that “rock salt, like all other salts, dissolves in the damp.” Our reconstruction sought to replicate the cool moisture of the night and/or cellar by using a specially constructed humidifier to both imitate and accelerate this process. We enveloped our “sand” in a damp piece of paper, laid it on a glazed ceramic plate, then wrapped it in a piece of linen, wet it with just enough water that it no longer dripped. (Fig. 7 and Fig. 8). Then we put the plate on an improvised open grill shelf system so that it could receive the steady stream of cool moist air from a cold vapor humidifier below (Fig. 9).

The author-practitioner did not specify the type of material from which the paper and napkin were made. In the early modern period, cotton was a luxury item from Asia, and household textiles were commonly made from hemp or linen; thus we used a 100% linen cloth for the napkin. Early modern paper was made from rags that contained raw flax and hemp fibers,20 however, current conservation of rare books has shown that “cotton and hemp blends provide [...] a paper that has the right color and is sympathetic to the original papers.”21 Therefore, we used paper made by the Center for the Book at the University of Iowa with a fifty-fifty cotton-linen blend.22

The ratio of the rock salt and the bone ash for “Sand of burnt ox bones and rock salt” on fol. 89r is specified when the author-practitioner writes, “I mixed as much of one as the other.” Similarly, on fol. 88v, the author-practitioner again mentions that he used the same quantity of rock salt and sand from a mine to make the molding material. Thus, we used equal quantities of bone ash and rock salt in our reconstruction.

We allowed the mixture (encased in paper and linen) to absorb the damp air from the humidifier for around one hour.23 We experimented with two kinds of mixtures: one was a mixture of commercial bone ash and rock salt (“commercial sand”), and the other was a mixture of the bone ash that we calcined in the lab combined with rock salt (“homemade sand”) (Fig. 10). The homemade sand turned gray after being moistened and felt like beach sand (Fig. 11). It was also quite coarse compared to the commercial sand (Fig. 12), which was likely due to incomplete calcination.24

Molds

We made five different molds in order to test four properties of sand made from calcined bone and rock salt: the strength of the sand, the relationship between regrinding and the quality of casting, the separation agent used on the mold and the casting objects, and the proper material for pouring. Our experiment showed that both the sands made from commercial sand and from homemade sand bound together well and formed a good, neat impression of the pattern. When the molds dried, both homemade and commercial sands hardened very durably, creating a solid mold. This result proved that sand made from bone ash and rock salt as described in fol. 89r binds with humidity from the air. This raises the question of which ingredient acts as the binder.

We knew that the bone ash could not be the binder because it is too dry and crumbly. Interestingly, in Ms. Fr. 640, rock salt is used both in liquid and solid form in making molds. On fol. 84r, dissolved rock salt is an ingredient in the magistra, or liquid binder, which the author-practitioner notes “will give it a bond to withstand several castings.” On fol. 89r, he mixes dry salt and calcined ox bones, and on fol. 88v, he mixes dry rock salt and quarry sand and dampens them with water. Both these methods were said to create durable molds, which the author-practitioner also clearly stated on fol. 89v in a recipe for sand made from sal ammoniac and alabaster powder: “There is no better bond than salts appropriate for metals, for once mixed in powder, they get moistened together & dry & reheat together.”

We concluded that it is the combination of rock salt and water that binds the sand. Without salt, water holds the damp sand together by its viscosity and hydrogen bonding. But when the water evaporates, the sand falls apart again into grains because there is nothing to hold the grains together. But, by adding salt to the sand, as the water evaporates, the salt begins to crystallize. The main content of rock salt, sodium chloride (NaCl), forms hydrohalite (NaCl • 2H2 O), and develops an elongated crystal structure during the crystallization process.25 The salt crystallized around the grains of sand holds the mold together.26 And, indeed, our molds made from rock salt and bone ash became very hard and durable when dried.

We tested the effect of grinding the sand after the moistening process. Because the binder created by rock salt and moisture formed crystals, we had to regrind the sand after moistening in order to get rid of coarse grains before molding with it. By regrinding, we also mixed the bone ash and the rock salt more thoroughly. Mold 1, made with once-ground homemade sand, left a rough surface on the cast, suggesting that regrinding is necessary (Fig. 13). In comparing the casts from Mold 2 (commercial sand) and Mold 6 (homemade sand), we found that the commercial sand created a sharper and more detailed cast (Fig. 14). The eagle’s feathers and the letters came out more sharply in Mold 6 (Fig. 15) than in Mold 2. This suggests that the finer the sand, the better and more detailed the resulting cast. Therefore we can say that the regrinding process has at least three effects: first, to mix the bone ash and rock salt well; second, to make the sand finer so that the cast is more detailed; and third, to break down the clumps in the sand and enable the rock salt to crystallize more evenly.

We tested brandy and charcoal powder as separating agents, as instructed by the manuscript, to keep the pattern from adhering to the sand. Brandy had little effect. In contrast, charcoal powder was effective in allowing release of the pattern, as well as in separating the two halves of the double-sided mold.

Finally, we poured tin into the mold. In order to test the author-practitioner’s statement that this mold produced a cast that came out very neatly in soft tin, we also poured sulfur into the mold. The sand took tin well and molded cleanly, while in the case of sulfur, the sand stuck to the sulfur and could not be removed (Fig. 16).27

What is Fatty Sand? The Categories of Fat and Lean

Where we attribute the binding power of this sand of calcined bovine foot bones and rock salt to the slow crystallization of the salt, the author-practitioner attributed it to the fatty nature of the salt, writing, “The bone of the foot of oxen is always so lean on its own that, without being mixed with one or two parts of some fatty sand & one that has bond” (fol. 89r). On fol. 89v, he notes that another frequently used binder, sal ammoniac, is also fatty. The terms “fat” (gras) and “lean” (maigre) appear in many entries in Ms. Fr. 640.

Clays

We get a sense of what characterizes “fatty” materials in the author-practitioner’s notes about clays, for example, in his instructions about the right type of clay to choose to make the mold walls in plaster molds: “Clay... should be very fatty & handleable .... one ought only to choose the quite fatty kind, and beat very well, & moisten it moderately with some water” (fol. 108v). Clay is in fact named as “fatty earth” (terre grasse appellee ardille) (fol. 112v). The clay-like substance, Armenian bole, is also viewed as fatty, indeed as excessively so: “Finely sieved brick is better, because the bole is too fatty” (fol. 12v) Clays are used by tanners and fullers, and the artisan who makes sheaths for swords uses “small, very delicate skins of lambs and young goats, almost like parchment ...When they want to imprint some history, they layer them in the hollows of their figure, then put over the top fine fatty earth with which cloths are rid of grease [i.e., fuller’s earth]” (fol. 57v). On fol. 12r, we hear that this material is known as “terre chimolée, otherwise known as fuller’s earth, with which they dress the cloth, is excellent for molding hollow or in relief; & if you want to reheat it, it must first be warm, & reheat it on a gentle fire at a distance & little by little, otherwise it would crack.... It is very soft, neat & beautiful.”

The utility of clay or “fatty earth” for metalworkers, and, in particular, for founders, is made clear on fols. 77r and 41r in “Fatty earth” (Terre graisse): “Founders, to make the handles of their bells, use it. And before it is used, they beat it very well, then leave it to dry in the shade, little by little & for a long time, and mold with it very neatly. But it must not be sandy &, but rather well chosen & soft” (fol. 77r). Further characteristics of this fatty earth that are useful to founders are revealed on fol. 41r:

Earth for casting, for founders

It renders itself fatty once beaten and also mixed with horse dung. Potter’s earth would be too fatty and would crack & would not hold in the fire, but one needs to mix it with half as much sand and a quarter or a fifth of horse dung. And leave it to dry, then turn it to powder, then sift it to render it fine & purged of gravel, which would prevent it from casting neatly. The dung renders the earth more amenable & easier to deal with, but it is necessary that it be well free of straw & other things. And when the earth is very fatty, one needs to give it more sand & more dung. But one does find fatty earths, in and of themselves mixed with sand. If they are not, do it by artifice. One always needs to reheat the earths before casting.

This passage makes clear that the natural properties of fatty and lean earths can be transformed by the artifice of the metalworker. It also indicates that fatty earths are soft, malleable, but also crack more easily on drying, and must be dried slowly. This is affirmed on fol. 165r in an observation about plaster molds: “When it is long to set & dry, it is a sign that it is too fatty & moist, which is what makes it contract in fire & crack, causing flashing. That which is drawn from lean soil is the best. Molds of fatty plaster ought not to be kept for a long time, for they act like fatty earth, which when drying by itself, cracks.”

Other Fatty Materials

Materials other than clay had the property of fattiness as well, including black pitch (fol. 44v), tallow, especially that “which is fatty & shiny, from the big kitchens” (fol. 93r), feather alum (fol. 107v), and sugar (fol. 126r). Interestingly, the author-practitioner states that “Sugar is fatty when runny and brittle & breakable when dry” and, additionally, sugar can also be sour & brittle (fol. 126r). Lye, when new, is not “fatty” and can be used to degrease objects cast from tin (fol. 157r). Pots and mortars used for heating or grinding certain materials can turn “fatty” and ruin good fish glue (fol. 159r).

Varnishes and Colors

Varnishes can also be fatty, for example, on fol. 3v, in “For varnishing:” “Turpentine varnish does not need any glue because it is fatty & viscous & it is not absorbed in the wood.” On fol. 4r, in “Varnish of spike lavender oil,” “the sandarac is well melted, which you will know by taking a little of the said varnish on a plate, and if it is fatty when you handle it with a finger, it is ready.” Fattiness is also a factor in gilding, for example, on fol. 6r, where we learn that “Ceruse & lead white is not appropriate for polished white nor for burnishing because it is fatty, but it is quite good for or mat which is made with oil.” Indeed, in discussing fatty oil in connection with gilding and painting, we learn quite a lot about the properties of fatty materials: “take only fatty oil, which is made in seven or eight days in the sun with walnut oil and lead white & with, stirring it often, or cooking it on the fire if one is in haste” (fol. 7r).28 In the entry on “Fatty oil” (Huile gras), we learn that it “is not good for working with colors because it makes them thick, & as difficult to work as azur d’esmail. Therefore use the clearest walnut oil that you can, & the freshest.” Some colors, however, need fat to give them body: “The azure requires a little fatty oil because it has no body” (fol. 58v).

Indeed, gold colors, including mordant gilding, are made successfully from “fatty colors:” “All fatty colors, such as ceruse and minium, massicot, ocher, lead white, are good for making gold color” (fol. 65r), and again on fol. 100r, in an entry on quickly making a “Gold color,” starts by producing a “fatty walnut oil in the sun or on the fire, as said above, for it thickens by itself in this way, although there is neither lead nor ceruse.”

On fol. 60r, we learn that oil needs to have a little “body,” which, as we have seen above, is created by exposing the oil to the heat and light of the sun, or by adding lead, by heating it, or by adding calcined pulverized glass.

Walnut oil extracted like peeled almonds is very white. The one of palma christi. And when the oil has a little body, the colors soften in it. For if the oil is too clear, the colors run & do not have bond, even those that hardly have any body. Fatty oil that is not easily imbibed is appropriate for varnish. The oil is desiccative enough when it dries out as quickly as common varnish. Oils do not dry as quickly in cold countries as in hot countries. Oil exposed to the sun is clarified well. But it fattens, if you put in ceruse or lead filings or lead white or calcined pulverized p glass.

Oil should not, however, have too much body or fattiness, and “To avoid this, put the vial [of oil] in clear water,” presumably to keep the sun from affecting it (fol. 60r). In painting in distemper (e.g., glue and pigment), “All colors that have no body in oil have none in distemper, and but in distemper they sink to the bottom. Distemper colors want to be fatty, which is recognized when they take hold on the palette once tempered in oil” (fol. 64v).

If we tally up these characteristics of “fatty” so far, we have “handleable,” malleability, softness, moistness to the point of liquidity (such as in the sugar example: “Sugar is fatty when runny and brittle & breakable when dry”, fol. 126r). This liquidity can be contrasted to dryness, and fatty things must be carefully dried because they crack easily. Fattiness also gives body or substance to colors, is especially useful in gold colors, and is associated with lead and lead-based pigments, but renders hard-to-work materials, such as azur d’esmail (here probably meaning a pigment made of blue glass), even more difficult.

The visual appearance of fattiness is indicated on fol. 10r in the recipe for “Counterfeit Jasper,” in which the “horn presents a luster & fatty polish like jasper.” In this process, the author-practitioner seeks to imitate the lustrous, silky “fatty polish” of jasper by using horn. Fattiness is also characterized by slipperiness: in “For diamond points for engraving,” “when these points are fatty & cannot bite, one needs to rub them diamond against diamond” (fol. 11v). The slipperiness of the diamond points means that they do not get traction and bite the engraving plate. More clarity on these nature of fattiness as sticky or slippery is found in entries to do with lead as a metal used in actions of cutting, filing, and chasing: in “Cleaning files,” the author-practitioner notes that “Sometimes they are fattened, & by means of the grease, the lead, which is fatty in itself, or other filings, attach to it. And you will be able to clean them either with hot charcoal or a scratch brush made of wires of latten” (fol. 159v). In “Cutting lead,” because the lead “is fatty & grips & sticks to the knife or chisel, wet it & you will cut it like glass” (fol. 154r). Here a fatty stickiness is resolved by wetting the material. In chasing (i.e., afterwork to an object after casting), “Lead is so fatty & soft that one cannot strike it boldly, & one needs to have a very light hand. The other great metals [i.e., the nobler metals] are easier” (fol. 156r).

Taking these passages all together, we can see that the property of fattiness is homologous with fat itself: shiny, moist, slippery, malleable, but also sticky (which can be remedied by wetting with water), easily transformed to a runny liquid, tending to viscous especially when mixed with some materials, slow of drying, and prone to cracking.

Fatty Sand

It is in the sand recipes, however, that the meaning, properties, and utility, as well as the means of transforming the fattiness by artifice come into sharper focus. Some sands are naturally fatty: “Sand from corp rock is always better, which seems like tuf in lumps, which has a beautiful & very fine grain, & a little fatty” (fol. 70r). Too much fattiness can render the sand unfit for casting because it can destroy its bonding capacity: “Thoroughly burnt sand loses its bond. Slate is reheated & molds neatly. Next, it is true that it often becomes porous, as burned earth also does, as fatty sand also does” (fol. 87v). If sand is “too fatty, one ought to reheat it and sieve it again” (fol. 82v). Good molding and casting sands must be sought out carefully; the author-practitioner tells us what to look for:

Sand

You should not choose for casting the one that is in any way so lean & arid that it does not have bond, like the one from the sea or desei from sandy paths dried out by the sun. But choose them or in t in the first place from quar reries ries or rock caves, because that one is best, provided that it is very fine. You will recognize the vein of it if, among the greyish rocks or quarries qu that seem to be made of arene, you see, after rainy weather, certain small patches like dust attached to the said rocks, or if due to the humidity, a small piece of it flakes off, which easily crushes between your hands. You will also find in lean soils some which comes off in lop large lumps like stones of tuf et ne , or among terres bolvenes, which are much better than those in fatty & strong earths. And one ought not to take it from the surface, for fear that it be mixed with common earth, but three or 4 feet below & the closest to the tuf or stone. Since it comes out in large lumps one would say that it starts to take shape as a stone. But when it is wet, it comes apart easily. One needs to make sure that when breaking it up it is quite granular, & that when crushed between the fingers, it renders itself into very great subtlety, like impalpable, keeping nevertheless its sandy asperity, without muddying the fingers like clay. It is dried slightly on the [illegible] fire, then it is pestled & passed finely through a double sieve or a linen sleeve, then is moistened with wine or magistra &c (fol. 69r).

Excellent sand can also be made by the artisan:

It is necessary that lean sands be more moistened than others, namely with magistra or good pure wine or boiled wine with elm tree roots & similar things. But very fine sands, like burned linen, which is fatty & soft on its own, want to be applied dry (fol. 69v).

Some molders, like those from Foix, “make their sand with crocum & calcined slate, but slate always retains its asperity & becomes porous, because it is fatty [....] They sieve their sand, grind it on porphyry, and wet it in water, & they take away the finest, which is on top, then reheat it” (fol. 143r).

The author-practitioner engaged in intensive experimentation with sands in order to find the correct mix of lean and fat:

The sand cast for casting should be chosen neither so lean that it has no hold, nor too fatty. And although it is found in nature, it is nonetheless not everywhere. And if you are in a place where it is not found, you can make it, but not with fatty earth, for the sand does not want it at all, for it makes it very porous. But you can give it bond with brick thoroughly ground on marble, or plaster or calcined alabaster or something similar, or the burned marrow of ox horn or burned aspalte throughout. If you grind it quite finely on porphyry, it s acquires hold & then you can burn it with aspalte or mix it with a quarter part of tripoli. Guard against bread falling into your sand because it makes it very porous. Try mixing in soot black (fol. 41r).

On fol. 89v, he emphasizes that color of the sand does not matter, but rather its bond and/or its small particle size:

It does not matter if the color is white or yellow. Above all it must be in one piece & as if taken from a quarry or rock formation, & the deeper one takes it from, the better. The signs of its goodness are that it is thus amassed, and that when removing itself in the form of rock, it comes out in lumps & qu bricks, which demonstrate its bond & that it is not too lean. However, it should break apart between your hands & have very small men & delicate grains & of the same nature.

But, again, he affirms that artifice can transform a deficient sand:

If it is not delicate enough, you can pass & grind it finely, either through water or through a sieve & when it or on the porphyry, & in this way, from sil lean they become fatty & well bound. You can mold with it in sa frame or en noyau without cloth waste, & try it with lead, for if with this it does not become porous & casts neatly, it will also behave well with copper. Some say that the fatty sands do not want the metal to be cast too hot. Artisans who work in large works &, who for profit, do not need to grind & seek the curiosities of artificial sands, benefit more from seeking some that is ready-made by nature, which has the finest grain possible, & for small works, they only pass it through a sieve. But those who work in small works, finely grind it & grind it impalpable, because they do not need a lot of it (fol. 89v).

And, indeed, he affirms that “any sand can be rendered good:”

Artisans who work in large works & who need to further their profit by seeking things already prepared in nature, because she does not sell her wares to her children, and to also save the time they would use for grinding finely & for artificially preparing sands, seek the one of the s mines, which is not too fatty, the one that is a kin of earth, not too lean & consequently without bond, but rather that which is pulled from the depths of the sand-bed in bricks & clods that show its natural compaction, which is quite difficult to break & which has a very small & delicate grain, & which is found soft when handling it between the fingers. And because the latter is only found near the rocks in mountainous areas or lean territories, & akin to the arene, it cannot be found in in the surroundings of all the bonnes villes where artisans willingly gather. And thus, if they do not have it close to their house, they prefer to have it come from afar, like from Lyon, Venice, Paris near the Sainct Chappelle & similar places, rather than prepare it. However, you can be certain that in all places you can render the sand from a mine good & proper for molding. For if it is coarse, pass it through a sieve, and if it is not yet fine enough, wash it, & when the water has rested a little, empty that which is still troubled in some separate vessel. The coarse will promptly fall down to the bottom of the first vessel, but the one which will have come from the troubled water, set apart, having settled, will be very fine. And then, if it does not have enough bond, grind it quite dry on porphyry, & you will render it impalpable, & which will have bond like chalk. Then, if it seems right to you, you will reheat it & again will grind it & mix it with salts, or linen, or burnt felt, or ashes of paper & similarly washed things (fol. 90r-v).

In order to be used for casting, sands need to possess binding capacity, which is created by the addition of fatty materials, often salts. These passages reveal, however, that very finely ground substances also work to create a bond.

Fatty Metals

Sands and the metals cast into them operate in a reciprocal relationship because metals, too, have fat and lean properties: “lead being fatty & weighty, carries more,” thus “one lb of the latter, two ℥ of tin” is needed to cast thick things, for if there is too little tin, porosity will result (fol. 131v). When beaten, tin does not crack as easily as lead “although [lead] is more fatty,” notes the author-practitioner with surprise (fol. 159r). Adding lead to silver will make the alloy “fatty” (fol. 164r). Lead’s low melting point makes it easily molten, and it is “ lively & runny & liquid” (fol. 131v), while gold is dry and lean (fol. 153v). Lead and tin can be better molded in stone molds than in copper molds, “because copper is fatty & sticks” (fol. 48v). The iron substance, crocum ferri, made from iron filings oxidized in vinegar, can work an ameliorative effect on molds: “And when your mold is soft & fatty when scraping it with the fingernail, it is a sign that the crocum is good, very fine, & well prepared” (fol. 132v).

While seeking a suitable sand for lead, the author-practitioner thinks back over his trials:

Since then I have realized that this sand, even though it is excellent & endures many castings & molds very neatly, nevertheless is fatty and makes things porous. Thus, soft lead and the does not come out so well. But try to mix it with a lean sand, such as pumice, scales & similar things to give them body & so that they release better. For lean sands barely release well and yet they receive metal well (fol. 85r).

Then, in his account of his “Experimented sands,” he has something like an epiphany, even underlining his important conclusion in the text:

I find that when a sand is so finely ground that it renders itself dense as ceruse & even, like without knowing it to be arid, rarefied, sandy & rather spongy, that it molds very neatly, but it does not receive metal so well as if it were porous to absorb the substance. But rather, once fatty & even, it becomes porous & does not receive fine features. I believe that the secret to cast well lies in finding a sand that receives the metal well, one for lead, the other for another, for each one has its particular one (fol. 86r).

And, on fol. 84v, he generalizes, “A fatty metal needs lean sand.”29

Fat and Lean, Sweet and Sour

Fat and lean are also connected in the author-practitioner’s material imaginary with another set of qualities—sweet and sour—especially as they apply to metals. Sweetness (dous, doulx, doux, douls) causes softness and malleability and is associated with purity, sonorousness, and newness (i.e., newness in a metal means not being reheated many times, e.g., fols. 48v, 150r).30 Sourness (aigre) is associated with brittleness and porosity. These properties in metals can be enhanced or ameliorated by the qualities of the sand: “Lead & tin come out well in white chalk but the softer [plus doulce] it is the better....Note that any brittle [aigre] metal comes out better than the fatty. Also, lean sand receives & drinks it better than the dense one” (fol. 53r).

Base metals like lead can cause souring (and brittleness): “Goldsmiths do not work at all with the silver from reals that is not alloyed, because it is leady & when forging it sours” (fol. 154r). Quicksilver (mercury) also sours metals, if left in molds after cleaning (fols. 115r, 117v). Sourness causes cracking (as does fattiness), and, in order to sweeten silver, the author-practitioner recommends throwing into the well-melted silver “some dry mortar composed of sand & good lime which has been worked, & pulverize it.” This appears to be an action analogous to adding dry, finely ground powder to remedy a fat sand in order to prevent porosity and cracking (fol. 154r).

Gold is especially sensitive to the presence of the base metals, and can be soured by lead and tin in the forge (fol. 124v), in the mortar (fol. 128r) and in the crucible, and even by a mold into which these metals have been poured (fols. 108v, 119v). Great care must be taken not to bring gold and silver into contact with lead, and, if “figures of wax composed of ceruse & lead white” were used as models in the mold, the mold should not be employed to “cast gold & silver... this would sour it” (fol. 131r). Sweetening the noble metals of gold and silver implies softening them so they run better when molten, and the author-practitioner includes recipes for making gold and silver more fluid by adding a substance that warms and sweetens (or softens) them. For example, in “Softening [or sweetening] gold,” he writes, “Sometimes, there is gold so dry it that neither cement nor antimony can soften [or sweeten] it. But only verdet can render it soft [or sweet]” (fol. 153v). On the next page, he includes a recipe for “Softening [sweetening] silver” (fol. 154r). The addition of what we would nowadays name a “flux,” such as that containing “verdet” (or verdigris) as is indicated here, can soften a metal so it becomes more liquid and “runs” better through a mold, thus showing itself more “tractable” (traictable, fol. 106r). The term adoulcir thus has several meanings that are largely interchangeable: it can connote “to soften,” “sweeten,” and “smooth.” In metals, this can mean to make them more malleable, tractable, or liquid; in painting, it can mean to blend the colors, shades, or shadows to create a more lifelike effect, while in drawing, it is used to mean blending a round form to give it an illusion of volume. The senses of adoulcir merge to a degree, all tending toward “tempering” and “mollifying” (as Cotgrave’s 1611 Dictionarie also gives), which signifies bringing a material or process toward a new, more desirable or useful balance among its qualities, or in its outcome.31 This understanding of the process as “tempering” is part of the early modern worldview in which all entities (living and nonliving) possess a natural balance that can be manipulated to bring about change in state, nature, or qualities.

Material Imaginary

The foregoing survey of fat, lean, sweet, and sour in Ms. Fr. 640 demonstrates that the fatty sand of fols. 84r and 89r with which this essay began is part of a material imaginary, in which substances are understood as embodying qualities that can be ranged along a spectrum framed by the binary categories of fat-lean and sweet-sour. These qualities are simultaneously experiential and conceptual, for they are known by experience and the senses, but they also organize thought and action.

These qualities can be observed visually by paying attention to the look of the sand bank, or the fluidity of a molten metal, as when the author-practitioner notes, “Lead wants not only to be red, but also lively & runny & liquid” (fol. 131v). The qualities of the materials are also sensed by feeling, for example, in how the sand runs between the fingers, whether the mold materials are dry and crumbly or soft and handleable. Material properties are sensed by taste, as when the author-practitioner specifies that sal ammoniac water used as a fatty magistra for molding sand must taste “moderately salty” (fol. 111v). In sensing the qualities of metals, even hearing comes into play, as in judging if your alloy of metals contains the correct balance of fat and lean metals: “If you put too much lead into the tin, it does not run very thin. And to know this, if l the tin cries loudly, it is a sign there is not too much lead. If it cries weakly, that means that there is too much” (fol. 131v).32

The ability to recognize these qualities and to generalize about the behavior of materials based on this framework enabled the artisan to engage in a process of balancing materials, not just by combining metals into different alloys, for example, but also in balancing the fatty metal with a lean sand.33 The artifice of the author-practitioner consisted in broad experience and much trial of materials, during which he paid close attention to their behavior, and he classified them within this framework—or material imaginary—of binary qualities. This gave him the ability to generalize and hypothesize about materials and to plan future trials (hence the many notes to “try” various materials throughout the manuscript). This material imaginary enabled him to make choices about the kinds of sands, binders, and metals he sought out, and to test them against his expectations about their interactions. And, above all else, the material imaginary underpinned his art of rectifying and rendering workable and useful the materials provided by nature.
