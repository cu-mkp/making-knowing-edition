Reverse Painting on Glass

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/m1ke-kd05
← Back to Essays

Abstract

In an entry on fol. 39v titled “For tracing some history on glass,” the author-practitioner describes a technique now known as verre églomisé that imitates basse-taille enamelling. This essay examines the reemergence of this ancient technique in early modern Italy and France, and it draws on early modern objects and technical manuals to how early modern artisans understood the tasks of the painter and the goldsmith as overlapping fields of artistic production with particular shared techniques, materials, and concerns for optical effect.

The recipe on fol. 39v is one of a number of recipes in Ms. Fr. 640 that deal with glass.1 Only one of them (fol. 36v) discusses the manufacturing of glass, while another one (fol. 20v) refers to different types of glass from different regions of France. Seven others are concerned with aspects of painting on glass, including making color for glass painting (fols. 37v, 31v, and 41v), laying color on glass (fol. 39v), gilding on glass (fol. 41v), and the techniques of painting on glass (fols. 31v, 37v, 39v, and 102v). This indicates that the author-practitioner was not particularly interested in how glass was produced but rather in glass as a support for painting. This recipe, along with the other recipes concerning painting on glass, can thus be situated within a subset of recipes in the manuscript that address various techniques of painting.

The recipe talks about three ways of “tracing some history on glass” (trasser sur le verre quelque histoire).2 In this essay, we focus only on the third technique, that is, to “make gilt histories on glass with a background of colors, which imitates the basse-taille of goldsmiths” (fol. 39v). The process can be broadly divided into four steps: gilding on glass, engraving patterns, applying color, and finally backing with a metal sheet. This technique is now called verre églomisé, an anachronistic term derived from eighteenth-century French decorator and art-dealer Jean-Baptiste Glomy (1711–1786), who was largely responsible for the technique’s revival.3 This paper uses the term verre églomisé to denote this technique; however, it is important to bear in mind the technique’s long history before the term was coined in the eighteenth century.

One of the many issues raised by this recipe is the link between painting and goldsmiths’ work in the early modern period and its implications for modern hierarchies of art and media. This link is implied by the author-practitioner’s description of the sought-after effect of the painted object in terms of goldsmiths’ techniques. Although the author-practitioner called it “tracing some history on glass,” this essay will consider it as a technique of “reverse painting on glass” because it was understood in the early modern period as technique primarily concerned with painting, both in terms of the characteristics of the objects it produced and the process involved to make it. It should be emphasized that the term verre églomisé is today associated with the “decorative arts” rather than with the “fine art” of painting. Thus, in addition to pointing out the fluid boundaries between the early modern professions of the painter and the goldsmith as well as their shared spheres of knowledge, this essay also aims to challenge the modern distinction of “artist” from “craftsman,” and it argues for a historicized understanding of “art” as ars.

Our first question before starting the actual reconstruction work was to figure out what the final product was that the author-practitioner was aiming for. The recipe does not mention any information about the intended use of this type of painted glass, nor does it mention its scale. Therefore, it was not easy to envision the outcome of this recipe, and we first needed to carry out a thorough textual analysis accompanied by object-based historical research.

One of the earliest surviving recipes concerning a technique similar to that mentioned on fol. 39v can be found in Cennino Cennini’s Il Libro dell’Arte from the last years of the fourteenth century. The description Ceninni gives overlaps to a great extent with that on fol. 39v. Following his discussion on stained glass painting, Cennini gives a detailed account of this technique, in which one shall “take a piece of white glass,” then use egg white to gild gold on the glass, to be followed by taking “a needle, fastened in a little stick as if it were a little brush” to “draw whatever figure you wish to make,” and finally applying “various colors ground in oil, such as ultramarine blue, black, verdigris, and lac.”4

Already in ancient Rome, a similar technique was used to decorate medallions, roundels of cups, and other vessels (Fig. 1). A manuscript compiled in 1431 by Jehan le Begue, notary of the Master of the Mint at Paris, from recipes collected by Jehan Alcherius, contains a recipe similar to the verre églomisé technique, which according to Alcherius, was inspired by ancient Roman works. Alcherius wrote that “the Romans made themselves phials of glass, artfully varied with gold, very precious, to which I gave great pains and attention.”5 He then tried to make a similar work himself, and the procedure he described is the same as the fol. 39v recipe in that the practitioner is to first wet the glass with gum water, then apply gold leaf onto it, and finally engrave images on the gold.6 Theophilus Presbyter (fl. early twelfth century) also mentioned the use of this technique in Byzantine goldsmiths’ work in his text De diversis artibus.7 However, there is little other evidence of this technique being used in Europe from the sixth century until its reemergence in the last third of the thirteenth century in Italy.8

A key clue in the recipe which enabled us to better contextualize the technique is the specific mention of the sought-after effect to imitate the basse-taille of goldsmiths. Basse-taille is an enameling technique in which the artist first creates a low-relief pattern on a metal surface by engraving or chasing, and then completely covers the metal surface with ground glass; when fired, the ground glass produces a translucent colored enamel, through which can be seen the low relief pattern.9 The technique was probably developed in Italy around 1280, which coincided with the reappearance of verre églomisé, both being attested first in Tuscany.10

Basse-taille enamelling increasingly began to appear across Europe, notably in France and Flanders. From the mid-fourteenth century, Toulouse, where Ms. Fr. 640’s author-practitioner was likely based, would become an important center for the production of basse-taille enamels, along with Montpellier in southern France, Rouen in northern France, and Paris.11 A drinking vessel (1320–1360) from the Metropolitan Museum of Art (Fig. 2), the bottom of which was decorated using the basse-taille enameling technique, likely comes from Toulouse. A manufactory of basse-taille enamel existed in Montpellier by 1317.12 Ms. Fr. 640’s author-practitioner also mentioned a technique which can be clearly identified as basse-taille enamelling on fol. 136r in the section titled “Enamelling thin works,” in which he notes that “goldsmiths scrape, with the brim of a burin, gold leaf & then they set enamel down on it.”

Most of the surviving basse-taille works from the late medieval and early modern period functioned as devotional objects and are characterized by their small size, glossy surface, and vivid color. Nevertheless, we cannot assume that this was the sole application of basse-taille enamel. Due to financial needs and the change of tastes, gold and silver works were often melted down or reused to create new types of objects, which could possibly explain the rare survival of basse-taille enamels for secular functions.

Recipes in the sixteenth century often associate basse-taille enamelling with painting. In his preface to the Lives of the Most Excellent Painters, Sculptors and Architects, Giorgio Vasari (1511–1574) discussed a variety of techniques used in architecture, sculpture, and painting. He listed “enamels over reliefs” under the section on painting, followed by a discussion on stained glass painting and engraving, and he described enamelling as “a kind of painting intermingled with sculptures” (e specie di pittura mescolata con la scultura).13 In his Treatises, the Italian artist Benvenuto Cellini (1500–1571) discussed in detail the basse-taille enamelling technique. He mentioned that “the method of enameling is much the same as painting,”14 and instructed his readers to “lay the nice clean colors over your engraved bas-relief just as if you were painting,”15 as well as “to apply them very thin and neatly, just as if you were painting in miniature.”16

In fact, many early modern painters, such as Cellini, were trained as goldsmiths. Vasari in his Lives, lavished praise on the spectacular enamelling craftsmanship of Antonio del Pollaiuolo (1429/33–1498), who is now predominantly associated with painting. According to Vasari, Pollaiuolo executed “some very beautiful paxes in San Giovanni, which were so colored in the fire, that they could scarcely be done better with the brush,” “but many of his works, [...] because of the city’s needs in times of war, have been destroyed and melted in the fire.”17

By conceptualizing reverse painting on glass in terms of basse-taille enamel, and basse-taille enamelling as a type of painting, we were better able to understand the procedures and intended effect of the recipe on fol. 39v. The processes of preparing a metal surface, engraving it, and then applying a solid coloring substance can all find parallels in the making of verre églomisé suggested by the author-practitioner.

Another connection between basse-taille enamel and reverse painting on glass is the shimmering and lustrous effect they both produce. Basse-taille enamel was also called translucent enamel (emaux translucides).18 Lustrous, metallic ground-work can be seen through a layer of enamel. Subtle tones and shading can be produced by varying the depth of the engraving. Translucency was a much prized quality in enamels. Cellini mentioned a kind of transparent red enamel (rouge clair) as the “most beautiful of all,” while another non-transparent red enamel “has not the splendid color.”19 A letter dated to 1556 from the archives of Modena addressed to Cardinal Louis d’Este asked him to seek in Paris the famous red translucent enamel called the “Old Christus”20 and send it back to Ferrara.21 Rouge clair also appears on fol. 124v, where the author-practitioner talks about the visual effect caused by the interaction between gold plate and red enamel, claiming that “gold [...] with its own pale color renders the enamel a dead color,” and further suggesting that one should put it back on the fire again after it is cut to make the enamel look more translucent and lustrous. A fine example of basse-taille work with red enamel is the Royal Gold Cup at the British Museum (Fig. 3), which shows a beautiful translucent red color lavished on the gold chalice (Fig. 4).

Interestingly, the author-practitioner also used the term “rouge clair” when he provided a recipe on fol. 6r for making a turpentine-based red lake varnish that could be glazed onto a gilded wood panel. This suggests once more the association of enamelling and painting noted above. This connection involving shared effects across a variety of techniques and materials may also apply to the author-practitioner’s imitation gem-making.

The same type of red lake varnish was also used as coloring for verre églomisé, as mentioned in the recipe on fol. 39v, which states, “fill the background with azur d’esmail or verdigris or fine laque platte22 tempered with clear turpentine, mixed with a little of tear of mastic if you want that the colors are more even & do not spread.” We made a red lake paint based on the instructions on fol. 6r, first mixing ground madder lake with walnut oil, to which we then added turpentine varnish. Next we applied the color according to the procedures on fol. 40v: “When you apply your turpentine colors to your glass panes, first place them on a hot tile &, once they are hot, spread your colors & leave it a while on the tile.” The result obtained is a translucent and lustrous red color, similar to clear red enamel (Fig. 5). We also put a tin sheet behind the glass pane and viewed it from the front side. Indeed, as the recipe said, the tin sheet “gives light to the colors” (Fig. 6). The red color appears much more intense and warmer with than without the tin sheet as backing (Fig. 7).23 Our reconstruction work also made clear that the author practitioner associated the translucent quality of the red lake varnish with red enamel, as well as associating painting with enamelling.

A case study concerning two reliquaries, one made with verre eglomisé and the other with the basse-taille enamelling technique, can offer insight into the fluid boundaries between basse-taille enamel and verre églomisé, as well as between goldsmiths’ work and painting. A Sienese reliquary frame (1347) in the Cleveland Museum of Art (Fig. 8) that originally enclosed a panel depicting the Virgin and Child enthroned, which is now at the Fitzwilliam Museum (Fig. 9), is an outstanding example of the application of the verre eglomisé technique. The frame itself has four verre eglomisé heraldic panels (Fig. 10) which identify the patron as Mino di Cino of the aristocratic Sienese Cinughi family and the location to which it once belonged—the Ospedale di Sta. Maria della Scala, Siena’s foremost hospital. As Dillian Gordon has shown, the shape, design, and iconography of the central verre eglomisé panel all have equivalents in local traditions of panel painting. The unusually tall, slender format of the glass panel finds equivalents in works by Bernardo Daddi and his workshop.24 The enthroned Virgin and Child was derived from paintings by Simone Martini and his workshop.25 This verre eglomisé panel has been attributed to the Sienese artist Lucas di Vieri, brother of the accomplished Sienese goldsmith and enameler Ugolino di Vieri. The most notable work of Ugolino di Vieri is the Reliquary of the Santo Corporale (1337–1338) in the Duomo of Orvieto (Fig. 11), one of the largest reliquaries ever commissioned from Siena. The eight basse-taille enamels in the upper and middle registers of the shutters on the front depict the Miracle of Bolsena, and the four enamels on the front and the twelve enamels on the back depict scenes from the Passion (Fig. 12). Those around the base depict scenes from the early life of Christ. The structures of these two reliquaries bear great resemblance to one another. They both feature a rectangular base with convex corners, finials, and buttressing, and they also share similar decorative elements, such as the acanthus leaf borders and receding tiled floors.26 We know very little about the lives of Lucas and Ugolino di Vieri brothers, but it is likely that knowledge of verre eglomisé and basse-taille enamelling circulated between the workshops of the two brothers, who perhaps worked for a time in the same workshop, which would have facilitated the translation of visual idioms from one technique into another.

It has been suggested that Lucas di Vieri tried to achieve a simulation of precious metalwork with cheaper materials through the verre eglomisé technique.27 However, this does not necessarily mean that verre eglomisé was valued less. The incredibly fine skills the technique requires and the splendid visual effect of the final product were highly praised in early modern Europe. Cennini, in the opening passages of the recipe concerning the verre eglomisé technique, wrote it was “beautiful, refined, and rare as it is possible.”28

Interestingly, at the end of the entry describing the process on fol. 39v, the author-practitioner seems to harbor the same sense, as he explicitly states the aim of creating something “without being at all expert in painting.” He even suggests that the practitioner of this art might want to “hide [the] secret” by covering it with tin leaf that also doubles as a reflective background. He thus seems to view this process as a kind of shortcut or trick—a secret—that a practitioner might want to keep to himself. Whether it was for reasons of portraying himself as more skilled than he is, or as a time-saving device, we cannot know. However, the process described in this entry seems to have been common practice among glass painters (Fig. 13 and Fig. 14). The practitioner requires hardly any expertise in painting because the colors need only to be crudely blocked out, and the gold leaf can be applied as entire sheets without careful shaping (Fig. 15). The special skill of the practitioner consists not in those aspects of the process, but instead in scraping off the gold leaf to create the painted “history,” or narrative of the piece. The author-practitioner collected other processes that imitated more labor- or cost-intensive practices—for imitation marble, imitation rouge clair, and imitation gold leaf—and it would appear that such imitative processes resulting in an impressive effect or a verisimilitude of a more precious material were, perhaps paradoxically, especially valuable in his estimation.

Towards the end of the fifteenth century and continuing through the sixteenth, thus contemporaneous with the compilation of Ms. Fr. 640, verre églomisé grew increasingly popular in the making of pendants. Paintings on glass in the style of verre églomisé, usually portraying religious scenes, were set into precious metal pendants and served as private devotional objects (Fig. 16). In the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries, the heyday of basse-taille enamel, many pendants were instead executed in the basse-taille technique. The popularity of employing verre églomisé to make pendants in the later period further enhances the links between this technique and goldsmiths’ work, in particular with regard to gemstones. In many of their works, goldsmiths placed circular verre églomisé glass side by side with gemstones. A reliquary cross (Fig. 17) in the Victoria and Albert Museum is adorned with precious gemstones around a scene of the Crucifixion in verre eglomisé. The shape, color, and texture of the verre eglomisé all show great similitude to the gemstones set around it. Another example can be found in a painting depicting the Virgin and Child at the Metropolitan Museum of Art by Paolo di Giovanni Fei (Fig. 18), which has retained its original fourteenth-century frame. The gilded frame is decorated with gemstones as well as verre églomisé medallions depicting the Annunciation, saints, and Christ. A recipe on fol. 40v brings together verre églomisé, enamel, and gemstones. In this recipe, the author-practitioner discusses the “Cross of the commanders of Malta.” Rouge clair pigment (not enamel) is first painted on silver leaf, just as in making verre églomisé, and then covered with white enamel. He particularly emphasizes that the silver leaf is “a thicker kind,” which is used by the makers of foil backings for gemstones. Applying a backing is also the last step to complete verre églomisé. Although the author-practitioner did not explicitly mention the link between verre églomisé and gemstones, their connection is clear both in the manuscript and in goldsmiths’ work from the same period.

The author-practitioner describes this technique as “tracing some history on glass,” and I have referred to it as “reverse painting on glass,” which associates it with techniques of painting, whereas verre eglomisé does not associate the technique with painting, nor with its elevated status. Instead, it relegates this technique to the lower status of a “decorative art.” As this essay demonstrates, however, by taking into consideration the language employed in a variety of historical sources, as well as the procedures of making and the appearance of the final product, we can understand this technique, like the basse-taille enamelling which it was intended to imitate, as painting. Both were executed by goldsmith-painters, and both were described as endowed with various attributes of painting. Thus we can conclude that there was much more to “painting” in the early modern period than is commonly thought today. The recipe on fol. 39v reveals to us how painting and goldsmiths’ work can be understood within the terms, materials, and techniques of the other as well as within the identity and knowledge shared by painter and goldsmith.
