Abstract

Divya Anantharaman came to the Making and Knowing Laboratory in fall 2016 as Expert Maker in residence, where she reconstructed entries from Ms. Fr. 640, including “Animals dried in an oven” on fol. 130r, for preserving animals intended to be displayed as lifelike specimens. This essay records her responses to the early modern methods she encountered in Ms. Fr. 640 and the reconstruction she and the Making and Knowing Team undertook.

PLEASE NOTE: this essay contains images and description of preparing dead animals for taxidermy - it may be disturbing to some.

Techniques of modern taxidermy have not changed much from the ways of ancient preparators. An animal is skinned, flesh and fatty elements removed, skin is then preserved, and the skin is sewn up around a form (anatomical replica). The techniques in BnF Ms. Fr. 640 stand somewhere between mummification and taxidermy. In “Animals dried in an oven” (fol. 130r), the entry calls for the removal of the “eyes & all their entrails” (which would go rancid) but leaves the muscle and other tissues intact (these essentially turn into “jerky” or mummify as they dry in the oven).

As a taxidermist, I was most fascinated by the use of gravity and hanging the specimen upside down in order to create a balanced standing figure when it was dry. Modern taxidermists will dry certain bird and mammal mounts hung upside down in order to make feathers or fur stand a particular way; however, in such cases, the interior is a synthetic foam form instead of the hardened muscle and tissue—modern taxidermy removes all of this, the only thing used is the skin.1

The painted lead and wax balls as eyes were interesting too, as the author-practitioner instructs: “Next, one places in the hollow of the eyes balls of lead or of wax, painted according to nature. One paints them with well-gummed ink so that they seem to be jet” (fol. 130r). Today, modern taxidermists are similarly concerned with making the prepared animal look lifelike through the use of realistic eyes, which now are made of glass, acrylic, resin, and more. Making naturalistic eyes for taxidermy has become a whole industry in itself.

There has been a resurgence in gaff making and fantasy style taxidermy, creating hybrid creatures like jackalopes, two-headed creatures, or winged foxes. Many attribute gaff making to Coney Island sideshows or Victorian whimsy, but seeing the author-practitioner’s suggestions that “one gives it a painted tongue, horns, wings & similar fancies. Thus for rats & all animals,” proves that human fascination with hybrid animals is probably much older than our documentation of it.

Ms. Fr. 640 gets closer to modern taxidermy in the recipes for “Keeping birds and animals” (fol. 48r):

skin them Take a measurement of their body, which is fleshy & more subject to decay, with some canvas that you will cut to their size & width. And having cou filled it with cotton & sewn it, skin them, birds leaving, however, the head, the neck, the wings & the feet on the birds, & the head, the legs & the feet & tail on the animals, because those dry easily. Then fit that skin on the a you mold made of canvas. Small ones are dried in an oven or prepared with sal ammoniac & [illegible].

In this entry, the author-practitioner removes the entire torso of the animal and creates something close to a taxidermy form out of canvas firmly packed with cotton. He keeps the head and limbs to dry out in the oven or with a chemical preparation. This comes a bit nearer to some nineteenth-century taxidermy methods in which skins were removed from the body, cleaned, defatted, and washed in arsenic soap, then mounted onto forms made of tightly bound wood, wool, and metal wire. Perhaps the author-practitioner saw shrinkage or an undesirable shape in his preparations, and thus his methods evolved?

The closest Ms. Fr. 640 comes to modern taxidermy is the marginal note on “Molding a bird” (fol. 157v):

They are skinned & filled with cotton cloth. & even better, one needs to dress them in the fashion that curriers do, in order that they do not lose a feather, weight. The dressing is alum & paste of flour.

Even today, tans and preservative powders include alum (along with other chemicals), and preparing a hide for taxidermy is somewhat similar to tanning a large skin for leatherwork. There are differences in thickness, quality, and other aspects, but the technique of using a chemical to preserve the skin is the same. The proper preservation of a skin is crucial to making sure it is long lasting, and that fur or feathers do not fall out due to decomposition, deterioration, or the pH of the preservative being too acidic or basic.

Interestingly, this marginal note to “Molding a bird” (fol. 157v) about the skinning and stuffing of the bird is actually part of a molding recipe:

One needs to give it its shape. And to do this, place it on its side on a clay slab, and hide & sink within it half the said bird, in order that it only shows one leg & one foot, then you will anoint it with butter to make it hold the feathers down, finally ofoil eau-de-vie. And then cast. And being set, clean & uncover whatever needs it. Next, mold the other side in the same way. But because the feet, being of bone that is difficult to burn & which are not able to be cleaned neither by quicksilver If nor by any other means, if the mold would not open, uncover the bottom of the feet & the toe or the little finger of the claw, & mold as a separate piece. Reheat & open your mold & clean the bones. The feet usually remain too weak to support the mass of a solid bird. And for this reason, one adapts an iron wire in the mold of the feet which passes into the body before casting; thus, they are reinforced. Some mold the wings in a frame. Others mold the head & the said wings separately en noyau [in a one-piece plaster mold], then join & repair them to first model a form that will be stiff & withstand the tempered sand.

This could be a modern tutorial on mold making in taxidermy if the materials used were alginate, silicone, plaster, etc. This recipe is one of my favorites because it seems like the author-practitioner is making a reference cast or replica, so he can recreate a bird more realistically. Modern taxidermists use similar mold making techniques to create taxidermy forms, or to make “death masks” of the parts of animals that tend to shrink in death (noses, paws, parts of the face). These casts are used to help rebuild the anatomy so the animal appears as lifelike as possible. This may also be a means of preserving an animal that is not available all year round, a concern expressed by the author-practitioner in “Molding snakes in all weather” (fol. 116v), where he notes methods for producing lifecast snakes in the winter when snakes “hide in the earth.”2

Reconstruction

As the Making and Knowing Project’s Expert Maker in fall 2016, I reconstructed the process on fol. 130r with the Making and Knowing laboratory seminar:

Animals dried in an oven

Small s cats are skinned and one removes their eyes & all their entrails. One puts a small stick vertically between their teeth to make them open their mouths wide. Then with iron points one attaches their feet to a small board, giving them the fitting attitude & gait. Once thus attached by their feet with small rings of iron wire thus [small sketch inserted], which, surrounding the fingers of the paws, stick in the jacket of clay, one needs to suspend them in the sun with the backbone downward. In this manner they take &d their shape & dry, & the belly tightens & the tail remains high or with the bend that you will have given it. Once a little dry by this means, one turns the head as one wants, securing it with some tool. Then one finishes drying it in an oven when the bread is drawn. Next, one places in the hollow of the eyes balls of lead or of wax, painted according to nature. One paints them with well-gummed ink so that they seem to be jet. One gives it a painted tongue, horns, wings & similar fancies. Thus for rats & all animals.

At the time of this reconstruction (Fig. 1), the Making and Knowing Project did not understand the difference between the three entries (fols. 130r, 48r, and 157v) and their two different methods of preparing an animal. Only fol. 130r specifies that the bones and muscles should be left in the skin to give structure; the other two entries (fols. 48r and 157v) replace bone and muscle with a shaped pouch filled with stuffing. Because at that time we understood the entries to give instructions about separate parts of a single process, we mixed techniques from the three different entries.

I started by skinning the kitten, since fol. 130r specifies that “small cats are skinned.”3 I intended to remove all its interior parts (Fig. 2, Fig. 3), but, after we re-read the recipe, I realized that only the entrails and eyes should be removed. I thus cleaned the fat from under the skin and put the kitten back into its skin.

We removed the eyes and entrails in all the animals, as fol. 130r specifies that “one removes their eyes & all their entrails” (Fig. 4, Fig. 5).

We followed the entry on fol. 157v, stating “they are ... filled with cotton cloth.” The kitten was filled with stuffing (Fig. 6), while the mouse (and the rats which the Making and Knowing team prepared months later) was given a sewed pouch as instructed on fol. 48r: “with some canvas that you will cut to their size & width. And having cou filled it with cotton & sewn it” (Fig. 7, Fig. 8, Fig. 9, Fig. 10).

I then applied a preservative preparation, as noted on fols. 48v and 157v: “one needs to dress them in the fashion that curriers [leather workers] do, in order that they do not lose a feather, weight. The dressing is alum & paste of flour” (Fig. 11).

I followed the instructions specifying that “one puts a small stick vertically between their teeth to make them open their mouths wide” (Fig. 12), and I gave a twist to the tail, for, as the animal dries “the belly tightens & the tail remains high or with the bend that you will have given it” (Fig. 13).

Next, “with iron points one attaches their feet to a small board, giving them the fitting attitude & gait” (Fig. 14). “Once thus attached by their feet with small rings of iron wire thus, one needs to suspend them in the sun with the backbone downward” (Fig.15). The author practitioner gives an alternative method on fol. 129v, in which the animal is given its shape, fastened to a clay slab with pins (as in lifecasting), placed in sand (as is also the method for drying flowers), then placed in an oven in which the bread has just been taken out, where it should stay for “a day & a night in order that it is dry, for otherwise it would be smelly.” He cautions that the heat of the oven should not be “too harsh, but rather like the heat of the sun in June, otherwise the animals cook.”4

The animal is then dried suspended upside down (which in our reconstructions took place in the lab’s fume hood for a minimum of two weeks). The entry notes that “next, one places in the hollow of the eyes balls of lead or of wax, painted according to nature;” however, we found that the eyes needed to be inserted before drying because the eyelids dried shut (Fig. 16).

The Making and Knowing team reconstructed the techniques again using rats (Fig. 17) sourced from Petco (where they are sold frozen as snake food).5 Then, following the instructions on fol. 130r that “One gives it a painted tongue, horns, wings & similar fancies. Thus for rats & all animals,” I attached sparrow wings to the rats during the 2017 Working Group Meeting focused on “Vernacular Natural History, Practical Optics, Perspective, and Mechanics, and Medicine” (Fig. 18, Fig. 19, Fig. 20, Fig. 21).

I pinned the wings to the rats (Fig. 22, Fig. 23), giving the wings different positions (Fig. 24, Fig. 25, Fig. 26, Fig. 27).

Reconstructing the recipes from Ms. Fr. 640 with the Making and Knowing Project has given me a much greater appreciation for the makers and artists from the past who used so much innovation and creativity, and trial and error, in order to create their works. Their techniques are surprisingly similar to today’s methods (Fig. 28).

Epilogue

The results of Divya Anantharamam’s residence as Expert Maker with the Making and Knowing Project have become an enduring source of fascination in the Making and Knowing lab and demonstrate one of the important sixteenth-century aims of creating such realistic hybrid animals. Like the lifecasting that fixed short lived creatures such as butterflies in metal,6 these hybrid creations invite speculation about the relationship between the artifice of nature and the artifice of the human hand. In early modern Europe, both lifecasts and these hybrid animals were collected and displayed in Kunstkammern—chambers of art—that brought together naturally-created and human-made specimens, often mixing them in ways that would provoke surprise and fascinated inspection.7 They were intended to amuse and confound, no doubt, but also to direct the viewer to a deeper contemplation of the relationship between nature and human art.
