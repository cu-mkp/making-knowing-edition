"Experience Will Teach You": Recording, Testing, Knowing, and the Language of Experience in Ms. Fr. 640

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/njnq-6q58
← Back to Essays

Abstract

This essay explores the language of experience in Ms. Fr. 640 to characterize the place of experiential knowledge in the author-practitioner’s epistemology. Based on a concordance search of the terms experience, experimenter, essayer, esprouver, and cognoistre, the essay highlights the author-practitioner’s often overlapping use of these words and how they map onto the modes of writing down experience that can be identified in the manuscript: inferences from repeated experience, accounts of singular events, specific material tests, and invitations to further testing.

Introduction

Following a description of the intriguing process of preserving flowers by drying them in sand, the author-practitioner lists a range of flowers which work well, including “amaranth & suchlike, and broom, and others that your experience will teach you” (fol. 121r). Clearly regarding experience as a good teacher and an important source of knowledge, he not only writes down his own experiences with different flowers, but also enlists the reader’s experience in a process of ongoing exploration of nature and the possibilities of human intervention.

By invoking experience as a teacher and a source of knowledge, the author-practitioner broached a lexical field which was becoming (and was still to become, over the following century or two) firmly associated with the practical knowledge of artisans and its increasingly confident articulations. It was, initially, a wide and not too finely differentiated field. Godefroy’s dictionary of medieval French, for instance, glosses expérience as epreuve or essai (themselves wide-ranging terms, as we shall see, including the meanings “proof,” “trial,” and “assay”) as well as the more general connaissance acquise (“acquired knowledge”).1 The Dictionnaire du Moyen Français (DMF) agrees with this definition, specifying that such knowledge could be acquired by observation, trial, or one’s own practice.2 Robert Estienne’s contemporary Latin-French dictionary of 1552 (first edition 1539) translates experientia as general expérience, but also again preuve, essay, espreuve.3 Randle Cotgrave’s 1611 French-English dictionary, much beloved of Making and Knowing Project translators, shows the fluidity between such terms: both preuve and espreuve, for example, are given the English equivalents “proofe,” “tryall,” “essay,” “experiment,” and “experience,” while expérience itself is rendered as “experience,” but also “cunning, skill, knowledge, wisedome, gotten by much practise, and many trialls.”4 Later in the century, Antoine Furetière’s “Universal dictionary containing [...] the terms of all the sciences and the arts” (1690) gives a more explicit account of the relationship between experience and knowledge. The main entry for experience reads, “reiterated trial (essay) or test (espreuve) of some effect which serves our reason to come to an understanding (connoissance) of its cause.”5 Citing the hackneyed Hippocratic aphorism that life is short and experience long, the entry continues, “modern physics is preferable to that of the Ancients, inasmuch as the one began to reason on causes, and the other only reasons on experiences. Descartes said that he trusted the experiences of artisans more than the speculations of all the learned.”6 In a sub-entry, Furetière mentions a more specialised use of the term: “among artisans and trade corporations, it signifies a half-masterpiece. Journeymen aspiring to mastership are obliged to make a masterpiece; the sons of masters only make a simple experience.”7 By the end of the seventeenth century, according to Furetière, experience, as a way of reasoning and as proof of practical skill, had become codified into regimes of knowledge, both learned and artisanal.

Ms. Fr. 640, written from within the artisanal culture of late sixteenth-century Toulouse, has a place in that story, which was of course not as linear as Furetière suggested. In fact, as far as the artisanal world was concerned, his assertion was little more than a learned acknowledgement of a culture of knowledge which had always been characterised by experience. When the author-practitioner invoked experience as a way of knowing, he participated in an already growing conversation about experiential forms of knowledge, their value, and their relationship with other epistemologies. This conversation spanned a range of written genres and was often associated with a set of common key terms denoting experiential knowledge, both in Latin and in the vernaculars which were increasingly becoming the language of choice for practical knowledge. In particular, the Latin terms experientia and experimentum (and their vernacular equivalents) embodied knowledge which was not derived by syllogistic deduction from universal principles but from personal experience of particulars. While experientia usually referred to experience or experiential knowledge in general, experimentum increasingly denoted specific snippets of insight from experience, in particular, individual medicinal recipes which had proven their worth in practice.8 Within Western scholarly thought in the sixteenth century, dominated by the Aristotelian hierarchy of knowledges, experience was deemed inferior to rational scientia, but in practice, scholars of medicine in particular acknowledged the value of knowledge from natural particulars rather than universals, and from observation of individual cases.9

New genres dedicated to observation and experience began to emerge, such as medical case notes known as observationes and curationes, or practicae containing experimenta.10 The advent of printing also saw the proliferation of Books of Secrets, a highly successful genre dealing with craft knowledge and nature’s hidden workings. Since these secrets often relied on specific knowledge of nature and the arts, or on the manipulation of “occult” properties which, by definition, eluded the grasp of scholarly reasoning, experiential knowledge was the unofficial name of the game.11

Recent scholarship, particularly the work of Elaine Leong, has characterised recipe books as another important (although heterogeneous) genre of experiential knowledge, especially outside the learned sphere. Early modern men and women, from nobles to modest householders, collected recipes, most often for medicines or foods, from friends and relations, correspondents, or books, according to their own means and interests. While these could initially be amassed haphazardly in rough books, many collectors developed inventive and rigorous regimes of testing their recipes through several iterations and transferred them to fair copy books or presentation copies, to be passed down the generations or to be published in print. At various points along the way, recipes changed in content and in epistemic status, from uncertain suggestions to tried-and-tested cures or foods, thus codifying in writing a way of knowing through repeated experience, as well as its results.12 As we shall see, Ms. Fr. 640 presents features which place it in close proximity to these experiential genres—medical casebooks and experimenta, books of secrets, and recipe books—although it does not precisely correspond to any one genre. As Vera Keller argues in one of her essays for this edition, Ms. Fr. 640 in fact marks a point in the slow emergence of what has been termed the “experimental essay” from recipes and related genres.13

In histories of science and medicine, discourses and practices of experiential knowledge are key to understanding the changes in early modern knowledge production from ca. 1500 to ca. 1700, often still referred to as a “Scientific Revolution.” Already in the 1940s, Marxist scholars posited that the experimental cultures and the shift towards inductive reasoning from particulars in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries had its roots in artisanal cultures of learning by doing and in detailed attention to materials and bodies.14 More recently, artists’ and artisans’ ways of knowing and their influence on scientific epistemology have been characterised by historians in more nuanced ways, but broadly confirming the central place of experiential knowledge in narratives of human understanding of nature.15 This complements histories of recording cases, testing drugs, and trying cures as experiential ways of knowing in medical practice.16 It is important to note, however, that individual references to “experience” or “experiment” do not signal participation in a single or well-defined scientific empiricism. As Alisha Rankin has pointed out, early modern approaches to nature were characterised by “a number of diverse, overlapping ‘empiricisms.’”17 Furthermore, individual practitioners differed in the way they used and understood such terms as “experiment,” “trial,” or “approved” facts, and their idiosyncratic languages of experience deserve case-by-case analysis.18

As an unusually detailed source of practical knowledge with a clear emphasis on things “which your experience will teach you,” Ms. Fr. 640 adds its unique voice to the manifold genres and histories of experiential knowledge. As this essay shows, it presents anecdotes of single instances similar to medical cases, secrets of craft and nature, tried and tested recipes, and open questions for future trials. While this source undoubtedly calls for a full investigation of its language of expertise and knowledge transfer—not least what it means to “teach” someone—this essay begins to approach these questions by focusing on author-practitioner’s concept and language of experience.19 It provides an analysis of concordance searches for the lexical field of the terms experience and the related experiment, but also the language of trying and testing centred around the author-practitioner’s use of the verbs esprouver and essayer, and the relationship of these terms to the concept of cognoissance as knowledge in a more general sense as well as the verb cognoistre in the sense of recognizing or knowing. In exploring these terms, I show how they map onto different modes of writing down experience which can be identified in the manuscript: inferences from repeated experience, accounts of singular events, specific material tests, and invitations to further testing.

Experience and Experiment

The French word experience itself is used in only three entries in the manuscript. They highlight the range of uses of the term in different modes of referring to experiential knowledge. In an entry on grafting trees, the author-practitioner writes, “one finds by experience that the graft that has its entrance into the incision on the sunny side takes very rarely” (fol. 88r). The insight into the process is asserted in the present tense and in a positive manner: it describes the usual and normal behavior of this kind of graft, presumably based on repeated observation of grafting in different ways. Similar assertions recur throughout the manuscript. The phrase “one finds by experience” (on trouve par experience) only occurs once, but the author-practitioner uses the shorter “one finds” (on trouve) to make similar claims, such as “one finds that [latten] comes out better alloyed with a quarter of copper” (fol. 70r), or, in one of many entries on sand for molds, “I find that one ought not to knock on very finely ground sands, for it shakes them loose” (fol. 87v). Nearly half the instances of the verb experimenter convey the same sense of knowledge from repeated experience. For example, the author practitioner’s assertion “I have experienced [experimenté] that grinding azur d’esmail with egg yolk & next washing it in several waters is good” (fol. 11r) speaks to a deep familiarity with the blue pigment which is evident in numerous other entries in the manuscript.

While many such insights are no doubt owed to the author-practitioner’s hands-on practice, he also uses experimenter to relate less immediate experiences. Describing a treatment for poisonous snake bites, he writes, “it is the same with toad’s venom, which I have seen experimented [experimenté]” (fol. 110r), implying that he had witnessed such cures but not necessarily been personally involved. In an entry on catching snakes for molding, he shares the observation that “they hardly ever bite in water, which crayfish catchers experience [experimentent]” (fol. 107r)—an experiential insight which was not his own but that of a group of experts on aquatic life. This kind of assertion, referring to the usual properties and behavior of things based on accumulated past experience, is one of the major modes of writing experience in the manuscript.

Another such mode is epitomized by the second occurrence of the word experience. Making ruby glass is a complex process in which gold leaf and a variety of mineral ingredients are melted at very high temperatures to achieve a red-tinted glassy substance, and it is not surprising that the author-practitioner did not succeed on the first attempt: “the first experience that I made of it, it only returned a yellow mass” (fol. 101r). Rather than making a positive claim from repeated experience, the author-practitioner relates a single, specific instance—in this case, an unsuccessful trial of a difficult operation, which informs his subsequent modifications and may serve as an example to the reader. Once again, the term experimenter is used in a similar way. In his many musings on casting sands, the author-practitioner recommends a specific composition which “is excellent for casting in frames, and I have experienced [experimenté] it thus: I pestled the pieces which had come out of noyau molds in a mortar, dragging the pestle, because this sand is very soft…” (fol. 118v). What follows on the next page and a half is a detailed account of the specific steps—grinding, sifting, moistening, molding, firing—which led to a satisfactory outcome. Written in the past tense to record isolated instances of complex procedures gone right or wrong, such anecdotes do not purport to offer general insights but deal with the singular, specific, and perhaps surprising. Whether they were intended as records to aid the author-practitioner’s memory of his own experiences or as cautionary tales for readers, in recording particulars of experience, these accounts take an important place in the manuscript.

Trying and Testing

The language of experience was intimately connected with that of trying and testing. Thus far this language has been observed in particular by scholars of medical genres such as casebooks, recipe books, or experimenta. A brief survey of key terms in the most prominent contemporary dictionaries shows that the same holds true for the French vernacular across a variety of sources. As we saw above, contemporary definitions of experience included notions of trial, test, and proof as well as such terms as essai and epreuve. The verb essayer itself is associated with the idea of trialling (eprouver) in medieval French,20 and in the Dictionnaire du Moyen Français, it ranges in meaning from trying food or drink before consumption and trying on clothes to more serious tests of quality, including for coin or minerals—what in contemporary and modern English would be called an “assay,” a borrowing from the French essai. Such tests could extend to workmen as well as their wares: guilds regulations from Poitou (the author-practitioner’s putative region of origin)21 in 1420 stated that no one should practice as a barber unless he has been tried (essayé) and approved (eprouvé) by the masters.22 The word also conveyed a more general sense of experiencing something, without the notion of a specific trial. That sense of learning from experience predominates in Huguet’s dictionary of sixteenth-century French, attested by Montaigne, the master of the essai: “those who have occasion to negotiate with stubborn women may have experienced [essayé] into what rage one throws them…”23 He also uses the term in the sense of a person “being tried”: “I am in the grips of the worst of all maladies… I have already undergone [essayé] five or six of them,”24 and, elsewhere, “it marvelously tries [essaye] the sick person.”25 Finally, Montaigne also writes of “discourses which try [essayent] to show our close resemblance to animals,” attesting to the use of essayer in its most common modern sense of “to attempt.”26

Conversely, the meanings of esprouver in the Dictionnaire du Moyen Français include performing an “assay” (faire l’essai), subjecting something or someone to an experiment with a view to verifying quality or truth as well as the outcome of such a trial, as in the etymologically related “to approve” (approuver).27 Experimenter has related meanings. In addition to experiencing something in the sense of living through it—such as Montaigne’s illness—it could signify learning through practice and by experience or putting to the test (mettre à l’épreuve). Another telling passage from the guild regulations of fifteenth-century Poitou states that no tailor should be allowed to set up shop until he had been experimenté de tailler—which we might translate, with Cotgrave, as “tried in tailoring.”28 Guild documents from Paris use similar language to regulate chandlers, who should be “tried [experimenté] and found to be expert.”29 For humanists and artisans alike, the vernacular of experience was inextricably connected with that of trying and testing, and the meaning of individual terms slipped fluidly between passive experience and deliberate trial.

It is no surprise, then, that similar patterns emerge in the experiential language used in Ms. Fr. 640. Even the word experimenter, which does most of its work in relating insights from repeated experience or singular events, is used on one occasion to describe a way of testing one’s materials. When salting artichokes, the author-practitioner advises, “take heed to test [experimenter] your brine with an egg, for if it is good & strong enough, the egg will float on top. If not, it is not strong enough and would not preserve the fruits” (fol. 98v). The strength of the pickling liquid is crucial to the outcome and requires a specific material test to verify. The manuscript is rife with testing procedures of this kind, involving an intervention by the practitioner specifically aimed at interrogating a process or a material.

More usually, these tests are signalled by the verbs essayer, whose main meaning is “to try,” and esprouver, which is etymologically related to “proof,” carrying a slightly more specific sense of testing or assaying. As we shall see, however, the author-practitioner does not always make a clear distinction between them. On the subject of the blue pigment azur d’esmail—one of the author-practitioner’s favourites—he says that it “cannot be worked if it is too coarse. Try [essaye] it, therefore, on the fingernail or the oil palette” to make sure it is not too grainy (fol. 11r). Elsewhere, he elaborates on expert assessment of azure before purchase: “to assay [essayer] it, painters bring their palette to the grocers, & temper & alloy it with a little white ground in oil, for in this way, the beautiful shows its turquine vivacity, but the bad is lavender grey” (fol. 93v). In a later entry on the same page, the author-practitioner recommends the same test for lake pigment, “which is also assayed [s’esprouve] on the palette with white” (fol. 93v). Describing a similar quality test for fermented cakes of woad or pastel, a blue dyestuff, the author-practitioner writes, “one assays [espreuve] it in the dyers’ vat” (fol. 39r); on the other hand, he describes the mandatory test shots for a new cannon as an essay (fol. 17r). His use of the verbs essayer and esprouver for similar quality tests—and even for the same procedure in the case of azur and lake pigments—suggests that he thought of these terms as interchangeable when it came to testing.

Both essayer and esprouver also appear in the context of testing or assaying in metalwork. One of the author-practitioner’s standard tests to determine the temperature of molten metal, for example, works by briefly dipping a small strip of paper into the melt. In the margin next to an entry on casting in molds made of cuttlefish bone, the author-practitioner notes that the alloy should be cooler than usual so as not to burn the bone: “test [essaye] for this effect with paper. If it turns the paper red, it is enough, it is good to cast, but if it blackens the paper, it is too hot” (fol. 139r).30 Elsewhere, on the other hand, he introduces a process for identifying a metal with the phrase “if you want to assay [esprouver] if it is tin or ☿ [i.e. mercury]” (fol. 92v). Although likely an expert metalworker, the author-practitioner does not seem to reserve a clearly codified and demarcated language of testing or assaying metals.

For specific tests aimed at verifying the properties of materials or the quality of a finished product, it seems that the author-practitioner does not make much of a distinction between essayer and esprouver. This is significant considering the connotations of these terms in their etymology and also later usage. For instance, given its etymological relationship with “proof,” one might expect esprouver to be used chiefly for official tests of saleable products; while the test for woad is indeed described using esprouver, the mandatory test shot of a cannon is referred to as essay. Nor do such tests evince a more stringent use of specific terms within areas of specialised expertise such as metalworking.

Where the use of the term essayer does differ from esprouver is in descriptions of a slightly different sense of trying and testing. Whereas the kinds of tests discussed above have a known outcome which indicates the quality of a material or product, the manuscript also contains more open-ended injunctions to try for oneself. In fact, these represent the majority of instances of essayer. In the margin next to yet another entry discussing sand, for instance, the author-practitioner notes, perhaps at some point after the completion of the body of the entry, “try calcined vitriol” (fol. 69r). Occasionally, materials are suggested as alternatives—“try also mixing, in place of pebbles, pieces of colored glass or enamels” (fol. 101r)—or explicitly as substitutes for others, as is evident in a recipe for stucco: “Lacking plaster, you can mix in well ground chalk or ceruse, & try bole & similar things” (fol. 29r). As well as materials, the author-practitioner suggests alternative processes. Rather than laboriously carving molds for tin casting into stone, one might “try to etch with distilled vinegar” (fol. 80v), a suggestion that appears in a marginal note. In the very next marginal note, the author-practitioner continues his speculative line of thinking: “try calcined oyster shells. They are said to be excellent for molding” (fol. 80v), a suggestion based on hearsay which he had clearly not yet tried himself but found promising.

Some injunctions to test things offer solutions to specific problems. When molding flowers, the author-practitioner describes how plants with very thin stalks often do not fully burn away during the firing of the mold because they do not have enough room—“try, therefore, to thicken the main stem with a little melted wax” (fol. 127r). In an entry on lifecasting animals, the suggested intervention—“try, having cast them in copper, to burnish them like gemstone foils, to see if they take color similarly” (fol. 129v)—seems to be motivated by a playful curiosity about the effect on the material. Whether they were intended as notes to himself or as calls to action for potential future readers, these invitations to try for oneself highlight the importance and open-endedness of experience as a way of understanding materials and processes.

As well as raising questions, the language of trying and testing can also be used to present the results of trials and experience. From his extensive testing of materials for molding and casting, he reports “I have tried [essayé] four kinds of sand for lead & tin: chalk, pestled glass, tripoli & burnt linen, all 4 excellent” (fol. 68r). Busting the myth of the allegedly unfading amaranth flower, he backs up his assertion that its colour can in fact be leached with white wine by noting “& I have tested [esprouve] it” (fol. 63r). The recipe for an “excellent water against the plague of Monsieur Montorsin” is pronounced “a very rare & tested [esprouvé] secret” (fol. 44v). Such endorsements in the language of testing are a common feature of records of practical knowledge. Householders keeping manuscript collections of medicinal and culinary recipes noted the success or failure of recipes in phrases such as “good,” or probatum est (“it is proven”), and authors of printed volumes used similar language to assure their readers that they were getting tried-and-tested information.

The language of trying and testing, then, evokes the whole process of knowledge-making by experience, from chance observations to deliberate trials to injunctions for further experimentation to tried and tested methods. Although this hardly constitutes a fully-fledged experimental program, with specific experiential terms precisely matched to different stages or levels of confidence, the author-practitioner’s entry on “Experimented sands” affords a glimpse of a somewhat structured epistemic process of trying and recording. The opening sentence, “I have experimented with sand from Th{oulous}e…” is followed by a meandering but detailed account retracing the author-practitioner’s steps through one of his many experiences with sand molding (fol. 85v). At the end of this anecdotal narrative, he draws his conclusions from this instance (and perhaps many others): switching to his italic handwriting usually reserved for headings, he writes and underlines “I find that…,” then summarises his general insights regarding the fineness of sand once more in his regular hand. A few lines down, a similar rubric in italic lettering reads “I believe that the secret…,” followed by his personal conjecture as to the secret of molding with sand—namely, to identify one sand that works well for each individual metal (fol. 86r). The visual emphasis of the different script and underline highlight the author-practitioner’s thinking: he literally draws a line under his personal, contingent, and messy experience to draw general conclusions and map out future experiments with sands.

While we should not over-interpret these few pages as anything like a rigorous research agenda, it vividly illustrates the significance of the author-practitioner’s language of experience. It encompasses specific tests which allow readers to ask directed questions of materials and products, probe their qualities, and assess whether they are suitable for the task at hand. It serves to formulate open questions and invitations to attempt experiments as yet untried; it also lends weight to assertions of tried-and-tested facts or remedies, illustrating a process during which materials and processes could change their epistemic status from whimsical idea to experiential insight.

From Experience to Knowledge

To understand the language of experience and testing in relation to the language of knowledge in a more general sense, it will be helpful to investigate the author-practitioner’s use of the word cognoistre (modern French connaître) and its derivatives, which can be translated as “to know” or “to recognize,” depending on context. With nearly seventy occurrences in the manuscript, it is key to the author-practitioner’s articulation of knowledge.

As a noun, it often denotes general knowledge or mastery, for example of perspective in drawing, which relies on “having the knowledge [cognoissance] of the point” (fol. 62r). More specifically, it may refer to intimate knowledge of a material. To work successfully with different kinds of plaster, one “needs to know [cognoistre] the nature of each” (fol. 125r). In the margin of an entry describing a complex and dangerous recipe for a flux to make silver melt and run more easily, whose many ingredients include toxic arsenic, the author-practitioner notes that “goldsmiths who have knowledge [cognoissance] of this substance sell a denier of it for 5 sous to other goldsmiths” (fol. 123r). Imparting a similar trade secret, he notes that a copper alloy will cast better if one adds a little saltpeter into the melt, but he recommends that this be done “by folding your additions in a paper in order that everyone may not know [cognoistre] what you mix in” (fol. 70r). Used in this way, cognoissance encompasses the craftsman’s expertise in a general sense: as hard-won knowledge of difficult materials and processes, and also as a saleable commodity which might warrant protection through secrecy.

As a verb, cognoistre often describes immediate recognition or insight in a very general sense. In two cases, it is even used for animals: a horse may be trained with sweet bread because it will come to know (cognoistra) its benefactor (fol. 54v), and birds who have never known (cogneu) their father’s voice can be taught a human tune (fol. 98v). Applied to the human senses, if often signals the recognition of straightforward clues. Small dogs used for retrieving birds from the water, for example, “are recognized [se cognoissent] by this, that they have a larger muzzle than others” (fol. 98v). Technical processes, too, yield such observable clues. When casting flowers, it is essential to ensure that the plant materials are completely burnt during the firing of the mold: “when you know [cognois] that the stem of a flower [...] is quite reduced to ashes, it is a sign that the rest is well burned” (fol. 157r). Reading the signs of materials also figures largely in the manuscript. Discussing cannon, the author-practitioner writes, “one recognizes [cognoist] the good alloy of a piece by seeing it. For if, with the greenness which they expel on the surface, they redden, that is to say that they are composed of a sufficient quantity of rosette” (fol. 17r). Interestingly, he goes on to say “if they themselves do not make this demonstration, scratch & you will see,” suggesting that cognoistre in this case consists in recognising cues which the piece itself reveals to the naked (although no doubt trained) eye, without further intervention.

If the material does not oblige, knowing it requires active probing, such as scratching the surface of the cannon. This, too, can be expressed by the verb cognoistre. The same entry states that old cannons are made of a characteristic alloy, and that “one recognises [cognoist] this composition with a burin,” by removing a small particle and thus assessing the consistency and colour of the metal (fol. 17v). Likewise, to determine whether azure pigment painted in oil has fully dried, a specific test is necessary: “if you want to know [cognoistre] if it is dry, breathe on it and it will not shine” (fol. 59r). These cases, where the properties of materials are not immediately “known” at a glance but only reveal themselves through intervention or manipulation on the practitioner’s part, resemble the material tests discussed above as one typical mode of testing denoted by essayer and esprouver.

The relationship between testing and knowing is more obvious in instances where they occur in close proximity. Discussing the famous Toulousain dyestuff, the author-practitioner writes, “the goodness of the pastel woad is known [se cognoist] when, put in the mouth, it gives a taste as of vinegar, or when crumbling & breaking it, it has some mold-like veins which are as if golden or silver. One assays [espreuve] it in the dyers’ vat” (fol. 39r). The initial assessment by immediate sensory experience, signalled by cognoistre, is followed by a more stringent testing procedure (espreuve) to determine how much fabric can be dyed with a given amount of woad. But cognoissance may also follow as the result of a contrived test. In an entry on varnish, we find the injunction to “test [esprouve] on a very clean knife, & you will know [cognoistras] then if it has enough body” (fol. 88r). In an entry on sand for lead casting, which is praised as “the most excellent of all,” the author-practitioner provides a first-person narrative of his own successful completion of the procedure. Starting, “I took ceruse and crushed it dry on porphyry…” the anecdote relates all the steps which led to the perfect sand (fol. 84v). Crucially, it also provides a clear example where the author-practitioner articulates the formation of insight from experience. When he heated the mold again to make a second cast, he writes, “my mold became hard like marble, and by this I knew [ay cogneu] that sands for molding high relief should be well moistened with some water, which gives them body & compactness” (fol. 85r). In other words, he points to a specific observation within an account of a single past experience to emphasise that he had “by this” (par là) a general insight about the way certain kinds of sand should be treated.

The language of cognoissance denotes knowledge of materials and processes in a general sense, abstracted from individual experience, but it is also often used in a similar way and in close conjunction with accounts of testing or personal experience. This close and overlapping relationship between experience, testing or trying, and more general knowing speaks to the importance of experiential insights in the author-practitioner’s concept of knowledge.

Conclusion

The language of experience pervades the manuscript, and is clearly central to the author-practitioner’s ways of knowing. While his use of the terms under investigation evinces his commitment to experience, it also shows that the individual terms do not necessarily correspond to solidified categories of experience but are often used in an overlapping manner. Experience and experimenter are used to convey a general sense of experience as a “teacher,” both in the form of insights about how materials usually behave from years of repeated experience and in accounts of single, specific events which, although anecdotal, hold valuable particulars. The author-practitioner collects such insights from his own experience as well as that of others, without apparently making a value judgment based on the immediacy of the experience—a concern which would become central to later regimes of experiential knowledge-gathering, for example in the Royal Society’s emphasis on witnessing of experiments, including what has been termed “virtual witnessing” through written accounts employing a rhetoric of experiential immediacy.31

There is also overlap with terms of trying and testing. While essayer and esprouver are consistently (if interchangeably) used for specific tests of materials and processes, on one occasion the author-practitioner tells readers to experimenter their artichoke brine. Interestingly, the verb essayer is distinct from esprouver in one sense: it alone is used for injunctions to try out alternative ingredients and new processes or just to see what would happen. It thus takes a special place in the author-practitioner’s language of experience as the word which opens up past experience to that of others, whether his potential readers or his future self, making his endeavor to learn from experience an open-ended one. In this, he fully recognises the epistemic power of collecting experience in writing, just as many early moderns did in their notebooks, recipe collections, and books of secrets. This opening up of his store of knowledge allows us to take seriously the closest thing we have to a kind of mission statement of his book: “from a desire to learn, [I] am exposing what little is in my workshop to receive, through a common commerce of letters, much rarer secrets from my benevolent readers” (fol. 162r).

Finally, in the terms cognoissance and cognoistre, we find the author-practitioner articulating a more general concept of what “knowledge” means to him: acquired skill and mastery of materials and processes, but also the ability to “know” or “recognize,” at a glance, touch, or smell, the properties of things or the key stages in a process. This marks a potentially revealing distinction from the more invasive and specific material tests signalled by essayer or esprouver. One could cognoistre the composition of an alloy by its colour, or at a stretch by scratching it with a burin, but one would essayer or esprouver the quality of pigments or dyes by putting them through their paces on the painter’s palette or in the dyeing vat. It is tempting to see in this distinct use of language the root of the categorisation of experiential knowledge that subsequently came to characterise the New Science. Taking into account how much Francis Bacon’s program owed to the epistemic practices of craft workshops,32 it is very plausible that his distinction between the observation of nature in its normal course and the “trials and vexations” of experimental interrogation reflects distinct forms of experiential knowledge that were the bread and butter of early modern artisans.

While a study of selected terms cannot lay claim to definite conclusions, it shows that experience was at the heart of the author-practitioner’s articulation of knowledge and how to get it. Although he does not always make fine-grained distinctions between his terms, the pervasive use of this language and its close relationship with his concept of cognoissance reveal a conscious use of experience as a source of knowledge, and a loose, eclectic, yet structured approach to experimentation.
