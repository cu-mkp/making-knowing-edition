Producing the Digital Edition of BnF Ms. Fr. 640

Naomi Rosenkranz and Terry Catapano

Introduction

How-to texts and technical treatises such as Ms. Fr. 640 elude traditional methods of editing and historical analysis. Ms. Fr. 640 contains entries on a wide range of making practices from the sixteenth century, requiring the expertise from numerous domains to decipher the text. Understanding the material may involve historians of science and art, curators, and practitioners familiar with the manuscript’s described processes. The Making and Knowing Project thus employed historical textual research, object-based study, and hands-on experiments to reconstruct the processes and materials of Ms. Fr. 640 to better understand the content. These methodologies are perhaps most obvious in the research essays that detail historical reconstruction of the manuscript’s entries, but they are present throughout all aspects of the Edition, including the editorial and digital development. The transcription and translation of the manuscript requires deeper understanding of the artisanal/technical processes in order to produce an accurate and informed digital text, as discussed in Soersha Dyon and Heather Wacha, “Turning Turtle: The Process of Translating BnF Ms. Fr. 640.” Similarly, as detailed in Celine Camps and Margot Lyautey, “Ma<r>king and Knowing: Encoding BnF Ms. Fr. 640,” the process of encoding the text was not a straightforward application of a predefined set of XML tags.1 The Project’s encoding emerged in light of additional insights gained about the manuscript’s contents as the interpretation of the text continuously evolved. The texts’ markup became clearer, more precise, and consistent and, like the translation, required editors who were able to integrate historical, technical, and practical information. This methodology is described in further detail by Pamela Smith in “Making the Edition of Ms. Fr. 640.”

The methodologies, decisions, and data management strategies employed by the Making and Knowing Project emerged and were refined continuously throughout the development of the Edition. The nature of Ms. Fr. 640’s technical subject matter and of the Project’s non-traditional research methods, as well as time constraints, defied working according to any pre-ordained plans for the Edition. The collaborative editing and integration of training throughout the research process resulted in digital development that itself was iterative, interdisciplinary, and collaborative. The transcription, translation, and encoding of the manuscript text were completed in yearly summer training workshops for graduate students. The research essays were composed by students in semester-long graduate laboratory seminars. Expert guidance, peer review, and contextual essays were provided by a wide range of scholars in yearly Working Group Meetings. Early digital development took place in text workshops and new course offerings in the digital humanities at Columbia University (Fig. 1, Fig. 2, Fig. 3, and Fig. 4). The design and development of the Edition site was a collaboration between Performant Software Solutions and the Making and Knowing Project team. All components and aspects were under continuous review, consideration, and revision by the core team of the Making and Knowing Project.

Background: The Making and Knowing Project

Through the creation of Secrets of Craft and Nature, the Making and Knowing Project aimed to:

  • Bring researchers together from different disciplinary backgrounds to work collaboratively

  • Train students and participants

  • Create an iterative flow of research to continuously revise the interpretation of Ms. Fr. 640

The Project involved students, scholars of the humanities and social sciences (such as historians of science and art, anthropologists, and museum curators), natural scientists (chemists, physicists, and conservation scientists), practitioners (conservators, metalworkers, taxidermists, and painters), and digital technology specialists (computer scientists and digital librarians and developers). Teaching and knowledge exchange took place in summer workshops, semester-long seminars, annual working group meetings, conferences, hands-on sessions, studio classes, and locations such as archives, laboratories, kitchens, classrooms, and even foundries and marble quarries.

In each year of development, the Project’s schedule of activities to iteratively study Ms. Fr. 640 was guided by an annual thematic topic such as Metalworking and Moldmaking (2014–15).2 The cycle would begin with an intensive summer Text Workshop, a graduate training workshop in paleography, translation, and encoding, during which students gained skills in middle French script and textual study by transcribing, translating, and encoding the manuscript. The draft transcriptions and translations produced in the Workshop formed the basis for textual, object-based, and hands-on laboratory research carried out by students in the Laboratory Seminar, a graduate-level course at Columbia offered each fall and spring semester. Each student would compose an extended annotation on one or more passages in the manuscript, resulting in the Edition’s Research Essays. At the end of each academic year, the Project convened a Working Group Meeting with invited experts and the Lab Seminar participants to discuss and critique the students’ work. The Meetings provided critical expert review and brought rich new insights from the scholars’ varied disciplines. In the same way, the year’s laboratory research informed the encoding schema and the transcription and translation activities of the next summer’s Text Workshop. The interpretation of the manuscript evolved continually, drawing from the material reconstructions of the Lab Seminar and knowledge exchange of the Working Group Meetings. Beginning in 2017, the Project offered new semester-long Digital Seminars for preliminary prototyping and textual analysis of Ms. Fr. 640. Through each cycle from Text Workshop through Lab Seminar to Working Group Meeting to Digital Seminar and prototyping, new insights were gained, generating questions and shaping research and development in the next phase in the cycle.

Digital Development

Introduction

Given the goals and methodologies of the Project, it was clear from the onset in 2014 that a flexible and gradual approach was required for the Edition’s digital development. The form of the Edition emerged from the Project’s iterative research design. It would have been impractical to make upfront decisions about particular technologies, software, or the encoding schema and nearly impossible to conceive of all component features. Instead, the Project began with two guiding principles that informed all decisions and modes of work for digital development:

  1. Generate and and preserve the Edition and key Project data in sustainable forms through commitments to minimal computing and open formats and technologies wherever possible; and

  2. Prioritize and facilitate the Project’s multifaceted collaborative research process

Digital Sustainability and Minimal Computing

The Edition’s development was firmly grounded in the principles of minimal computing, which generally refers to work that is undertaken with significant technical, financial, or social constraints such as limitations in resources, funding, personnel, hardware, or software.3 For the Project and other colleagues at Columbia University, minimal computing is closely tied to digital sustainability: emphasizing durability of digital publications through reduced complexity and the use of fundamental technologies and workflows that have a greater likelihood of persisting. This commitment is founded on the conviction that the Edition should serve as a resource to a wide and diverse audience, enabling knowledge exchange now and into the future.4 To publish a digital product like the Edition, this means creating static web sites that minimize server-side technology (see Static Site Generation below). This approach reduces maintenance overhead, security risks, and exposure to technological obsolescence. The Project committed to using a small, common, and well-established technology stack: HTML/HTML5 with CSS and JavaScript served statically from a webserver. This stack harnesses fundamental technologies—ones that have existed and endured since the early days of the web—so that products created with them are more likely to persist into the future.5 The Project’s technological considerations reduced the need for continued resource expenditure on active maintenance, addressing issues such as security vulnerabilities or version upgrades of components and their dependencies. For example, without the use of (proprietary) databases or Content Management Systems (such as WordPress or Drupal), creators are better able to maintain control of their publications because they are not reliant on third-party products or platforms. This foregrounded the Project’s emphasis on the separation of prepared data from its representation via interfaces, which are highly subject to technical change and obsolescence.6 Data format and storage decisions prioritized the ability for that data to be readily transformed to multiple target formats so that content could be routinely exported and converted to open standard formats, maximizing reuse and persistence.

Facilitating the Research Process

The digital development of the Edition took place in tandem with content development in a workflow which maintained a dialog between technical and editorial activities. This was (and, to some degree, still is) an unusual mode of work for a publication, particularly for digital scholarship. A more usual practice found across academic institutions and editorial projects is to separate the “scholarly” from the “technical” work.7 Researchers produce scholarly information and style it in written form, at which point they hand it over to digital technicians who prepare it for publication. Experts work within their own domains until they pass their part along to the next set of experts. For the digital critical edition of Ms. Fr. 640, following this traditional model might have meant the transcription was completed by Lead Paleographer Marc Smith, whose transcribed text would have subsequently been translated by Director Pamela Smith. She would then have provided Digital Lead Terry Catapano with plaintext that he would have encoded using a very basic TEI tagset, which would have then been given to Designer-Developer Nick Laiacona for rendering online. Critical commentary could have come from the transcription and translation processes, and additional research (through reconstruction, for example) would have been conducted from the static French or English versions of the text. Each of these steps would have been completed with little input or crossover between them.

As the Project has shown, this traditional approach would have prevented the deep engagement and understanding required for the study of Ms. Fr. 640 and similar technical, artisanal, or instructional how-to texts.8 The manuscript codifies procedures that are often difficult or impossible to understand without the appropriate technical knowledge, hands-on experience, and historical understanding to carefully decipher terminology and processes of historical making practices. This applies to the study and presentation of Ms. Fr. 640 from both the editorial and digital perspectives. The interdisciplinarity of the Project’s historical and material research extended into the digital development to take better advantage of digital technology (and scholarship) for the study of historical artifacts and texts.

How, then, to best facilitate such an integrated research process while maintaining careful management of the evolving digital research assets? Every yearly cycle (of Text Workshop to Lab Seminar to Working Group Meeting) resulted in additional and revised transcribed, translated, and encoded versions of Ms. Fr. 640, student essays, associated images and videos, and commentary to be incorporated from new insights. The Project’s strategy needed to support several conditions:

  • Digital development taking place in tandem with new and revised content produced in multiple editing campaigns, requiring workflows that could adapt accordingly

  • Growing volume of data in various formats

  • An encoding schema that evolved over the course of years

  • Data edited collaboratively by up to 50 people at once (sometimes more)

  • Access to data in development by Project team members as well as collaborators from different disciplinary backgrounds, possessing varying degrees of expertise, from inside and outside the Columbia community, domestically and internationally

The treatment of Ms. Fr. 640 as a digital text was thus not just a technical or digital matter, but a social one, involving coordination of people and of scholarly inquiry.

The Project’s approach also had to be consistent with the commitment to minimal computing. Many technologies that best enable exploratory and collaborative work are not always congruent with best practices for long-term sustainability of data. Additionally, some kinds of scholarly content and experimental work developed in an educational setting cannot adequately conform to minimal computing standards and thus require a different approach.

The Project had no set plan for creating the digital critical edition from start to finish, thus there were no precise workflows, sets of tools, management systems, or concrete instructions laid down in advance. Instead, the Project was adaptive and flexible, developing strategies and methodologies that were responsive to participants’ needs as they arose. The development of creative workflows and workarounds occupied much effort and time on the part of the core team. Protocols had to be reviewed frequently, with much revising, adjusting, and sometimes outright reversal. Most critically, the entire team collaborating at any one time needed to understand that all work was part of a larger endeavor and thus that modes of work should serve the Project’s overarching goals and needs. This often required compromise and negotiation, especially in the face of policies developed to ensure longer term consistency, which seemed tedious to the individuals who participated perhaps for only a few weeks in the Project activities (see examples below).

Collaboration and a flattened hierarchy were key elements in making this halting and sometimes ad hoc process work. The manuscript at the core of the Project was an intriguing and puzzling research object and it attracted participants who were eager, motivated, and passionate to unravel more of its knots. Moreover, Project staff, students, participants, and collaborators were all equally unfamiliar with much of the content of the manuscript. They thus felt comfortable raising questions and suggesting new workflows. Students and team members were hungry to learn new skills through the Project. The variety of disciplinary backgrounds and skill levels led to an extremely generative atmosphere of enthusiastic sharing and learning from one another. Students and instructors had to be equally courageous in admitting ignorance, being open to experimenting and learning together, and potentially to failing, which led to new approaches.9

Early Visions of the Edition

While the Project avoided making “final” commitments for the Edition, its development was informed by a vision of the purposes it should serve as well as a few key features. The first feature was a side-by-side dual pane interface that would allow a user to view any combination of two versions of the manuscript, choosing from the facsimile images, the French transcription, the lightly normalized transcription, and the English translation. Partly inspired by the fluid text edition of Herman Melville’s Typee (Fig. 5),10 the Project felt that this viewing experience would enable a reader to better comprehend the experiential knowledge contained in the writings of the author-practitioner. The dual-pane view also maximizes the transparency of translation decisions, presenting the graphical and textual sources for comparison.

Another key feature was the integration of critical commentary alongside the rendering of the manuscript text, making editorial notes and links to the multimedia research essays readily accessible. Taking inspiration from the digital essay “Kafka’s Wound” by Will Self (Fig. 6 and Fig. 7), the embedding of relevant resources into the manuscript presentation would offer immediate illumination of technical processes, materials, language, and translation decisions.11 Finally, it was felt crucial that the manuscript be searchable to quickly find specific results and draw new connections across Ms. Fr. 640’s contents. Together, these features would open the manuscript’ content and allow others to engage with its rich record of making practices and exploration of the natural world.

To help envision the eventual elements of the Edition, the Project created early wireframes, mockups, and prototypes. Some of these were one-off projects developed in brainstorming sessions with digital humanists and textual scholarship colleagues. In 2014, a fruitful session with Columbia’s Group for Experimental Methods in Humanistic Research (xpmethods) led by Dennis Tenen helped the Project team visualize ways in which the text of the manuscript could be represented.12 These proof-of-concept mockups modeled ways in which the layout of the transcribed text could mirror the layout of the manuscript page itself (Fig. 8) as well as how field notes taken by students as part of their research on the manuscript could be represented (Fig. 9). To test some of the desired features seen in Herman Melville’s Typee and “Kafka’s Wound,” Research Associate Claire Sabel created a few webpage wireframes with dual-pane views and a variety of multimedia resources associated with manuscript pages (Fig. 10 and Fig. 11). When Rosenkranz joined the Project in 2015, she and Sabel further refined these mockups (Fig. 12). Notably, these early wireframes stacked the viewing panes one atop the other rather than side by side. The side-by-side layout, which would later become a hallmark of the Edition, was explored by Project Digital Programming Assistant Bingyan Hu in a 2015 Wordpress prototype that incorporated the Project’s preliminary transcription of the manuscript as well as the facsimile images of Ms. Fr. 640 (Fig. 13). All of these prototypes helped to visualize and spur discussion about the Edition and its components as well as to convey these ideas to team members, collaborators, and potential funders. Additional working prototypes, including two minimal editions implementing the Ed Jekyll theme and the Prototype Edition created by the Project’s Designer-Developer, would play a more integral role in the editorial process, as described in further sections below.

Preliminary Digital Development

Before the Making and Knowing Project’s work on Ms. Fr. 640, access to the manuscript was limited to microfilm files or physical viewing of the object at the Bibliothèque nationale de France (BnF) in Paris. In 2013–14, Project Director Pamela Smith worked from these black and white facsimile copies with Research Associate Claire Sabel and a few collaborators to create preliminary draft transcriptions.13 Smith launched the Making and Knowing Project in 2014, with the first Text Workshop held in the summer. Then called the “Paleography Workshop,” it took in applicants interested in gaining skills in the study of historical writing and letterforms (paleography) by working on the transcription, translation, and basic digital encoding of the Middle French text of Ms. Fr. 640. The Project called upon the Columbia Libraries’ Digital Scholarship Coordinator for the Humanities, Alex Gil. Gil helped develop early strategies for representing the manuscript as digital text, bringing in Terry Catapano (then Special Collections Analyst, Libraries Digital Program Division) to consult on the textual encoding.

To begin, the Project needed to transform the handwritten script on paper of Ms. Fr. 640 from a physical historical object into a digital one. Housed at the BnF, the manuscript is a bound book of 170 folios, where one folio is the front (recto) and back (verso) of one half a folded physical piece of paper, resulting in 340 pages of written text.14 For example, the first folio includes both 1r (recto) and 1v (verso). For each of the 340 pages of Ms. Fr. 640, a separate Google Doc was created for the three separate versions of its text: diplomatic French transcription, lightly-normalized French, and English translation. The Google Drive folder housing these Docs was organized by page through folders and standardized naming conventions (Fig. 14 and Fig. 15).15 With three Docs per folder and 340 folders, the manuscript was represented by 1,020 text files. The organization system allowed the Project to manage the digital surrogates of the pages and easily introduce editors and participants to the files.

Google Drive

The editing of the manuscript text largely took place in pedagogical settings: a series of summer training Text Workshops. The editing workflow needed to facilitate two important activities: 1) collaborative work on the same document at the same time, and 2) the transcription, translation, and encoding of the manuscript to make productive progress towards establishment of the text.16 Rather than gain literacy in specific applications or tools that they may not use outside of the Workshop, participants were encouraged to spend as much time as possible engaged with Ms. Fr. 640 and build transferable skills in paleography and textual editing.17 Accordingly, Google Drive and its application suite (Google Docs, Google Sheets, Google Slides) were chosen for their low entry-barrier to simultaneous, internationally distributed digital collaboration. The advantage of Google Drive’s free, cloud-based editing platform with sophisticated commenting and collaboration features offered sufficient benefits to offset the disadvantage of proprietary technology. Crucially, Google Drive enabled collective editorial and scholarly work—a task traditionally undertaken by only one person—as participants worked simultaneously on the same text and saw edits in real time, also permitting all collaborators to write and view comments on any part of the shared documents. Participants left questions, citations, and notes about their decisions, which fed the iterative process and informed future avenues of research. Furthermore, prior to their work on the Project, many participants had become comfortable using similar applications, such as Microsoft Word, or were already familiar with Google Drive. To this day, the Project has yet to find any other viable alternatives to Google Drive that would so well meet the peculiar demands of its research process.

Through additional customization, the Project was able to adapt the Google Drive workspace to account for some of the friction resulting from using an environment that was not “XML aware” to encode text. Earlier Text Workshops (2014, 2015, 2016) focused more on paleography and translation than on encoding. Training in TEI markup was ad-hoc and minimized. Because the workshops involved 20 people for only 2–3 weeks a year, dedicated training in an XML editor such as oXygen was impractical, so simpler tools to assist in markup were needed. These tools had to work within the context of Google Drive to fully exploit its key feature at this time—the ability for participants to collaboratively work on and see the current state of actively evolving texts in real time. Therefore, the Project had to compensate for the lack of the advantages of “XML aware” features such as validation, which ensures that the markup is correct. Toward this end, Programming Assistant Philip Cherian created Google Docs Add-ons18 that provided lightweight validation of the markup. The tools addressed some key markup features prone to error such as start tags and end tags correctly matched to prevent overlap, and consistency in element names. This solution minimally affected the core workflow of the transcription and translation, but ensured that data would be clean for later stages of development.

The Project’s commitment to Google Drive might seem to lie in opposition to its commitment to minimal computing. Google Drive is a proprietary, feature-rich application with frequent updates, limited transparency and documentation, and focus on consumer usability and interactive experience. Using Google Drive as an editing platform was an unusual decision for an editing project that planned to encode the text with XML markup. Google Docs are “rich” text (as opposed to plain text) documents containing styling, formatting, and other information beyond the text itself. In order to produce the core data of the Edition, the encoded transcriptions and translations had to be exported from Google Drive, stripped of any rich text features introduced by the platform, and transformed into encoded plain text. Working in Google Drive introduced a number of unique challenges that each required customized solutions. Reliance on Google Drive thus caused the Project to accumulate “technical debt,” preferring to employ a solution to save time in the present and deferring the costs and time to implement a longer term solution. Manipulation of the text within and out of Google Drive was a significant area of work for the digital team, especially a number of creative programming assistants and interns.

While the debt incurred was certainly not minimal from a technical perspective, the Project did hew to the principles of minimal computing in another sense: minimizing and easing the overall human work of the editorial process. Textual editing, encoding, and manipulation—or even simply working within an XML-aware environment—requires training and a specialized knowledge base. The paleographic, lexical, and historical work requires its own domain-specific expertise, which the Project decided to prioritize over the technical skills, accepting “significant constraints” in technical training in order to facilitate the scholarly and editorial work of the Edition’s creation.

Tracking and Maintaining Iterative Work

At the onset of the editing process, an iterative workflow was foreseen, but not to the full extent realized by the Project over the course of almost six years. The idiosyncrasy of Ms. Fr. 640 and its collective editing would require several passes to produce its transcription, translation, and encoding, particularly if insights from laboratory and archival research were to be integrated. At least three Text Workshops were planned from 2014 through 2016, and each of those years saw continuous progress. However, the necessity of additional time for more advanced treatment of the manuscript became evident, so Workshops were planned for 2017, then again in 2018, and intensive sprints were peppered throughout 2018 and 2019. Milestones toward completion were pushed forward, as “final” became second, third, and fourth “drafts.” This was the result of methodology (rather than just the text) evolving through each cycle. As described by Dyon and Wacha: “Only through the act of transcribing and translating did the team of directors and paleographers begin to understand how best to accomplish the task at hand.”19

Tracking work became integral to assessing collective progress and the status of each version of the manuscript text. This required greater control of “administrative metadata”—information about the creation and management of digital objects (rather than about the data itself).20 For the Project, this included dates and times of when a file was last modified, the number and identity of the people who did so, and what application or program they used to make changes.

In 2014 and 2015, the Project relied on information contained in file and folder names for tracking purposes. Workshop participants added their initials to the folder name as they completed transcription, translation, and markup work (Fig. 14, Fig. 15, Fig. 16). This alerted the Lead Paleographer and Project Director that it was ready for their review, after which they would add plus signs (+) to let the participants know the files had been reviewed. As the encoding of the manuscript advanced and the Project’s data management strategies matured, it became increasingly clear that overloading the file names with administrative metadata had significant limitations. The Project needed to be able to assess and analyze that information more often and more accurately than what was possible when it was locked in file names.

A different strategy for keeping track of each folio’s status was implemented in preparation for the 2016 summer Workshop. In the spring of 2016, the Project was fortunate to be joined by two exceptional interns, Philip Cherian and Margot Lyautey, from École Polytechnique through a partnership with Columbia’s Alliance Program. With their assistance, progress tracking was moved to a spreadsheet, “BnF Ms. Fr. 640 Index Tracking” (Fig. 17). Going folio by folio, Margot and Philip assessed each version of the text (transcription, normalized, and translation) and recorded its status for both editing and encoding. It allowed the Project to determine the remaining transcription and translation work to be completed as well as whether any encoding had been applied to the folio and which version of the schema (the 2014 version or the updated one from 2015). The enhanced data informed a work plan for the 2016 workshop and a way for the participants to see their assignments and log their progress. The participants were broken up into groups and assigned sections of the manuscript (i.e., a range of folios) to be worked on sequentially. By the end of 2016, the transcription of the manuscript was largely complete, so the majority of the work to be done in the following year’s workshop was translation and encoding across all versions of the manuscript.

Beginning in 2017, the “BnF Ms. Fr. 640 Index Tracking” spreadsheet was transformed by Assistant Director Naomi Rosenkranz into a multipurpose management hub for progress tracking, record keeping, planning, version control, and metadata (Fig. 18). The Index Tracking spreadsheet now listed the manuscript by entry rather than by folio, a major conceptual shift.21 Up to this point, the Project organized the manuscript by folio and this dominated the way that work on the transcription (and, by extension, the translation and encoding) was managed. The organization of the manuscript files in the Project Google Drive inadvertently prioritized one conception of the manuscript, emphasizing the folio as a significant unit of text. Reference to folio arrangement is standard for bound manuscripts and mirrors the manuscript’s physical construction. This may not seem particularly significant. After all, this breakdown follows the conventions we associate with books. Text on the same page usually discusses the same topic and thus generally goes together, perhaps organized into paragraphs or separated by titles and headers. Books are cited by page number, as opposed to paragraph, sentence, line number, or some other unit. However, as discussed previously and elsewhere, Ms. Fr. 640 is not a traditional book. Its text is fragmented: a collection of recipes, instructions, observations, and experimental records. One folio can contain multiple entries, each treating distinct topics as far-ranging as medical remedies, varnish applications, and cannonball calibers, and the relation between these entries is not always clear. When categorized by folio, the text on each folio is considered as a unit, inadvertently obscuring the fact that it may contain topically unrelated textual units. The folio organization, therefore, can impede many avenues of investigation.22 While the Project generally discussed “entries” as the significant textual components—for example, it was entries (or “recipes”) that were reconstructed and analyzed in the Lab Seminar—all references to the manuscript were by folio.

As the markup of the manuscript progressed and its XML markup schema stabilized at the end of the 2016 Text Workshop, the Project benefited from the shift to an entry-centric view of the manuscript from a folio-centric view, resulting in methodological changes and productivity improvement. In preparation for the 2017 Text Workshop, the Index Tracking spreadsheet listed the manuscript not by folio but by entry, recording each entry’s unique “div” (i.e., textual division) ID and heading (in both the original transcription and translation for quick reference). Each entry was assigned a general theme such as casting or painting to enable workshop participants to work on topically related entries (rather than sequentially by folio) as well as develop expertise and a more consistent translation voice in entries treating similar subject matter. The participants were also divided into separate groups of translators and encoders.23 The translators worked ahead of the encoders, tracking and dating their work in the spreadsheet so that the encoders could follow closely behind them. Columns in the spreadsheet recorded which groups of translators and encoders had worked on each entry and when they had last made revisions. The groups also used the spreadsheet to enter notes, questions, or open issues for further review.

The more granular and detailed tracking provided the Project Director, Lead Paleographer, Assistant Director, and Digital Lead with a rich, coherent dataset to assess the new workflow’s efficacy and the Project’s overall progress. It was becoming ever clearer that the translation and encoding process could proceed indefinitely as the Project worked towards an improved, more scholarly and accurate product in the digital text of Ms. Fr. 640. As the two weeks of the 2017 Workshop were underway, the Project needed to begin thinking pragmatically about how much longer “completion” would take. Separate tabs were created in the “BnF Ms. Fr. 640 Index Tracking” Google Sheet to track completion totals (Fig. 19) and daily completion rates (Fig. 20). These let the team gain a sense of how many entries could be updated and reviewed, and the effects of factors such as number of participants, group size, and thematic category of the entries they were editing. Towards the end of the 2017 Workshop, the team used this data to approximate the length and capacity needed for future workshops. As a result of these projections, the Project held a week-long “mini-workshop” with a handful of experienced participants in March 2018 and then another full two-week workshop in the summer. In October 2018, the final workshop was held, a “micro” version with just Senior Translation Participants Heather Wacha and Soersha Dyon, encoders Celine Camps and Melissa Reynolds, and the core Project team. This brought an end to the larger “crowd-sourced” phase of transcription, translation, and encoding of the manuscript.

Move to Github

The Making and Knowing Project maintained the texts of Ms. Fr. 640 in Google Drive from 2014 to 2019. In February 2019, the Project moved all revision of the manuscript texts to a repository in the software development platform Github, a version control system for code management and issue tracking.24 Throughout the years of editing the manuscript text in Google Drive, the Project considered utilizing Github to track and maintain the folio files and an XML-aware environment (such as oXygen XML Editor) to edit the files. With so many changes and so many editors making those changes, however, Google Drive still seemed to serve as the best platform for the Project well into 2019. Making the change to Github was repeatedly postponed because it never seemed like the right time for the ongoing editorial activity. For the core editorial team, it was also difficult for them to envision how making the move would actually enable an editorial workflow that, once enacted, would greatly improve the process for producing an accurate and consistent digital text of Ms. Fr. 640. The workflows developed for Google Drive were easy to learn regardless of technical expertise and had become familiar to the editorial team. Because moving away from Google Drive would require training in unfamiliar tools and modes of work, there was fear of losing time that was better spent on engaging directly with the manuscript. On the other hand, it was hard to see how to reduce the growing technical debt accumulated by the many accommodations made in order to facilitate work in the Drive (which was largely invisible to those working in the Drive).

By February 2019, the focus of the editorial work shifted towards enhancing consistency, accuracy, and readability of the encoded texts: the Project’s “consistency campaign.” Dyon and Wacha describe the efforts of the later years of the Text Workshops to make translation decisions consistent throughout the manuscript to remove competing interpretations of similar words or subject matter. Similarly, Camps and Lyautey detail the work undertaken to ensure consistent application of the tagset not just throughout the translation but across the encoding of all three versions of the text (diplomatic, normalized, and translation).25 Until the move to Github, checking for compliance with these decisions was extremely difficult and time consuming: each manuscript page was housed in a separate Google Doc and all checks had to be completed by a human reading through all textual passages. Even a seemingly simple string-matching search (i.e., the kind of pattern matching function familiar to most as “find in page”: Ctrl+F or Command+F) could not be performed easily across the manuscript as a whole in Google Drive. The decisions and workflows previously employed to aid and enable the editing of the text were preventing other important editing and development tasks. As the digital development of the Edition intensified, continuously extracting the manuscript files from Google Drive and converting them to XML documents for use and rendering in a development/prototype website was also becoming impractical.

The costs of using Google Drive had started to greatly outweigh the benefits. At this stage in the editing of Ms. Fr. 640, it was clearly time for computational intervention. The Google Docs of each folio were exported and transformed to XML files one final time. The XML files were housed in a directory, ms-mxl/, of the Project’s m-k-manuscript-data Github repository, and became the active working files. The Google Drive was frozen by removing write permissions to most Project participants, and Github replaced Google Drive as the public and working environment of the manuscript pages.

The Project team as a whole had limited experience with Git and Github as well as with text editors such as Sublime Text, Atom, or the XML-aware oXygen.26 Under the guidance of the Digital Lead, the core editorial team (Director, Assistant Director, and the current three postdocs) developed a protocol for editing the text of Ms. Fr. 640 as true XML—at the same time as training in using Github, the command line, and oXygen (the kind of hands-on, learning-by-doing methodology typical of the Project). During an intensive, dedicated sprint, the team eased into this new workflow of editing and data management.

Github’s built-in project management and issue tracking features served as the new hub of activity and documentation. For each consistency task already identified by the previous editing campaigns (over a hundred), a new issue was opened and added to the “Consistency issues in ms text” Project (Fig. 21 and Fig. 22 and Fig. 23). As new consistency tasks were identified, additional issues were opened. The team organically developed a number of issue “labels” to help classify and manage the tasks. “Translation” and “markup” distinguished between the type of consistency issue: for example, issue #96 (“translation”) called for empreinte to be translated as “imprint,” while issue #105 called for the instances of smoke to be tagged as <material>. To help manage time and allocation of tasks example, issue #165 to confirm that all symbols, such as ʒ (dram), were not tagged as <mark>. The “problematic” label helped identify issues which were more complex because there were outstanding questions about how something should be translated or marked up, or further research was needed. For example, see issue #125 and the discussion about how to encode (and, ultimately, translate) languages other than French that appear in the manuscript; and issue #599 about how to treat bain (bath) in the context of metalworking.

After the move to Github in February 2019, the team worked on almost 500 issues labeled as “consistency.” The issue tracker served as a project management tool, but also as a place to collaborate, share information, cross-check, document, and record methods of work.27 A demonstration of this is exemplified in issue #92, a task to make consistent the spelling of “eau-de-vie” (distilled wine or spirits), which had been irregularly styled throughout the translation (e.g., “eau de vie,” “eau de vye,” “eau-de-vie”). The comments to this issue capture how the task was first undertaken in oXygen with a simple search, then, as the team became more familiar with the tools available, employing search patterns (“regular expressions” or regex) to find all instances. The issue records our early trials and errors as the team gained more skills in utilizing digital tools: discovering that the search did not capture all desired results; crafting an initial regular expression pattern that was also eventually found to be not fully effective; and finally calling in a more experienced user to help create another regex pattern (which then served as a model for other consistency tasks).

Issue #92 was one of the earliest issues the team opened and undertook as part of the consistency campaign. It shows the Project developing standards for how to record what interventions were made and what methods were employed. It was important to continue documenting the Project’s process for our own editorial workflow as well as for larger transparency of editorial interventions (and in the spirit of open scholarship). Issue #92 exemplifies the Project’s pragmatic approach to learning digital tools for editorial work. Training was practical and gradual, driven by the problems or needs as they arose and devising best ways to tackle or solve them. As discussed elsewhere, this learning-through-practice philosophy is characteristic of the Project’s iterative, research-driven pedagogy (and pedagogy-driven research), reflected throughout almost all aspects of the Project.

Github as the “Digital Lab”

In Spring 2017, the Project offered a new course modeled on its Lab Seminar, the “Digital Seminar” What Is a Book in the 21st Century? Working with Historical Texts in a Digital Environment. The goal of the Digital Seminar was to provide students with the intellectual, technical, and organizational skills to prepare text for digital presentation and analysis through the collective creation of a minimal digital edition of the Project’s preliminary English translation of Ms. Fr. 640 (Fig. 24 and Fig. 25).28 Minimal digital editions are an application of minimal computing: a textual edition of a manuscript (or other forms of text) that utilizes a small technology stack, emphasizing durability and reduced technical dependency over richness of interface features. In partnership with the Project’s collaborators at the Columbia University Libraries, including long-time consultant Alex Gil, the seminar’s minimal digital edition used “Ed,”29 a theme for the Jekyll static site generator.30 What Is a Book in the 21st Century? built upon Gil and Catapano’s experience working with Susanna Allés Torrent in 2016 on her Columbia course, Creating a Minimal Edition: From the Manuscript to the Web, which produced one of the first student-led collaborative digital editions using Ed, Mini lazarillo: edición mínima del Lazarillo de Tormes.31 The minimal edition developed during the What Is a Book in the 21st Century? course (the “GR8975 Edition”) in Spring 2017 informed the creation of a minimal edition in the summer 2017 Text Workshop that included all three versions of the manuscript text (the “Workshop Edition”) (Fig. 26).

In the Digital Seminar, the Project’s Github repository emerged as a “digital laboratory.” In the same way that the Project’s chemical laboratory provided a place for hands-on experimentation with the methods and materials of the past, the Project’s Github repository created a platform for knowledge-sharing, collaboration, and pedagogy (Fig. 27 and Fig. 28). Since 2014, the Project’s chemical lab, a laboratory space among the labs of Columbia’s Chemistry Department, served as a meeting place for introductions to the unfamiliar: hands-on experimentation, safety protocols, documentation of process through laboratory “field notes,” and collective investigation and understanding. Github was not unlike the chemical lab, a place for collaboration, communication, and experimentation enabled by safety management: “a virtual space providing tools for coordinated work and designed to contain damage related to failure so as to foster experimentation.”32

To understand the affordances provided by Git and the issue tracker, it is helpful to review the way files are managed, maintained, and revised in Github. Github maintains a remote “main” set of files (a “branch”) that serves as the definitive version of the repository (Fig. 29). Collaborators “pull” the repository to duplicate it on their local machine. To make changes without affecting the work of others, they can then check out a “branch,” creating a snapshot of the repository with which to work. Revisions, additions, and other contributions are made to the user’s branch and do not change the main branch. When complete, the user “commits” their work, enabling the work done in the local branch to be tracked and shared with other collaborators. The next step towards integrating these changes into the main branch is the opening of a “pull request” which enables review of the work and additional testing to ensure that no errors or conflicts are introduced. This is also the opportunity for a team to confirm that internal standards and protocols are followed in the new work before it is deployed. An approved pull request is then “merged” into the main branch, and the main branch reflects the updates undertaken in the local branch.33

The workflow and versioning capabilities provided by Git enabled the Making and Knowing team—who were largely inexperienced and unfamiliar with Github—to feel comfortable jumping into the new method of editing the manuscript in XML. Using branches and pull requests provided a degree of safety by minimizing damage to the main files. As described above, the Project developed a workflow for the consistency campaign of editing the manuscript texts, described in M&K’s Github & Digital Workflows documentation.34 Under the guidance of Catapano, the team created step-by-step tutorials and guides specific to the Project’s utilization of Github and oXygen, from the commands to open a new branch to regular expression searching tips. As issues were opened, a team member created a new branch to work on it in isolation. When ready, that member initiated a pull request for review by Catapano or Rosenkranz who, as repository administrators, merged or rejected the changes. With oXygen’s built-in well-formedness checks and validation against the schema, the integrity of the XML files was easily preserved.35 Eventually, the Project also employed the services of automated well-formedness checks, instilling even more confidence in merging pull requests.36

The workflow and various checkpoints for editing the manuscript texts acted much like the safety protocols prepared by Lab Seminar students in order to undertake experimental work in the lab. Clear steps and areas of potential risk were set out not just for the main user (or experimenter) but for the larger community. In the chemical lab, the safety protocols were always reviewed and overseen by Project team members with requisite knowledge (often in communication with Columbia’s Office of Environmental Health and Safety) to ensure the physical safety of people, the space, and materials at hand. Similarly, the digital protocol served to maximize the comfort and readiness of editors to work (the people), the integrity of the Github repository (the space), and the “safety” of the XML files (the materials). The lab safety protocols always served as open reference materials for future generations of lab students or team members who would undertake similar work. The editing workflow documentation similarly served as a continuous reference for subsequent editors. Far from inhibiting, these safety checks can be powerfully liberating for those who might not otherwise feel comfortable contributing or participating in collaborative digital work. One is more likely to give something a try if there is assurance that one will not actually “break” anything to the detriment of the group.37 The check points also provide feedback as one works (for example, running a well-formedness check in oXygen will highlight common mistakes, such as a missing closing tag) and proved to be effective tools for learning and gaining confidence with a new skill.

The Github repository issue tracker offers another parallel to the Project’s offline work: it is analogous to the Project’s laboratory Field Notes. Field notes are kept for all activity in the Project’s chemical laboratory (largely the skill building activities and historical reconstruction experiments of students in the Lab Seminar). They are modeled on both scientific lab notes (analytical, documentary, and reproducible) and anthropological field notes (reflective, narrative, and descriptive). Field Notes are key for documenting the preliminary research process, bringing together protocols, hands-on work, textual references, and object study. They encourage the experimenter to be as transparent and descriptive as possible about their process. Critically, they enable others to provide feedback, include their own work or recommendations, and take part in the discussion.38

The issue tracker is one of the fundamental features associated with project management in Github.39 Issues are online discussion threads and can be used to report bugs, identify tasks, and collect feature requests. Each issue can be added to a Project board (such as the Project’s “Consistency issues in ms text”), assigned to specific team members, labeled with topical tags, and linked to pull requests—all of which are filterable and searchable. For the Project’s consistency campaign, the issue tracker was fully integrated into the workflow—it is even the very first step of the protocol, as discussed above. Some of the Project’s issues are fairly straightforward, like issue #1524: “change figure id in tc, tcn, tl (fig_p130r --> fig_p130r_1)” which identifies an error in the ID of a manuscript figure and tasks the assigned Rosenkranz to correct it across all versions of the manuscript. As recorded in the issue, the error is fixed and Rosenkranz references her commit of this change as well as the pull request that integrates the change before commenting that it has been completed and closing the issue. Other issues are much more complex, involving discussion, additional research, and sometimes even the creation of related issues to parse the problem into more discrete tasks. For example, issue #1112: “Scope black / charcoal / coal / charbon, etc.” begins as a task to gather information about how the Project had treated the translation of words associated with “coal.” The comments between team members show the methods undertaken to scope the problem, summaries of the results of that research, ways in which the Project had or had not been consistent, suggestions for what should be done, and discussions about the “right” decisions to be made (referencing the Translation and Encoding Protocols, secondary literature about “coal,” and questions of grammar and sense). While the issue was complex, the issue tracker structured, focused, and recorded the work and discussion to resolve it. This kind of record of collaboration, practice, experience, and decision making serves a very similar purpose to the Laboratory Field Notes.

Design and Development of the Edition

While the content for Secrets of Craft and Nature was being prepared in Text Workshops, Lab Seminars, and Working Group Meetings, the development of the Edition’s website got underway in 2017 with the selection of Nick Laiacona as the Project’s Digital Designer-Developer. Laiacona, President and Founder of Performant Software Solutions, came to the Project with a strong background in developing and designing applications for projects in the digital humanities.40 Laiacona worked with the core Making and Knowing Project team to create the infrastructure, interface, and deployment method for the Edition website.

To develop the site, the Project presented Laiacona and his team with the challenge of constructing an engaging, multifaceted, and resource-rich online scholarly publication that utilized a minimal (i.e., common and well-established) technology stack comprising HTML/HTML5 with CSS and JavaScript served statically from a webserver. The commitment to such fundamental web technologies strives to reduce obsolescence and the need for active maintenance, as discussed above. Early brainstorming sessions quickly determined that the Edition would be developed as a static site.

Static Site Generation

Static sites are distinguished from dynamic sites which generate webpages each time a user accesses them according to specific settings or environment. For example, news sites change their content as time passes, updating headlines or stories featured on their homepage every day (and often every hour). Social media sites, as another example, adjust the content presented to a user based on their login information, customizing the user experience based on relevance (e.g., location, interests, or the activity of other connected users). Most websites familiar to anyone browsing the internet are dynamic sites, featuring interactive components such as responsive animation, commenting features, or customized searching.

The majority of dynamic sites are typically created using a content management system (CMS) in which the site's content is kept in a database and rendered into HTML when requested.41 This approach often requires the employment of proprietary software as well as a large number of resources, broader institutional support, or constant maintenance. The two key features of dynamic sites—database storage and the underlying server-side scripting language (such as Ruby or PHP)—are particularly difficult to maintain due to the advanced technological expertise/training they require, the complex dependencies they introduce, and their large digital footprint. There are a number of popular platforms, such as Wordpress or Drupal, which offer user-friendly templates and solutions for creating a dynamic website regardless of technical proficiency.42 These sites, however, are not immune to obsolescence or costly maintenance, evident most obviously in the challenges presented every time there is a software update or even a change in navigation or sitemap. Linked pages are no longer available or have moved, sometimes resulting in lost content if those pages or features are not preserved and migrated. Many services and any associated content are discontinued altogether, such as Yahoo GeoCities, Google Play, and Apple Aperture (not to mention social networking sites like Vine and Friendster).43

Static sites, unlike CMS or database-driven projects, deliver static webpages where all content and styling is created in advance. Content is served in its final form and sent as-is to a user—there is no dynamic creation or presentation of content through server-side scripts querying databases. To change or update what a user sees, the static file itself is revised or regenerated with new content as opposed to relying on software that dynamically and responsively changes the site based on new content.

Brian Rinaldi defines a static site as one which meets the following four requirements:

  1. Static site files are delivered to the end user exactly as they are on the server

  2. There is no server-side language

  3. There is no database

  4. Static sites are HTML, CSS, and JavaScript44

A very helpful illustrative analogy is offered by Eduardo Bouças, who compares visiting a website to visiting a newsstand for a newspaper.45 A static site generator would have compiled in advance all of the content into a newspaper provided to the newsstand. A visitor can then take a newspaper immediately and it will be the exact same newspaper obtained by the previous and subsequent visitors that day. On the other hand, a dynamic site creates the newspaper from scratch each and every time a visitor comes to the newsstand. The newsstand would alert a slew of phone operators to contact news agencies to get the content for the newspaper—representing the way that a server-side script queries multiple databases for site content. The information collected by the operators is then sent to a publisher—playing the role of a templating engine—who formats and presents the results as a newspaper (the HTML webpage). Dynamic sites are thus much more laborious on the backend, collecting information and presenting the content anew for each visitor. There are many moving parts and dependencies, and increased potential for something to go wrong, change, update, require attention, or simply break, creating a chain reaction of maintenance for all parts in the system.

Generating a static site offers a number of advantages. Because static sites use well-established, fundamental web technology, the techniques for serving static sites are likely to persist into the future as well as be universally understood by IT professionals tasked with keeping the assets online. Critically, static site generation allows websites to be created with significantly fewer dependencies, and reduces maintenance overhead, as well as minimizes the risk of technological obsolescence. Furthermore, open source web servers like Apache and Nginx46 can very efficiently serve large amounts of static data and respond to increased user traffic, leading to lower server costs.

The biggest challenge in developing a website using this approach is the need to adopt or build an appropriate static site generator for the project. There are a number of existing static site generators47 including the most well known, Hugo and Jekyll, as well as Github’s service, Github Pages, which allow a user to easily turn a Github repository into a static website featuring integration with Jekyll.48 The Making and Knowing Project had employed Jekyll and the Ed theme for two minimal digital editions (the GR8975 Edition of the preliminary English translation and the follow-on Workshop Edition of all three versions of the manuscript, described above). While Jekyll was initially considered for the Secrets of Craft and Nature Edition, the specific requirements and desired features of the Edition led to the decision to build a custom generator instead of adopting an existing system.49

Creating our own static site generator allowed the Project to accommodate simultaneously the ongoing editorial and digital development. Designer-Developer Laiacona created a static site generator using Node.js. The Project’s infrastructure was developed and maintained using Apache HTTP Server, React JavaScript Library, Github, AWS CloudFront and S3, and DigitalOcean virtual servers.50 The Project has developed a generalized, community version of the Edition infrastructure, EditionCrafter, that is openly accessible to any user interested in employing our static site generator in their own settings or for their own editions.

The Edition Prototype as Editorial Tool

The working prototype of the Edition became another tool in the editing process, providing an additional layer of review and an additional method to check consistency and accuracy. The Project had conceived of the Edition as comprising 1) the textual core—the text of Ms. Fr. 640, 2) the critical apparatus—the essays on the manuscript’s content, and 3) additional resources, not yet defined, that would emerge as part of the research process. The areas of focus for digital development followed this progression. Designer-Developer Laiacona thus began with development of the dual-pane display of the manuscript pages (Fig. 30). For each page of the manuscript, the user would be presented with two side-by-side viewing panes from which any combination of two of the four versions of the manuscript (the diplomatic transcription, the normalized transcription, the English translation, or the facsimile images of the folios) could be chosen. Because the text was at the core of the Edition, the functionality of these viewing panes consumed much of the attention of both the “editorial team” (Director Smith, Assistant Director Rosenkranz, and the postdoctoral scholars) and the “digital team” (Developer Laiacona, Digital Lead Catapano, and Rosenkranz). Furthermore, because the development of the content (i.e., the translation and encoding of the manuscript text) was still underway during the digital development of the content’s rendering, an important workflow emerged, affecting both the editorial and digital work: the ability to see real-time changes of the content in place.

Rendering of the Folio Pages

In order to render the transcription and translation as close as possible to the layout of the text in the original manuscript, it was necessary to see how the transcribed XML looked in the diplomatic rendering on the website. A “staging site” was set up using DigitalOcean and AWS Cloudfront which enabled almost real-time updates from the XML files in Github by automatically refreshing the folio views every few minutes. This allowed the Project team to commit changes to the XML files in Github and then see the effect of those changes “in situ” in the rendering of the manuscript texts in the Edition site.

The reason for such emphasis on the diplomatic rendering of Ms. Fr. 640’s folios comes from the manuscript’s composition itself and the Project’s commitment to echo this composition in the transcribed and translated texts as part of the Edition’s reading experience. Ms. Fr. 640 is not a “clean” text: almost every page contains some combination of authorial additions and deletions, marginalia, sketches, and indications of continuation. Through the process of transcription, translation, and encoding of the manuscript, the encoding schema had grown to accommodate these elements and the Project’s deep understanding of the relationship between them (such as how to relate marginal notes with the entries to which they belonged).51 The encoding of these elements as well as their visual presentation are critical for direct engagement with the primary source material—something that is often lost in printed as well as existing digital editions. How-to texts especially benefit from consideration of their composition—what can be learned or deduced about the author-practitioner from the words he crossed out or added? Why does an entry continue in the margin, fitted awkwardly around the text of the entry below it? Were these added notes appended to entries after a trial of the technique?

The rendering of the physical placement of each page’s elements began with an assessment of the manuscript as whole. The manuscript consists mostly of units of text under headings, which the Project has called “entries.” Each page of the manuscript typically contains a few entries (a notable exception is “Furbisher” which begins on fol. 94r and ends five pages later on fol. 96r). The headings of these entries are written in an italic script larger than the handwriting of the entry’s main body and are usually centered on the page (Fig. 31). Some entries have what the Project has characterized as marginalia, text that appears outside of the main body (approximately 90% appear in the left-hand margin) (Fig. 32). To translate these patterns of placement in the Edition, Laiacona developed the Grid Layout rendering, designed to faithfully render the layout of a single column of text, consisting of multiple entries, surrounded by marginalia (Fig. 33). The encoding of the text dictates how the Grid Layout should render it:

  • Headings: <head> is large, bolded, and centered

  • Entry (main body): text inside the <ab> (an “anonymous block” of text) is smaller and centered under the <head>

  • Marginalia: <margin> attribute of an <ab> with valid values, such as “left-middle” or “right-top”52

To produce renderings that were as close to the facsimile page as possible, the editorial team worked iteratively and in tandem with Laiacona. Adjustments were made to both the encoding of the manuscript texts and to the Grid Layout behavior. Still, there are limitations to perfectly rendering the manuscript’s irregular composition, especially for more complex pages such as fols. 3r (with marginal notes above, to the left, and to the right of entries), 66r (which has an entry with its own heading in the margin), and 124v (where the drawing that gave the Project its lizard logo is found, Fig. 34)—despite meticulous work spearheaded by indefatigable postdoc Tianna Uchacz. This work would have been incredibly difficult without the ability to see newly enacted changes in (almost) real time.

The representation of more granular textual features was another area of focus. These textual features include both authorial (i.e., made by the author-practitioner) and editorial (i.e., made by the Project) interventions which the Project felt were important to indicate visually to the reader. The rendering decisions aimed to make clear what type of intervention was being represented (authorial or editorial), to minimize overwhelming each page with too many different visual elements, and to strive to deliver a familiar scholarly reading experience that also meets W3C Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG).53

After much discussion and review of different options in the Edition prototype, the current rendering elements were finalized. Authorial features are exemplified in this excerpt from fol. 3v:

But on wood & on colors which have do not have gum or distemper glue

Where the author-practitioner struck through a word (“have”) to delete it, the text is rendered in red and struck through. Where he added words to the main body text, the text is rendered in blue, in a smaller font, and superscript. The editorial features—commentary on the manuscript text by the Project—needed to be represented in a manner that made clear that these resources were a layer apart from the manuscript text itself. In-line editorial comments which provide insight into the translation, meaning, or composition of the manuscript’s contents are accessed by clicking on red asterisks found on the folio page. Research essays that discuss the reconstruction of an entry or its place in the larger sixteenth century context are indicated by flask icons to the right of an entry’s heading. All textual features are fully summarized in a table in How to Use the Edition and Principles of Transcription, Translation, and Encoding (Fig. 35).

Prototyping for Consistency and Agenda-Setting

During the intensive consistency campaign, the editorial team worked to uniformly apply the encoding schema across and within all versions of the manuscript transcriptions and translation. These tasks were greatly eased by the ability to see the text in the prototype’s dual-pane display, enabling side-by-side comparison of versions. This setup was what Project members had been trying to emulate for years, with multiple computer windows, screens, and devices (Fig. 36). The team’s active editing also resulted in a feature request that has since remained a feature available to all users: the folio XML view (Fig. 37). For the Project, quickly pulling up the encoding of the text greatly aided the consistency work and saved incredible amounts of time previously spent toggling between applications and manuscript pages. For users of the Edition, this feature provides further transparency about the Project’s encoding as well as insight into the manuscript’s text.

The features and workflows discussed here focus on the Edition’s display of the manuscript folios. A similar method was utilized for rendering the Research Essays, working back and forth between digital affordances and particular editorial desires for a familiar scholarly essay (akin to a journal article) with multimedia elements.

Setting Intellectual Agendas as Minimal Viable Products

As digital development of the Edition site was underway by Performant, the Making and Knowing Project was faced with making decisions about the site’s interface and features as a whole. Since the team was still generating and preparing content for the Edition, there was a sense that the Project had a very clear vision for what the Edition was. The translation and encoding protocols—now in states reflecting over five years of development—were being applied to the manuscript text. All essays produced during eight semesters of the Lab Seminar were being intensively edited and styled according to a model that had evolved over just as many years. This kind of “consistency” work gave the team a sense of “finalizing” this content and, by extension, the Edition itself.

While this was a critical part of the development of the Edition, it did not, however, address larger questions about what the Edition should do and how it should do it. The time had come for the team to again zoom out from the details and re-contemplate the intellectual agenda of the Edition as a scholarly and digital object. What information should the Edition provide? What questions should it answer? How could it be used for textual criticism, analysis, and interpretation of the manuscript? What audiences should the Edition serve and what should users be able to take away? The answers to these questions, which had been considered and refined successively throughout the Project’s years of research, now required translation into features and interfaces. Scholarly ambitions also had to be weighed against the technical applications available and the defined timeline. User experiences and desired features had to be identified and prioritized in order to set milestones and clear deliverables.54

This process shows the “dark side” of collaborative and open-ended work, where the affordances made possible by digital publication also become potential pitfalls. Undertaking a project requires resources, personnel, and time, but completing a project also requires a definition of “complete.”55 The Project had committed to funders and collaborators to publish the Edition by late 2019 which was pushed to February 2020 in order to avoid launching during the holiday season as well as to coincide with New York City Digital Humanities Week.56 Within this timeline, what needed to be completed? One way to answer this question was to move away from traditional modes of scholarship in the humanities and towards cultures of group work found in the technology industry: defining the minimum viable product (MVP), an approach to project management from agile software development and Lean Startup.57 While an MVP is typically defined as “the smallest amount of functionality [a project] can deliver,” it is also useful as a tool for the team to elicit feedback both internally and externally in order to focus work and adjust the development plan accordingly.

Defining a “good enough” version of the Edition for its first release (the MVP) was a difficult task for the Project. In order to move forward, however, it was imperative that the features and functionality of the MVP be identified so that the Project’s collective workforce could be focused and strategically directed. While the team desperately wanted to include as many Research Essays as possible and also continue to refine the content and markup of the manuscript texts, there were fundamental elements that still needed to be defined: navigation, landing pages, the homepage, and search functionality.

The process of determining how and what to prioritize was “a negotiation between thoroughness and expediency, idealism and pragmatism, [with a] generated collective buy-in to a vision.”58 In the summer of 2019, the team established MVP conditions for editing of the manuscript texts and the Research Essays to help curb the scholarly instinct to continue working on these tasks that could (and would) proceed indefinitely. For the manuscript texts, the MVP versions would reflect the state of the XML after any consistency campaign tasks (determined and tracked as Github issues) that were completed by September, followed by two full copyediting read-throughs of the versions and a thorough check of the encoding across the versions to ensure they all contained comparable number and type of tags. For the Research Essays, the team agreed to prepare a select number of essays, choosing essays composed in the most recent offerings of the Lab Seminar (Fall 2016 through Fall 2018) which more closely aligned with the developed editorial style, as well as a few essays composed by Project team members or invited scholars that were crucial for the first release (such as Smith’s “Introduction to the Edition”) or that were in a more completed state. Setting these “restrictions” (as they were perceived by much of the editorial team) was critical for allowing the team to work within the timeline and with the available resources to prepare all elements for the first MVP release.

The MVP plan also needed to include definitions for the navigation and content of the Edition’s webpages. What information would a user need and how would they access this information? Brainstorming sessions resulted in a number of ideas and wireframes which the team used to come to a consensus about what resources the Edition would provide and how they should be organized and presented to the user (Fig. 38 and Fig. 39 and Fig. 40). Anticipating that the editorial team would continue to revise the content of the site’s webpages and their organization, the Project developed a flexible/agile approach that would enable collective work on these resources. The Github repository cu-mkp/edition-webpages was created to house and maintain the pages in Markdown so that they could be edited by any Project team member, as could the JSON file that dictated the site’s menu.59 Whenever the Edition site was regenerated, the Markdown files were converted to HTML and the JSON file provided the layout for the main menu and defined the path that each Markdown file should take.

Collaborative and Open Ended Work: The Dark Side

Working together to produce a digital object is not something that humanists often experience or are rewarded for. Typically, an article is written, submitted, and published; a CV grows by one citation, and it is done. The work is solitary and final. However, our world abounds with products, particularly digital products and ventures which are, for reasons of complexity or scale, the results necessarily of the work by groups of people. These products, additionally, have open-ended lifecycles, requiring ongoing updates, maintenance, and support. A challenge for the development of the digital critical edition of Ms. Fr. 640 was to bring the participants out of the domain where solitary and final work dominates, to one in which involves collaborative and open-ended work (Fig. 41).60

The “cultures of work” characteristic of each of these domains are not inherently superior or inferior to one another but have emerged in response to the differing circumstances, demands, and rewards. Each, however, has their own virtues and vices. While the collaborative and open-ended culture of work was more appropriate and offered more advantages conducive to the development of a digital critical edition, it has its own pathologies that tend toward failure and must be recognized and avoided.61

The MVP and Subsequent Releases

The first public release of the Edition was launched on February 6, 2020 using the tagged releases of the Project’s repositories: m-k-manuscript-data v1.0.4, edition-webpages v2.2.0, and making-knowing-edition v1.0.0. As of June 16, 2025, there have been twenty three subsequent tagged releases of m-k-manuscript-data (and an additional 756 commits), twenty releases of edition-webpages (366 commits), and thirteen releases of making-knowing-edition (236 commits). Each of these releases is tied to a site build with new content and/or new features, where the website was regenerated and packaged as a static site. From the frontend (i.e., for a user accessing the site), there is no interruption or even indication that a new build has occurred; users need only refresh the page in their browser to see the new content. The majority of these releases are tied to new content, specifically the publication of additional research essays, which is where the bulk of editorial work was required after the initial February 2020 release.

For the next four months, the editorial team created a two-week release schedule as a way to structure work on the essays, using another Github project to manage the tasks (Fig. 42). For every essay under editorial review, a separate Github issue was opened with the essay’s unique identification number and short title. They were then added to the project board with custom editing-specific columns, where a number of Github features were employed. The essays were grouped into bundles, indicated by a specific issue label (“bundle 1,” “bundle 2,” etc.), to signal their review order and release to the Edition. The primary editor was “assigned” to the issue, making clear what was actively being worked on and who was responsible. The editors moved the issue through the project board columns as work was completed, such as content review and stylesheet adjustments. When ready, the primary editor handed the essay off to a formatter (another team member) to complete formatting, accessibility, and media checks. These were also tracked with issue labels; the associated task was completed when the label was added. For example, “fol links” marked that references to Ms. Fr. 640 folios were linked properly in the text; “figures” indicated that the essay’s media was not only formatted correctly but also indexed in the Project’s master media list. As the essay moved further along in the board columns, another editor was added for secondary review and copy editing. After two editors completed a pass through the essays, a release to the staging site was built for all essays in the “Ready for staging” column. As with the folio pages, each essay underwent multiple “render checks” by the team in situ in the internal staging site, also tracked by board columns. Finally, when the essay reached the “Done” column, it was released to the Edition on the public-facing site. This cycle repeated every two weeks, publishing each batch of new essays. Essays that required further review were held back from publication until editorial revisions were completed.

Feedback and Site Redesign

As discussed above, significant time and resources went towards the rendering of the folio pages and the Research Essays. Consequently, with less time available for the design and features of the homepage, section landing pages, and other pages framing the edition content, the Project accepted the limitations of a simple markdown-based HTML authoring in order to take advantage of its efficiency. Editing this content as markdown files in cu-mkp/edition-webpages and subsequently converting the files to HTML for presentation in the Edition allowed for some formatting and design elements, such as the use of headings, emphasized text (bold, italics, underlined), and embedded images and videos. Basic styling was agreed upon by the editorial team (such as font, spacing, and sizing) and implemented through a CSS stylesheet (Fig. 43).

While this approach was effective, it left room for more “dynamic” design features. In the fall of 2020, Designer-Developer Laiacona brought in one of Performant’s in-house graphic designers, Christyan Cadena, to take in feedback from the editorial team and present a few design options for the landing pages. The homepage of the Edition was redesigned to show facsimile images of Ms. Fr. 640 more prominently, to encourage users to visit resources intended to help them get started, and to feature pages that provide background about the manuscript, the Edition, and the Project (Fig. 44). A new logo for the Edition was also workshopped (Fig. 45) and eventually chosen (Fig. 46 and Fig. 47). The other landing pages were changed so that the content of all subsections of a menu item (e.g., “The Manuscript” and “The Edition” under the menu item “About”) are shown in one page as opposed to requiring the user to click away or into the sections as a new page (Fig. 48). These design decisions were motivated by the desire to create a website that is welcoming and inviting to visiting users. The goal is to have clear points of entry for engagement with the material and to present the (sometimes overwhelming amount of) information contained in the Edition in a digestible way that clarifies instead of confuses.

User testing of the Edition throughout its digital development was conducted in a number of settings. Ongoing review by the editorial team, especially during the period when the internal staging site was used as part of the editorial process (as described above), identified bugs and errors as well as generated new feature requests. Targeted user testing took place in the Project’s Working Group Meetings where collaborators could offer feedback about the Edition. In numerous presentations and workshops, Project team members have showcased the Edition to new audiences to encourage its use and elicit feedback for continued improvement. Regular meetings of the digital team reviewed the feedback and implemented changes. One of the most effective forms of evaluation has been through the active use of the Edition by collaborators in their research and by students in courses at Columbia and other institutions.

Beyond the Edition: the Sandbox, Fieldnotes, the Research and Teaching Companion, and EditionCrafter

One of the guiding principles for the digital development of the Edition was to trade feature richness and functionality in favor of greater longevity. Complex web applications are inherently more vulnerable to both security and technical risks. Additionally, as more features and functionality are introduced, the costs required to maintain these elements increase. However, not all scholarly and pedagogical work is adequately served by a minimal feature set and tech stack. To address this dilemma, the Project adopted a “barbell” strategy (often used in investment and risk management)62 for managing and publishing our digital work. Our use of this strategy posits an inverse relationship between functionality and persistence (Fig. 49 and Fig. 50). On one end of the barbell are projects such as the Edition where sustainability is prioritized over the feature set. On the other end are projects where rich feature sets are gained at the price of their sustainability. Ability to engage in projects on this end of the barbell—where the cost to do so is high—is increased if the commitment to maintaining them is minimized or completely eliminated: if something breaks, it breaks. It is understood that projects on this end of the barbell are potentially ephemeral and costs are saved because no work or time is expected to be spent to fix it; maintenance is minimal, and failure is tolerated. This strategy deliberately avoids projects with moderate functionality and sustainability, as these tend to be less successful overall.

For the Project, the Edition sits on the “sustainability” end of the barbell (Fig. 50) and a separate publication, the Making and Knowing Project Sandbox, was developed to sit at the other end of “functionality.” Publicly launched in January 2021, about a year after the public launch of the Edition, the Sandbox is an environment and area of activity for exploration and experimentation, allowing the Project to investigate new avenues of analysis, rapidly prototype, and share preliminary research or methods of work. Innovative and exploratory research would be best served by reducing the commitment to preservation of the results in favor of maximizing freedom to experiment with and implement diverse tools, technologies, and methods. Many of the elements now made public through the Sandbox had been made, used, and referenced by the Project throughout the making of the Edition. These include mockups and wireframes, visualizations and analysis of Ms. Fr. 640’s text, and syllabi and lesson plans from the Project’s courses In addition to the Sandbox, the Project published the Research and Teaching Companion to the Secrets of Craft and Nature in 2024. It makes available resources created in the process of studying Ms. Fr. 640 and creating the Edition for teachers, researchers, and makers who wish to integrate hands-on and digital components into their classes, research projects, and art practice.

Conclusion

We are finishing this essay in March 2025, more than five years after the initial launch of the Edition in 2020. As a form of conclusion, we thought it might be useful to articulate some of our thoughts on the overall development process since that initial launch.

Our process was necessarily iterative and collaborative—continuously adapting to the circumstances as they changed, weighing tradeoffs in moving the project forward, particularly before and after each intensive work sprint. For example, not requiring specialized technical expertise and choosing familiar and general applications such as Google Docs, lowered the bar for participation and expanded opportunities for collaboration. This was an intentional deferment of costs such as technical debts due to workarounds and workflows. There were, of course, also unintended outcomes. Despite our efforts to minimize future maintenance demands, they could not be fully eliminated. There was also still a temptation and a need to make changes and fixes to both the content (twenty three releases since the initial launch!) and to unanticipated technical issues. There was no textbook to be followed in undertaking a project such as this, nor did a textbook emerge from it (although this very lengthy essay is a contender for such a title!). What did emerge was a recognition that opportunities and demands will present themselves continually, requiring constant careful consideration of their costs and benefits. It would have been naive, and futile, to determine upfront a set of TEI elements to use, develop specs, find developers, deploy a web application to display the edition, then expect to hand it off to someone to host somewhere in perpetuity as a web application, and move on. For the reasons discussed, this would never have been possible! Ultimately, the Making and Knowing Project's approach demonstrates the potential of digital tools and methods to transform scholarly practice. By embracing collaboration, iteration, and flexibility, the Project successfully created a digital edition that reflects the multifaceted nature of Ms. Fr. 640 and facilitates ongoing research and engagement with this unique historical text.
