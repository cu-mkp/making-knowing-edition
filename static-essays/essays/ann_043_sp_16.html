Abstract

A set of recipes on fols. 33r–43v in Ms. Fr. 640 is dedicated to sleight of hand. These tricks are part of a larger canon of “secrets” performed at public places, such as markets and taverns, and transmitted over several centuries and across the globe. Traditionally, conjuring tricks were judged to be deceptive and therefore illicit. Prominent sixteenth-century scholars like Reginald Scot and Agrippa von Nettesheim were determined to debunk such performances as fraudulent. Meanwhile, these feats of practical magic also conveyed early forms of specialist knowledge in mathematics (riddles), physics and astronomy (optical effects), chemistry (transmutation), and theatrical display (mechanical props). This essay examines the social life of sleight of hand in its widespread cultural transmission. This study also discovers striking similarities between entries in Ms. Fr. 640 and Jean Prevost’s Clever and Pleasant Inventions (Lyon, 1584), especially on fols. 33r, 33v, 34v, 35r, and 35v.

Historical Background: Conjuring and Experimentation in the Sixteenth Century

Why would anyone want to reveal a conjuring or a sleight of hand trick? By doing so, magicians, tricksters, conmen, and anyone whose profession is built on the skillful deception of others would betray fellow practitioners by revealing what is really happening. Yet, a plethora of texts on this topic have existed through the ages.

The inherent allure of conjuring tricks lies in their persistent ability to surprise and fool us. The conjuror masters an understanding about how we normally perceive the causality of movements and events, in order to perform an act that counters these expectations and builds on an underlying but unperceived sequence of gestures. When analyzing a series of conjuring performances, the nineteenth-century psychologist Alfred Binet, inventor of the first intelligence test, realized that “through generations of cumulative experience, magicians had developed an elaborate working understanding of the mainsprings of perception along with a corresponding repertoire of sleight of hand techniques calibrated to induce perceptual error.”1 The perceptual error raised here relies on the observer’s neglect of unconsidered causal alternatives while his or her attention is led astray by the conjuror’s skillful (mis)direction. The creative force behind the invention of conjuring tricks through the discovery and embodiment of perceptual errors has never failed to provoke both rapt attention and wariness in audiences.

Conjuring may be one of the three oldest professions in the world (with the least obvious of these three being perfumery, perhaps).2 Systematically-written records of magic and sleight of hand tricks appear to emerge no earlier than the sixteenth century, when there is an explosion of reports of conjuring feats and “how to” manuals for diverse forms of trickery and descriptions of pranks, illusions, games, and riddles.3 Not all of these “revelations” can be viewed as having the same purpose. While some of these manuals on the art of conjuring present the reader with instructions about how to learn certain tricks for entertainment, others have a more serious agenda, which grew out of concern about the occult and ambivalence towards new empirical approaches to nature.4 Magic and science (or, natural philosophy) were not seen as opposed, but rather formed two ends of a spectrum of keen interest in the workings of spirit and matter.5 What counted as legitimate experimentation with the things and materials of nature was up for debate and existed in tandem with what was vehemently objected to as unprofessional charlatanry or heretical activity.6 Sleight of hand tricks are one arena where this contestation of authority plays out.

In a Janus-faced opposition over conjuring throughout the sixteenth century, two kinds of agenda can be discerned. On the one hand, Reginald Scot’s 1584 The Discoverie of Witchcraft presents a seminal treatise against superstition and the contemporary prosecution of witches, and differs from such predecessors as Agrippa von Nettesheim’s De Occulta Philosophia (1533). While von Nettesheim’s work is largely an anecdotal collection of magical hearsay and reports of supernatural abilities, Scot’s Discoverie of Witchcraft presents the systematic exposition of such abilities as fraudulent. Scot was influenced by physician Johann Weyer’s 1506 De praestigiis daemonum. Like his contemporaries, Weyer believed that supernatural forces were at work in the human world, but he differentiated between the delusionary and demonic influences to which he considered practitioners of magic to be subject. Weyer also attempted to distinguish methodically between the licit arts of natural magic and the illicit diabolical arts of revelation. Moverover, instead of merely recounting the many reports of occult events circulating at his time, Weyer further exposed cases of apparent magic or supernatural abilities as fraud. Scot followed Weyer’s example by debunking some magical appearances as staged tricks.7

In the same year, another related text appeared which represented the most extensive manual for sleight of hand tricks yet published, La Premiëre Partie des Subtiles et Plaisantes Inventions by Jean Prévost (Lyon, 1584).8 Although less well known than Scot’s Discoverie, it is of seminal importance to the history of magic, containing eighty-five diverse conjuring tricks described in surprising detail. Scholars consider Prévost’s treatise the earliest known work concerned entirely with conjuring secrets.9 The unusual title speaks not only of “clever [subtiles]” but also of “pleasant [plaisantes]” inventions. Such a title signifies an entirely different attitude to the art of conjuring: creating playful pleasure, not maleficent deceit. Referring to conjuring tricks as “inventions” also marks their inherently creative nature as a form of entertainment and wonder, rather than as a source of crude or demonic distortion.

We are confronted with two strikingly different attitudes to the art of conjuring here, a duality which permeates Scot’s and Prévost’s presentations of conjuring tricks. Both represent conjuring, on the one hand, as a deceit, an almost arrogant claim of possessing powers outside a specific natural and legitimate world order, and, on the other hand, as a skillful practice of creative invention, recreation, and pleasure. They both expose “jugglers,” or bateleurs, as they were commonly called,10 as tricksters who deceive the common man.11 While their exposure of conjuring tricks as deceitful practices is the proclaimed intention of their works, both Scot and Prévost convey a certain admiration for the creativity and entertainment value of these trickeries.12

Alongside these two seminal treatises on conjuring and sleight of hand, a number of books of secrets and other “how-to” texts were written by the end of the sixteenth century.13 Ms. Fr. 640 is one of them. This particular manuscript is a compendium of “recipes” for, and observations on, a wide variety of artistic and practical techniques. As an apparently unsystematic compilation of artisanal practices, its inclusion of tricks, pranks, and illusions initially appears puzzling to the modern reader.14 What role do these sections on trickery play in this manuscript? What attitude towards conjuring do they convey, and how do these accounts of seemingly deceptive practices relate to the rest of the manuscript? Finally, what can reconstruction tell us about the social life of these tricks?

Sleight of Hand and Trickery in Ms. Fr. 640

Ms. Fr. 640 contains a range of recipes for pranks and sleight of hand tricks, which are unusual when compared with the rest of the manuscript. These particular recipes are not scattered throughout the manuscript (as, for instance, are recipes for oils and varnishes); rather, they appear as a fairly discrete section in the first third of the codex. Furthermore, they contain hardly any annotations or revisions (compared to other such recipes in the manuscript as, for example, on fol. 64v). Thus, the organization and presentation of the tricks in Ms. Fr. 640 indicate that these recipes were collected from a single source and perhaps compiled at the same time.

The following summary contextualizes these tricks through an analysis of their character and potential sources of origin and distribution. These categories are not intended to be exhaustive but to illustrate the general character of the trickster recipes for a comparative analysis (Table 1). At first glance, the recipes can be subsumed under the following categories of effects:15 production of an effect, disappearance, transformation, transposition, mental and physical riddles, and pranks.16 Recipes in italics also appear in Scot’s Discoverie of Witchcraft and Prévost’s Clever and Pleasant Inventions.

The categories in Table 1 indicate different options and venues for the tricks. While all constitute close-up performances, some recipes are more suited for spontaneous social gatherings in places such as taverns and inns (e.g., riddles and pranks), whereas others seem more appropriate for places that can accommodate prepared or staged entertainment, such as marketplaces and theaters (e.g., translocations and transformations).17 This becomes apparent when we consider the different requirements of skill, materials and props used in the tricks. Some need little or no preparation nor instruments; they are at most relying on quotidian objects (e.g., candles, bedpost). Other recipes build on different levels of skill and training (e.g., timing and disclosure of gestures in sleight of hand maneuvers, sewing, relighting the candle with a small twig), and the preparation of specific materials and instruments (e.g., tinted water, eggs filled with sulfur and quicklime, box with a double bottom).

Indeed, the author-practitioner himself refers to different venues for these tricks when he mentions an audience in some of his instructions and descriptions. For example, on fol. 34r he suggests making “a servant hold it [a candlestick] to the wall,” which evokes a simple social context such as a tavern as the intended venue. Another recipe describes making someone hug a bedpost (fol. 34r), which could relate to potentially shared accommodations during travels at inns or brothels. In contrast to these kinds of impromptu performances, and in one of the few marginal notes in these pages of tricks, the author-practitioner explicitly situates his trick on fol. 33r as part of a planned, perhaps staged, performance: “The conjuror ought not to amuse himself by watching what he is doing, but look at the spectators and give them plenty of good hocus-pocus words so that they look at his face & not at his hands.”

The importance of the potential venue and audience for understanding these trickster recipes is rendered visible in the case of fol. 43v, a recipe for “transmuted wine.” Transmutation refers to the idea that substances can change in their material state. Experimental inquiry into the principles of the transformations underlying such material changes was the occupation of artisans and alchemists alike. That said, it is not entirely obvious whether fol. 43v should be read in the tradition of alchemical transmutation or as part of a trickster’s repertoire:

Grate brazilwood very finely, put it to soak one or two hours in clear water, then take this tinted water & add to it some clear water & you will make wine as claret colored as you like. If you please, put a drop of lemon or orange juice in it & it will immediately turn white. It can be drunk without danger.

Here, the description of transmuted wine as “tinted water” might read almost like a mockery of wine to some modern readers, but water could have a range of meanings from everyday water to a quintessence, which might be metaphorical or a material substance produced by distillation, and used in the creation of medicinal and cosmetic products. Alcohol in particular is referred to as “burning water” in this context, for example, by John of Rupescissa in the fourteenth century.18 The reconstruction of this recipe sheds light on the meaning of “water” here.19 Perhaps fol. 43v is a recipe to probe the nature of substances by the artificial recreation of some of their properties (e.g., color), or it may be a recipe for the generation of a property to simulate but not recreate a substance.

My reconstruction of this recipe opened up several issues about its ambiguous character: the absence of any quantitative description or any guidance regarding the proportion of Brazilwood and water to be used is not unusual in the context of the manuscript, but the recipe also lacks any qualitative descriptions by which to judge the coloring of the mixture. Fol. 43v does not contain a single color term for the tinted water, only the descriptor “white” for its “de-wined” version. Likewise, the qualitative description for the preparation of Brazilwood (grate very fine) allowed for variation as well as deviation from the manuscript’s instructions.

To explore the range of coloration possible with the recipe on fol. 43v, I decided on three variations:

  1. V1: powdered brazilwood (ground in a stone mortar)

  2. V2: grated brazilwood in a “tea bag” made of cheesecloth

  3. V3: grated brazilwood without a bag, in the manner of brewing loose leaf tea

All three “wines” were prepared with 0.04g Brazilwood in 50ml of tap water and soaked for an hour. They differed fundamentally in the way they took on the color of the brazilwood. V1 (powder) colored immediately, and, the water turned a deep and saturated red after the mixture was shaken. With V2 (tea bag), coloration was barely noticeable at the beginning and remained the weakest of all after an hour, the water having merely adopted a slight rose tint. The last trial, V3 (“loose leaf”), was the most true to the manuscript. Here coloration of the water also started immediately, albeit more slowly and with less intensity than in V1. Nonetheless, given that they differed only in the use of the “tea bag” and not in the preparation of the material, the difference in coloration and temporal change between V2 and V3 was striking (Fig. 1).

Since none of the liquids looked like they could pass for wine after one hour’s soaking, I soaked a second batch for three-and-a-half days (Fig. 2). The samples differed in both taste and smell. V1 had a markedly bitter taste and a medium earthy smell; V2 and V3 had a more earthy taste, but while V2 had a weak earthy smell V3 was almost odorless. The bitter taste was surprising as Brazilwood is said to have a sweet taste in the literature.20

For the second step in the recipe, the “de-wining” procedure, I took three samples of the extensively soaked variations to pour freshly squeezed lemon juice into each sample (Vid. 1). As V3 was already of a yellow instead of a reddish color, no real change was visible. Meanwhile, a small drop into V1 and V2 presented an immediate change of color from red to a deep yellow. It could have passed as a white wine, though, possibly a Grüner Veltliner. It was not, however, as the manuscript predicted, of a white, water-like appearance.21

Is the recipe for transmuted wine a form of trickery or artful imitation? As an experiment to recreate an imitation, my reconstruction resulted primarily in a proof of concept. In terms of its use at the time, the recipe appears to have served as entertainment. It could have been used either as a prank, that is, if one were trying to fool someone into drinking it by having them expect wine, or as a prop in a stage play or theatrical setting. Indeed, with regard to such potential venues, the author-practitioner was careful to point out that “you can drink it without danger.” Overall, as part of the manuscript, fol. 43v conveys an interest in creating material transformations within performative contexts.

The trickster recipes themselves are not unique to Ms. Fr. 640. Indeed, they are part of a larger canon of sleight of hand tricks at this time. The most compelling evidence for this can be found on fol. 33r (knife cutting nose) and fol. 35v (traveling grains) (Table 2).

These other recipes provide even more compelling evidence for the overlap of tricks between Ms. Fr. 640 and Prévost’s Pleasant and Clever Inventions (1584), namely in the cases of fol. 33v (blood on forehead), fol. 34v (jewel in a box), and fol. 35r (water bucket on knives). It appears that the author-practitioner likely copied some of his recipes directly from Prévost: both writers were likely based in Toulouse.22 The eloquent and witty style of Clever and Pleasant Inventions and the preface’s mention of an association between Prévost and poets indicates that he may have had poetic aspirations, or at least, was an educated man in the occasional company of poets.23 Further research may be able to determine whether Prévost and the author-practitioner were part of the same social circle in the College de l’Art et Science de Rhétorique, a literary society based in Toulouse. On this point, certain textual clues in Ms. Fr. 640 suggest that the author-practitioner may have been somehow connected to the College de Rhétorique.24 However, this must remain speculative, as the name of the author-practitioner is unknown, and, to date, no records for a “J. Prévost” have been found in the College.

Even so, there are strong textual reasons for identifying Prévost as the likely source for the trickster recipes in Ms. Fr. 640. Similarities in the presentation of the shared tricks are striking, especially in the accompanying illustrations. We see a very strong resemblance between the illustrations in Prévost’s Clever and Pleasant Inventions and in Ms. Fr. 640. In addition to the illustrations of trick knives (see Figs. 3, 4, and 5 above), compare, for instance, the diagrams of trick funnels to make blood appear on someone’s forehead (Fig. 9 and Fig. 10), or those illustrating a trick jewel box (Fig. 11 and Fig. 12) or the arrangement of knives capable of balancing a bucket (Fig. 13 and Fig. 14). These images contain the same interpretive and descriptive components, such as letters to label parts of the props relevant to the description of the ploy. This is an important difference to Scot’s illustrations in his Discoverie of Witchcraft, which rules out this work as a common source for Prévost and the conjuring tricks in Ms. Fr. 640.

Despite these striking visual similarities, there are also clear differences. In the case of the jewel box, the author-practitioner adds illustrations of separate parts not included in Prévost (see letters B and C in Fig. 11), and also substitutes a ring for the coin used in Prévost’s instructions. In the entry on making blood appear on someone’s forehead, the author-practitioner adds the suggestion to work the same trick on a wall.25 Furthermore, Ms. Fr. 640 contains sections that do not appear in Prévost or that diverge markedly in both content and description. For example, the author-practitioner mentions a “conjurer’s pouch” attributed to one “Maistre Gonin,” a legendary magician who appears throughout sixteenth- and seventeenth-century literature on magic and demonology.26 For another trick borrowed from Prévost, “For relighting an extinguished candle between your hands without blowing” (fol. 33r), the author-practitioner gives a more succinct description than the original, but provides an additional drawing showing the performer’s hands at work.27 The trick detailed on fol. 35r, “For boiling an egg in cold water without fire,” appears in Prévost as “To Cook an Egg Without Fire” (p. 83). However, despite using the same ingredients (eggs, quicklime and water), these two recipes differ fundamentally in their method (Table 3). This divergence seems to indicate that some tricks were widely disseminated or part of trickster hearsay, but their interpretation and technique, and their knowledge thereof, varied. Given the apparent implausibility of some methods, together with Prévost’s skepticism about some of them (Table 3), it is possible that the author-practitioner simply copied certain recipes without fully understanding them or putting them to the test. Where Prévost’s recipe “to cook an egg without fire” would create the desired effect by the exothermic reaction between quicklime and water, only further reconstruction can determine the efficacy of the author-practitioner’s method of “boiling an egg in cold water without fire” (fol. 35r) by emptying an egg of its white and yolk, then filling it with quicklime and sulfur.

Most sleight of hand tricks that circulated in the sixteenth century represent a repertoire of widely-used ploys and their variants. Tricks were recorded and disseminated in household manuscripts and books of secrets as well as in contemporary engravings and paintings.

The Social Life of Trickery and the Transmission of Secret Knowledge

The record of trickster recipes is striking for its sheer diversity: in every collection, one can find an array of different tricks spanning subtle number or mental games and riddles,28 crude pranks and latrine humor,29 material transformations,30 translocations and animation of objects such as coins and rings,31 and elaborate mechanical props,32 which may even present us with forerunners of cinematic effects. It is intriguing to see these tricks in their playful relation to early modern knowledge of mathematics (riddles), physics and astronomy (balancing objects and optical effects), chemistry (material transformations), and theatrical performance (optical and mechanical props).

Examining the tricks in Ms. Fr. 640 in the context of their times, we find depictions of jugglers and conjurors to be a rich source for illustrating the popularity and dissemination of these trickeries. As discussed above, the ploy described on fol. 33r (the illusion of piercing one’s body parts without harming the flesh by means of a prepared prop knife) also appears in Scot’s Discoverie of Witchcraft and Prévost’s Clever and Pleasant Inventions. Interestingly, this trick appears in visual form in the engraving by Pieter Bruegel the Elder, The Fall of the Magician Hermogenes (1564) (Fig. 15). This image portrays the magician Hermogenes surrounded by the various demons he had initially ordered to bring him St. James, who were then sent back to capture him instead. On the bottom left of Bruegel’s engraving we see a round demon with a knife piercing his hand and a bodkin piercing his tongue. Looking closely at Bruegel’s engraving, on the bottom left we see yet another trick from Scot’s manuscript: the “decollation of John the Baptist.” This trick shows a beheaded trunk with a severed head lying on a table, which, in Scot’s “discovery” of the trick, is played by two people. Bruegel’s engraving presents an intriguing dual vision of the ontology and meaning of conjuring: while the overall print narrates a contest of divine and demonic power, the scenery breaks down into detailed, individual descriptions of marketplace performances (such as juggling and plate spinning). This split vision between the spiritual realm and a quotidian human landscape as the place of the conjuring is shared by Scot and Prévost, while the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 appears preoccupied only with the mundane practice of these deceptions, untroubled by larger significances.

This mundane preoccupation also seems to inform the many variations of the same tricks that appears in numerous contemporaneous texts. For example, from the realm of mathematics,33 a trick in Prévost’s manuscript, “That One May Know What Hour It Is, Using the Sun and Your Hand, Six Months Out of the Year” (Fig. 16), presents the same procedure and depiction as an earlier text on mathematical games, astronomical measurement, and instruments: the Instrument Buch by Peter Apianus (Ingolstadt, 1533) (Fig. 17).34 Among the other earlier and contemporary textual sources containing instructions for sleight of hand tricks and pranks are a late fifteenth-century collection of recipes known as Solomon’s Games of Nature (also referenced as a Supplement to Ludi Naturae or Experimenta Salomonis missa Sibille sapienti)35 and the Livre nouveau nomme le Difficile des Receptes by Jacques Moderne (Lyon, 1540). These works contained several inventive descriptions, such as “how to carry a fire in a handkerchief without burning it,” or how to make “a dye to make a horse bright green.”36 Several of the simpler tricks from these texts, such as making coins duplicate in handkerchiefs, surely formed part of a repertoire of conjuring performances played for curious spectators at markets and other public places, and are still found today as circus tricks and in magic kits for children.

The diversity and wide textual dissemination of conjuring tricks shown in this brief survey, however, makes it infeasible to trace relations between texts with any certainty. The broad transmission of conjuring tricks no doubt occurred by means of texts as well as by conjurors moving from market to market, where their transience as foreigners aroused both fascination and suspicion, and perhaps reinforced the narrative of contest between licit pleasure and illicit deception.37 In any case, problems of tracing transmission are illuminated by the fact that neither Prévost nor the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 discuss what was perhaps the oldest and best known sleight of hand trick: the cups and balls.38 This trick is played as follows: several small balls are placed under a number of overturned cups and, by dexterous sleight of hand, are made to appear as if travelling from one cup to another. This game has numerous variations, and some routines even end with the substitution of these balls with other objects.

The omission of this from Prévost39 and Ms. Fr. 640 is interesting because it is the signature trick by which the conjuring trade was perceived and portrayed, and it appears as the prominent activity of tricksters and conjuring artists in fifteenth- and sixteenth-century images, including several variations of The Conjuror or Charlatan by the School of Hieronymus Bosch (Fig. 18), and the aforementioned Fall of the Magician Hermogenes after Pieter Bruegel the Elder (Fig. 15). In parallel with card tricks, the cups-and-balls remained the most popular depiction for sleight of hand artists from the sixteenth century onwards.40 The fascination with tricksters, conmen, jesters, and conjurors, seen in the large number of texts and prints depicting their practices, goes along with the greatly increased publication of “how-to” literature. While Scot’s and Weyer’s works on witchcraft and trickery point to the larger intellectual and theological context of this fascination with deceptive practice, Prévost’s book reminds us of the neglected context of humor and entertainment, which often showed delight at misfortunes and misery, and could possess didactic aims in revealing the gullibility and follies of others.41

Conclusion: Imitation as a Source of Variation and Innovation

The examination of these recipes for trickery in Ms. Fr. 640 opens up a new dimension of this manuscript and of books of secrets in general. Conjuring tricks would seem to require great secrecy, yet they clearly had a wide circulation.42 This contrast between wide dissemination and secrecy reveals an essential feature of the “secret knowledge” contained in sixteenth-century “how-to” texts. As Scot perceptively remarked about his exposition of jugglers: while the principles of conjuring tricks can be discovered, the art of conjuring performance “cannot be taught by any description or instruction, without great exercise and expense of time.”43 As we have seen, most of these conjuring tricks belong to an established tradition of performance and circulated in this way well before they were systematically recorded; tracing any of these tricks back to a single original source or author may be impossible. Rather than pointing to an ancient source, these texts instead ground their authority in the practitioner’s experience, an experience that could allow him to “discover” the techniques behind the deceptions. Prévost, Scot, and the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 emphasize their own sleight of hand know-how by including observations or statements of experience when performing these instructions:

The true art of juggling consisteth in legierdermaine; to wit, the nimble convenienance of the hand… All other parts of this art are taught when they are discovered: but this part cannot be taught by any description or instruction, without great exercise and expense of time. And for as much as I professe rather to discover than teach these mysteries, it shall suffice to signifie unto you, that the endevor and drift of jugglers is onelie to abuse mens eies and judgements.44

The conjuror must not amuse himself by watching what he is doing, but must look at the spectators while entertaining them with hocus-pocus words to make them look at his face and not his hands.45

But because this has no appearance of truth, and seems utter fantasy (as do many things that are in this book), I shall content myself with mentioning this method alone, which indeed is true, as you shall find out by experience, which is most easy.46

Like most artisanal techniques, conjuring is a practice in which knowing how is not the equivalent of know-how. Know-how requires more than the mechanical execution of a principle: it instead involves the close-up and often extemporaneous performance of a deliberate and explicit deceit by which the audience’s attention is carefully directed in order to keep hidden the sleight of hand. The ability to distract requires an immense degree of skill and dexterity as well as a knack for improvisation to handle unforeseeable, real-time factors such as environment (e.g., light, wind) and other unpredictable circumstances, such as audience (e.g., age, size, education, mood, level of sobriety).

How better to approach the understanding of this skill of improvisation than by the recreation of such practices? The importance of dexterity in sleight of hand indeed became clear to me through my own experimentation with conjuring.47 Although Ms. Fr. 640 does not contain any recipes with playing cards, I decided to learn two card tricks, and it soon became obvious to me why both the author-practitioner and Prévost were lavish with illustrations in their trickster recipes. Understanding what action to show or present to the audience at a given time in parallel with what action to do or perform without the audience recognizing it was best achieved through visual depictions. Despite these aids to understanding the principles of the tricks, the time needed to learn the gestures and timing was, like learning any embodied practice, disproportionate to the relatively rapid acquisition of understanding of the principles. Similarly, a talent for improvisation requires deeper hands-on experience to develop. A conjuror stands before observers who may be enchanted by a trick but fail to comply with instructions (for example, they may forget the card picked halfway through the trick). Other observers might heckle the conjuror by trying to figure out the trick’s principle in order to expose it or to disrupt it. And still others may inadvertently discover the principle without fully realizing it and require further distraction.

In essence, then, constant practice by means of imitation and variation of a working principle presents a common element between secrets and recipes in both conjuring manuals and other how-to books, as “the repetition with variation teaches that matter is something to work through, and something through which to explore resistances, and in which to seek out the characteristics of a material in different situations.”48 Rather than presenting concrete, step-by-step instructions for mastering conjuring effects, recipes for sleight of hand can convey only the general working principle of a trick. Revealing the principle and providing a description of its possible practice (most effectively done by an image) is all that a written text can provide to a would-be practitioner. Such a description forms a basis on which to imagine variations for different contexts and venues, and practice allows the trickster to explore his own understanding and inventive extension of the principle. By means of such continuous variation through reflective practice, the conjuror might develop an alternative description of the trick, or perhaps even invent a similar, yet hitherto unconsidered, principle.

Weyer’s and Scot’s efforts to “discover” the human-produced mechanics of witchcraft and tricks aimed to delineate the boundaries by which human and demonic deception, and the licit and illicit, could be discerned. Jean Prévost’s Clever and Pleasant Inventions by which “One May Discover the Trickery of Jugglers and Charlatans” conveys in its title a less apocalyptic goal of providing “Numerous Games of Recreation.” It was perhaps Prévost’s emphasis on “Feats of Agility,” however, that attracted the attention of the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640, whose recounting of these tricks purely as practices that one could imitate and vary to attain proficiency resembles so much of the rest of the artisanal practice contained in the manuscript.
