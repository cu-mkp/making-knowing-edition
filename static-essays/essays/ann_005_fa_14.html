Molding Paper with Raised Letters

By , , DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/sb3y-cs42
← Back to Essays

Abstract

“Letters and molded paper” on fol. 131r of BnF Ms. Fr. 640 describes how to lay thick ink on paper, apply eau-de-vie, mold it, then cast it in metal. The purpose of “lifecast” paper with writing was not clear to the first cohort of students who reconstructed this procedure, however, over the course of two subsequent reconstructions of molding paper with raised letters, students gained further insight into this brief entry. These reconstructions considered and experimented with various materials in the entry, and compared this entry to others in the manuscript that show the author-practitioner’s concern with practical techniques for casting thin, delicate things, such as insect wings.

The entry “Letters and molded paper” on fol. 131r explains in just one sentence the process of laying thick ink onto a piece of paper and casting it into clay or plaster:

Letters and molded paper

Write with well gummed ink or with any color which has body & which does not erase once moistened with eau-de-vie. Then, place your paper on a clay slab & moisten it with eau-de-vie and cast on one side & the other.

This entry seems to be without exact precedent in previous written sources, and it does not make clear its intended final product. While a reconstruction of “Letters and molded paper” sheds light on its possible outcome, it does not illuminate its purpose. The limited nature of the text suggests that the process was not a subject of careful experimentation. Rather, as this essay will suggest, this entry likely represents a less developed idea inspired by two broader groups of recipes in the manuscript: those related to the casting of thin things and those related to writing techniques and tricks.

Materials

Ink

The entry starts with instructions to write with either well gummed ink (ancre bien gommée) or a colored liquid with body (quelque couleur qui aye corps). It is evident that the viscosity of the writing liquid is more important than its appearance, which implies that the paper onto which the ink is laid is unimportant to the final result. The recipe offers no indication of how to make ancre bien gommée, although for a contemporary reader, this may have seemed a straightforward process.

As Jo Wheeler has noted, the majority of surviving Renaissance ink recipes are related to the making of iron gall ink, which had been used since the twelfth century and involved a combination of iron salts with gall from animal or plant sources.1 A common source of such recipes are Renaissance books of secrets.2 The Secreti of the pseudonymous Alessio Piemontese (1555), for example, is filled with numerous ink recipes, each with different merits (cost-effective ink, white ink, etc.). One recipe, “To make ink to write that will allow you to make a large quantity, and very quickly, and at very little expense, and that will be perfect. And to make furthermore ink to print,” calls for the primary ingredient of fish gall, particularly that from ocean regions such as Venice.3 To improve an ink of simple fish gall, the Secreti calls for adding dust of cuttlefish or dried fish, charcoal, glass, gall, and gum (gomma). The practice of varying the ingredients in ink to fit a desired purpose, writing surface, or color was common in this period. Recipes in numerous books of secrets call for some form of gum—typically gum arabic—to be added to ink to adjust its viscosity.4 To render ink bien gommée would thus likely have involved an increase in the amount of gum in the ink.5

The manuscript itself contains two recipes for making ink within a larger entry on fol. 51r–v for making prints using copper plates.6 The two recipes call for using linseed and walnut oil, respectively, and combining the oil with crushed garlic cloves and bread crusts. Because such ink was made expressly for the purpose of printing, in the reconstruction we decided to use an ink designed for writing. The primary properties of ink with which the author was concerned were its viscosity and its permanence when moistened. The recipe explicitly states that the ink must not run when moistened with brandy (eau-de-vie), and in a marginal note for a later recipe the author again discusses the need to use waterproof ink when molding paper (fol. 142v).7 In our reconstruction, we used two inks: a modern waterproof ink and an iron gall ink made to historical specifications by the Phoenix-based company “Old World Inks” (Fig. 1, Fig. 2).

Gum

Selecting gum proved a greater challenge. Gum arabic seemed a natural choice, given the prevalence of gum arabic in this period, its use as an ingredient for ink in many books of secrets, and its specific mention on fols. 74v and 79v in Ms. Fr. 640.8 In our reconstruction, however, a gum arabic solution was found to have a diluting effect when mixed with ink (Fig. 3). Rather than forming raised letters, the surface of the ink remained flat, even when multiple layers of ink mixed with gum arabic were applied (Fig. 4). Other types of gum occur in the manuscript as well, for example, to dilute paint pigments.9

In the reconstruction we decided to find a different gum to increase the ink’s viscosity, and we therefore used powdered tragacanth gum mixed with water (Fig. 5). A resin derived from the sap of an eastern Mediterranean plant, tragacanth gum was available in Europe in the sixteenth century and was widely used. It is explicitly mentioned in Renaissance art treatises, often as a binding agent known for becoming hard with time,10 and is used in recipes in Ms. Fr. 640 on fols. 29r, 29v, and 52r. In our reconstruction, the tragacanth gum was mixed separately with both types of ink, forming a thick, gelatinous paste (Fig. 6, Fig. 7). Initially, we applied this mixture with a calligraphy pen, but the gum proved so viscous that it was necessary to paint it onto paper with a brush.

Process

Application of “well gummed ink”

We inscribed a sheet using modern ink with the word “CRAFT” (Fig. 8, Fig. 9), and a sheet using iron gall ink with “ART” (Fig. 10, Fig. 11).

After the ink dried over a period of several days, numerous changes took place. The ink shrank but remained thick and clearly raised above the surface of the paper. Although we applied the ink mixture to paper made of hemp and cotton fiber sized with gelatin11 that approximated the qualities of sixteenth-century paper, the sheets curled up after the ink mixture hardened over several days (Fig. 12).12 The curling of the paper may partly explain why the entry calls for dampening the sheet with brandy, a step which allowed the paper to relax in shape, albeit causing small cracks in the ink as it spread out. Brandy with a high alcohol content (92 proof) was used out of concern that the tragacanth gum was partly water soluble. An important question was when exactly to apply the brandy. As the sheet with writing was meant to be imprinted into a clay slab (plastre d’ardille), one might expect to apply the brandy to both sides of the paper before impressing it, as it would allow the paper to release from the clay more easily. The entry however seems to call for the opposite order of operations: “Then, place your paper on a clay slab & moisten it with eau-de-vie and cast on one side & the other.” While the curled paper relaxed in shape after the brandy was brushed on, our action of applying the brandy to the reverse of the paper after placing the paper on the clay may have created problems (Fig. 13).

Imprinting on a Clay Slab

We pressed both sheets hard against the clay by rolling them with a small marble cylinder (Fig. 14), and, when we lifted out the sheet with the iron gall ink, the ink became stuck in the clay and had to be forcibly pulled out with the tip of a knife (Fig. 15). It may be that the author’s ordering of steps was not meant to be followed verbatim, although this difficulty suggested that the entry may not have been subject to careful experimentation by the author. Nonetheless, the ink created a clearly-visible impression in the clay (Fig. 16, Fig. 17, Fig. 18, Fig. 19).

Once the paper was pressed into the clay and removed, the reverse was also molded according to the entry’s order: “cast on one side & the other.” While the flat side of the paper created a slightly visible texture in the clay (Fig. 20), the objective of the recipe and especially the instructions to cast both sides remained unclear. The pieces of clay were left on a flat marble slab to dry for a period of several days, which caused them to warp slightly (Fig. 21).

What was to be done next? Were the dry slabs of clay with an imprinted word the desired product? Their thinness made them brittle and impractical, causing one to break when handled (Fig. 22). It seems likely that such clay slabs would have been used as molds for a different substance such as plaster, which could simply have been poured onto the clay. The instruction to cast both sides of the paper may indicate that the author intended for the mold to be cast in two dimensions with the clay sheets pressed together. It may be that the clay was meant to have been inserted into a box mold or some other form. Such unknowns suggest that this entry was not subject to rigorous experimentation.

It is noteworthy that the manuscript folio on which the entry is found is very clean, with no strikethroughs in the main body of the text, as occur frequently on many other pages of Ms. Fr. 640. Furthermore, the entry should be considered in relation to that immediately above it, which is entitled “Plants that are awkward to burn in the mold,” and offers one sentence of instructions to mold such objects “in two to three casts.” The word essay (trial or try) in the margin adjacent to the plant-molding recipe likely indicates that the author-practitioner meant to try the plant recipe, suggesting that he copied these recipes from another source, or without having actually tried them himself.13 This hypothesis is supported by the brevity of the recipes on this folio. Alternatively, the author-practitioner may have meant to encourage another reader to try the experiment on his own.

One might surmise that the entry for molding paper with raised letters on fol. 131r was simply a “thought experiment,” an idea jotted down without empirical testing. The sources for such an idea are likely not textual, as no exact precedent for this technique in printed books could be found. Rather, this entry seems closely related first to a number of recipes related to writing found much earlier in the manuscript: on fol. 19v a recipe titled “For writing as well from the left as from the right” states: “Write as best you can with well-gummed ink on as many little cards as you want [illegible] to write words. & once each letter is well saturated with ink, put it down on your paper & rub with a tooth the back of the card.” This recipe provides a nearly identical process to that described in the first half of the entry on fol. 131r, to create an imprint of inked words on another surface. It is significant that on fol. 19v the recipe explicitly focuses on using this technique to reverse the direction of the writing, as this same process obviously occurred when reconstructing the entry on fol. 131r. In the case of the entry on fol. 131r, however, if something were cast into the clay impression, it would emerge with the writing again facing in the normal direction. This may indicate why the reversal of text was not explicitly discussed on fol. 131r.

The manuscript features an additional group of entries related to writing that show the author’s broader concern with practical techniques related to this process. Fol. 46v presents a series of such recipes, “Scribe, Oil of sulfur” (used to clean a quill), “Shoemaker” (writing permanent text on shoes), “Erasing a letter” (removing previously written text), “Writing without ink,” “Making a letter à jour on paper and other work,” and at the end of the page, “On stone, black letters.” All such recipes are composed of very brief descriptions with no strikethroughs or marginal notes, and many of them involve the same material: oil of sulfur, or sulfuric acid.14 It is difficult to know how thoroughly these recipes were actually tested, although the entries reflect knowledge of the properties of sulfuric acid and its potential uses. The entry “Shoemaker” on fol. 46v employs the third-person to describe the process of writing on shoes, beginning: “If he wants to do some tanned work on black leather shoes, he can dip a writing quill in oil of sulfur and paint with it what he would like on shoes, boots, and colets.” It may be that the author learned about oil of sulfur from a shoemaker and experimented with this material in developing his own writing tricks.15 Another possibility is that the author speaks of a hypothetical shoemaker who could use a technique the author himself discovered when using sulfuric acid, just as a scribe the author-practitioner mentions in an earlier entry on fol. 46v could have used sulfuric acid to clean quills. In either case, the entries on this folio show a primary concern with the material, oil of sulfur, as a substance for writing. While some of these writing techniques may seem playful or trivial, there are numerous examples of recipes in books of secrets for erasing ink, as well as for the production of invisible ink, among other such “tricks.”16

The combination of paper with raised ink and casting in the entry on fol. 131r also relates to the author-practitioner’s interest in molding very thin materials, which is borne out in numerous recipes on nearby folios in the manuscript. On fol. 142v in an entry on “Molding grasshoppers and things too thin,” the author-practitioner begins,

If you have a written paper to mold that is too thin, after you have made the first cast & it has set, give a little thickness to the reverse of your paper with melted butter, which is the most appropriate means there is, & for fortifying the wings of either a butterfly or a grasshopper, or some delicate part of an animal to which you need to give thickness. But take heed to apply this melted butter underneath the wing or in whichever place it cannot be seen.

The technique of adding butter to the back of paper recalls a similar recommendation for the casting of rose petals (fols. 129r, 154v–155v).17 As this and other entries suggest, the author-practitioner has established a category of very thin objects that are either common subjects for casting or of particular interest to him, such as paper, flower petals, and insect wings. The author is developing a set of techniques for casting such delicate and fragile objects, such as spreading and stiffening them, to mitigate the understandable difficulties in molding and casting thin items.18 As our reconstruction showed, fine clay would likely warp too much when dried to make an effective cast of paper. It is thus understandable that the author would recommend a shortcut for casting thin items, as he does in a separate recipe for molding a fly, noting that if its wings are imperfect, they can be repaired by cutting out a small piece from flattened metal.19 In the case of the entry on fol. 131r, however, such a shortcut would have been impossible, as the objective was to cast the impression of the lettering. With further experimentation, perhaps the author could have devised a more comprehensive recipe for casting raised writing on paper.

Our reconstruction thus ended with an open question about the purpose and process of “Letters and molded paper.” Two subsequent cohorts of students took up our question and their efforts shed further light on this obscure process. In 2015, Shiye Fu and Zhiqi Zhang reconstructed the process of molding paper treated within a lifecasting entry, “Molding grasshoppers and things too thin,” on fol. 142v,20 and, in 2018 Baris Gokturk, sculptor and MFA student in the Columbia University School of the Arts, tried the short entry on fol. 131r once again.21

Further Experimentation — Baris Gokturk

Baris Gokturk made significant advances in interpreting the procedure, which are detailed in the following excerpt of his essay, edited by Pamela Smith.

The Hand of the Author-Practitioner

The prompt to write on a piece of paper impels a short processual moment that is then fixed as a form of gestural artifact in cast metal. Casting a piece of writing forms an intriguing trajectory from the fluid gesture of the hand that leaves a mark on paper to the sculptural object that is eventually cast in metal. I wished to consider the individual gesture of writing by hand, today often thought of as an extension of the body and the unique identity of a writer, and the ambition in this entry to fix it in metal. Can the cast handwriting that is the aim of this entry be compared to a portrait medal, or a painted or sculpted portrait, or even to a coin that represents state power? Is it an attempt to fix the author-practitioner’s “hand” in metal, something like the maker’s mark on a piece of metalwork?

My reconstruction showed that the intersection between handwriting, signature, and identity was somewhat different than I suspected. The fluid gesture of the hand that I had imagined being captured in the final metal cast was very different from the sculptural processes of working with “well gummed ink,” which put the final object not too distant from a lifecast lizard in its creation and final form. And yet, what is the meaning of the artisan’s hand—is it a fluid stroke that carries meaning as an extension of the maker, or is it something else?

With this question in mind, I reconstructed the entry, staying alert to how much the author-practitioner’s hand was present in the process as an extension of his identity in the form of his handwriting. I was intrigued by the possibility of capturing a gesture that is an extension of the body, as opposed to a living thing outside the body (as in lifecasting), and of capturing the self in the act of making/creating by transforming fluid and momentary gesture into static and fixed object. I therefore planned to track the process from the fluidity and the originality of handwriting manifested in the first part of the text to the static mold-constrained fixity of its casting in metal at the end of the process. The relationship of this process to casting “things too thin” and casting from life was also relevant

Reconstruction

Two previous annotations deal with molded letter paper, one by Raymond Carlson and Jordan Katz in 2014, focusing on the entry on fol.131r, and the other by Shiye Fu and Zhiqi Zhang in 2015 that treats the entry on 142v and focuses on the marginal note which instructs to “write with cinnabar wetted with oil on oiled paper & press in.” The main focus for Fu and Zhang was the molding and casting of things too thin in the form of pansies. Their reconstruction, done a year after Carlson and Katz, directly refers to the reconstruction that preceded them and reuses some of the same ingredients that were still available in the lab. At the same time, they point out a contradiction in the author-practitioner’s instructions between the entries on fols. 131r and 142v in the use of gum. On fol. 142v, the author-practitioner advises against the use of gum, replacing it with cinnabar mixed with oil for the writing.22

I was able to solve some of the questions left hanging by Carlson and Katz. The answers to these questions came through reconsidering the materials and procedures. As detailed above, Carlson and Katz used gum arabic to achieve “well gummed ink,” a common type of gum in the period. However, they used a modern gum arabic medium made specifically for watercolors (Fig. 3). This gum arabic in liquid form proves too watery and fails to build thick letters to be cast, even after being laid on in several layers (Fig. 4). They ultimately switched to powdered tragacanth gum and achieved better results (Figs. 8–11). Fu and Zhang, for their part, also used tragacanth gum, then cross-referenced a second recipe on fol. 142v and added linseed oil to their mix of venetian red pigment and tragacanth gum in another misunderstanding of the recipe, which nevertheless gave them satisfactory results. With the background of these two previous trials, I decided to use powdered gum arabic in my reconstruction.23

The main question left open by Carlson and Katz’s reconstruction was the process of making the mold. The entire entry in Ms. Fr. 640 is very short, but the passage on casting is especially brief: “Then, place your paper on a clay slab & moisten it with eau-de-vie and cast on one side & the other.” While these instructions were reasonably taken by Carlson and Katz to mean that impressions should be made in a clay slab (Figs. 14–20), I realized that this process definitely belongs to the family of recipes dealing with lifecasting, and that “cast on one side & the other” was to be understood as an instruction to make the mold in the same way that other thin things, such as beetles, leaves, and pansies were cast, that is, posed and arranged on a clay slab, surrounded by clay walls, into which plaster is cast, first on one side, then on the other, to create a two-piece plaster mold. Fu and Zhang, because they were working on lifecasting, had realized this before I did, and they had succeeded in making a cast of paper with writing on it, although their molten tin alloy did not fill the entire mold. In this third reconstruction, I therefore intended to try to cast the writing in one full successful lead-tin alloy cast, however time did not permit the cast to be carried out, so the mold is still waiting to be cast as of this essay’s writing.

Upon the close reading of the recipe itself and the two previous reconstructions, and based on my interest in investigating the transformation from the fluid state of the writing (fluid both in material and in metaphorical terms) to the static final state of the cast, as well as the relationship between the spontaneous and the crafted, I decided to focus on two aspects that required clarification in the previous reconstructions: the viscosity of the gum and ink mixture, and the making of the two-piece mold.

In reconstructing with the knowledge of the two previous reconstructions, my intention was to explore the materials used to apply the writing, as well as the consistency, strength, and viscosity of the “well gummed ink.” I thus experimented with the ratio of ink to gum arabic, in order to increase both fluidity and thickness of the ink. I mixed both iron gall ink and powdered pigment with powdered gum arabic in two separate formulas (Fig. 23, Fig. 24). The word I chose to spell out was “BODY” (Fig. 25).

I discovered that one could apply the mixture onto paper in layers, thus building up the writing surface little by little. Adding warm water in the beginning so the ink would adhere to the paper, then adding more gum in layers to build thickness were effective strategies. This, however, made the whole process very different from my previous assumption that the writing would be captured as a flowing hand with an expression of individual identity. It instead was a step-by-step, careful process of building layers (Fig. 26).

I began my reconstruction with the assumption that the recipe captured a moment of the author-practitioner’s individuality in the form of his handwriting by turning his touch into a cast artifact in a way similar to other lifecasts from nature. However, upon completing the reconstruction, I was convinced that any potential mark of individuality lay elsewhere, in the process of carefully ensuring that the letters were raised well by working them meticulously in layers.

Instead of a mimetic process by which the original and personal gesture of the author-practitioner was turned into an artifact (a process perhaps analogous to his writing of the manuscript itself), the action of the hand and its uniqueness lay elsewhere: in the application of these subsequent layers and the careful building of smooth and even surfaces (Fig. 27).

Conclusion

Through the labor and necessary care my reconstruction entailed, I came to realize that where I had seen the signature of the hand as the mark of creativity before I started, by the end of my reconstruction, my understanding had shifted. I came to see that proficiency in craft was an indicator of the artisan’s identity, and this proficiency recorded his particular bodily gestures and gestural knowledge. I came to experientially understand that the identity of the artisan, and the significance of that identity in relation to an artisan’s craft, lay not in a spontaneous creative and individual gesture, marked by a quick signature, but instead in the careful accuracy and perfection of his work to produce a consummate object, a masterpiece.

In reconstructing “Letters and molded paper,” the process of shaping the letters in layers while still keeping them clean and in proper shape brought me to an appreciation of the type of work that creates the virtuosity of molding “things too thin.” I realized that lifecasting—the recipe group to which molding lettered paper oddly belongs in the manuscript—necessitates similarly meticulously executed care. Its virtuosity lies in transforming that careful labor into a final object that appears to have been caught in the midst of life and nature itself. The creative act of such a work is to give the impression that the artist has simply transformed life into a durable simulacrum, eliding the careful labor of the artisan’s body that has gone into that transformation.24 That the author-practitioner understood virtuosity in this sense is indicated by his frequent use of the term “curieux” and “curieusement” (fols. 100r, 157r, 64r, 112v, 99r, 113r, 52v,148v, 97v, 142r, 86r, 117r, 113v, and 66r). While “curious” and “curiosity” were beginning to be associated in new ways with virtue, ingenuity, and creativity at the end of the sixteenth century, the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 uses them only in the older sense—close to their Latin root of cura—to mean care and careful execution.25

My initial interest in “Letters and molded paper” had arisen out of my fascination with the way in which the process aimed to transform the act of writing as a fleeting expression of a unique individual into a durable artifact. Through the reconstruction, I learned that the author-practitioner’s brief account aimed for an object that could only be achieved by meticulous and laborious processes of making. This is where the real individual mark of the maker, “his signature,” is located. The reconstruction made clear that the labor one had to exercise to create the illusion of effortlessness was the mark of the artist (and perhaps brought a sense of his own creativity), rather than what had been my initial fascination—the individual authenticity of the handwriting and the message it signalled. Instead, in my new understanding, the medium was the message.
