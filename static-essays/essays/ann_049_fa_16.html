Keeping Dry Flowers in the Same State all Year

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/jg4j-wx69
← Back to Essays

Abstract

The recipe “Keeping dry flowers in the same state all year” (fol. 120v–121r) reflects the author-practitioner’s wider preoccupation with the preservation of flora and fauna for display. In this recipe, he provides instructions for drying a particular set of flowers with sand and preserving them in vases of vinegar. Through an examination of the language of the recipe as well as his uses of vinegar, sand, and flowers elsewhere in the manuscript, this essay seeks to highlight the author-practitioner's familiarity with sand and vinegar as preservative materials and postulates that an accumulation of botanical knowledge likely informed his attempt to achieve a more lifelike result. Moreover, his selection of flowers suggests that he was attuned to the cultural meanings of flowers and floral decoration in the late sixteenth century. A reconstruction of the recipe demonstrates that extensive knowledge of the materials is important not only to the outcome but to their handling. The author-practitioner’s vague instructions suggest that he was familiar with the processes of preservation he describes, further underscoring his experience with and interest in preserving organic materials.

Introduction

The recipe “Keeping dry flowers in the same state all year” (fol. 120v) is one of many recipes in Ms. Fr. 640 that demonstrates the author-practitioner’s preoccupation with the preservation of flora and fauna. In this entry, the author-practitioner supplies a set of instructions for preserving flowers with sand or vinegar, and he dedicates a significant amount of space to the description of these materials as the means by which the most lifelike preservation can be achieved. The author-practitioner recommends particular flowers for preservation in sand and vinegar, for which he may have consulted contemporary herbals and texts. Based on the mention of herbals in Ms. Fr. 640, and certain phrasings of plant descriptions (e.g., fol. 63r, “although it is said to be…”), it is possible that he was part of the networks of botanical knowledge exchange in early modern Europe. “Keeping dry flowers” may have been informed by his contact with natural historians, physicians, apothecaries, and botanists; it may also be the result of his own experimentation.

Through a reconstruction of the recipe, we can better understand the author-practitioner’s approach to preservation in the manuscript as a whole, such as the importance of the materials used and the freshness of the specimens involved. Furthermore, by looking at the availability of botanical knowledge, the uses of dried flowers, and the culture of floral decoration in sixteenth-century France, we can situate the author-practitioner and his interests in the preservation and collection of the natural world within a context that extends beyond the workshop walls.

The Recipe

From the title and first line on fol. 120v, the author-practitioner clearly articulates the purpose of the recipe: to provide indoor dried floral decoration for “tables, rooms, & cabinets” when “winter denies flowers.” In order to select the best flowers for decoration, in addition to those possessing the best properties for drying, the author-practitioner would have required knowledge of the flowers commonly used for such purposes. The recipe suggests the following flowers: amaranth (amaranthe), lupines (palta lupina), cornflowers (boufains), roses (roses), carnations (œillets), marigold (soulci), broom (genest), and ranunculus (fleurs jaulnes des prés appelés ranunculus). Rembert Dodoens (1517–1585) comments in his sixteenth-century herbal that amaranth flowers are remarkable in that they “do not lightly fade or decay” if they are dried in an oven at “half heat.”1 John Gerard’s 1597 herbal states that “no broths are made well without dried marigolds” because they lend a “yellow brilliance to a pot.”2 These passages show not only that these flowers were commonly used for drying, but also that drying flowers was not an unusual activity, whether for decoration or for use elsewhere. Additionally, De stirpium collectione tabulae by Conrad Gessner (1516–1565) features a section on the collection of plants in general, which includes descriptions of how to dry different flowers and seeds, demonstrating that knowledge of preservation techniques was a part of botanical knowledge.3

The recipe “Keeping dry flowers” does not include much specific direction for drying flowers outside of the setup of the boxes of sand containing the flowers; this could indicate that processes of drying flowers in sand were commonly known. The author-practitioner simply advises laying a thin layer of sand at the bottom of the box and standing the flowers up in the container while filling it with more sand. Similarly, on fol. 145v (“Molding plants and flowers”), he recommends that certain flowers be cast from life in a standing position, indicating an approach that crossed different domains of preservation.

By contrast, “Keeping dry flowers” provides detailed information about the qualities of the materials to be used, paying particular attention to the type of sand best for preserving flowers. The author-practitioner recommends “the leanest and driest [sand] you can find,” such as the type “goldsmiths use to work enamel, or like that for stampings.” He adds four notes on the sides of the main body of the recipe to provide more information about the type of sand he regards as ideal for this process, recommending that if river sand is used, it must be dried, and that “coarse” sand should be avoided because it can weigh down the petals and cause the flower to “lose its form.” The author-practitioner provides extensive commentary on the types of sand which should and should not be used for different recipes elsewhere in the manuscript as well.4

The recipe also features a brief set of instructions for preserving flowers in vinegar. Squeezed in above the title, this part of the recipe was likely added after the sections on sand, and is significantly shorter. It seems possibly an afterthought of the author-practitioner.

Flowers in the Manuscript and the Recipe

In order to preserve the appearance of the flowers in the most lifelike manner possible, the author-practitioner would have needed to have some knowledge about the plants themselves. Elsewhere in the manuscript, he demonstrates that he has an understanding of the properties of flowers he uses and the methods necessary to cultivate them (see Appendix). He includes flowers mainly in recipes concerning lifecasting, medicines, or dyeing. In many of these recipes, flowers are used in conjunction with sand, spirits, or oil. He also usually notes the particular colors of the petals and where the flowers can be found; in two entries, he also provides some information regarding cultivation practices.

Although in some recipes the author-practitioner only makes mention of flowers in nonspecific terms, there is some overlap between the varieties listed in “Keeping dry flowers” and all other flower-related recipes in the manuscript. Of the flowers listed in “Keeping dry flowers,” at least six are noted elsewhere: amaranth, broom, marigold, rose, carnation, and pansy, the latter four appearing in recipes for casting and molding. Only ranunculus and lupine are unique to this recipe. Of the eight flowers which appear outside of “Keeping dry flowers,” three are tree and shrub flowers,5 four are wildflowers,6 and one is a garden plant.7

A survey of sixteenth-century herbals shows that most of the flowers in the recipe for “Keeping dry flowers” served medicinal functions in addition to their decorative uses. For example, ranunculus plants were used as a remedy against “corrupt and evil” growth on the body, such as warts, to make them fall away.8 Also, pansies were known to be good for “cleaning the lungs and breast” when boiled, and were “very good for fevers.”9 It is likely that the author-practitioner was familiar with one or more of the many herbals circulating in print. The herbals of Dodoens and Pietro Andrea Mattioli (1501–1577) were widely disseminated in the sixteenth century. Dodoens’s herbal, Cruijdeboeck, had been translated into a number of vernacular languages, including French, by 1568.10 Rembert Dodoens’s name appears on the beginning pages of Ms. Fr. 640,11 and the name “Mathiol,” presumably referring to Pietro Mattioli (latinised Mathiolus), is noted in a recipe on fol. 35v.

The flowers recommended in “Keeping dry flowers” are also all noted in various sixteenth-century herbals as having bright colored petals. While this would not have been of concern in the processes of casting or molding,12 in indoor decoration, color was likely an important characteristic of the flowers chosen to be displayed. Five of the flowers highlighted in “Keeping dry flowers” were appreciated for their vibrant hues. Ranunculus, which the author-practitioner also called the “yellow meadow flower,” is described as possessing a “fair yellow color as gold, and shining” in Dodoens’s herbal. It continued to be valued for its brilliant color, and many nineteenth-century florists considered it to be among “the most brilliant and showy and beautiful flowering plants in the world.”13 Marigolds were similarly praised for their “bright and shining yellow flowers” which “close at the setting of the sun” and “open again at the sun rising.”14 While the author-practitioner highlights the yellow color of ranunculus and marigold plants, lupines came in a number of different colors in the sixteenth century, including yellow, blue, and a “reddish” color.15 On fol. 63v, a recipe for scudegrun uses broom flowers boiled in water to create a yellow lake pigment, pointing to the desirability of their color for painting. Amaranth was noted for its deep crimson color,16 and pansies produced flowers of “three diverse colors, whereas the highest leaves of the most part are violet, others of bluish or yellow, with black and yellow freckles along the frame.”17

Four of these flowers are also specifically described in contemporary herbals as having strong stems which do not bend easily. Dodoens’s herbal names lupines as having “hard stems,”18 and the English term for the broom plant derives from the use of the plant’s branches for sweeping in the fifteenth century, imparting a sense of how sturdy the stems are.19 Stalk strength may have been important to the actual process of covering the flowers with sand; flowers with weaker stems might have been damaged by the weight of the sand.

Culture and Cultivation of Flowers: Knowledge

Increased activity in botanical study in the sixteenth century could have contributed to the author-practitioner’s knowledge about plants and flowers as well. In addition to the widely disseminated herbals mentioned earlier, scholars and practitioners of all sorts exchanged information through letters, pressed flower books, and whole flowers shipped from around the world.20 As Florike Egmond has pointed out, “natural history research was in nearly every respect a collective enterprise in which men (and a few women) cooperated who belonged to different classes of society and who participated in diverse institutional spheres, ranging from courts to urban guilds and from universities to international trading houses.”21 Interest in plants and flowers increased steadily in the early modern period, as Europeans ventured beyond Europe and brought back reports as well as the bulbs or seeds of exotic flora.22 Ranunculus flowers were introduced to Europe from the Levant through botanist Carolus Clusius (1566–1609).23 Clusius’s informal correspondence network was wide-ranging, and he exchanged information on naturalia as well as the plants and seeds themselves.24 Indeed, he has been credited with transforming the appearance of gardens of northern Europe through introduction of new floral species.25

Dried flowers played a role in these networks of exchange as well. Naturalists, physicians and apothecaries created collections of dried plants, both local and exotic, which they kept between the pages of bound volumes, called herbaria, and they could turn to such collections year-round to study the plants.26 Herbaria were sometimes called winter gardens,27 and they allowed students of botany to examine pressed plants and flowers out of season or from distant locales. Herbaria also served as a record of the common and exotic plants encountered.28 That said, it is important to bear in mind that the herbarium also provided a distorted image of the flower by flattening the three-dimensional object. The recipe for drying flowers in Ms. Fr. 640 is therefore notable for its ability to preserve flowers in their “same state” and form.

Culture and Cultivation of Flowers: Decoration

By the end of the sixteenth century, the increasing exchange of plants and botanical knowledge across the continent led to new uses of flowers by early modern Europeans. People increasingly used cut flowers in home floral arrangements, and the cultivated gardens of affluent merchants and aristocrats displayed collections of new and exotic plants. The choice of flowers may have been influenced by the symbolic meanings ascribed to particular species. On this point, some of the flowers listed in “Keeping dry flowers” held such cultural significance. Pansies, for example, take their name from the French noun, pensée, meaning “thoughts,” and, as such, the pansy symbolizes remembrance. In a renowned invocation of this meaning, the character of Ophelia in Hamlet gifts Laertes with a bloom and remarks, “Here’s pansies, that’s for thoughts” (4.5.56). The term amaranth referred to an imaginary flower that never dies; for this reason, the flower has been used to represent immortality.29 Calendulas, or marigolds, were thought to bring good luck.30 The symbolic meaning of these flowers could have contributed to their selection for cut flower arrangements as well as for still life painting of the period.31

Representations of flowers, as metal castings or in paintings, were valued at this time. As noted above, many of the recipes in Ms. Fr. 640 that feature flowers are recipes for ornamental objects such as lifecasts. The Jeux Floraux (Floral Games) in Toulouse—an annual literary competition—offered winners of the poetic prizes casts of metal flowers, which included wild roses and marigolds.32 Still life paintings of floral arrangements also became popular at the turn of the seventeenth century. They came to be understood as “microcosmic representations of gardens” as well as expressions of the transience and ephemerality of human life, and they could be hung indoors.33

It is possible that dried flowers were used by artists of floral still life paintings in this period. Many such paintings feature bunches of flowers that did not bloom in the same season.34 Artists emphasized that these paintings were ad vivum, that is, made “after life,” and “painted from an actual bouquet in front of the easel,”35 usually depicting the most expensive flowers available.36 Although it is possible, as historians such as Florike Egmond and Sachiko Kusukawa have suggested, that flower painters were working from floral study drawings or other paintings, drying seasonal flowers might have permitted these artists to examine the blooms to create their still life artworks year-round.

Cut flower arrangements of the kind featured in still life paintings and increasingly in home decoration were new in the sixteenth century. In previous centuries, cut flowers had been incorporated into religious ceremonies in the form of garlands and crowns, but by the end of the sixteenth century, bouquets began to take their place in these rituals and ceremonies, and in adorning church walls and banquet tables.37 Fresh cut flowers in vases (or water jugs), arranged as bouquets, became more commonly used for home decoration as well. Wealthy households used flower arrangements to fill empty fireplaces; flowers appeared on dining tables during important banquets, and bouquets were also presented as gifts, according to an early historical study of the gardeners’ guild of Paris (established 1473).38 This shift towards bouquets can be seen in paintings and illuminated manuscripts of the time, as artists moved away from floral border decorations towards including bunches of flowers in pots or jugs within the paintings themselves.39

The types of flowers used for decoration changed as well, as transcontinental exchange of plants and botanical knowledge increased at the end of the sixteenth century. Mattioli’s herbal, published in 1563, devoted three pages to an index of flowers which could be used for decoration.40 Olivier de Serres (1539–1619), a landowner who developed a popular theory of estate management of gardens,41 distinguished between four types of gardening: “potager, bouquetier, medicinal, et fruitier.”42 The increased importation of exotic flowers and herbs served to increase the desire of European gardeners for new plants to display.43 Gardens could be a source of wonder to share, as Justus Lipsius (1547–1606) of Leiden University wrote in his De Constantina Libri Duo in the late sixteenth century: he invites visitors to the garden to “bring your scrupulous eyes here and regard for a moment this sheen and the beautiful colors” of the flowers.44

At the turn of the seventeenth century, the collection and study of imported plants and their display in aristocratic or princely gardens provided opportunities for elites to exhibit their “intelligence in the use of territorial resources,” as well as their ability to manage nature and improve it.45 This marks an important shift from earlier domestic practices of cultivation. In the fourteenth-century tract on household management Menagier de Paris, roses, violets and carnations (all noted in “Keeping dry flowers” as well) are included among the types of flowers found in bourgeois gardens in France.46 By the late sixteenth century, the exotic flowers brought to France and Europe were expensive due to their rarity and novelty, and their cultivation was originally only the purview of the very wealthy.47 The growth of a European flower market at the end of the sixteenth century, however, began to allow a wider audience to purchase and grow flowers.48 As Paula Findlen notes in her description of the Kunstkammer, “having a collection was one means by which a prince or merchant might proclaim his ability to command the world,” resulting in the creation of “a microcosm in which to receive visitors and to demonstrate [one’s] place in a world of global commerce and conquest.”49 One might extend this argument to indoor floral decoration. If an outdoor garden could serve as a large collection of curious and exotic plants, then indoor decorations could provide a similarly impressive display.

Preservation Materials: Sand and Vinegar

The keeping of flowers for decoration as detailed in fol.120r–v is achieved through the use of sand or vinegar, two materials the author-practitioner invokes repeatedly throughout the manuscript as mediums for preservation. His attention to the varieties and properties of these materials suggests his deep familiarity with their behavior and uses.

The author-practitioner devotes much of “Keeping dry flowers” to describing the type of sand required for drying flowers. As mentioned above, the sand should be lean and dry, fine grained, but not dusty; he explicitly likens it to the type of sand that goldsmiths use when sanding enamel. On fol. 121r, he writes that the sand should “weigh down” the plant and help it to keep its form as it dries. Throughout the manuscript, sand is often used as a material in casting and molding to hold the shape of objects being cast, and the author-practitioner includes detailed instructions for the type of sand that should be used for this purpose. He believes that using fine sand works best on delicate objects such as flowers. In “Sand forflowers” (fol. 160r), he writes that the sand used to mold a flower for casting should not be too thick or the flowers will be crushed; this is in line with his call for “lean” (dry and crumbly),50 fine-grained sand in this recipe as well.51 However, he also warns that the sand used to dry flowers should not be too “dusty,” or it will not come off the petals. This would have only been an issue in a recipe such as this one, where the flowers were removed from the sand; in casting, there would have been no need to ensure the sand did not stick to the petals.

The section of the recipe devoted to keeping flowers in vinegar is much shorter than that for sand. It is perhaps an afterthought or the result of experience: the instructions for this method are squeezed in the margin and above the title, below the recipe for silver which precedes it. In further marginal notes, the author-practitioner records that distilled vinegar works best for preserving blooms. Distilled vinegar could have referred to vinegar that was purely acetic acid and water, without the vegetable acids and other additives common in household vinegars.52 The most common process of making vinegar was the slow-process Orléans method. This method was first developed in Orléans, France, in the late fourteenth century by a guild dedicated to researching the best processes for making vinegar. The process is still in use today, and involves the continuous slow fermentation of wine in wooden casks.53 Today, this method typically yields a vinegar that is 7% acetic acid by volume.54

The manuscript also includes a number of uses for vinegar and its progenitor, wine, including for preservation. Vinegar is specifically mentioned in Ms. Fr. 640 as a material to preserve animals, plants and even oneself,55 and on fol. 117r, the author-practitioner recommends that when carrying flowers which need to remain fresh for casting from life, one may “soak their stems in a bottle of water, or better still wine.” He thus recognized that wine possessed a preservative quality. However, the author-practitioner also had first-hand experience with vinegar sometimes causing discoloration in flower petals. On fol. 63r, in the recipe “Amaranth color,” he warns readers that “white wine removes its tint & turns claret-colored, & the dry flower ends up white.”

Recipe Reconstruction

In following the author-practitioner’s recipe for keeping flowers, I decided on three trials for both the sand and the vinegar methods, using three types of organic flowers.

Because this reconstruction was done in early winter, the flowers listed in the manuscript were out of season, and I instead used delphiniums and larkspur, in addition to a set of roses (Fig. 1). Delphiniums and larkspur are both in the ranunculus family. Delphiniums also feature tufts of blooms similar to amaranth and ranunculus flowers, and larkspur bear smaller flowers whose petals are morphologically similar to sixteenth-century pansies. I chose the roses specifically to test the effect of vinegar on their preservation, as the author-practitioner notes that the “residue of common vinegar makes them rot.”

In testing the sand method, I set up three tall wooden boxes, and assigned a different variety of sand to each box (Fig. 2). I used beach sand collected from the Jersey Shore, commercial play sand, and refined quartz powder to approximate the different types of sand mentioned in the recipe, paying special attention to the coarseness of the sand grains (Fig. 3). Each box with its different sand contained one of each type of flower. The author-practitioner specified that the flowers be placed in the boxes “so that the flower touches neither the bottom nor the sides of the box, but remains in the air,” and that the sand be filled up around the flowers and sprinkled on the top (Fig. 4). Because his instructions on this aspect of the recipe were not explicit, I devised a method of pouring sand against the walls of the boxes in order to cover the flowers with sand without harming the petals.56 The tops of the boxes were left open, as stipulated by the recipe (Fig. 5). They were then set in a warm, dry place in the lab for ten days.

After ten days, the flowers were dug out of the boxes of sand. The recipe does not mention of a method of removal, so I used tools such as spoons and small bowls to extricate the sand from around the blooms in order to delicately withdraw them (Fig. 6). After removal, the larkspurs and delphiniums retained much of their color and were remarkably lifelike. In particular, the dried larkspur were the most similar to their original color and shape prior to drying (Fig. 7). However, the color of the roses placed in the sand became visibly darker, a deep maroon instead of a bright red, and the point at which the stem meets the bloom appeared pinched (Fig. 8).

As the flowers were removed from the boxes of sand, however, the delphiniums lost a number of petals, particularly those buried the beach and play sand. The delphinium from the box of quartz sand lost comparably fewer clusters of petals, based on before and after photographs of the flowers. It is possible that the author-practitioner favored finer-grained sand because it does not shift as easily. Not only was it easier to bury the flowers evenly in the quartz powder, but as the flowers were being removed, the fine quartz sand stayed in place and continued to support the bloom. The coarser beach and play sands shifted easily, and it seemed that the weight of the grains often made the petals of the delphiniums break off in the process of extraction. It is also important to note that the flowers placed in each sand box were not at peak bloom: when placed in the boxes, most had begun to wilt and lose petals, and this could have determined how easily the petals broke.

My reconstruction of the recipe for drying the flowers in sand leads me to a number of speculative hypotheses about the author-practitioner’s recommendations for sand and flowers. It is probable that author-practitioner favored smaller-grained, dry sand because it was less likely to crush the petals of the buried flowers. The fine quartz powder we used produced the best results with the larkspur, and the delphinium in this box lost fewer petals than in the other sand varieties.57

The color of the delphiniums and larkspurs in all three boxes was still fairly vibrant, so it may be the properties of the flowers themselves that make them better for drying and maintaining their lifelikeness, not the material they are dried in. The author-practitioner’s selection of certain blooms over others might be evidence of his botanical knowledge.

To test the vinegar method with both distilled vinegar and “common vinegar,” I used 99% pure acetic acid diluted with distilled water to 7% and also 50% (hereafter 7V and 50V, respectively), and apple cider vinegar with a live mother (hereafter ACV), poured into one-quart mason jars. Because the recipe is unclear as to how much vinegar should be placed in the sealed vessel with the blooms, I set up two trials for each type of vinegar: one in which the flowers were partially submerged (only the stems sat in the vinegar), and one in which the flowers were fully submerged. In submerging the flowers completely, we placed a piece of parafilm inside the jar at the height of the liquid to ensure that the flowers stayed under the vinegar. All mason jars were covered with two layers of parafilm and plastic tops to keep them “well sealed,” as specified by the author-practitioner. The vinegar trials were monitored every couple of days.

Even after only twenty minutes of submersion, however, it was clear that the vinegar had an effect on the color of the petals. The delphinium placed in 50V immediately began to leach color into the clear solution, turning it slightly pink. It was possible to see the stream of color coming out of the petals. This recalls the author-practitioner’s warning on fol. 63r regarding the discoloration of the amaranth flower after the application of white wine on the petals. After a week, the petals of both the delphinium and larkspur in the 50V jar were translucent (Fig. 9).

All three flowers in all three types of vinegar began to wilt slightly after one day, and after a week, the delphiniums in the 7V and 50V partial submersions had lost all of their petals. It is possible that this was the result of the less-than-optimal freshness of the flowers when they were placed in the jars. The flowers used in this experiment had been picked five days earlier, but the author-practitioner recommends that they be used when at their peak, “when they are n full vigor & growing.” Because the flowers used in our experiment were no longer in “full vigor” this may have had an effect on the outcome. The flowers in 7V and 50V also began to slump in the jars; their stems were no longer as robust as they were on their first day. The flowers in ACV did not droop as much (Fig. 10).

It is also interesting to note that the delphinium and larkspur flowers placed in the ACV retained the color in their petals for longer than those placed in 7V and 50V. It was possible to see droplets forming on the petals of the flowers in partial submersion in all three types of vinegar—droplets which ran down the sides of the jar where the petals touched the glass and led to a light pink coloration in the acetic acid—but this seemed to have the least effect on those flowers in the ACV.58

Conclusion

In “Keeping dry flowers in the same state all year,” the author-practitioner demonstrates his expertise in preservation and reveals his place in the exchange networks of botanical information in early modern Europe. His understanding of the properties of the materials involved in the process of preserving organic materials is extensive, as suggested by the many different methods he compiles and experiments with throughout the manuscript. His additional marginal notes in this recipe regarding sand varieties and properties also suggests that he tried this process more than once, as in order to achieve the most lifelike results possible, he would have had to determine which flowers were more amenable to drying and to experiment with different methods of preservation.

Many of the details in this recipe were not made explicit by the author-practitioner; as a result, there are a number of other avenues for further research. There are five other variables which could be tested: time, flower type, water’s ability to keep flowers alive as opposed to vinegar, the effect of water on dried plants, and how the flowers are sealed. We chose to take the flowers out of the boxes of sand after ten days, but the author-practitioner does not specify the amount of time (he only writes “several days”). It would be beneficial to test the amount of drying time required to achieve the most durable result. Also, further experimentation could include the flower types the author-practitioner specifically lists in the recipe in order to get as close to the original process as possible and to see if these flowers do keep their color and the appearance of vitality. Third, the vinegar method included in this recipe is the least detailed, and therefore has more scope for interpretation. For this reason, it would be good to test each of the vinegars against a control of plain water. Fourth, Carolus Clusius is known to have dipped dried flowers in water in order to “revive” and study them;59 it would be interesting to see if flowers that did not retain their color or vibrancy through drying could be restored after being submerged in water. Perhaps Clusius’s dried flowers were soaked in water before they were displayed. Finally, the author-practitioner does not make it clear how the flowers should be sealed. We tested sealing the flowers inside the jar completely, but it is also possible that the author-practitioner meant for only the stems to be sealed in and the blooms left open to the air.

The lack of a definitive set of instructions may have resulted from author-practitioner’s familiarity with the processes, which further underscores his experience with and interest in preserving organic materials (Fig. 11).

And if someone brags about molding anything presented to them, give them the downy head of the herb called dandelion to cast or a pappus, which comes from the seeds of burdock & takes flight at the slightest sigh of wind.”
142v Molding grasshoppers and things too thin “For giving thickness to a pansy or other flowers, butter is not good, but rather wheat oil, which is soon dry & holds firm.”
145v Molding herbs and flowers Summarizes the methods for molding flowers by keeping them standing in the air
145v Flowers Describes the steps used to cast a marigold flower: “ The stem on which I cast a large branch of marigold with its flower, its buds and its leaves came out neatly, just like nature, was made of one lb. of fine tin mixed with two ounces of lead.”
155v Rose Methods of casting roses successfully
159v Carnation “Mold a carnation like a rose, marigold, and all other flowers as discussed.”
160r Sand to cast flowers “In casting with thick sand, flowers crumple; it squeezes them into a mass. For this reason, be sure to dilute your sand very thinly, and blow on it so the flowers are not entirely covered.”
162v Peach trees “Because they cast and blossom from the outset, and so are caught by the frost. We expose their roots during winter so that the cold slows them down, and they blossom later, which benefits their flowers.”
166r Casting plants and flower bouquets “One must cast them as soon as they are picked because they wilt and dry out. Dunk them, therefore, right away in good spirits and then put them in your sand mold, soaked like snakes.... For bouquets, the sand must be finer than for snakes because, if it were thick, it would crush the flower.”
