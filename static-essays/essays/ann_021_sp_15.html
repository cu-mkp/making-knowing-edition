Powder for Hourglasses

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/kje3-xa60
← Back to Essays

Abstract

The hourglass had symbolic valences in early modern culture which, in the historiography, have often eclipsed its practical aspects, such as those involved in creating “powder for hourglasses” from lead and salt as described on fol. 10r of Ms. Fr. 640. This essay situates the entry among other medieval and early modern recipes for hourglass powder, which make use of a variety of ingredients (including pulverized eggshells, coral, and marble “grease”) and appear in texts ranging from fourteenth-century marital instructions to an eighteenth-century guide to maritime navigation. The entry in Ms. Fr. 640 sheds light on the fluidity between domestic and urban space in early modern Europe, the frugal and experimental quality of the domestic setting in this period, and even the material referents of certain metaphors in early modern religious writing.

The symbolic valence of hourglasses has now superseded their practical use-value: their status as a visual icon—in vanitas paintings, for example, as a reminder of human mortality—is more prevalent today than their function in a practical context. This, however, was not always the case, as the recipe for hourglass sand on fol. 10r in BnF Ms. Fr. 640 makes plain. What might this recipe be doing in the working manuscript of a sixteenth-century French practitioner? Research and reconstruction on the recipe for “powder for hourglasses” gave us valuable insights into the flexibility of ingredients in early modern recipes, the material referents of certain metaphors in early modern religious literature, and the experimental activities of domestic settings in this period.

Reconstruction

The recipe for “Powder for hourglasses” requires two ingredients: a pound of lead and “ground common salt” (sel commun).1 We used a “Sherpa Pink” natural Himalayan salt that was already present in the lab (Fig. 1).2 As for tools, it calls for the use of an iron tool to stir the molten lead mixture (presumably because iron has a higher melting point than lead) and a marble slab on which to grind the lead-salt mixture if it is too “coarse.” We approximated the iron tool with a spool of tightly-wound iron wire (Fig. 2). Although a marble slab was available in the lab, we used a granite slab.

The first step was to finely pulverize the salt with a mortar and pestle (Fig. 3). During the grinding process, we found that salt could indeed be reduced to a consistency that felt velvety rather than granular, although this required working the salt against the wall of the mortar for a fairly sustained period. The amount of salt mentioned in the recipe is four ounces, however our final, most successful trial required only 25 g of salt in combination with a pound of lead, much less than the four ounces. Perhaps the author-practitioner meant to write dram (ʒ) rather than ounce (℥).3

After grinding the salt into what we considered a suitably fine powder, we melted down the pound of lead (which was a surprisingly small amount, due to the metal’s density) (Fig. 4). Given lead’s relatively low melting point, the metal became molten within a matter of minutes. As the recipe stipulates that one should “skim” the molten lead and remove any “filth” from it, we removed the dross using a small piece of paper and a spoon (we found this somewhat akin to skimming off the film of milk or cream that can form on coffee left to sit).4

A few minutes after the addition of the first measure of salt, the mixture clearly began to “incorporate,” and it became more resistant to our stirring. At the same time, what looked like powdery, grey grains were beginning to form on the surface of the crucible. At this stage, we poured the mixture into a stainless steel dish (Fig. 5). We found that some of the lead had not incorporated with the salt, but had instead solidified at the bottom of the crucible; we therefore decided to repeat the previous steps of the recipe again, melting the lead remaining in the crucible and combining with more salt in order to yield as much “powder” as possible. After the second addition of salt, the remainder of the lead incorporated, and we poured it onto the dish along with the first batch of lead-salt mixture.

Before we began to work the mixture on the granite slab, as stipulated in the recipe, we decided to sieve it, in order to separate the largest pieces from the very fine powder, the former of which we would work on reducing into smaller pieces first. Removing the pieces of lead-salt mixture that did not pass through the fine sieve we used, we set these onto the granite slab (also placed inside the fume hood), and began to grind them with a pestle (the instrument one should use for this process is not specified in the manuscript, so we decided to make use of the pestle we had previously been using to grind our “common salt.”

At this stage, however, we discovered that the larger pieces of material that we were trying to grind down into powder were very resistant. Indeed, this material seemed to be solid pellets of lead, rather than lead and salt. After a few minutes of work, it seemed that we were not going to get anywhere trying to grind down pure lead, so we decided to just work with the powder that had passed through the sieve.

The next step in the process, washing the powder in water and “throwing out” the powder that settles on the surface of the liquid, was one about which we were initially very sceptical: the consistency of the lead-salt mixture seemed it would work well as hourglass sand. It was velvety and, while not totally impalpable, sifted easily through our (gloved) hands without sticking to them. We were unsure what would happen to our mixture if we washed it in water: if we had just combined molten lead with salt, and the reaction that had occurred was purely physical rather than chemical, wouldn’t passing the mixture through water simply dissolve the salt?

We were so incredulous about the efficacy of this “washing” action that we decided to only use half of the powder we had produced from the lead-salt incorporation. We decanted about half of the velvety lead-salt powder into a white plastic bowl, and simply poured in lukewarm water from the tap. The effect was immediate: the water turned a very deep grey, while our powder remained at the bottom, now a sludge-like consistency (Fig. 6). It now became apparent that the powder would settle at the bottom of the bowl, and could be washed without actually disturbing the powder at the bottom. We were reminded of Ken Albala’s adjuration against scepticism when recreating historical recipes, and the importance of avoiding the temptation to amend processes and actions in accord with what seems more logical to our modern-day perspectives. “[To] learn about historical…procedures,” as Albala writes, “we must trust what is on the page.”5

The lack of reaction between the water and the lead-salt mixture might also explain why the substance was used as hourglass sand. Willem Morzer Bruyns and Stuart Talbot, subscribers to an Oxford-based mailing list (RETE) dedicated to the history of scientific instruments, both pointed out via email correspondence that “it was essential that, whatever materials were used in sand glasses, they had to be non-hygroscopic.”6 Hygroscopy refers to a substance’s ability to attract and hold water molecules from its surrounding environment, through either adsorption or absorption—sugar is an example of a hygroscopic substance, as is salt (NaCl). This was important due to the fact that glass-blowers could not blow hourglasses in one piece until the middle of the eighteenth century: up until that point, the two bulbs were blown separately and held together by sealing wax or pitch. Any moisture that entered this join could compromise the hourglass’s accuracy.

We repeated the washing process six times, until the water was running as close to clear as we thought likely to achieve. Finally, we strained the last batch of water into a bowl with cheesecloth stretched over the top of it, and put our lead-salt mixture that did not pass through the cheesecloth into a small bowl and left it in a fume hood to dry. Examining the composition of our newly-washed powder, what had before felt smooth and velvety in texture now looked like minuscule lead spheres and felt much more granular to the touch. The texture of our washed powder actually tallies with recent research in the mechanics of hourglass sand flow, which has suggested that the best material for sand are “ballotini,” which are “tiny [glass] beads of subspherical shape.”7 They are ca. 40–160 microns in diameter, and because of their round edges they flow smoothly through hourglasses.8 Although our lead-salt mixture was not composed of grains that small, they were roughly spherical in shape, which might suggest why this process produces an effective hourglass sand (Fig. 7).

A Brief History of the Hourglass

The historian A. J. Turner has stated that “the origin of the sand-glass…is historically a mystery.”9 Although the device has a precedent in the ancient Egyptian water clock known as the clepsydra, the hourglass as we know it appears to be a medieval invention and has “a surprisingly brief history.”10 The earliest depiction has symbolic import: it appears in a series of frescoes by Ambrogio Lorenzetti dating to 1338 in the Palazzo Pubblico of Siena, entitled The Allegory of Good and Bad Government, and it is held by the female figure of Temperance, one of the six virtues of good government. This symbolic meaning resonated into the Renaissance, when it became ubiquitous as a memento mori in vanitas paintings of the period (Fig. 8). The hourglass also appears as one of the accoutrements of an embodied Death, for instance, in Albrecht Dürer’s copper engraving Ritter, Tod, und Teufel (Knight, Death, and the Devil, 1513), the grisly Death figure clutches an hourglass in a skeletal hand, a reminder of the brevity of the Knight’s life (Fig. 9).

The hourglass is also found in the Trachtenbuch (“costume book”) of Matthäus Schwarz (1497–1574), a German accountant who worked for the powerful Augsburg merchant Jakob Fugger. The Trachtenbuch is Schwarz’s record of the clothing he had made and wore between the years 1520 and 1560, a lavish series of more than 100 watercolors depicting Schwarz in various garments. In one of these images, Schwarz is depicted wearing an hourglass attached on his yellow hose to his calf (Fig. 10, Fig. 11), which the accompanying text identifies as an eight-minute hourglass (“mit einem uhr am schenkel von acht minuten”). In this image, the hourglass seems to function as a vanitas object, gesturing toward the larger mundus transit trope but perhaps also to the ephemerality of the fashions that Schwarz is recording (indeed, the “fickleness of fashion and the vanity of sumptuous apparel” was well-trodden ground in early modern sermons).11

Both the hourglass and fashion, then, seem to act as a reminder that glory and life are fleeting. Schwarz himself appeared to be fascinated by the passage of time, which he marked by having portraits painted of himself. On his 29th birthday Hans Maler zu Schwaz painted Schwarz with an hourglass around his neck (Fig. 12), and a later portrait from 1542, painted by Christoph Amberger, includes Schwarz’s horoscope (next to the glass of wine) and records Schwarz’s exact age (45 years, 30 days, and 2¾ hours) as well as the time of the painting, 4:15pm on 22 March 1542 (Fig. 13). Perhaps in Schwarz’s case, the hourglass does not so much function as a memento mori but more as part of a celebration of the power of art “to fix in both time and space something that [is] ephemeral and changeable.”12

The first extant textual references to hourglasses, however, occur within a maritime context. In a receipt dating to 1345, Thomas de Stetesham, Clerk of the King’s Ship called La George, is recorded as buying sixteen “glass horologes” while in Flanders.13 Thirty-five years later, an inventory of the personal effects of Charles V of France upon his death in September 1380, describes an item located in the king’s study at his chateau at St. Germaine en Laye: “Item ung grant orloge de mer, de deux grans fiolles plains de sablon, en ung grant estuy de boys garn d’archal” (Item a large sea clock, with two large phials filled with sand, in a large wooden brass-bound case).14

Hourglasses were ideal for measuring time during marine journeys, as the movement of the sand between the two glass ampoules was not affected by the movement of the ship (unlike a water clock). A. J. Turner, however, has suggested that accounts of the hourglass originating primarily in a maritime navigational context do not properly represent its diverse early usage: identifying the first nine allusions to the hourglass or “sandglass,” Turner reveals that these objects fulfilled diverse functions: they were given as wedding presents, for instance, and played a role in civic life. Indeed, one Swiss town council order established that “the tolling of the town bell for the quarter and half hours should be regulated by a sand glass.”15

Recipes for Hourglass Powder

The earliest recipe for hourglass sand that we located was in Le ménagier de Paris, the so-called “Goodman of Paris,” written in the last decade of the fourteenth century by a wealthy Parisian burgher, ostensibly for the instruction of his wife. Under the miscellaneous heading “Other small things that be needful,” and immediately preceded by a “cure” for toothache and followed by “Poisons for slaying a stag or a boar,” a recipe for hourglass sand appears:

TO MAKE SAND FOR HOURGLASSES. Take the grease which comes from the sawdust of marble when those great tombs of black marble be sawn, then boil it well in wine like a piece of meat and skim it, and then set it to dry in the sun; and boil, skim and dry nine times; and thus it will be good.16

Although the ingredients detailed here differ from those in Ms. Fr. 640, the steps are analogous to the Ms. Fr. 640 recipe: heating and skimming are both required, and the fact that the three principal steps of the recipe (boiling, skimming, and drying) need to be conducted a total of nine times suggests that a very fine powder should be the end product, just as in our manuscript’s recipe. Most interesting, however, is the placement of this recipe for hourglass sand in a domestic vade mecum, suggesting that the production of this substance was, in the late fourteenth century, part of a standard repertoire of household chores. Indeed, as we found, the making of “powder for hourglasses,” does not appear to have been particularly difficult.

These instructions can be compared to two seventeenth-century recipes. The first appears in Richard Polter’s A Pathway to Perfect Sayling (first published 1605, reprinted in 1644), a popular, albeit outdated, guide to maritime navigation composed by a Master of the King’s Ships. Polter points to the difficulty in constructing an hourglass that will measure the passage of time accurately: “they cannot be made without their imperfections, and need to be most carefully made, and by the precisest workmen.” Although Polter is likely referring here primarily to the difficulty in creating the two bulbs of the hourglass, the sand used in the device also needed careful consideration. Polter goes on to discuss the inaccuracies that result from using a “metal” sand in an hourglass, stating that such a substance

will rust somewhat, and be sometimes moyster, then at other times, likewise the hole that the sande runneth thorough, will grow wider with the force of the sand, the rather being violated by the surges of the sea: which imperfections considered, the glasse must needs deliver the time, sometime shorter, and sometime longer.17

So while Polter is attuned to the problems that can arise from it, the metallic sand he is discussing seems fairly similar in composition to that detailed in Ms. Fr. 640.

The four-book Horologi elementari of Domenico Martinelli, published in Venice in 1669, features a more extensive list of potential ingredients for hourglass sand. In this text, Martinelli—born in Lucca, Tuscany, and now principally remembered as an architect—details the construction of various timepieces, including one of the first descriptions of the cuckoo clock. One of the timepieces he discusses is the hourglass, including its sand. Martinelli writes, “What could serve very well is ground marble, or the sand left by rivers…but above all these I esteem best the ones made from lead, or from tin.”18 In contrast to Polter’s text, which speaks only of the “metal” powder that can be used in hourglasses, Martinelli claims that various substances would serve very well as hourglass sand—ground marble, as in the recipe from Le ménagier, or the “sand left by rivers”— although the best, he concedes, is made from lead or tin. The architect also foregrounds the importance of a fine, dry sand, writing that it must be “well-dried, so that with the humidity the grains of it do not unite together.”19 In commenting on his 1694 translation of Martinelli’s text, the French mathematician Jacques Ozanam noted that well-dried and finely-milled eggshells would produce a white powder, unaffected by humidity, another ideal material for use as hourglass sand.20 The early sixteenth-century Swedish writer Olaus Magnus, remembered now for his Kunstkammer and his ethnography of the Nordic countries, A Description of the Northern Peoples, also mentions eggshells for use as hourglass sand.21

These many recipes and ingredients for hourglass sand indicate how widespread was the need for and knowledge about good hourglass sand.

Constructing Hourglasses

Once the sand was made, how was the hourglass constructed, and most importantly, how was it calibrated? We do not know how this happened in a domestic setting. But the hourglass-maker was clearly a common sight in an urban environment. In their 1568 so-called Ständebuch (generally known in English as the “Book of Trades”), portraying artisans in their workshops, Hans Sachs and Jost Amman included a clockmaker. Although the woodcut appears to depict a mechanical clock, the verse outlines the making of an hourglass, mentioning the use of clear glass filled with small-sized sand, and the wooden housing that helps hold the two bulbs tightly together (Fig. 14).22

An expanded version of this book of trades, Abbildung der gemein nützlichen Haupt Stände (Portrayal of the Principal Estates of the Common Good) published in Regensburg in 1698 by Christoph Weigel, provided a much more detailed explanation of the making of hourglasses. His “Sand-Uhrmacher” (hourglass maker) is portrayed surrounded by his products. The verse above and below the image draws upon well-exercised symbolic meanings of the hourglass, advising “Beim Stünden-Glas der Zeit, denck an die Ewigkeit” (When looking at the hourglass of time, think of eternity), and below the verse concludes with a couplet that reads, “Wol dem, der dieses Glas umwendet/ Zum güten Laüff, eh er bös’ endet” (Happy the one who turns this glass/ to hasten to good, before he ends in evil) (Fig. 15).23

On the following pages, Weigel gives a short history of clock making, outlining three stages from sundial (impractical because it could not be used in cloudy weather), to the water or sand clock (flawed because they must be capable of being seen, which makes them useless in a dark night), through to the Schlag-uhren (chiming clocks), which use steel springs or a weight, and can be heard as well as seen.24 Citing Pancirollus’s description of the water clock, Weigel goes on to state that he cannot say when the sand clock first appeared: “wann und von wem,” he writes, “diese neue Art der Stunden-Gläser hervor gebracht worden ist mir unwissend” (when and by whom this new type of hourglass was invented I do not know).25

Weigel’s account notes that the sandglass maker was originally a free trade, but in Weigel’s own time is part of the scheduled trades in Nuremberg, where four types of “Sand-Uhren” (sand clocks) were required of any master:

(1) a small clock with lead sand, (2) a clock with four glasses of white sand, of which the first indicates the quarter, the second the half, the third three-quarters, and the fourth the full hour, (3) a clock of three hours, likewise filled with white sand, (4) a clock of two glasses, of which one runs out at the half hour, the other at the full hour.26

His list, which illustrates the diversity of hourglasses available at this time, also notes that they are varied in size, and then provides rich detail about the construction of hourglasses:

When it comes to size, one finds those that are barely finger long, that nevertheless run for a full hour; others measure a good ell long, that one must turn after three, four, or more hours [...]. The sand is either red when it is dug up, and is washed by the sand clockmaker, dried or desiccated and burned in a pan so that the color becomes nice and red, and then is sifted through many sieves, one narrower than the next, up to twenty times. Or, the sand is white and burned from egg shells and then treated as explained with the red sand. The tin and the lead is similarly made into a sand so that the glasses—to indicate the hours correctly—are filled with it. The clocks’ housings are usually made from wood or brass and there are some of the latter kind which one can fold up to carry them safely and unbroken in a bag [...]. And when all this, glasses, sand, and casings are made, the clock is put together in the following manner: one glass is filled with sand, the brass sheet is laid on top, then a small hole is made in it with a needle or an awl, the other glass is set on top and connected with pitch. Then one puts the clocks made in this way together, lifts it all up and turns the measuring clock [Eichuhr]. When this one runs out, then the new ones are all turned, opened up again under light, and, what has not yet run out, is poured out. Then it is closed with pitch again, string is wound around it, and it is placed in the housing.27

Scavenging and Reuse of Materials in Daily Life

While the techniques of production might be similar—the repetition of several purification steps to ensure a very fine sand—the ingredients for the sand are diverse, including marble dust, lead, tin, river sand, and eggshells. These apparently miscellaneous ingredients suggest that the sand could be produced from almost any materials that a person had to hand. Thus, lead and salt may be the principal ingredients of the author-practitioner’s recipe because these two substances would have been in ample supply in his workshop, and eggshells may have been used in households because they would be both easily accessible and an efficient use of domestic waste. While the marble grease that features in the recipe from Le ménagier seems a little more exotic than lead or eggshells, we should bear in mind that great marble tombs were being constructed in Paris in the fourteenth century. Moreover, this interesting use of waste material brings to light the role such construction projects played in the daily life of a city, an insight that would not be available from other sources.

The notion that hourglass sand might be patched together with any scraps of material readily available tallies with citywide changes in methods of waste disposal. In late sixteenth-century England, for example, but from the fourteenth century in many other European cities, local authorities appointed “scavengers” or “gunge-farmers” to patrol the streets of urban areas with carts and tumbrels, removing waste which was then often used to sustain an artisanal trade in waste materials.28 Ashes, bones, and shells were all repurposed as useful products; in fact, as Emily Cockayne notes, “many waste products did not make it onto the piles, as they could be profitably reused elsewhere.”29 Neighbours scavenged reusable wood, fat went to the tallow chandlers to make candles, and dogs and pigs were fed waste organic matter.30

If the ingredients for hourglass sand could be constituted from domestic waste at the householder’s disposal, hourglass sand could be produced frequently and with ease, thus cementing its importance in the fabric of daily life, where people would regularly encounter hourglasses. These timepieces had become increasingly widespread in Europe during the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries—referred to by the Delalandes as “the golden age of hourglasses”—and were used to measure intervals such as the length of sermons, cooking time, and breaks from labor.31 The hourglass was also employed in somewhat more specialist domains: it marked the length of lectures for the students at Oxford University, curtailed “the prolix orator’s flow of words” in law-courts and the House of Commons, and even helped medical practitioners in measuring pulses.32 They were also used in craftsmen’s shops in order to regulate working hours, which might suggest why the recipe for hourglass sand was included in Ms. Fr. 640.

Domestic and Artisanal Spaces

The household production of hourglass sand points also to the interpenetration of domestic and artisanal spaces that characterized the period. Speaking of early modern England, Amanda Flather has claimed that the boundary between home and work was remarkably blurred, stating that “in towns, artisans often had [their] ‘workshops’ attached to the main dwelling house,” as an outgrowth of or appendix to domestic space, rather than as a separate, fully-fledged “creative arena” in its own right.33 Relatedly, Paula Hohti has argued for the importance of domestic space in the forging of artisanal identity in sixteenth-century Siena.34

It was in the kitchen, however, that this separation between domestic space and artisanal workshop was most porous. As Simon Werrett has observed in his history of recycling in early modern science, the kitchen could make quite a serviceable stand-in for a custom laboratory space, particularly given the access it provided to fire and ingredients which often had both culinary and chemical uses.35 Ms. Fr. 640 and other books of recipes, such as Hugh Plat’s The Jewel House of Art and Nature (1594), mingled recipes for sugar-paste with metal molding techniques. Recipe books also often encouraged women to further blur the boundaries between kitchen and apothecary by preparing medicines in their kitchens. Elaine Leong’s study of medicine production in the early modern home of Elizabeth Freke, for instance, shows that her recipes were primarily based on “common herbs and spices,”36 and that Freke used common household staples, such as “butter, eggs, and cream” in salves and balms.37 Recipes for hourglass sand made from eggshells further spotlight the interpenetration of these two spaces. The domestic production of hourglass sand suggests that these timepieces were a significant, even necessary, part of the fabric of daily life, meriting a place in the economy and manufacturing processes of the household.

The Hourglass as Physical and Symbolic Presence

The fact that the hourglass was used to measure the length of sermons can perhaps help us to understand more comprehensively why the device features so prominently in sixteenth- and seventeenth-century English religious literature. References to the hourglass are particularly prevalent in printed sermons, emphasizing its symbolic function as a reminder of man’s mortality: for instance, in a sermon of 1641 by Jeremiah Burroughs, the author states that “all men in worldly honours are like an houreglasse; now this end is uppermost, by and by this end is done.”38 The hourglass is also often coupled with the candle, an emblem of the fragility of life. In the Puritan church leader Richard Baxter’s A Christian directory (1673), Baxter admonishes his reader to “remember that as soon as you begin to live, you are hasting toward the end of your lives: Even as a Candle as soon as it beginneth to burn, and the Hourglass as soon as it is turned, is wasting, and hasting to its end.”39 This pairing is also found in Thomas Bentley’s The Fift [Fifth] Lamp of Virginitie (1582), in which a prayer “to be vsed by old women” features the line “the houre glas is almost run, my candle is almost burned out, and thine everlasting iudgement draweth on.”40 The obvious explanation for the hourglass’s recurrence in sermons is thus its symbolic significance, but the fact that an hourglass, measuring the length of the sermon might be standing beside the preacher, could have amplified the import of that sermon. Recognizing that the production of the hourglass could be a domestic chore, and a ubiquitous part of quotidian life, makes even richer our understanding of the symbolic significance of the hourglass in early modern culture. It was both a memento mori and a tangible, physical presence woven into daily life and the affairs of the household.
