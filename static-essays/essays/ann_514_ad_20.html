Two Author-Practitioners in Dialogue across Time

Abstract

Artist Michael Price has dedicated his career to understanding the materials and techniques of early modern painters through historical painting recipes, technical analysis and conservation literature, and experiments using natural pigments and traditionally prepared binding mediums. His experience publishing his research findings and making processes position him as an ideal interlocutor for the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640. In this essay, Price surveys the manuscript’s entries on the topics of painting and varnish, offering insights from his own practice and arguing that the author-practitioner’s knowledge of the painter’s craft is largely second hand.

Michael Price

I have always been drawn to the works of the great masters from the Renaissance through to the seventeenth century. After graduation from the Central School of Art in London back in 1974, my first desire was to make drawings from the Poussin paintings in a variety of Museum collections. As my career as a professional artist started to take shape, I began to realize that I needed to understand much more about these works of art. By 1976, I was living in Holland and making notes from works by Vermeer and Rembrandt. In 1977, Munich became my home for the next twenty-two years. Little by little, I visited many museum collections from Paris and Vienna to Florence and Rome. During these years, the composition and subject matter was my main concern with regards to my own painting. However, there was one nagging question—why could I not get the same depth and subtly of color with my modern tubes of “industrialized” oil paint compared to the Flemish masters? Through a meaningful coincidence, I became acquainted with the head conservation scientist and later director of the Doerner Institute who introduced me to the paint layer analysis of a restored Tintoretto painting. This encounter changed my life! At first, I bought a few natural pigments from the shop opposite the museum, Kremer Pigmente, including azurite, ultramarine ash, and cinnabar. I mixed the blues with walnut oil and the cinnabar with linseed oil. Everything looked promising. Then within a month, the azurite and ultramarine ash were changing color. After one year there was an ugly discoloration. No one had any answers. I began to scour medieval manuscripts as well as museum conservation reports on binding medium analysis. This textual research proved frustrating. Not only were there inconsistencies in the way terms for materials were used from one recipe to another, but it was difficult to know with any certainty what those materials consisted of, especially binding mediums. At some point, I decided to approach the problem from the opposite direction—that is, by experimenting with mineral and organic pigments in my studio. Over time, my techniques evolved through an informed trial and error, my knowledge grew, and I came to understand a great deal about the preparation and properties of natural pigments. My results proved to be interesting enough that I was able to get seven-year ageing tests carried out on my paint samples by the Technische Universität in Vienna as well as binding medium tests by the Doerner Institute. Later, after my move to New York, I was able to do tests on lake pigments with the MoMA conservation department.1 In 2016, I published a two-volume compendium of my research on historical painting techniques, Renaissance Mysteries.2

As an artist and author who has spent my career interpreting historical craft recipes, experimenting in the studio with natural materials, and struggling to set down my own processes in words, I feel a particular affinity for the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640. His compilation of recipes and observations—particularly his entries on the topics of painting and varnish—has proven far more interesting than I had expected. Some entries demonstrate a detailed practical knowledge, while others share surprisingly superficial or basic observations. Other entries still are obscure, provocative, or outright wrong. In the paragraphs to follow, I offer my response to select entries, from my perspective as a modern artist and researcher. My careful consideration of the entries on painting and varnish leave me with the impression that the author-practitioner had limited if any training as a professional painter. Nevertheless, there are great insights in the second-hand knowledge that he did collect, and his entries suggest fruitful directions for future research (Fig. 1).

Painting and Varnish Entries in Ms. Fr. 640

The Making and Knowing Project has assigned categories to each of the entries in the manuscript. Collectively, these two categories, painting and varnish, account for nearly twenty percent of the entries in Ms. Fr. 640.3 Thus, the art of the painter was of considerable interest to the author-practitioner. The table in appendix gives an overview of the entries on the topics of painting and varnish.

Some of the author-practitioner’s observations are quite basic and essentially correct, such as his comparison of the skills required for working in oil- and water-based media on fol. 32r, or his list of black pigments on fol. 58v (to which I would add pyrolusite and tourmaline black, which I prefer for their crystalline qualities). His comment in “Painters” on fol. 9v that “green earth” (vert de terre) should not be bound in oil is correct; it will change hue within five years.4 Other general observations seem almost like ethnographic descriptions of the painter’s profession and practices, such as the marginal notes to “Painter” on fol. 56v. These notes, which, for instance, contrast the working methods of the Italian and Flemish painters and remind one to wipe excess oil from the brush, “otherwise, the work would run & would soon be all disfigured,” sound amusingly like the first lessons for an apprentice—this text could be used in most MFA courses today!5 In other cases, the author-practitioner’s observations strike me as so obvious that they hardly need mentioning, such as the comment elsewhere on fol. 56v that “all marble on which a knife can prick is worth nothing for grinding fine colors.”

Other entries are surprisingly complicated. A rather complex and curious method “for gilding on wood” is given on fol. 75v, which involves improving the color of gold with the smoke of partridge feathers. Additionally, “Mastic varnish dry in a half hour” on fol. 97v offers an unusually complex method of making what amounts to a problematic varnish. The final sentence of the entry says everything—it yellows. And, as with most of the varnish recipes the author-practitioner records, it would require extreme caution to make, since hot oils and resins easily auto-ignite. A recipe like “Varnish for lutes” on fol. 98r requires extremely dangerous heat, as amber dissolves in oil at 700ºF.6

A handful of entries are odd or confusing, such as the need for long paintbrushes when working “azure” (azurite?) on fresco—never mind the comment that fresco is not used on wood (fol. 58v). The instruction in “Mirror” on fol. 65v to “have a cristallin mirror” to hand when painting flesh colors is interesting. Indeed, looking at one’s own painting in a mirror can teach a painter quite a lot, although I doubt this is what is meant here. The advice about layering colors in “Neat work” on fol. 60r is contradicted by much paint layer analysis in conservation literature. “Faces in distemper” on fol. 93v, which presents an odd way of working with bistre, seems to contradict my experience that it can be controlled very well with colors in distemper. I found the recipe “For making varnish” on fol. 74r–v to be very strange; frankly, I cannot imagine how this varnish will work; moreover, “Another recipe for making varnish” on fol. 73v would yield something questionable—this varnish will most probably yellow. Some entries are confusing for what was either missing or tacitly understood. I wondered whether a ground layer was assumed for the technique described in “Painting in distemper on wood” on fol. 66v, since one would not paint with distemper directly on the panel. Similarly, some additional step must be understood in “For gilding on wood” on fol. 75v, which instructs to apply a glue layer to the wood and to leave it to dry before putting down the layer of gold—yet presumably there still needs to be a tacky surface for the gold to adhere! Finally, some topics are simply unfamiliar to me, such as the methods described in “Painting on glass” on fol. 31v and the manner of pressing walnut and almond oil in “Painter” on fol. 57v.

In some cases, the content of an entry is difficult to judge because the precise meaning of the terms used is uncertain, there are internal contradictions, or the process described is indecipherable. For instance, in “Thick varnish for planks” (fol. 3r–v), the author-practitioner describes how garlic was boiled in oil “to extinguish it & rid it of grease,” but the meaning of the term “extinguish” (estaindre) is unclear. There seem to be numerous contradictions in “For laying down and seating burnished gold and giving red or green or blue” on fol. 6r as well as both bad and plausible advice. The instruction to “temper verdigris with walnut or linseed oil” is a disaster, guaranteed to turn brown, whereas the injunction to temper azur d’esmail, whether it is azurite or lapis lazuli, with “turpentine varnish” is correct—that is, if a fir-balsam resin varnish is meant. “Fatty colors” on fol. 65r is difficult to understand and the intended outcome of “For varnishing” on fol. 3v is uncertain, while “Flesh colors from arsenic” (fol. 13r), “For layering gold in distemper” (fol. 32r), and “Painter” (fol. 31v) are difficult for me to evaluate without closer study or reconstruction. Despite my extensive experience making and using varnish, two varnish entries on fol. 4r are challenging to judge. Regarding “Varnish of spike lavender oil,” one has to be extremely careful with spike oil of lavender. It is a brilliant solvent for removing colors bound in a fir-balsam resin. Varnishes consisting of sandarac in any oil will yellow considerably. I am not too sure what the result would be of dissolving mastic in turpentine as a varnish (presumably the fused fir-balsam resin with spirits of turpentine is meant—the ratio would probably work better with 20 to 30% resin to 70 to 80% spirits of turpentine).7 As for the entry “For removing varnish from an old panel that is yellowish,” it could work! Again the “turpentine varnish” (unknown exact mixture) could work. My own observation over the past 15 years would support the principle of fusing any fir-balsam resin with pure spirits of turpentine with at least a 1:1 mixture. The question is still, do you add a little oil or mastic or sandarac to the “varnish”?

If some recipes are odd and others difficult to evaluate, others still are simply too dangerous to try. I cannot recommend testing the recipe “For making vermilion” on fol. 74r, which involves cooking sulfur and mercury in a lead pot (with provisions about what to do should it ignite!). “Painters” on fol. 9v, which instructs to melt orpiment, an arsenic sulfide, is “crazy,” dangerous, and obviously was never applied in practice! (In addition, orpiment will never oxidize in any oil. The most beautiful golden yellow is achieved in a fir-balsam resin, with the inclusion of drops of walnut oil.)

In many instances, however, the author-practitioner’s recipes and observations were consonant with my experience and research. When he uses the terms “turpentine” (tourmentine) and “turpentine oil” (huile de tourmentine), he can be understood to mean a fir-balsam resin and distilled turpentine respectively. His entry “Varnish for panels” on fol. 3r is basically correct. Venice turpentine is a fir-balsam resin which is quite viscous and is diluted with a distilled turpentine (here turpentine oil). When heated together, which in this recipe is quite dangerous due to the flash-point, the two components are fused and do not separate again. It dries very quickly. It should be applied very thinly otherwise it could yellow. As a binding medium for pigments of low refractive index a fir-balsam resin (larch turpentine) fused 1:1 with a double rectified turpentine (turpentine oil) is a perfect binding medium with a few drops of walnut oil mulled into the color. The entry “Varnish for distemper” (fol. 56r) is a more difficult subject, since it depends on what is understood here as distemper—a variety of water-based binding mediums. A very diluted fir-balsam resin (roughly 80% pure spirits of turpentine fused with 20% fir-balsam resin—basically equivalent to Venice turpentine) would not affect the refractive index of most pigments bound in a distemper, although any layers of white pigments such as calcite would be altered slightly. Presumably the spike oil of lavender is the diluent for the resin, but pure spirits of turpentine as the diluent will probably work better than spike.8 I understood a number of observations in “Painter” (fols. 56v–57r), such as the note that lake pigments “lose their color & die in the air” (56v), which is presumably due to oxidation or the UV effect on the dry pigment. The use of ground cristallin (glass) with lake (57r) is well documented in conservation literature.9 When lake colors are bound in a fir-balsam resin and a few drops of oil (to avoid paint layer friability), the color is dry the following day. The observation a few lines later that ground vermilion is wan and pale unless it is ground after lake, suggests to me that he is using a manufactured vermilion that is not of high quality. “Colors in oil that are imbibed” on fol. 66r instructs to use varnish to unify a paint surface with patches of shiny and matte color. Indeed, if too much oil is used when mulling a pigment, the colors will with time become dead and flat—even patchy. Nevertheless, varnish is only a temporary solution. Further, I would suggest that by “imbibed,” he means saturated. My experience also suggests that the entry “Drying of oil” on fol. 63r is correct and that “Essential oils” on fol. 67v seems to describe the process of adding a little walnut oil to a diluted fir-balsam resin, which dries remarkably quickly. Finally, my own studio practice reveals that the method of sabotaging a rival’s work described in “Spike lavender oil” on fol. 60v would function extremely well, as pure spike lavender oil easily destroys colors to which it is applied (Fig. 2).

Most exciting were those entries that struck me as worth trying out, such as “Varnish” on fol. 71v and “Varnish of spike lavender oil” on fol. 4r. Similarly, “Varnish dry in an hour” on fol. 60v seems extremely difficult to produce from the instructions, as there are no quantities, but interesting nonetheless. I suspect it would yellow, but I have no experience with “eau-de-vie,” probably a strong alcohol, which could change the varnish chemistry enough not to yellow. Perhaps it is worth an experiment. Two entries (“Painter” on fol. 58r and “Verdigris and another very beautiful bright green” on fol. 63r) call for grinding verdigris and orpiment with urine before binding them in oil in order to prevent the color from dying. I have never tried this—I wonder if it would work since these two colors cannot be bound in oil. It seems worth an experiment. I also find the author-practitioner’s comments about the uses of spike lavender oil on 57v interesting and worth trying out.

Particular Problems: Azure, Imprimatura, and Lead Whites

My own interests and practice overlap with those of the author-practitioner and the Making and Knowing Project in three notable instances. I offer my response to the Project’s ongoing inquiries into the meaning of the term azure (and its variants azur d’esmail, esmail d’azur, etc.) and the terms that refer to ground layers in painting (impression, assiete, etc.). I also respond to the author-practitioner’s abiding interest in producing lead-based whites.

Ambiguous Azur d’Esmail

There has been much discussion among Making and Knowing Project members about the identity of the blues in Ms. Fr. 640, which might plausibly refer to lapis lazuli (Fig. 3), azurite (Fig. 4), or smalt.10 Ambiguity has led the Project to keep some of the blues in their original French rather than translate them, most notably, the elusive azur d’esmail. Moreover, it is not clear that the author-practitioner is consistent in the way he refers to blue pigments, and so it is advisable to consider each entry on its own terms. In what follows, I bring my own experience to bear on the discussion of blue pigments in the manuscript.

Already on fol. 10r, in “Purple color,” we encounter different blues in the same entry: “making the first ground of common azure, or better yet azur d’esmail, & next they glaze it with [red] lake.” Here, presumably, “common azure” may mean azurite and azur d’esmail lapis lazuli (although it could mean blue bice or smalt). The dye of the lake is likely precipitated on alum to produce the red lake pigment. Natural ultramarine (i.e., lapis lazuli) glazed with a red lake will produce a more beautiful purple compared to azurite and a red lake. This is not the only entry in which azur d’esmail points tentatively toward lapis. Consider “Azure” on fol. 93v:

Azur d’esmail always wants to be cleaned, because the filth that can be perceived in the wash water makes it die. One needs to layer it two times, & the first very thick, moving the paintbrush by layering it first lengthwise then across. It is better used on canvas, where it is imbibed immediately, than on wood. Varnish returns it to its vigor, because being imbibed, it becomes dark. To try it, painters bring their palette to the grocers, & temper & alloy it with a little white ground in oil, for in this way, the beautiful shows its turquine vivacity, but the bad is lavender grey. The most delicate is the best for working. It is thinned working by washing it.

The first sentence seems to indicate lapis lazuli, since the crushed rock, when washed, produces particularly dirty water (Fig. 5, Fig. 6). The difference between the dry pigment and pigment bound in oil is that the latter immediately becomes dark blue. However, the word “turquine” is confusing (as it typically has more to do with azurite), but again a lavender gray color is consistent with a lower quality of lapis lazuli.

Despite these suggestions that azur d’esmail might refer to lapis, there is no cause to generalize. In “Painter” on fol. 56v, the author-practitioner writes,

To use azur d’esmail in oil, one needs to choose the most delicate. And to render it subtle, one ought not to grind it, for this makes it whiten. But one needs to wash it, & the coarsest going to the bottom, choose the one that is above in the water or, by inclination, pour out the cloudy water, then gather the azure.

It is truly difficult to know which blue is being referred to here. When either azurite or lapis lazuli are ground, they will always appear lighter (Fig. 7, Fig. 8).

The difference is greater with azurite. The word “delicate,” as I suggest above, most probably refers to pigment that has already been prepared. If water is added to either color when the rock has been ground, one will produce a dirty blue-gray color (Fig. 9, Fig. 10, Fig. 11, Vid. 1). If the author is talking about a prepared pigment, then I would say that azurite is meant here, since further levigation, even if it is just using water, may produce the range of blue that is suggested. If, however, lapis lazuli is meant, and the extraction of lazurite from the lapis followed the “pastille” method, most famously described by Cennino Cennini, then the extraction process would already produce a variety of blues to blue-grays and the author-practitioner’s suggestion would have no validity.11

There are various blues to disentangle on fol. 59r, in “Paintbrushes,” where the author-practitioner notes,

every kind of azure is difficult. And to make beautiful azure, one needs to layer it not with large strokes of the paintbrush but with small strokes of the point. Not only ash & azur d’Acre but mainly azur d’esmail which one needs to choose to be very delicate, for it otherwise you will not v be able to work with it except with much work, and even then you need to allay it with turpentine to give it body, and mix it with a little lead white. Any azure wants neither to be ground nor washed for it loses color & becomes pale. But putting it powdered on your palette, you will thin it little by little either with walnut oil or turpentine oil.

Again the differences of blue are difficult to discern. “Azure” here seems to refer to azurite since one needs to layer it from small to large particles to achieve the deepest blue. However, it appears that azur d’esmail needs to be “delicate,” i.e., prepared. It makes no sense, then, to say that none of the blue pigments should be washed or ground because they become pale and loose color. Even with the purest crystals, preparation of the pigment is necessary. With any of the mineral blue pigments, it was common knowledge that adding a little lead white would reduce any discoloration in walnut oil. This part of the text is not from first-hand knowledge due to some basic confusion of pigment preparation and binding mediums.

There is more to suggest that the author-practitioner’s knowledge of blues is indirect in “Azure” on fol. 58v:

The azure requires a little fatty oil because it has no body.

Azur d’esmail hates more than any other to be ground, especially with water, for it dies & loses all its color. However, because it cannot be worked if it is coarse, grind not with water but with oil & grind it thickly, and in this way it will not die as much.

Always choose the most delicate one.

This sounds like second-hand information and it is really unclear whether azurite or lapis lazuli is meant, though here too I take “delicate” to mean prepared pigment. Both azurite and lapis are the most stable in a fir-balsam resin fused 50-50 with spirits of turpentine, tempered with a few drops of walnut oil. When a few drops of oil are mulled into these pigments in resin, they suddenly have more body.12 Too much oil and they discolor quite badly within a few years. The comment about grinding in water does not make much sense. If lapis lazuli raw stone is ground in water it appears to become gray, but this is due to release of impurities. Large particle azurite is difficult to work with, though this produces the deepest blue, but mulling it in oil makes it almost impossible to work with and the discoloration will be dreadful within two years.

I am not confident that there is a neat resolution for the identity of azur d’esmail. The properties that the author-practitioner describes seem to refer plausibly sometimes to one pigment, sometimes to another, such as in the entry “Azur d’esmail in oil” on fol. 61v, where it may be azurite, and in “Azure” on fol. 59r, where it could be lapis lazuli. This ambiguity is compounded by the author-practitioner’s rather vague and sometimes confusing descriptions, which suggest that they do not derive from first-hand experience. If, in fact, he is collecting the practices and observations of various painters, then this could help explain the seeming inconsistencies in the meaning of azur d’esmail.

Imprecise Imprimatura

The author-practitioner’s often imprecise directions for the preparation of panels and canvases once again suggests that his knowledge of painters’ practice is not first-hand. In some cases, his descriptions are clear and correct, such as in “First whitening of a panel” on fol. 60r, where he writes,

One layers two or three times with chalk tempered with distemper glue on the panel, not with a paintbrush but with a brush in such a way as if you wanted to pounce, & leave to dry. And reiterate up to two or three times, then even out the last ground well with a knife, then give a coat of glue on top, upon which you will next be able to make your imprimatura and then paint. But make sure that your first white is not too thick for it would break easily. Flemish painters have such panels made by the dozen.

This advice is more or less the correct method of applying traditional gesso to a panel followed by the first imprimatura with a coat of (hide) glue. Also the gesso should become slightly thinner with each layer so as not to crack due to the tension between layers.

Elsewhere, however, the author-practitioner is less precise with his use of “imprimatura” (impression). Consider his words in “Painter” on fol. 56v:

L One always needs to apply imprimatura don wood to paint there in oil in order to fill the holes & unevenness, and make imprimatura with some stil de grain yellow & ceruse tempered in oil, then soften with a feather, which flattens better than a paintbrush. Or when the imprimatura is dry, scrape strongly with a knife.

There seems to be some confusion of terminology here regarding “imprimatura.” First of all, one needs a gesso ground of some description to paint on wood. There are numerous recipes for either chalk or gypsum gesso grounds. This then needs to be sealed either with a hide glue, if any tempera is to be used in the first layer, followed by a dilute fir-balsam resin with a little oil for any further paint layers in oils and/or resins. The particular imprimatura the author-practitioner describes here would be perfect for oil painting and, presumably since the ceruse is tempered in oil (preferably walnut), once dry, the slightly off-white could be a pleasant surface on which to work. It is important to have as little oil as possible in such an imprimatura to avoid yellowing. I would suggest here that ceruse refers to lead white, since cerussite would be too expensive and it yellows in walnut oil.13

The author-practitioner’s vagueness about the kind of imprimatura he intends raises some issues for interpretation. In “Drawing” on fol. 62v, he notes that “when the imprimatura has been long done, it becomes greasy. One needs to rub it with ash & water.” This is a correct observation, but again it depends on the imprimatura. If it is a glue-size, it will have become completely water-proof after about a month and may be difficult to do the initial preparatory drawing. Ambiguity about the kind of imprimatura continues, in “Semi-lively colors” on fol. 65v:

When colors aabsorb into the canvas, it means that it has been given imprimatura only once, & for this reason they become matte rather than being shiny. But this is the best, as long as you re-coat it twice, for in this manner, the colors, having more body, do not die & are all the more beautiful for it, especially azure, lake, & those do not have body. But those who want to rush their painting apply imprimatura twice on the first go, to be done with it. Thus the colors do not absorb, also they will not last as long. The second layer of these does not absorb & remains shiny.

There is an important point here, but the author-practitioner is not specific. The imprimatura will vary according to the binding medium of the first paint layer or layers. Before working with resin-oil, the painting needs to be sealed again, with a diluted fir-balsam resin with a drop of oil and allowed to dry for a couple of days. In this way the following paint layers will not sink in and become dull.

Loose Lead Whites

In a handful of entries, the author-practitioner discusses various white pigments, most often “lead white” (blanc de plomb) and “ceruse” (ceruse). While “ceruse” was used in the seventeenth century to refer to “any white pigment made from tin or lead plates,”14 the author-practitioner must have had a different meaning in mind; in “Painter,” on fol. 57r, he notes that “lead white [blanc de plomb] is made with plate lead, beaten thin & put under the dung heap.” Earlier in the same entry (fol. 56v), he contrasts the two pigments:

Chalk has no body in oil. Ceruse is appropriate. But lead white more excellent. Ceruse is the whitest, when ground first in water, the lead white grayish. But the white it takes on its perfect whiteness in oil.

This comment does not come from the author-practitioner’s personal experience and is somewhat contradictory. It would appear that “ceruse” here refers to the mineral cerussite (Fig. 12, Fig. 13), and a comparison is made with manufactured lead white. However, in period texts, sometimes “ceruse” does refer to a Venetian lead white, which was often regarded as the most brilliant lead white available. However, since the most brilliant white identified in Titian’s paintings was cerussite, I think it is reasonable to assume that we are comparing cerussite with lead white.15 In addition, when preparing either cerussite or lead white, both are ground as finely as possible and washed. There are many methods for levigation, and by producing a froth with a little hide glue or gum or casein in water, the finest particles will float and can be poured off (Fig. 14). This will ensure the best and most brilliant white. These whites are the most stable in walnut oil and fir-balsam resin binders. The mineral cerussite, when used on its own, must be bound in a fir-balsam resin/spirit of turpentine binder with eye-drops of oil. Bound in walnut oil alone, it will yellow within one to two years.

Similarly on fol. 10v, in the entry “For whitening ceruse,” it is difficult to know whether a manufactured lead white or the mineral cerussite is meant. However, the mineral cerussite when finely ground does produce a white that is more brilliant (Fig. 15) than lead white produced by corroding strips of lead to produce “flake white” (Fig. 16). As noted above, Titian is an example for his brilliant white from cerussite, which he most probably procured from his native Dolomites in north Italy.

In other instances, the author-practitioner calls for “lead white,” such as in “Painter” on fol. 57v, where he notes that “the Flemish do not use any whites for flesh colors in oil other than lead white because the ceruse turns yellow. 4- or 5-year-old walnut oil which is clear is the best color, it keeps off dust.” Here, however, we encounter the confusion of terminology for white pigments. The “ceruse” in this case may refer not to the lead white manufactured from plates but to cerussite, as this comment is correct insofar as cerussite bound in just oil alone, even walnut oil, turns yellow within one to two years. There seems to be less confusion in “Dessicative,” on fol. 59v, where we read, “lead white & massicot are the most desiccative, nevertheless they need a good two days. If you want to prove whether an oil is desiccative, temper lead white with it, & if it produces a crust in a day soon it means that it will dessicate.” While I am not too sure about the use of “dessicate” here (is the oxidation of the oil meant?), lead white in any oil indeed speeds up the paint layer drying.

Conclusion

After surveying the entries devoted to painting and varnishes, I am left with the overwhelming impression that the author-practitioner was not a painter. He has compiled a variety of “instructions,” some of which may have come from his own experience, but due to the lack of details when dealing with some pigments, it becomes clear that the experience of others is being recorded. Some entries even suggest this by their more distant and vaguely descriptive phrasing, such as “Oil” on fol. 60r or “Faces in distemper” on fol. 93v.16 But in many cases, my years of experience as an artist who works with natural pigments suggest that the author-practitioner did not try out many of the painting recipes with his own hands. If, for instance, he had actually tried to make lead white “with plate lead, beaten thin & put under the dung heap,” as I have done, he would have known that the process is not that simple, and he would have provided additional details. In any case, it is not a process I would recommend especially in the city, or for one with a sensitive nose! Finally, with regard to writing down recipes as clearly and concisely as possible, I receive emails from artists worldwide with questions about a variety of processes in my publications. Fortunately, I am still around to answer their questions whereas with most texts and manuscripts we do not have that luxury.
