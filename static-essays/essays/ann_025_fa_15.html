Painting in Oil on Taffeta Without the Oil Running

By , DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/y0cv-7p82
← Back to Essays

Abstract

A recipe on fol. 10v, “For painting in oil on taffeta without the oil running,” advises using batture, a vaguely described priming material, to mitigate the halo-like spread of oil from its pigment when applying oil paint to taffeta. This essay investigates the efficacy of batture, a mixture of strong glue, water, and honey, as well as the role of its various components. Artisanal recipes and our reconstruction experiments suggest that the honey functions to “soften” (adoulcir) the taffeta, that is, allow the primed taffeta to dry flat rather than crease and curl under the effect of the strong glue in the batture. This recipe belongs among those entries in Ms. Fr. 640 that attest to the author-practitioner’s interest in ornamenting fabric and in the production of ephemeral works meant for short-lived, all-weather use, such as banners and pennants.

BnF Ms. Fr. 640 contains two recipes for painting with oil on taffeta (fols. 10v and 42v).1 Considering the scarcity of recipes related to fabric in the manuscript, the recurrence of this subject seems noteworthy. These recipes reveal not only diverse ways to prepare fabric for paint but also suggest a demand for taffeta treated in such a way. Fol. 10v contains the longer of the two recipes and provides more details, potentially suggesting that the author-practitioner had a greater practical engagement with this recipe. The author-practitioner’s (later?) addition to the title “For painting in oil on taffeta” of the words “without the oil running” in a smaller hand might also indicate this.

The manuscript contains few other recipes related to fabrics. In addition to the shorter recipe for painting in oil on taffeta, fol. 42v also contains a recipe entitled “Pounced design for scratching satin;” fol. 59v has one for painting “folds in clothing;” and fol. 15r includes a recipe for “damasked cloth.” While the recipe on fol. 59v relates to imitating fabric rather than working with fabric, the recipes for scratching satin and for coloring cloth in the fashion of damask bear some similarities to the taffeta recipes. All of these involve putting some sort of design on fabric. This suggests that the painting on taffeta recipes might have been techniques for ornamenting fabric, perhaps in place of embroidery.2 This is suggested by the recipe on fol. 67r, which to some degree explains the interest in taffeta:

The Germans make boxes covered with painted paper & varnish it with glair of egg mixed with gum & a little oil, not of spike lavender, but another fragrant one that resembles olive oil. Every work done with glair supports oil. It is with this that painters trick the poor peasants, painting their bands of taffeta with this glair, to be done sooner. But the first rain carries away everything.

Here the author-practitioner mentions that taffeta was used to make something similar to a banner for peasants, and he comments on the inferior quality of egg whites (glaire doeuf) for underlayers. Similar information on the use of taffeta for banners, but with an underlayer calling for animal glue, starch, and honey, appears in the De Mayerne manuscript, compiled by Swiss physician Sir Théodore Turquet de Mayerne (1573–1655) in the seventeenth century:

You need to prepare the cloth with fish or leather scrap glue, mixed with a bit of starch for strengthening. Heat it with a little honey and apply this mixture to your fabric. Let it dry and then coat it with oil with lead monoxide. […] When I wondered whether the honey would dry, he answered yes, because painters who make flags and emblems for the purposes of war use it as well out of fear that the taffeta, coated with glue to avoid the pulling of the colors applied with oil paint, will become brittle and show cracks and creases.3

According to these two recipes, pretreated taffeta was commonly used by peasants and soldiers for making banners painted with oil paint, but doubt remains as to what type of taffeta was used and whether it is the same as that used for clothing. Notably, the recipe in the De Mayerne collection is informed by practice, indicated especially by the many detailed marginal notes.4

The use of taffeta for banners can also be found in Il libro dell’arte of Florentine craftsman Cennino Cennini (ca. 1360–before 1427), who, in the section “Work on Cloth: Painting and Gilding,” mentions that “sometimes these banners, which are made for churches, get carried outdoors in the rain; and therefore you must take care to get a good clear varnish, and when you varnish the painting, varnish these diadems and gold grounds a little, too.”5 Elsewhere in the text, Cennini refers to painted silk (though not taffeta specifically) and how banners made of it are to be varnished.6 Banners for military, religious, and other short term usage, including in triumphal entries and festivals, were made of materials such as paper and fabric that were not meant to last.7 Both banners and depictions of their use survive (Fig. 1, Fig. 2).

Although intentionally ephemeral, such works were nonetheless destined for outdoor use and thus needed to be waterproof. The ability to withstand water was clearly a concern for the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640: the recipe on fol. 67r notes that the layer of egg white on the taffeta will be ruined by first rain. Similarly, a recipe on fol. 32r mentions a layer of batture, said to be damaged in the rain:

Common painters & scribes make batture, that is joiner’s glue tempered with water on the fire, moderately clear, mixed with very little honey, that is to say a few drops to make it stick. And with it they form letters, or that which they want to gild, with a paintbrush, and immediately after layer the gold, but they never do their work quite neatly, and if there is a lot of honey it dries only with great difficulty. This layer is undone in the rain.

Outside of Ms. Fr. 640, there is little evidence for techniques to paint on taffeta, with one notable exception: an entry entitled “For painting in oil on taffeta” (Pour peindre à huile sur le taffetas) in Élémens de peinture pratique. This volume, authored by Roger de Piles (1635–1709) and originally published in 1684, was augmented and reissued in 1766 by Charles-Antoine Jombert (1712–1784). It is Jombert’s edition that that includes the recipe for oil on taffeta:8

One must first prepare the taffeta with a gum, of which here is the composition. Take fish glue as large as a bean, cut it in little pieces, & soak it for twelve hours in a glass of water. After, melt it on the fire just until boiling, then drain it and let it get cold again. When one wants to use it, one must heat it & having stretched your taffeta well, apply it well-heated there with a sponge, as evenly as possible. The taffeta being dry, you can lay your colors there, without worrying that they will soak in or that they will spread more than they should.9

While this recipe contains fewer components than that on fol. 10v of Ms. Fr. 640, the general procedure (i.e., applying glue on taffeta) is the same. Most interesting in Jombert’s edition is the description of what this glue layer prevents. While fol. 10v mentions oil running, Jombert’s recipe says it prevents colors from soaking in or spreading more than they should. This particular word choice better reflects the outcome of our reconstruction, as will be discussed below.

Materials and Methods

While a relatively simple recipe, many of the components on fol. 10v are vaguely described. Chief among these vague materials is batture, which the Making and Knowing Project has left untranslated. Although the author-practitioner defines batture as “strong glue [colle forte] soaked for 24 hours in water then gently brought to boil so that it is hardly strong,”10 this explanation raises further questions. Randle Cotgrave’s French-English Dictionarie (1611) translates colle forte as “ordinarie white glue; as in Colle de cerf,” and in turn translates colle de cerf as “white, and cleere-coloured glue; made of the skinnes of all kind of foure-footed beastes.”11 This suggests that the author-practitioner might have used an animal-skin glue for the batture. Another recipe in the manuscript, “For layering gold in distemper” on fol. 32r, also calls for batture. In this recipe, batture is described as “joiner’s glue tempered with water on the fire, moderately clear, mixed with very little honey, that is to say a few drops to make it stick.” A search through the manuscript for “joiners” and “joiner’s glue” reveals a relevant recipe on fol. 7r, “Fish glue or isinglass and mouth glue.”12 On fish glue the author-practitioner writes,

It is made from codfish skin, boiled rather than being salted. Joiners glue their masterpieces with it and guitar maker[illegible] use it for delicate works. It wants to be strongly beaten, then soaked gently in barely boiling water.

And he gives alternatives to using water in a marginal note on fol. 7r, writing, “One beats it and soaks it in white wine for one night, then one melts it on a slow fire. Others soak it in eau-de-vie.” Interestingly, the author added “and mouth glue” in a smaller hand to the title “Fish glue or isinglass,” suggesting that he might have added the short paragraph on mouth glue later, thus grouping together different glue recipes that he could have tried himself or observed others making at different times.13

The Technical Art History students at the University of Amsterdam reconstructed a fish glue made from sturgeon stomach, though they did not pursue the experiment using a batture made from this. In their experiments, skin glue and parchment glue provided the best adhesive results. Accordingly, we tried two different procedures for our reconstruction of batture, one using parchment glue—although fish glue might have been the more appropriate choice—and another using rabbit skin glue, relying on Cotgrave’s definition of colle forte as glue from the skins of four-footed animals.

The place of honey in the batture was clarified further by comparing the recipe on fol. 10v, in which a little honey is used to “soften” (adoulcir) the batture, with the more detailed recipe on fol. 32r, which specifies that only a few drops should be used in order to thicken the mixture, for too much honey would make it hard to dry. This provided information about the quantity and function of the honey.14

Fol. 10v specifies “taffeta” as the material to be employed, a term that has denoted a variety of fabrics at different times. The Dictionnaire du Moyen Français describes it as a thin, smooth, and shiny silk fabric (tissu de soie, mince, uni et brillant).15 The Oxford English Dictionary (OED) defines it as “in early times apparently a plain-wove glossy silk (of any colour). [...] In the sixteenth century mention is also made of ‘linen taffety.’”16 Etymologically, the OED mentions the Persian word tāftah, which means silken cloth or linen clothing. The key words “silk” and “plain weave” appear in almost every dictionary, although opinions seem to differ on whether it is a less or more luxurious and costly fabric.17 Silk used as a substitute for linen canvas as a support for painting also appears in Mary Merrifield’s compilation of recipes.18

Alum water is the last ingredient mentioned in the recipe on fol. 10v, which gives it as an alternative to batture for preventing oil paint from running on taffeta. This is one of two ingredients found in the other “oil on taffeta” recipe on fol. 42v, which calls for honey, alum water, and starch water.19

We made rabbit skin glue and parchment glue (the latter following the recipe for mouth glue on fol. 7r in Ms. Fr. 640) as the basis for two trials of batture. The rabbit skin glue was viscous, while the parchment glue remained liquid, which may have affected the efficacy of the batture. We separated our taffeta (plain woven silk) into four squares for several trials in order to isolate certain variables. We were curious about the function of batture, particularly the efficacy of a diluted strong glue (colle forte). Thus we made a batture by diluting our “strong glues” in water as described on fol. 10v (using a trial both with the rabbit skin glue and with the parchment glue), and for comparison we also applied undiluted strong glues. To determine the effect of the honey, we added different quantities to determine whether it worked to soften the fabric or to change the drying speed.20

Observations and Conclusions

After applying the batture and the undiluted rabbit skin glue, both mixed with different amounts of honey, and allowing the layers to dry on the taffeta, we painted a line vertically and diagonally on each square. We made oil paints of linseed oil (both with cold pressed and with stand oil) and madder lake pigment.21 As we painted the lines, we noticed that the oil spread further beyond the edges of the lines on some test squares, namely those which were coated with batture, leaving a dark shadow of oil around the painted line (Fig. 3). These oil “haloes” did not appear on other squares that we prepared with undiluted rabbit skin glue (Fig. 4). After a moment, the oil shadows became increasingly obvious, and a week later the oil shadows appeared on all squares although to varying degrees. The control taffeta (with no priming layer applied before the oil paint) had large oil shadows (Fig. 5). However, these shadows were obvious only when examined carefully. Moreover, although the oil spread further over time, it also became less obvious (Fig. 6). If the taffeta had been used as a banner, as we assume, the spreading oil might not have been visible from a distance.22 Additionally, the effects of the spreading oil might be different if it were interacting with more oil paints and different pigments (e.g., in a painted scene). Though this kind of interaction was not observable from our experiments, the Technical Art History students at the University of Amsterdam23 did a trial with a more detailed scene painted in different oil colors and covering a larger area of taffeta that demonstrated the same tendency of the oil to spread. This spreading does not appear to have affected the other details in the painted image (Fig. 7). They also applied gold to the cloth, following the suggestion of the recipe on fol. 10v that the batture can alternatively serve as a base for a gold layer. Their results show that the gilding appears visually unaffected by the spread of the oil from the parts painted in oil.

On the taffeta with the undiluted rabbit skin glue layer, the shadows of the oil “running” did not appear immediately but only after about two weeks. This might suggest that the preparation of textiles for oil painting with glue layers could have been intended for short-term use.

Our experiments with rabbit skin glue batture (i.e., rabbit skin glue diluted in water) were less successful than undiluted rabbit skin glue, as shadows appeared within an hour on the former. Although our batture was more effective in preventing the oil from running than was the control taffeta with no priming layer whatsoever beneath the oil paint, it did not significantly alter the spreading of the oil over time. A possible explanation may be that we diluted our glue with too much water. However, we were able to determine that the stronger the glue (i.e., the less diluted), the more stable the oil paint (i.e., the less it spread). This observation was supported by our experiments with parchment glue. The parchment glue that we made was much less strong than the strong glue (colle forte) made from animal skins: it became very dilute in the water, was hardly viscous, and was not adhesive. The oil spread further on the taffeta on which it was applied (Fig. 8).

Why then did the author-practitioner experiment with a batture of diluted glue if undiluted, strong glue apparently works best for the application of oil paint on taffeta “without the oil running”? Our experiments with undiluted rabbit skin glue provide a possible answer: the stronger the glue, the more textured and creased the taffeta becomes, and its edges have a tendency to curl (Fig. 9). This suggests that the reason for diluting the glue might be to soften the rigidity of the fabric, with the honey perhaps increasing the flexibility further.24 This hypothesis appears to be corroborated by a detail in the above-cited recipe from the De Mayerne manuscript, which uses honey to decrease the wrinkles in the taffeta.

In the experiments with rabbit skin glue, we made trial groups to compare the effects of adding 1 ml versus 2 ml of honey. As mentioned in the De Mayerne recipe, adding (some) honey did not seem to prolong the drying time significantly. Indeed, both trial groups dried quickly (i.e., within half an hour), and the one with 2 ml honey even dried several minutes sooner. However, our results showed no obvious difference in the effect of 1 ml versus 2 ml of honey to soften (adoulcir) the taffeta. Yet, we did observe another difference when we increased the amount of honey. The taffeta square primed with our parchment glue batture, which included 5 ml of honey, remained more even, lying flush with the surface (Fig. 10). It must be noted, however, that the parchment glue was so liquid that it flattened the taffeta significantly, unlike the rabbit skin glue trials, which dried with noticeable ridges.

We succeeded with our trials in isolating and understanding the effects of the different ingredients in the recipe “for painting oil on taffeta without the oil running.” Our results, and those from Professor Bol’s Technical Art History students at the University of Amsterdam, suggest that the author-practitioner was interested in ephemeral painted cloths, such as decorated banners, produced for public or military (outdoor) events, that were probably meant for short-term use and likely to be seen from afar rather than close up.

Our experiments show that the batture layer containing glue is an important agent in preventing oil from spreading beyond the confines of the pigmented areas on painted taffeta. One effect of glue, however, is to pull and crease the fabric upon drying. To mitigate this, honey is added. The taffeta recipes in Ms. Fr. 640 seem to suggest that the author-practitioner was experimenting to achieve an optimal visual effect, one that best mitigated the effects of oil “running” and of the creases and ridges that appeared when a glue-based priming was applied.
