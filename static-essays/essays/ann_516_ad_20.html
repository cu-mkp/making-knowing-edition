“Perspective is Very Difficult”: Practical and Impractical Tools for the Painter

By , , DOI: https://doi.org/10.7916/091b-1c16
← Back to Essays

Abstract

The entry “Perspective” on fol. 62v describes and depicts practical tools to help an artist construct the underdrawing for an oil painting according to the rules of linear perspective, including a ruler for laying down orthogonals that converge at a single vanishing point. The author-practitioner describes two different designs for this tool and even provides an illustration of one of them. However, this drawing, when reconstructed, yields a sub-optimal result. It is unclear whether the author-practitioner had first-hand knowledge of such a tool, invented it as he wrote without thinking through the design, or exaggerated certain features in his drawing that, when reproduced as depicted, made the tool problematic to use.

The author-practitioner’s interest in linear perspective, or the system of creating illusionistic renderings of spatial depth on a planar surface such as a painted panel, was practical in orientation rather than theoretical—much like his interest in the art of painting in general.1 Linear perspective was supposedly discovered by Florentine sculptor and architect Filippo Brunelleschi (ca. 1410–1425) and first described by Leon Battista Alberti in De pictura (ca. 1435).2 By the time the author-practitioner was writing in the late sixteenth century, numerous treatises on perspective were in circulation, including, to cite but a few, those by artists Piero della Francesca (ca. 1470), Albrecht Dürer (1525), and Jean Cousin the Elder (1560)—father of the Jean Cousin mentioned on fol. 1r of Ms. Fr. 640.3 An entry on fol. 62r, entitled “Perspectives,” notes a few practical issues in producing a painting with an illusion of depth. A further entry on the subject, this one entitled “Perspective” (fol. 62v), describes workshop tools for constructing a perspectival drawing or underdrawing, and it also reflects on the difficulties of painting in perspective. This second entry is the focus of this essay. The author-practitioner’s ambiguous phrasing and his illustration of an apparently unworkable “ruler” for drawing in perspective raise questions about how well acquainted he was with the painter’s craft.

The Entry

“Perspective” (fol. 62v) is part of a cluster of entries between fols. 56r and 67r dedicated to the art of painting. These entries address various aspects of the craft, including the preparation of panels and pigments, the application of paint for the depiction of flesh and drapery, the use of varnish, and the practical training of apprentices. Though there seems to be little logic to the organization of these entries, and we cannot discern among this cluster the attempt to offer a treatise on painting per se, it is clear that “Perspective” is part of the author-practitioner’s information gathering on the painter’s art. In fact, the description of tools for drawing seems to follow up on the ideas expressed in the preceding entry on fol. 62v, entitled “Drawing,” which notes, “to become an artist, one needs to draw by eye, without compass or ruler. Masters do not allow apprentices to do this.” This entry may have prompted the author-practitioner to explore the drafting tools that a young artist needed before he or she had learned to draw by eye.

In an entry dedicated to linear perspective, we might expect the author-practitioner to provide a means of setting down on a blank support a grid-structure for a brand new depiction (Fig. 1, Fig. 2, Fig. 3). However, the opening line of the entry seems to suggest that orthogonals are already in place: “to know the [vanishing] point, one needs to lay down a ruler over the lines, & at the intersection [desmenee] of these, the point will be.” If we take the author-practitioner’s phrasing at face value, he seems to offer a means to understand the perspectival construction beneath an extant drawing—or at least a means to gauge the accuracy of a preparatory sketch that could be further improved.

The author-practitioner goes on to describe two types of ruler that could be helpful in accommodating a picture to the exigencies of linear perspective. In each case, the tool is characterized by the ability to pivot a ruler around a fixed or hinged point set along the horizon line at the vanishing point. The first design (hereafter, “pivot ruler”) seems to involve piercing a hole through the end of a ruler so that it can be rotated around a pin set in a panel: “Some make a hole at the end of their rulers to fix the point by that hole & move the ruler about.” The second design (hereafter, “double ruler”), which he draws on fol. 62v (Fig. 4), involves two rulers—one hinged by its end with a screw to the center of another: “Others lay down a ruler across the panel, then on this laid-down ruler they set the tip of another ruler, which, attached to the former by means of a screw, moves about & reaches as far as necessary without losing the point.” While it is clear that the base ruler of this second tool should lie across the horizon line of the panel, a marginal note clarifies that the tool was only to be used at the design stage to construct the underdrawing of a panel: “To paint perspective in oil, you ought not to use a ruler, for you would smudge everything. But when layering your colors you need to keep to the & follow the lines of your first drawing.” This note expands on information given about the “first drawing” in the entry “Perspectives” on the previous page (fol. 62r): “Perspectives in oil take a long time because of several lines that need to be done with a ruler, and for this reason one usually does them in distemper.”

The entry on fol. 62v also makes provision for drawing reliable vertical lines—an essential part of architectural views especially (Fig. 5). Below the drawing of the ruler, the author-practitioner instructs to make a plumb line from a piece of lead, a thread, and a small hook, so that the line can be hung from the top of the panel. The author-practitioner even draws this tool in the margin (Fig. 6).

The author-practitioner appears to have made his drawing of the double ruler after he wrote the penultimate sentence of the “Perspective” entry. The final sentence seems to have been written in afterwards, as it is in a slightly more compressed script and is partially written over the drawing. Significantly, this sentence states that “the ruler and compass without the judgement of the eye cause errors. Perspective is very difficult.” As the rest of this essay will show, the double ruler is a problematic tool. Therefore, it seems likely that the author-practitioner wrote the description of the tool, drew what he had described, tried to make the device, discovered that it was problematic in design, and thus “warned” the reader about the need to exercise judgment when using such tools.

The Questions and Preliminary Research

Given our respective professional experiences in architecture (van Buren) and fine woodworking (Wang), we approached the entry with questions about the potential utility of the tools and their practical construction. Is the description and illustration of the double ruler based on observation? That is, did such a tool exist at the time? Is it possible to follow the instructions given in the entry to build this tool? And how well might this tool function in constructing a drawing in perspective? We also wondered about the role of the artist’s eye in drawing, guiding, correcting, or supplementing the points and lines determined by the tool.

Our research did not yield evidence that a tool like the author-practitioner’s double ruler was known or used. The logic behind the pivot ruler resembles, however distantly, the artisanal practice of snapping taut chalked or dyed lines against surfaces being prepared for painting, while the plumb line was a very commonly used tool among painters.4

The Double Ruler Reconstructed

In Spring 2017, Ariane van Buren constructed paper and very preliminary wooden prototypes of the author-practitioner’s double ruler. These gave a poor result (Fig. 7, Fig. 8, Fig. 9, Fig. 10, Fig. 11, Fig. 12). The receding lines did not converge in a single vanishing point because the rulers were wide and the screw, or pivot point, was located at the center of the width of each ruler (i.e., a half-inch from the ruler’s edge). The width of each of the rulers prevented the optimal functioning of the tool, and the greater that width, the greater the problem.

Successive trials moved the pin joining the two rulers closer and closer to the rulers’ edge. This allowed the orthogonals to come closer to converging at a single vanishing point, located at the center of the pin. It became clear that in order for the double ruler to function, the two components would have to be joined in a way that offset the pin. The final mock-up put the pivot at the actual edge of the rulers, which was only possible by adding a flap to each of the rulers that could then overlap. Our design took inspiration from images of early modern drawing instruments in the Museo Galileo, Florence, in the National Museum of American History, Washington D.C., and in the History of Science Museum, Oxford.5 With this final mock-up, a single vanishing point was neatly drawn. Nevertheless, this mock-up tool certainly did not match what the author-practitioner had depicted in the entry, although a generous reading of his description that ignores his drawing might result in a functional tool.

In sum, the double ruler tool does not seem to work as drawn. To obtain a consistent vanishing point, the sides of the rulers used to draw the horizon line and orthogonals cannot overlap as the mobile arm pivots. Rulers overlapping by half their width, as illustrated on fol. 62v, prevent the receding lines from reaching a single point. Significantly, this problem is equally relevant for the pivot ruler—the first design described by the author-practitioner in the “Perspective” entry.

In Fall 2017, Casa Wang reconstructed wooden prototypes for the double ruler depicted on fol. 62v and, by way of comparison, a nineteenth-century tool for making two-point perspective drawings, the centrolinead. For the double ruler, Wang constructed the base ruler as the author-practitioner depicted it, and he made three versions of the mobile arm, one following the drawing on fol. 62v and the other two experimenting with the placement of the hole in order to improve the design (Fig. 13). He gave the base ruler squiggly ends, as seen in the author-practitioner’s drawing, surmising that these ends may have been used to affix the base ruler to the panel or drawing using nails or pins, rather than being decorative alone.

Unsurprisingly, the reconstructed double ruler that followed the author-practitioner’s drawing yielded results no different than the paper prototypes; that is, the lines did not converge at a single vanishing point. By contrast, the lines drawn using each of Wang’s experimental arms were on track to converge successfully at a single vanishing point positioned beneath the screw, though the breadth of the base ruler impeded the final few millimeters of the convergence proper.

Wang’s reconstruction of the centrolinead (Fig. 14, Fig. 15) gave us a better sense of how artists, albeit later ones, might have used drawing aids to help construct more complex perspectival drawings. The utility of the centrolinead reinforced our sense that the author-practitioner had less first-hand experience in the arts of drawing and painting than in other areas of craft, such as fine metalwork.

“Perspective is very difficult.”

If the author-practitioner was trying to use the double ruler he described and depicted on fol. 62v to draw in perspective, then his comment that “perspective is very difficult” is understandable. As demonstrated even with our paper prototype, there are obvious impracticalities with the double ruler’s design. Yet there are good reasons to question the author-practitioner’s familiarity with the rules of one-point perspective. On fol. 62r, in the entry “Perspectives,” he writes: “One takes the points at one’s discretion. The main thing in this lies in having the knowledge of the point. One commonly makes two or three of them, sometimes five.” If by “the point” (poinct), he means the vanishing point, then this is a rather puzzling statement.6

Given the author-practitioner’s interest in virtuoso feats of craft, such as molding and casting extremely delicate works of nature like butterfly wings, and the contemporary vogue for virtuoso depictions of complex polygons in perspective, such as those seen in Wenzel Jamnitzer’s 1568 Perspectiva Corporum Regularium (Fig. 16), it is all the more surprising that his entry on perspective is so cursory. Perhaps his drawing of the double ruler reflects an experimental idea he had for a useful tool—something he intended to expand upon later, much like his quick sketch of the mechanism for a spinet that plays by itself on fol. 104v. Perhaps it was a drawing akin to his illustration of a perpetual fountain on fol. 80r, which he likely did not realize was an impossibility.7 Or, perhaps the drawing exaggerates certain features of the double ruler, namely the width of the two arms, for the sake of legibility, which effectively renders the drawing impractical as a guide for construction. When the entry “Perspective” (fol. 62v) is read in conjunction with “Perspectives” on the previous page, which seems to suggest that up to five vanishing points were customarily placed within a composition, we can rightfully question whether the author-practitioner aimed to convey the process of creating works using one-point perspective or something else entirely. Whatever the case may be, the author-practitioner’s final words in the “Perspective” entry ring true: “The ruler and compass without the judgement of the eye cause errors. Perspective is very difficult.”
