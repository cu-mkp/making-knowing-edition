Glassworking in Ms. Fr. 640

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/4b00-br57
← Back to Essays

Abstract

The entry “For cages” on fol. 6v describes a specialized glassworking process, the manipulation of glass canes for lampworking and beadmaking. Third person description suggests that the author-practitioner observed glassworkers lengthening hollow canes, and his familiarity with particular tools, materials, and processes used in lampworking and beadmaking support this hypothesis. While functional cages for keeping animals are discussed elsewhere in the manuscript, “For cages” is situated among other entries concerning the embellishment of readymade objects and suggests the author-practitioner’s interest in applying the decorative techniques of lampworkers. My reconstruction suggests ways to fuse and bend wire and glass that could be learned by observation or developed experimentally by someone without extensive training. The author-practitioner’s attention to various glassworking industries and their specialized products in Ms. Fr. 640 suggests that he used technical knowledge gathered from encounters with other artisans to inform and augment his own wide-ranging artisanal activities.

BnF Ms. Fr. 640 contains several entries that use or refer to vitreous materials. There are seven related to reverse painting on glass,1 six for pierrerie that describe melting glass from raw materials,2 and seven that use traditional vitreous enamel techniques.3 These entries are spread out across the manuscript: the first half of the manuscript includes seven references to raw materials or tools used by glassmakers; a cluster of three entries between fols. 107r and 121r discusses those used by glassmakers; and a set of four entries between fols. 20r and 37v explicitly refer to glassworkers by profession and describe their methods of working with the material.4 Two entries (fols. 147v and 154v) even use material analogies to describe a metallic alloy (latten) as making or leaving a tail “like melted glass.” Though it does not mention glass or glassmakers directly, the entry “For cages” on fol. 6v describes a specialized glassworking process: the manipulation of glass canes for lampworking and beadmaking.5 The entry includes instructions for working with glass on a small enough scale to be reproducible in the Making and Knowing Laboratory. The references to glass in the manuscript together with my research and reconstruction suggest that the author-practitioner used technical knowledge gathered from encounters with other artisans to inform his experimentation in the workshop.

The manuscript’s descriptive entries indicate that the author-practitioner had a ready knowledge of both the raw materials used by glassmakers and the techniques used to work them into functional objects. Through a consideration of the glassmaking profession in general and a reconstruction of the entry “For cages” in particular, this essay situates the author-practitioner’s diverse activities within the broader context of artisanal practice in France during the sixteenth century. The author-practitioner’s use of third person plural when describing some glassworking techniques indicates that he likely observed glassmakers at work in a glasshouse. Furthermore, his technical knowledge of the equipment and materials used in lampworking and beadmaking suggests some connection with these industries. The content and context of the entry “For cages” within the manuscript suggests that he used materials, processes, and techniques that he learned from these artisans in his own work.

In researching and reconstructing the entry on fol. 6v, I explore the extent of the author-practitioner’s knowledge and understanding of artisanal techniques that he has ascribed to particular professions, perhaps outside his own. I focus on the way the entries about glassmaking speak to the extent of his expertise in professional glassworking practices, questioning why he may have collected these descriptions, what it meant for him to source raw materials from other artisans, and what kinds of knowledge-sharing between artisans these interactions may have prompted. What did the author-practitioner know about glass, and how was this knowledge utilized in Ms. Fr. 640? Ultimately, I conclude that the author-practitioner applied knowledge of materials, tools, and processes gained from observing other artisans to his own activities in the workshop.6

Glass in Ms. Fr. 640

A survey of mentions of glass in Ms. Fr. 640 reveals that the author-practitioner had a high degree of familiarity with glass, including the tools, raw ingredients, and processes used by glassmakers and workers, which indicates that he visited or had access to glass workers or workshops.7 To assess the impact that this access might have had on his experimentation, I limit my analysis to instances in which the author-practitioner demonstrates knowledge of specialized glassworking and making techniques, tools and equipment, or raw materials; applies the material in recipes not directly related to glassmaking (i.e., in the entries for painting on glass); or evinces knowledge of the behavior of the material when molten.8

The multiple mentions of tools and materials used by glassmakers in the manuscript indicate that the author-practitioner was at least moderately familiar with the activities and techniques of glass workshops. In several disparate entries concerning other processes, he specifies the use of “glassworkers’ solders” (fol. 13r), “varnish & salt & other drugs that the glassworkers put on” (fol. 20r), “glassworkers’ black” (fol. 41v), “crucibles… for glassmakers” (fol. 47v), “solder that glassworkers use” (fol. 49r), glass salts (sel de verre) (fol. 85r), a “glassmaker’s fournaise” (fols. 107r and 120r), and “a sand, white, like salt, and lean, that glassmakers & potters use” (fol. 89v). These types of tools and materials are also common to goldsmithing and metalworking workshops, and they appear elsewhere in the manuscript. However, the deliberate designation of some tools and materials in the manuscript as those of the glassmakers may suggest there were specialized versions of more common tools that were appropriate for particular uses.9 In addition, on fols. 20r, 32v, and 37v, the author-practitioner directly discusses the raw materials and compositions of glasses that come from different regions in Europe. This kind of detailed and specialized knowledge points to his familiarity with the profession or encounters with glass professionals.

The author-practitioner also had enough familiarity with the behavior and qualities of vitreous material to use this knowledge to describe the action of other substances. Discussing latten, he states that “it always makes a tail, like melted glass” (fol. 147v) “it always leaves a tail like melted glass” (fol. 154v), and “it always leaves some type of tail, like glass, when you cast it” (fol. 136v). Writing of lead, he declares that “it withstands as many castings as you wish, for it is as hard as glass” (fol. 85r). This use of glass as a material reference for the behavior of certain metals shows that he had at least some experience of melting, casting, and handling glass.

The detailed discussion of the techniques on fols. 20r, 32v, and 37v also indicates some knowledge of various glassworking professions and their specialized products. He describes in detail the glassblowing processes of making sheet glass, both the plate and crown glass methods used in Lorraine, Flanders, and Rouen. He also describes the red sheets of glass that German glassmakers produced by painting a mixture of pewter, lead, and iron dust onto both sides of a glass pane. On fol. 37v he details the materials used by “those who discovered the invention of working in small works of soft enamels,” which could indicate either lampworkers or vitreous enamelers proper. Such descriptions are mostly in the third person and clearly designate the activities in question as those of other practitioners employed in workshops outside the author-practitioner’s immediate environment. This suggests that, at the very least, he discussed glass work with practitioners.

It is likely that the author-practitioner visited glass workshops or retailers to purchase glass for his own use, and he may have made and recorded some of his observations about glassworking processes on or after such visits. There are fifteen entries in which the author-practitioner employs readymade glass as a raw material to enhance or improve other processes. The author practitioner would have needed to purchase panes of glass for his entry concerning “tracing some history on glass” (fol. 39v).10 The entry “Glassworker” on fol. 20r refers directly to the cost, sale, and observations about the quality of both Lorraine glass and plate glass from France. The former, he notes, is “sold by the bundle & each bundle is composed of three tablets. The bundle costs usually x sous,” while the latter is “is sold by paniers. Every panier contains 24 plates that are commonly sold at x or twelve lb.” Such information suggests firsthand knowledge of the glass business.

Finally, it is likely that the author-practitioner recorded his observations about glass with an eye to improving his own work. Several of the entries involving glass include brief recommendations. “Glassworker,” again, advises that when French plate glass is “commonly blown … it is better to make little diamonds rather than large square pieces in the shape of a frame, because they cannot be set quite evenly” (fol. 20r). It also states that while Lorraine glass is smoother than French plate, “commonly it is stained by veins, in straight lines as if tanned, which happens because the glassmakers, having made it, put it when totally hot on straw, which stains it in this way.” One can, however, rub off the stains with some “varnish & salt & other drugs that the glassworkers put on.” “Glassworkers’ glass” on fol. 36v describes how French glass “can be cleft again with the light of a candle by wetting, but not as precisely as with the hot iron.” Finally, “Glassworker” on fol. 37v warns that “if the glassworker who is working has a stench from his nose or his mouth, & he breathes on the glass, the color will not take on it.” In all four entries, the author-practitioner has recorded tips or reminders for improving glass that are specific to his own interests in the material. In this, he mobilizes knowledge gathered from observations of and experiences with other artisans, apparently in order to benefit his own activities.

“For Cages”

Most of the entries on glassmakers and glassmaking processes describe glassblowing techniques that require large-scale facilities and specialized expertise, making them difficult to reconstruct in the Making and Knowing laboratory. “For cages,” a short entry that appears on fol. 6v, however, describes a small-scale manipulation of vitreous material over the heat of a lamp or brazier, which was feasible to reproduce and allowed me to gauge the author-practitioner’s familiarity with and experience of glass.

Some conclusions can already be drawn from an examination of the manuscript page. The entry on fol. 6v was written in a consistent hand, likely in a single sitting. It is neatly laid out, with one exception: the title itself seems to have been revised. While Pour and cages are legible, they are separated by two scratched out and indecipherable words, making it difficult to conclude anything concrete about the author-practitioner’s thought process in titling.11 Underneath the main body of the entry text is an image that illustrates a process for lengthening hollow cane in a furnace (Fig. 1). Unlike many of the other illustrations in Ms. Fr. 640, the furnace is not marginal; it is detailed and placed in the center of the page with plenty of space between the bottom of the entry and the heading of the next one. This suggests that the author-practitioner rendered it deliberately as part of the entry “For cages.” A marginal comment next to the image gives additional information about the functioning of the furnace.

The neatness of the entry’s layout on the page belies some inconsistencies in its content. The entry is divided into two parts, each focused on a different method for the manipulation of “enamel cannules” (canulles d’esmail). These two parts—which represent two stages in the glassworking process—are out of sequence, with the preparatory process requiring a furnace described in the second half and the finishing process using the heat of a lamp for decorative work detailed in the first. Indeed, the first half of the entry specifically concerns instructions for embellishing cages with “enamel cannules of various colors by covering the latten or iron wire with the said cannules,” though what he means by cages is not immediately evident. He goes on to instruct: “these [canes] you will break neatly to the length that you want if you slightly notch with a cutting file the place to be broken, and they will not break at any other place. You can bend them with a wood model over a chafing-dish or else by the heat of the lamp.” This half of the entry is written in second person. The use of “one” or “you” to direct the reader also occurs in the glass entries on fols. 20r and 36v, where the author-practitioner presents his instructions for how to cut or preserve glass to be used for verre eglomisé.12 The personal tone of this half of the entry suggests the author-practitioner is describing an activity, in this case the breaking and bending of glass canes, of which he has firsthand experience, either as a witness or practitioner. That he avoids the first person, however, makes it unclear whether he has attempted this activity himself.

The second half of the entry begins in the second person by describing how to prepare canes using a furnace: “it can also be drawn as long as you want in a small furnace made like a reverberatory furnace but which is pierced on both sides.” In the next sentence the author-practitioner switches voices to the third person:

And when the large cannule is red, they seize the hot end using small pincers with a long beak, such that one prong of the beak of the small pincers enters inside the end of the cannule, & thus it is lengthened without becoming stopped, & the other end of the cannule is held with the hand, because it is not hot. When the cannule is stretched enough, the one who works seated, having his furnace the size of a carnation pot before him, breaks it off & continues. This is for making cannules for capes, which are cut, as said, with a file.

This switch from the second to third person suggests that the author-practitioner directly observed others undertake the process described. The tension between the author-practitioner’s use of the second and third person across this entry makes it unclear whether he actually attempted this work of embellishing cages, whether he recounts a process he witnessed, or whether he offers an idea for decorative work based on observations of glassmakers’ practices.

There is one extant example of a glass-barred cage that may help visualize the intended outcome of the work described in “For cages.” It was manufactured during the sixteenth century in the glass workshop at the court of Archduke Ferdinand II of Tyrol between 1570 and 1590, and it is now in the collection of the Kunsthistorisches Museum and housed at Schloss Ambras (Fig. 2). Too delicate for practical use, it contains an unusual glass and papier-mâché Handstein listed in Ferdinand’s 1596 inventory as a Glasberg. Photographs of the cage led me to presume that its blue bars were made of wire dipped into blue glass. On a visit to Schloss Ambras in December 2017, however, I surmised that the cage is formed of solid canes of translucent dark blue enamel set, seemingly with glue, into a frame of wooden slats (Fig. 3). Inserting solid canes into a wooden frame with glue is a cold process and does not correspond to the author-practitioner’s description of “covering” wire with canulles d’esmail by the heat of a lamp. The Schloss Ambras Glassberg thus provides only a visual reference for what the author-practitioner intended to produce. Nevertheless, it may be worthwhile to explore the method of assembly used for the Ambras cage in a further reconstruction.

The nature of the materials and techniques detailed on fol. 6v suggests that the author-practitioner generated the entry after observing practitioners in a lampworking and beadmaking workshop. Lampworking—a modern term that I will use throughout this essay—refers to the use of a naked flame to manipulate rods and tubes of colored glass (and even to melt them onto wire) to form small objects.13 Sources on sixteenth-century lampworking are scarce. The earliest textual source on lampworking is a Venetian legal document from 1574, in which a glassworker describes his profession as that of working rods and tubes in front of a flame, but this gives little helpful detail.14 The earliest description of the technique appears in a 1679 supplement to Johann Kunckel’s (1630–1703) translation of Antonio Neri’s (1576–1614) Ars Vitraria. The illustration included with the text shows artisans working at a table furnished with small oil lamps, just as Kunckel describes them (Fig. 4). A bellows beneath the table is attached to a system of tubes that transports air to the lamp’s flames, concentrating them to make them hot enough to soften glass rods or tubes. The artisans then blow through the cool end of these tubes to create bubbles of glass, which they manipulate with tools like pliers and looped wires to create “picture figures, crucifixes, small vessels; whatever you can conceive.”15 Kunckel thus calls lampworking the “little glass blowing as is done with the lamp.”16 This description of the use of a lamp as a heat source accords with the author-practitioner’s entry “For cages.”

An important difference in terminology distinguishes Kunckel’s description of the “little glass blowing” from the author-practitioner’s “For cages” and complicates our understanding of the Ms. Fr. 640 entry and any reconstruction of it. While Kunckel specifies that lampworking is done with glass tubes and rods, in “For cages” on fol. 6v, the author-practitioner does not use the word verre. Instead, his method involves the use of canulles d’esmail. As Amy Chang, Olivia Clemens, and Yuanxie Shi have shown, the author-practitioner’s understanding of enamel was complex and does not map neatly onto the traditional art-historical definition of enamel as a vitreous material fused to metal, ceramic, and glass at high temperatures.17 In Ms. Fr. 640 the term esmail generally refers to both heated and unheated processes for imitating the visual effect of vitreous materials or precious gems on a glass or metal substrate.18

The author-practitioner’s use of the term canulles d’esmail on fol. 6r may indicate another layer to the early modern understanding of glass, enamel, and lampworking; it also associates the author-practitioner with practices of sixteenth-century beadmaking. The 1573 post-mortem inventory of beadmaker Jehann Gourlin (d. before 1573) differentiates between four different kinds of lampworked glass beads, including two separate entries for cristallin and émail.19 As Laurier Turgeon argues, in the context of beadmaking during this period, the difference between the two terms rests in the kind of glass used to form the beads.20 Cristallin refers to high quality translucent glass of the kind used to make crystal drinking glasses (i.e., the famous Venetian cristallo), while émail refers to the less expensive and lower quality opaque or colored glass used to make beads, prefabricated enamels, and the glass tubes for lampworking.21 The author-practitioner certainly understood cristallin in the same way as the beadmaker’s inventory. On fol. 57r, he directly refers to cristallin as a specific type of crushed glass.22 He also discusses cristallin on fol. 68r, where he differentiates it from common glass as “more excellent” because it “contains both salt of tartar & saltwort all together, which both help fusion.”23 The author-practitioner understood cristallin as a high quality glass composed of specific ingredients. In addition he was familiar with the function of those ingredients in yielding glass of a higher clarity. This suggests that for the author-practitioner, in addition to traditional vitreous enamel and its resin imitations, esmail also referred to the low quality opaque glass used to manufacture canes for beadmaking and lampworking, which further situates “For cages” within the wider context of lampworking and beadmaking in sixteenth-century France.

Lampworking and Beadmaking

The second half of the entry “For cages” seems to describe techniques used by lampworkers and beadmakers, two groups who were closely allied in the early modern period. Both beadmaking and lampworking, like so many other Renaissance glass technologies, arose in Italy and later spread across France. Beadmaking was an established trade in Venice as early as the thirteenth century.24 By 1443, French patrons were encouraging the emigration of glassmakers from Murano and Altare for the production of Venetian-style glass, known as Façon de Venise.25

Though these two professions arrived in France separately, by the end of the sixteenth century they were integrated under the same guild structure. Glass guilds in Italy were strictly divided according to product. For example, the beadmakers who produced rosary beads were known as paternostreri, whereas those who produced the smaller seed beads called marguerites were known as margariteri.26 The movement of knowledge from Italy to France, however, did not involve a simultaneous reorganization of the guilds to reflect the distinction. Techniques were disseminated by itinerant Italian glassmakers who had learned them in Murano and from other regions of northern Italy.27 Any early categorical division between lampworkers and beadmakers in France is thus unclear. During the first half of the sixteenth century, both were known variously as émailleurs and paternostriers.28 By the second half of the century, they were allied under the guild of glassmakers and faience workers as the official manufacturers of rosaries, buttons, chains, necklaces, and bracelets by the heat of lamp and furnace.29

Not only were French beadmakers and lampworkers allied under the glassmakers guild, but there is evidence that they were active in the same workshops. By 1584, two post-mortem inventories of Parisian beadmakers’ workshops listed lamps and bellows.30 The inventory of Symon Grue (d. before 1584) contains “a bellows [soufflet], a lamp [lampe], and iron wire,” and that of Anthoine Grandsire (d. before 1630) lists a “bellows ‘for bead makers [de paternostrier]’ and a workbench with ‘several lamps [plusieurs lampes].’”31 By the end of the sixteenth century in France, lampworking and beadmaking largely took place in the same workshops, along with the production of a variety of small-scale specialty glass products.

It is likely that the author-practitioner was familiar with one of these workshops and its furnace. When describing the oven used for lengthening canes, the author-practitioner states that, “glass button makers also avail themselves of the said furnace” (fol. 6v). Significantly, buttons were one of the chief products of the beadmaking and lampworking industries in sixteenth-century France. “For cages” also includes a detailed image of the stove that provides information on both its design and use (Fig. 1). Given the detailed nature of the sketch, it is likely that the author-practitioner based it on his experience of the ovens used by lampworkers and beadmakers.32

The author-practitioner’s mention of a reverberatory oven suggests that he intended to build a glass-button maker’s furnace for himself. He describes the furnace as “like a reverberatory furnace but which is pierced on both sides” (fol. 6v). Reverberatory ovens appear five other times in the manuscript. On fols. 115v, 127v, and 161r, the author-practitioner directs the reader to cook or dry various materials in a reverberatory furnace. On fols. 16r and 124v he uses the reverberatory oven as a technological reference, describing an equal spread of heat in a different kind of oven as “like the flame in a reverberatory furnace” and asserting that “molds are reheated better & more surely in a closed fire, such as in a reverberatory furnace.” The author-practitioner’s association between the glass-button maker’s stove and a reverberatory furnace may have functioned as a hint or reminder about its design based on a technology that he was most familiar with.

The author-practitioner describes how the furnace on fol. 6v is used to lengthen hollow canes of the kind used by lampworkers and beadmakers in the sixteenth century. In lampworking, artisans followed the process described by Kunckel discussed above. In beadmaking, hollow canes were cut into small segments, which were then placed on forks and gently reheated in order to round their edges.33 This process, called the a speo method, is illustrated in The Glass and Coral Factory, painted by Jacob van Loo (1614–1670) (Fig. 5). Fragments of such rods and tubes were discovered among the archaeological remains of a sixteenth-century beadmaking workshop in Rouen.34

Some French lampworkers and beadmakers made their hollow canes themselves. Producing hollow canes required a full-scale glasshouse because the process demanded a large amount of space, blowpipes, at least two trained glassworkers, and a large glassmakers furnace for fusing glass.35 One glassworker would gather molten glass on the end of a hollow iron blowpipe and blow through the other end of the pipe to create a bubble. Another would then grasp the cooling glass with pincers or irons and run at a measured pace across the workshop, pulling the glass after him until it became too cool to be pulled any further. The long, thin, hollow tubes of glass could then be cut to the desired length.36 This process is very similar to the process for lengthening glass tubes by drawing them through a furnace that the author-practitioner describes in the second half of “For cages.” Late sixteenth-century beadmaking workshop inventories reveal that several of the thirty-seven Parisian beadmakers had the equipment necessary to “draw” their own glass tubes for the production of beads, while the rest were purchasing premade glass and enamel tubes from the larger glass manufactories.37 In addition to furnaces, workbenches, and irons, the inventory of Benoit Vincent lists eighty pounds of enamel tubes in various colors and forty pounds of white enamel tubes.38

Nevertheless, there is evidence that lampworkers and beadmakers also obtained canes from more specialized glass manufactories. Of the tube remnants found at the beadmaking workshop in Rouen, though all were made of soda-lime silica glass, trace elements in the composition of the rods suggest that the workshop primarily “used oil lamps to make various ornaments from glass rods or tubes produced by other specialized shops.”39 In addition, Kunckel’s first instructions to the aspiring lampworker are: “first one must order from a glasshouse and have made an infinite number of stalks and small tubes, which are hollow and made of good, pure glass, and all sorts of colors.”40 By the end of the sixteenth century, specialized glasshouses that produced and sold rods had been established in Nevers, Paris, Rouen, Nantes, Bordeaux, and Saint-Germain-en-Laye.41

At the end of “For cages” on fol. 6v, the author-practitioner explains that the process of drawing canes through a furnace to lengthen them is “for making cannules for capes, which are cut, as said, with a file.” This use solidifies the connection between “For cages” and French traditions of lampworking and beadmaking. The author-practitioner uses the word cappes, which Randle Cotgrave translates as “hooded; or, that hath a short, and capuched, cloake on.”42 This suggests that the author-practitioner is referring to a cloak or hood of some kind adorned or fastened with drawn glass beads. Beginning in the last quarter of the century, the inventories studied by Laurier Turgeon begin to list sewing supplies in addition to glass and glassmaking tools. Turgeon attributes this to beadmakers’ increasing involvement in the clothing industry, prompted by the developing Renaissance fashion for embellishing clothing with gems, beads, and buttons.43 Given the connections between his description of the process for lengthening cane and the process for manufacturing cane, the designation of the furnace as that of the glass-button makers, and his reference to canes used to embellish capes, it is likely that the author-practitioner witnessed the techniques described in “For cages” in a workshop where lampworking and beadmaking took place. Nevertheless, I have found no evidence that lampworkers and beadmakers were involved in the manufacture or embellishment of cages.

“One of the daintiest pieces of glass art”

The author-practitioner likely mobilized the knowledge he gained from witnessing beadmaking and lampworking processes in order to augment his production of cages. Lampworking and beadmaking were largely used for decoration and embellishment. In his account of the process, Kunckel describes lampworking as “not the most useful, but one of the daintiest pieces of glass art.”44 Period accounts of lampworked objects almost exclusively describe beads, buttons, figurines and other small ornaments, jewelry, and goblet stems.45 As early as 1454, Olivier de la Marche described a banquet at the Chateau de Lille where was displayed “a very beautiful fountain partly in glass and partly in lead of a very new aspect, for there were small trees made of glass with marvellous leaves and flowers; and in an empty space, as a clearing, surrounded by rocks there was a small St. Andrew standing erect with his cross in front of him.”46 A seventeenth-century inventory of one of Augsburg merchant Philipp Hainhofer’s famous cabinets includes three “colorful and gilded glass chains made in France, which women wear around their neck,” as well as some other glass jewellery made by the Venetian Luca Trono.47 In France, by 1605 high-quality lampworked objects were considered gifts fit for royal children; the king’s physician Jean Héroard reported the young dauphin’s pleasure in playing with small dogs and marmosets made of glass and enamel.48

Unfortunately, no French lampworked buttons, beads, figures, or jewelry are known to survive from the sixteenth century. The only extant sixteenth-century lampworked objects are from Austria, though it is likely that French lampworkers and beadworkers would have produced similar objects. In any case, the extant examples enhance the association between lampworking and embellishment. In 1568, Archduke Ferdinand II requested a Venetian lampworker with special expertise in the fabrication of glass chains from the Venetian government to manufacture jewellery for the women of the Innsbruck court.49 Artisans were active at the glass workshop between 1570 and 1590, producing everything from small human and animal figurines (Fig. 6, Fig. 7) to earrings (Fig. 8) to delicate buttons (Fig. 9) to adorned Deckelpokals (Fig. 10). These objects, which remain in the collection of Vienna’s Kunsthistorisches Museum, as well as similar items in the collection of Schloss Ambras in the hills above Innsbruck, show that lampworkers foremost made products for personal adornment or to embellish blown-glass objects.

It is likely that the author-practitioner also understood lampworking as a technique used for ornamentation, and the entry “For cages” makes this clear. Elsewhere in the manuscript, cages are primarily functional objects. They appear on fols. 50v, 98v, 105v and 120r as containers for animals, notably lizards and birds, and the entries on these folios give instructions for their fabrication. Those on fols. 105v and 120r, concerning the capture and treatment of nightingales, are the most detailed. They specify that thick iron wire must be used for the construction of bird cages, that the cages must be made in the shape of a barn, lined with fabric, and constructed with a removable bottom that may be slid out from underneath in order to “refresh its fresh earth everyday” (fol. 120r) with ants for the bird to eat. The specificity of these entries suggests that the author-practitioner likely fashioned his own cages for catching and keeping live animals, some of which he likely used in the process of casting from life.

While fol. 6v’s “For cages” also specifies the use of “latten or iron wire,” it is the only entry in which the author-practitioner does not consider cages in relation to their function but for the way they can be embellished (tu les peulx embellir). Moreover, this entry is separated from the more functional treatment of cages by at least fifty pages. While the entries surrounding “For cages” are not united by technique or material, they do share a common theme: the decoration or embellishment of readymade objects.50 “For cages” is preceded on fol. 5v by the entry “For bronzing in yellow and white,” which describes a method for enhancing the appearance of wooden candlesticks, followed by the entry on fol. 6r “For laying down and seating burnished gold and giving red or green or blue” on wood. Both of these describe methods of embellishing common substances. In turn, “For cages” is followed on fol. 6v by the entry “For stampings to embellish and encrust or cover the edges of mirrors, the tops of chests, or the friezes of bed valances,” on fol. 7r by “For gilding with gold color and tinsel” and “For coloring stamped trunks,” and another on fol. 7v “For giving the color of all kinds of metals to wood & other things.” The separation of “For cages” from other entries concerning the production of cages for practical purposes thus suggests that this short section of entries between fols. 5v and 7v are particularly concerned with the embellishment of pre-made objects that the author-practitioner already had, or at least already had experience of making.

Reconstruction

“For cages” clearly relates to the materials, tools, techniques, and uses of lampworking and beadmaking in sixteenth-century France. As suggested above, the second half of the entry, written in the third person, seems to be the author-practitioner’s record of a process for lengthening hollow canulles d’esmail that he may have witnessed in a workshop in which lampworking and beadmaking was taking place. The first half of the entry seems to describe lampworking techniques, which—given the association between the two industries—the author-practitioner could have witnessed in the same workshop. While the materials and equipment required for the first half of the entry are clear, namely, glass and the heat of a lamp, the technique is opaque. The author-practitioner provides no detailed instruction for “covering” wire with enamel canes. I undertook a reconstruction in order to better elucidate the meaning of the first half of the entry and to explore the extent of the author-practitioner’s potential familiarity with lampworking techniques.51

It is impossible to know whether my three technique trials described below, though based on material evidence of historical techniques, at all approximate those actually used by sixteenth-century glassworkers.52 My reconstruction should be viewed as an informed exploration of the author-practitioner’s possible interpretation and reapplication of techniques learned from encounters with other artisans in the context of his own workshop practice.

My reconstruction was informed by my own familiarity with the material and lampworking techniques. Sociologist Erin O’Connor argues that the development of proficiency in glassworking has two parts. First, it involves acquiring bodily knowledge of techniques by closely imitating the actions of experts. Second, it requires the development of an embodied understanding of the changing properties of glass as it reacts to heat, an understanding which can only be gained by repeated handling of the material.53 Having practised both glassblowing and lampworking from a young age, I have both an understanding of technique and the behavior of the material when heated. There is a way to feel, not just observe, when the end of a cane of glass will be soft enough to manipulate. This tacit knowledge informed my handling of the glass and how I undertook my reconstruction experiments.

In addition, my lampworking experience informed the questions I kept in mind about the author-practitioner’s own material knowledge. Could he have used these techniques after observing them once, without the embodied expertise of a lampworker or beadmaker? What might have been the differences between the way a lampworker or beadmaker approached the material and the way the author-practitioner used it? Finally, I questioned how the extent of my material knowledge of glass might differ from the author-practitioner’s and how it might impact the way I interpreted my reconstruction. I thus developed a reconstruction that would examine a few ways in which the author-practitioner might have envisioned “covering” wire with hollow enamel canes. I conducted several technique trials based on uses of wire in early modern lampworking. What follows is a discussion of my most relevant trials.

I decided to attempt to reconstruct three sixteenth-century lampworking techniques for adhering glass to wire known from object sources: building, lamp-winding, and embedding. Building is a term I am using to designate a technique found in the objects from Innsbruck and later described by Denis Diderot (1713–1719) in the 1759 Encyclopédie, ou Dictionnaire raisonné des sciences, des arts, et des métiers, which involves melting hot glass onto a wire armature. In lamp-winding, the glassworker winds heated glass around an iron wire to form a spiral bead or a bead with rounded edges.54 Finally, research into early seventeenth-century figurines from Nevers has revealed that they were made with copper wires embedded in rods of glass, a way of combining glass and wire that I have called “embedding.”55

My first trial was based on a technique in which glass is built onto a wire armature. This sixteenth-century method of fusing wire and glass can be seen in the objects from Ferdinand II’s glass workshop. The trees on the Glasberg inside the cage discussed earlier seem to be fused directly to the ends of thin wires (Fig. 2). In addition, the ball on which the statue of Fortuna stands in Fig. 6 is impaled on a thick iron wire, which likely extends up through the figure as a wire armature. The use of wire armatures in the eighteenth century is evidenced in Diderot’s Encyclopédie:

If the enameller plans to make a human or animal figure that is solid and of some height, he begins by forming a small structure of brass wire; he gives this small structure the general shape of the limbs of the figure for which it will serve as a support. He takes the structure in one hand and a baguette of solid enamel in the other: he exposes the enamel to the lamp; and when it is sufficiently fused, he attaches it to his brass wire, on which he models it…56

In all these cases, glass is fused directly onto wire and built up into various forms.57

My second trial followed the technique of lamp-winding. Similar to the standard methods that lampworkers use to form beads today, lamp-wound beads were made by heating the ends of glass canes with a lamp until they reached a sufficient temperature to wind around an iron wire to form a spiral or a bead with rounded edges.58 Turgeon argues that by the second half of the sixteenth century this technique was common in many beadmaking workshops.59 Though it is difficult to see in photographs, the wire support of this Cupid figurine in the Kunsthistorisches Museum seems to be wound with green glass (Fig. 11).60

The third trial was inspired by the practice of embedding threadlike copper wires in cane figurines. This technique was revealed by radiograph scans done on eleven eighteenth-century Nevers figurines in the Robert Lehman Collection at the Metropolitan Museum of Art.61 The radiographs unveiled both thick iron wire armatures and delicate networks of copper wire (Fig. 12) entwined throughout the glass bodies of the figurines (Fig. 13). Dwight P. Lanmon and David B. Whitehouse posit that these wires were embedded in the individual canes used to form the figurines and were added to increase stability and reduce the risk of the glass cracking.62

I was not able to combine glass and wire in a way that seemed suitable for ornamenting a cage until I abandoned my attempts to reproduce period techniques and attempted a more literal interpretation of the recipe. The only instruction that the author-practitioner gives for combining cane and wire is that “you can embellish them with enamel cannules of various colors by covering the latten or iron wire with the said cannules.” Using a tube of glass with wire inserted, I heated it at one point in the middle of the cane until the glass was malleable. Then, holding the cane in my hands at either end, I bent it into a near seventy-degree angle (Fig. 14). When I removed the cane from the heat and let the glass cool, the wire and glass were stable. While the glass was not completely fused to the wire, the bend prevented it from slipping out of the glass.

My experience with the reconstruction raises some interesting points for consideration. In my first three trials, which imitated period lampworking techniques, I found fusing glass to wire and then bending them together difficult even with my prior knowledge of the material. I found, however, that the more trials I completed, the easier it became. Without a great deal of practice, it would be extremely difficult to produce an embellished cage that was structurally sound. This suggests, as Erin O’Connor argues, that a certain amount of technical and material knowledge must be built up through practice before these processes can be completed successfully. It is improbable that the author-practitioner would have been able to undertake these techniques without significant practice in lampworking.

The technique that proved most successful for the task of fusing and then bending wire and glass was not directly based on a known historical technique. Rather, it developed only when I attempted a more straightforward reading of the recipe. Unlike my first three trials, this process was easy and intuitive, though it is not clear whether this was due to the nature of the process itself or to my growing experience with the particularities of fusing and bending glass and wire in the lab. It seems possible that someone might be able to reproduce this technique from observation alone. Alternatively, the ease with which I was able to bend the wire and glass using this method suggested to me that the author-practitioner could have developed it himself.

Ultimately, my reconstruction remains inconclusive. While the materials (hollow canulles d’esmail) and tools (lamp and furnace) used in “For cages” relate to lampworking and beadmaking, I found no direct precedent for the processes the author-practitioner describes of covering wire and lengthening canes. In addition, there is no evidence that French lampworkers and beadmakers were engaged in the manufacture of embellished cages. There might, of course, be records of processes of which I am unaware. The combination of my experience with the reconstruction and my research into sixteenth-century lampworking and beadmaking techniques leads me to speculate that “For cages” is the author-practitioner’s attempt to replicate several tools, materials, and processes he observed in a lampworking workshop in the service of his own idea: a decorated cage. It is also possible, however, that the entry describes an attempt to directly imitate a process of manufacturing an embellished cage that the author-practitioner witnessed.

In either case, in light of the general context of glass in Ms. Fr. 640, “For cages” can be read as evidence that the author-practitioner informed his own activities with technical knowledge that he gained from observing a lampworking and beadmaking workshop. While it is impossible to know whether the author-practitioner actually visited a beadmaking workshop and observed other artisans or if he followed this recipe to produce cages embellished with canulles d’esmail, my reconstruction and research raised several questions for further pursuit. Why might the author-practitioner have included such scant instructions for how to cover canes in glass and such detailed instructions for how to lengthen cane? To what extent was his execution of these techniques informed by his knowledge of other techniques and materials from outside lampworking and beadmaking? A closer analysis of the other entries featuring glass and enamel in Ms. Fr. 640, perhaps paired with comparative reconstructions of both “For cages” and the author-practitioner’s entries concerning heat-processed vitreous enamels, would help to address these questions.

A thorough study of the author-practitioner’s use of different voices throughout the manuscript might be used to determine a pattern of his practice of observation. In what contexts does he use the first, second, and third person, and what does that suggest about the way he gathered and employed technical knowledge? In addition, deeper research into the glassmaking and beadmaking industries in Toulouse might clarify the relationship that the author-practitioner could have had to these artisans. How was glass retailed as a raw material? Did glassmakers sell premade glass from their workshops? At markets? More research on this topic might reveal other avenues of knowledge exchange between artisans working in different materials. Expanding these contextual questions through research on and reconstructions of Ms. Fr. 640 entries concerning other trades such as carpentry or metalworking would better situate the author-practitioner within networks of artisanal knowledge exchange in sixteenth-century France.
