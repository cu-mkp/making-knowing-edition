Excellent Sand from Alabaster

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/294x-nk86
← Back to Essays

Abstract

The search for an “excellent” molding sand preoccupied the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640. Fol. 83r contains an entry for an “Excellent sand” made with calcined alabaster powder. This essay examines why the author-practitioner might have believed that alabaster would make an excellent mold material.

The entry for “Excellent Sand” on fol. 83r of BnF Ms. Fr. 640 is one among a variety of entries on sands and techniques for their use in frame molds. Most “sands” (sable) in the manuscript consist of a dry powder, which is combined with a liquid binder. This recipe follows such a pattern, calling for the powder of burned alabaster, ground and well-sieved, mixed with a certain ratio of sal ammoniac and left to humidify until it forms a paste.

Two different types of stones are known as alabaster. One, calcite alabaster, is found primarily in the Middle and Near East. The other, which would likely have been used by the author-practitioner, is called gypsum alabaster and is found throughout continental Europe and the United Kingdom. Gypsum alabaster is a fine white stone understood today to be of the same chemical composition as gypsum, CaSO4·2H2O. When heated, both alabaster types release their water molecules and become calcium sulfate hemihydrate, which is plaster.1 The use of plaster (also denoted as a “sand” in Ms. Fr. 640) occurs throughout the manuscript, notably as a mold material used in lifecasting. While the plaster for lifecasting is a liquid “sand” poured around the organic object (plant, animal, flower, etc.) in a clay casing, the sand referred to on fol. 83r is for frame molding, in which the object to be reproduced is pressed into damp sand contained in a wooden frame. The author-practitioner was clearly aware of the material connection between plaster and alabaster, as he notes in the margin of fol. 125v, next to an entry for plaster molds into which wax is cast, that “alabaster, commonly called so, which is nevertheless plaster, is very hard but it shrinks a lot. It is very good to make medals, but it wants to be very finely sieved.” Because the author-practitioner equated plaster and alabaster, we aimed in our reconstruction to determine whether a qualitative difference existed between sand made with pulverized alabaster and that made with plaster that would cause the author-practitioner to mention alabaster by name as a specifically “excellent” material for making sand for frame molds. And if there was no clear difference between the products cast in alabaster and in plaster, what might account for his inclusion of each as a distinct mold material? We investigate here not only the physical properties of alabaster sand but also its symbolic connotations, as they reveal much about alabaster’s status as an elite material in late medieval and early modern Europe, which might have encouraged the author-practitioner to highlight its excellence.

The search for an “excellent” molding sand preoccupied not only the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640, but many early modern craftsmen whose technical writings have come down to us.2 The entry for alabaster sand on fol. 83r is comparable to those found in several other sand recipes in Ms. Fr. 640, most notably one for sand using calcined ox bone and rock salt on fol. 89r. Both use a calcined raw material that is then finely ground, mixed with salt, and humidified to form a paste for molding. Yijun Wang and Pamela Smith’s essay on the entry on fol. 89r details how dry salt acts as a binder in this class of recipes, in contrast to the various forms of liquid binders mentioned in other recipes.3 It is clear that for the early modern craftsman, mold materials were of equal intellectual and experimental interest as the metals themselves. Of course, these molds rarely survived past the production process; thus text-based reconstructions are one of the only ways to shed light on the properties of these various mold materials.

The author-practitioner was not the only one to describe burned alabaster as a mold material. Hugh Plat, in The Jewel House of Art and Nature (London, 1594), notes in a section on “The Art of Molding and Casting” how to prepare molds for casting branches or flowers from life from a mixture of “burnt alabaster and plaister of Paris” in equal portions.4 As in Ms. Fr. 640, Plat recommends mixing three parts of this alabaster and plaster mixture to one part ground brick or terra cotta tile, a small amount of “spawd,”5 and water with sal ammoniac. He also mentions that if one wants to cast in wax, the mold can be made from plaster or alabaster alone, or a mixture of the two. Plat’s instructions are for a liquid molding “sand” for lifecasting, rather than a damp “sand” for a frame mold. It is interesting to note, however, that Ms. Fr. 640’s author-practitioner is not alone in seeing a distinction between the chemically identical materials of burnt alabaster and plaster of Paris. Indeed, contemporary pigment companies such as the German Kremer Pigments sell “Alabaster Modellgips” (modeling plaster), which they claim is “of prime crystallinity, and hardens especially hard and translucent.”6 This provided further impetus for our comparative experiment.

Reconstruction

We began our experiments with 2 kg of raw gypsum alabaster stones.7 After laboriously breaking the raw stones into smaller chunks to facilitate even calcination, we calcined them in both a toaster oven in the lab and our home ovens. With a bit of research, we determined that alabaster calcines at approximately 170℃.8 Fol. 83r instructs to calcine the stone until, “touching it, it turns into powder.” Initially we assumed this meant that the stones would disintegrate with pressure when they were fully heated, but we eventually realized that it more probably referred to a visually powdery surface that would form on the stone fragments, which then would still need grinding in a mortar once cooled. By an hour and a half in the oven, the stone fragments had turned a pure white, and the small pieces could be crushed with relatively little force (Vid. 1). We took this to indicate the completion of the calcination process. The next step was to “pulverize it finely & pass it through a double sieve & render it as if impalpable.” We ground the stones with marble mortars and pestles, and sieved them through tightly-woven cheesecloth lining a plastic sieve. Despite calcination, this was a labor-intensive process that required many rounds of sieving to achieve the desired impalpable consistency. Even at the end of our efforts, the powder was not quite impalpable: small granules could be detected when the powder was rubbed between our fingertips.9

The next step was the addition of sal ammoniac (i.e., modern ammonium chloride, which today comes in the form of small granular crystals similar to table salt). On fol. 83r, the quantity is noted “with one lb of alabaster, one needs one ℥ of sal ammoniac.” Interestingly, in a recipe a few pages later, on fol. 89v, also dealing with sal ammoniac and calcined alabaster as materials for sand, the instructions are somewhat different: “Sal ammoniac, well pulverized mou & ground dry on marble, molds very neatly & is of a very beautiful release, & once mixed with alabaster, pulverized similarly, ii ℥ per lb of alabaster makes it release well.” Here, the author-practitioner recommends two ounces of sal ammoniac for a pound of alabaster. We divided our calcined alabaster and made one mold using one ounce of sal ammoniac, and the other with two. We mixed the sal ammoniac with the alabaster powder.

The next step was to simulate the “cellar or a damp place” in which the powder was meant to rest until it formed a “paste.” As we did not have a humidifier on hand, our initial attempt involved soaking fabric in water and leaving these rags in a cabinet with our sand overnight (Fig. 1). This produced no discernible change in the powder mixture, and we resorted to mixing water in slowly by hand until a paste-like consistency was reached that, when squeezed, stuck together firmly without being too wet. We had to add the water very slowly and mix vigorously with our fingers, because if we poured in the water too quickly, the mixture formed large hard clumps that had to be reground.10 An apparently appropriate consistency was reached after we added 20 ml of water (Fig. 2).11 We were then ready to pack our wooden frame molds. We applied vine charcoal as a separator on the surface of our plaster medals (Fig. 3) before carefully packing the damp sand around them to fill the molds (Fig. 4).12

The recipe indicates that the molds must be hot to be cast into, so our next step was to heat them. We chose to do so in a toaster oven at 400℉ (Fig. 5). Unfortunately, at this point our wooden frame molds caught fire and we had to start the project from scratch.13 To avoid having to repeat the laborious process of grinding alabaster, we decided to purchase pure powdered alabaster ground from raw stones mined in Volterra, Italy. We additionally bought pure powdered gypsum mined in Utah in order to determine if there was a difference between alabaster and gypsum once both were reduced to powder. These substances were calcined at 170℃ in the kiln for an hour and a half (Fig. 6), and then the two different ratios of sal ammoniac were mixed in.

To humidify this batch, we acquired a humidifier and set up a system that would expose the powder to moisture in a more constant way. The different calcined powders were put on terracotta plates and elevated so that the humidifier could blow directly beneath them (Fig. 7). These powders were then covered with a domed plastic lid, and the whole apparatus was draped in linen to trap moisture (Fig. 8). The sand was left to humidify for seven hours. The resulting sand was quite satisfactory for packing, fairly hard to the touch but easy to break apart with light pressure, and it held its form readily when pressed, as described on fol. 118v, “Casting in frames” (Fig. 9). It was interesting to note that the gypsum powder remained fairly white, while the alabaster powder looked slightly more yellow in its paste form.

We then packed four molds, a first with calcined commercial alabaster powder and one ounce of sal ammoniac, a second with calcined commercial alabaster powder and two ounces of sal ammoniac, a third with the leftover calcined alabaster powder ground from the raw stone mixed with one ounce of sal ammoniac, and a fourth with calcined commercial gypsum powder and one ounce of sal ammoniac. These molds were left to dry overnight in the fume hood (Fig. 10).

The next day, we removed the models from each mold.14 Most came away relatively cleanly (Fig. 11, Fig. 12, Fig. 13); however, the portrait medal we used in the commercial alabaster with one ounce of sal ammoniac did not (Fig. 14). A huge chunk of the mold material stuck to the surface of the medal, and a chemical reaction occurred leaving a blue residue on the medal’s surface, rendering this mold unusable.

The remaining three molds were placed in the kiln to warm for thirty minutes at 80℃ (Fig. 15). We cast a tin-lead alloy in a ratio of approximately 1.5 parts tin to 1 part lead, as directed on fol. 72v (“lead & tin is per one lb of lead, one lb & a half of tin”),15 and we tested when the metal was hot enough to pour using a piece of paper (Fig. 16), as described in a marginal note on fol. 139r. When the paper turned brown, we poured the metal into the open top of each mold (Fig. 17). In order to maintain pressure and allow the metal to flow and cool evenly, as soon as it was poured, we placed flat pieces of soapstone over the open mold until the metal had cooled (Fig. 18).

The results did not surprise us: there was no discernible difference between the impressions of objects cast in alabaster versus those cast in gypsum (Fig. 19, Fig. 20, Fig. 21). As to the “excellence” of our sand, the finished objects did not come out as cleanly as we would have expected from our subjective understanding of what “excellent” sand for molding could accomplish. This is likely due to our inexperience with frame molding rather than any fault with the material.16 While a certain amount of detail was picked up in each, the most subtle surface gradations were not captured. This was especially evident on the cast of a necklace pendant with a relief of a bird perched on a branch (Fig. 21). The original had quite fine outlines of individual feathers on the wings and tail, which were barely registered in the cast replica. However, the very fine feet of the bird, as well as the branch on which it was perched, were picked up clearly. The flat surface surrounding the bird showed evidence of granularity. The edges of each object were not cleanly cast, because the edges of the impressions had crumbled slightly when the models were removed from the molds. Despite these imperfections, it was clear that this was a sand that packed quite well and dried quite hard, but, again, being made of gypsum or alabaster made no difference. So, if alabaster is no different from plaster when calcined, in terms of the impression it picks up, why was it specifically indicated as an excellent sand for casting?

Alabaster

Alabaster is best known as a fine material for carving sculpture. Prized for its pure white color, its surface is capable of being highly polished, and its softness facilitates carving. Both alabaster and gypsum are extracted from the same quarries, but alabaster is rarer. Most of the material quarried in alabaster quarries was in fact gypsum, which is too soft and crumbly to be carved and was sold as raw material for plaster.17

Delicate alabaster sculptures were produced in several artistic centers in Europe during the Middle Ages and early modern era. Well-known workshops were located in Britain, in South Derbyshire and Staffordshire, and, beginning in the mid-fourteenth century, these regions produced thousands of relatively small-scale alabaster devotional images.18 The objects produced in these prolific workshops were so popular and affordable that they were purchased and transported across Europe, at least until Henry VIII put a stop to production during his campaign against the Church and its devotional furnishings.

More relevant for Ms. Fr. 640 are likely the Burgundian ducal workshops that produced breathtakingly detailed alabaster tombs for several generations of dukes in their administrative capital of Dijon. These tombs (Fig. 22, Fig. 23) consist of fully painted white marble effigies reclining on black marble slabs, which are in turn supported by elaborate micro-architectural arcades of alabaster, populated with small alabaster mourning figures (Fig. 24). It is these mourners that have received most scholarly attention, due to their arresting detail and lifelike gestures. Unlike the marble effigies, these sculptures were only sparingly painted, highlighting the glowing white surface of the alabaster. The choice to leave the alabaster surface exposed indicates a certain reverence for the stone itself.19 Indeed, the contracts for each tomb explicitly specify alabaster as the material to be used for the tomb,20 despite the documented delays in production due to the difficulty of securing sufficient quantities of alabaster.21 The importance of alabaster specifically as a material for such elite commissions is evidence that it held a high position in the hierarchy of stone for sculpting in Burgundy. Alabaster sculptures also survive from the Toulouse region (Fig. 25), and they, too, seem to have been left unpainted, likely circulating among an elite audience.

One can imagine a metalworker visiting the workshops of these sculptors to collect the dust left over from carving in order to use it for molding sand. Knowing of sculpted alabaster’s hard shining surface and crisp details (due to the ease of carving the stone), the author-practitioner might have hypothesized that it would form a hard, durable mold material especially suited to capturing the minute details and contours of the model to be reproduced. Indeed, in the recipe on fol. 89v, which also addresses alabaster sand, he specifies that upon drying, the mold will become “hard as stone,” which makes the cast easier to remove from the mold.

The use of alabaster for tomb sculptures was not only tied to its fine appearance but also to its theological significance. Alabaster is referred to in the gospels, in Mark 14:3, Matthew 26:6–7, and Luke 7:36–38. Though the setting of the story differs in each gospel, the basic story is the same: a pious woman comes to Christ bearing an alabaster jar full of precious ointment, which she uses to anoint him. Mark additionally specifies that the woman breaks the jar of alabaster to access the ointment. This moment received sustained exegetical attention from early Christian theologians such as St. Augustine22 and St. Jerome.23 Aleksandra Lipinska sums up the interpretation of this moment: “The woman bringing the jar signifies the Church; the alabastron [alabaster jar] filled with perfume oil symbolizes the body of Christ filled up with faith; and the breaking of the vessel stands for the death of Christ on the Cross.”24 This trope was extended to encompass the bodies of the faithful as well. Twelfth-century English abbot Aelred of Rievaulx writes to his followers to “break then the alabaster of your heart and whatever devotion you have, [...], pour it all out upon your Bridegroom’s head, while you adore the man in God and God in the man.”25 The association of alabaster with the body of Christ, and by extension the bodies of the faithful who modeled themselves in the image of Christ, led to the stone’s use in funerary sculpture as a symbol of the body’s potential for resurrection. This, coupled with the remarkable similarities in tone and texture between highly finished alabaster and human flesh, made the stone a potent, polyvalent material.

Marble was another material celebrated for its ability to mimic flesh. In this vein, there is evidence in late medieval Europe that alabaster was sometimes called marble, and vice versa. For instance, the arcades of the tomb of Duke Philip the Bold were referred to in medieval sources as alabaster; however, recent analysis has shown they are actually white marble.26 Aleksandra Lipinska argues that this indicates that up until the eighteenth century “alabaster, like other polishable minerals and rocks, was counted among the family of marbles.”27 Marble, with its ancient imperial connotations, was a stone of great power and prestige, and alabaster took on those associations.28 Pliny the Elder was perhaps the first to associate marble and alabaster, referring in Book X of his Natural History to a stone called onyx marble that is sometimes called alabastrites. Though he is likely referring to calcite alabaster, since he mentions the stone is found in Egypt, he specifically states that this type of stone “is suitable too, when burnt, for plasters.”29 Thus we see as early as the first century CE the continuum between marble, alabaster, and plaster. Medieval lapidaries continued to group alabaster as a type of marble, for instance Isidore of Seville in his Etymologies. There, alabaster is included among a list of other marbles, described as “a white stone, tinted here and there with various colors.”30 However, Isidore too may be referring to calcite alabaster, as he states that the whitest stones are to be found in Egypt, Syria, and India. Whether medieval patrons and sculptors understood the difference between the two types of alabaster remains unclear. It is nevertheless evident that alabaster’s close connection to marble lasted through the Middle Ages.

In fact, one recipe in Ms. Fr. 640 seems to make this connection as well. On fol. 101r–v, a recipe for “Salt for melting” describes the process of making a modeling material out of common salt and saltpeter that, when ground and heated, will make a “substance white, hard & even, like alabaster, with which you will be able to cast medals which will resemble marble” (fol. 101v). Here, where the resulting material is as white as alabaster and creates objects that resemble marble, the two terms seem almost interchangeable.

It is notable that the author-practitioner also makes clear reference to alabaster’s relationship to plaster. Lipinska states that alabaster’s chemical relationship to gypsum was not widely known before the early eighteenth century,31 yet the working knowledge of the author-practitioner seems to indicate that the connection between alabaster and the “raw” plaster stone32 was indeed a part of artisanal knowledge by the end of the sixteenth century. This knowledge of the similarity between plaster and alabaster broadens our understanding of marble’s early modern taxonomy, connecting it both to alabaster and plaster. In fact, Theophrastus’ De lapidibus (third century BCE) makes the connection between gypsum and alabaster, when he refers to an earth called gypsos and a stone called alabastrites that can be found within this earth, both of which give off “stickiness and heat, when [...] wet,” after having been burned.33 He states that this substance is “used on buildings and is poured around the stone or anything else of this kind that one wishes to fasten.”34 He even mentions that painters in Italy use it for their art (ground alabaster and gypsum are still used in the preparation of gesso today).35 Theophrastus clearly refers to gypsum plaster here and distinguishes the hard stone form of it as alabastrites. Thus we can see that the author-practitioner’s experiential view of a continuum of marble, alabaster, and plaster stands in a long lineage of knowledge about stones.36

If we consider that our author-practitioner was interested in his chosen materials for their “experimented” properties, it makes sense that alabaster, an elite stone closely associated with marble and celebrated for its ability to produce luminous and delicate carving in a sacred and commemorative context, would be assumed to have finer and more “excellent” properties for molding than the much more easily attainable plaster, which was not used for art objects of as high status and caliber as its chemically identical counterpart. Moreover, the symbolic and biblical connotations of the material, connected to the transformation of the bodies of the faithful through resurrection, could have held special interest for the author-practitioner, as he experiments with the transformation of materials in many experiments. Therefore, it is perhaps this symbolic potency of alabaster that leads him to assume that it produces more “excellent” sand than plaster. This raises the question of whether the author-practitioner had actually experimented with this sand or was simply hypothesizing that it would be a fine material. A comparison of the entry on fol. 83r, or that using ox bone and rock salt on fol. 89r, with certain other recipes, such as that for “Sand for lead, the most excellent of all, for high and low reliefs” on fols. 84v–85r, or that detailing “Experimented sands” on fols. 85v–86v, reveals a striking difference in rhetorical style. While the alabaster recipe and the ox bone recipe contain relatively clear, step-by-step instructions in declarative statements and specific ratios of materials, they do not include the full narration of first-person work found in the entries on fols. 84v–85r and 85v–86v. These recipes describe multiple trials with accounts of failures and successes.37 The author-practitioner makes note of changes in his technique over time and of the new knowledge he uncovers.38 Why would he insert such copious detail in some recipes and relatively little in others? We might take this to indicate that he had actually attempted certain sand recipes, while others he had merely collected, perhaps for posterity, perhaps for later experimentation. The use of alabaster was perhaps merely a thought experiment, as he thought about materials within the characteristic sand paradigm of finely powdered substance mixed with a binder, and considered what materials might translate well into mold making. As our reconstruction showed, alabaster sand is certainly functional, but perhaps no more “excellent” than others.
