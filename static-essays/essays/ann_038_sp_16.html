What is Esmail in Ms. Fr. 640?

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/mmbd-2191
← Back to Essays

Abstract

This essay explores the meaning of esmail in Ms. Fr. 640 and places it in the context of historical enameling techniques. The term translates as “enamel,” but whereas this usually implies a vitreous substance produced using heat, in Ms. Fr. 640 it also encompasses materials with a glossy, enamel-like appearance without the application of heat, such as “cold enamels” which can be painted out, and in fact surpass vitreous enamels in their versatility and ease of application.

There are thirty-six entries in Ms. Fr. 640 that mention esmail, a term which is frequently translated as “enamel.” Today, the term “enamel” is generally used to mean a colored, vitreous material processed by heat to create a shiny, hard substance that decorates metal, glass, or ceramic surfaces.1 The descriptions of esmail in Ms. Fr. 640 make clear, however, that although esmail is accurately translated as “enamel,” the author-practitioner’s understanding of esmail was significantly different from the modern definition of “enamel.”

The author-practitioner included recipes in Ms. Fr. 640 for making different colors of esmail, directions regarding which substrates different types of esmail should be applied to, and how best to combine colored esmail with various surfaces.2 The various uses of esmail in the manuscript encompass heat-processed (“hot”) and unheated (“cold”) varieties,3 oil- and resin-based recipes, and mixtures intended for metal, glass, canvas, and panel. Esmail is mentioned in relation to diverse substances and processes, including imitation coral (fol. 3r), putting fake spots on sapphires (fol. 38r), painting on glass (fol. 37v), reverse painting on glass (fol. 39v), making buttons (fol. 40r), essential oils (fol. 67v), enameling gold roses and daisies (fols. 116r and 117r), and preserving fruit for more than a year (fol. 121r).

With such a wide variety of related usages, a single definition of esmail seems elusive. Nevertheless, the recipes in the manuscript that involve esmail may be grouped into three thematic clusters: painting on glass,4 imitating precious stones, and painting on metal. This essay seeks to understand how the author-practitioner would have defined esmail by examining the commonalities among the diverse mentions of esmail, and exploring the applications of it within each of these three areas. Ultimately, the essay focuses on two areas of practice which Ms. Fr. 640 illuminates: cold esmail (or, cold-painted enamels), and esmail that imitates or enhances gems.

In researching and reconstructing the recipes for enamel in Ms. Fr. 640, we sought to better understand what the author-practitioner meant by esmail and whether the term was used to describe a visual effect, rather than a specific material, or a making process. The principal questions we explored were: What are the underlying qualities that the author-practitioner considers to be definitive of esmail? What are the desired optical qualities of esmail? How can these same qualities be created with a variety of techniques that all employ differing materials? Once we began investigating these questions, we discovered there was an opportunity to concentrate on the topic of cold esmail, a technique that emerges as an important one for the author-practitioner, but one which has been little-recognized in modern scholarship as a historical category of esmail. As such, we wanted to focus on understanding the primary applications of cold esmail recipes, and how common they were in the early modern period. Ultimately, we concluded that cold esmail recipes were intended to imitate the visual effect of vitreous enamels and precious gems, principally on metal and glass substrates, and that the use of cold esmail was not uncommon among early-modern practitioners—a fact that may prompt us to reconsider what was understood as “enamel” in early modern France.

What is Esmail?

Of the thirty-six recipes that refer to esmail in Ms. Fr. 640, only seven mention heating enamel or a substance like it.5 Fourteen of the recipes for esmail mention oil and many have ingredients in common with varnish, four recipes mention varnish itself as an ingredient in esmail, while mastic is mentioned in three esmail recipes, and turpentine in eight.6 Looking at these frequencies it becomes clear that there is a discrepancy between the modern understanding of esmail as something vitreous and the author-practitioner’s understanding and use of esmail, which often consisted of grinding pigments and mixing them with binders at room temperature before applying the finished product to a metal or glass surface.7 What, then, does the author-practitioner mean when he refers to esmail, and what distinguishes it from paint, or from varnish?

Among the recipes in the manuscript that refer to enamels produced with heat are the recipes for lampwork (fol. 6v), whitening cornaline (fols. 12v–13r), “working in small works of soft enamels” (fol. 37v), vermeilles buttons (fol. 40r), topaz (fol. 101r), enameling a cornaline (fol. 104r), and applying and recovering the color of rouge clair (fol. 124r).8 They are either predicated on having a vitreous substance already made which can be manipulated with heat (fol. 6r, fol. 40r), or they are focused on using heat in combination with enamels in order to change or restore color (fol. 12v–13r, fol. 101r, fol. 104r). These recipes fall into a group that is more representative of the modern concept of enamels: the recipes on fol. 6r and fol. 40r seem to describe lampwork, and the recipes on fol. 12v–13r, fol. 101r, and fol. 104 describe the use of heat-processing to make the colors appear like those of more valuable stones—a practice that has been documented since the time of Seneca and Pliny.9

While the recipes for heated enamels are therefore familiar, the cold enamel recipes in Ms. Fr. 640 do not resemble the material or technical definitions of known categories of enamels. In modern scholarship, esmail—meaning a vitreous enamel that has been fused to a metal substrate through the application of heat—is most commonly divided into champlevé, cloisonné, basse taille,10 and “painted enamel.”11 The last of these—“painted enamel”—is often discussed in relation to the application process used for vitreous enameling in sixteenth-century Limoges, in which different layers of pigment were applied to the metal surfaces with a brush before they underwent various stages of firing into a final form, on top of which additional painted details could be added (Fig. 1, Fig. 2).12

Evidence for the fact that the term “painted enamel” was associated with vitreous “hot enamels” in early modern France can be found in the official title of one of the most famous enamellers of Limoges, Léonard Limosin. In 1548 Limosin was promoted to the position of peintre émailleur et valet de chambre du roi, and the combination of the title and his work demonstrate the association between hot enameling and the terminology of “painting” in sixteenth century France.13 The author-practitioner apparently refers to this type of enamel when he notes in an entry on fol. 37v entitled “Glassworker,” that

Those who discovered the invention of working in small works of soft enamels use only esmail d’azur, which is blue, & esmail colombin, which is the color of purple, which they soften with rocaille [small beads of glass?] or leaded glass. As for the yellow, they make it from silver, the red from sanguine, as is said elsewhere, the black & gray & shadows with noir d’escaille [scales black], either strong or weak, the flesh color with clear sanguine. The green is made first from yellow, then on top they coat esmail d’azur, either strong or weak, according to whether they want to make it bright or dark.

While the author-practitioner shows a lively interest in vitreous enamels in this and other entries, his use of esmail in Ms. Fr. 640 demonstrates that there was another category of esmail in the sixteenth century which current scholarship has largely overlooked—“cold” esmail. This term best describes the remainder of the esmail recipes in the manuscript, for which the method described is to mix a powdered pigment into a resinous solution or an oil-based mixture, and then to apply it to a substrate before leaving it to dry.14 Such “cold esmail” is essentially paint, and is not to be confused with the “painted enamel” technique associated with Limoges.

Thus, another term is necessary to differentiate this type of enameling from heat-processed vitreous enamels, and for this, “cold enamel” seems most appropriate as it highlights what is most distinctive about this method. Indeed, in a key contribution Tamar Davidowitz suggested the term “cold enamel” be used to describe translucent paint layers most commonly found on metal substrates.15 Davidowitz concluded that such translucent layers were used to enhance the appearance of the metal substrate, which should show through so that the surface of the metal would still be discernible underneath.16

While rouge clair is translucent in the recipes discussed in Ms. Fr. 640, such as the “Cross of the commanders of Malta” on fol. 40v, the instructions for azure esmail recipes include the recommendation to mix it with lead white (the recipe on fol. 11r recommends this in general, while the recipe on fol. 61v recommends mixing with ceruse for highlights)—which makes it opaque.17 This suggests that the enhancement of an underlying surface was not always the sole aim. This may for instance hint at particular attention to imitating the painted enamels of Limoges which were contemporaneous to the writing of Ms. Fr. 640. Consider for instance that copper had been the typical substrate for vitreous enamels in Europe from the twelfth to the fourteenth century, and blue enamels were the most common support for the “painted enamels” of Limoges produced in the sixteenth century.18 Limoges enamels typically included a base of blue and mulberry-toned enamels (in line with the author-practitioner’s remark on fol. 37v about “esmail d’azur, which is blue, & esmail colombin, which is the color of purple”), but also included opaque enamels, and by the 1630s a new technique called grisaille had been invented which combined them with a white ground to create opaque sculptural effects.19 A recipe on fol. 39v lists many colors of enamel, noting that they all take more readily to copper than to silver, while recipes for opaque blue enamels may suggest a specific interest in imitating the effect of Limoges enamels on copper.

The category of cold enamel may be more literally understood as “painted enamel” than the vitreous enamels associated with sixteenth-century Limoges because such “cold enamels” are essentially oil- or resin-based paints,20 and their ingredients and fabrication resemble those of glazes used for painting.21

Etymology and Antecedents: Émail Brun and Cold Enamels

To understand the relationship between cold enamel and vitreous enamel, it is helpful to look at the etymology of the term “enamel” and the history of its associated objects. The oldest European form of the word “enamel” seems to be the Middle High German word smeltzen (to melt), which was followed by the old French esmail, from which we derive the English “enamel.”22 The vitreous glass form of enamel has been extant for at least 4,000 years,23 so this material history as well as the etymology of the word suggest that the term was originally associated with vitreous enamels and then applied to oil- and resin-based enamels that were capable of achieving a similar glossy and translucent effect.

A possible antecedent to “cold enamel” can be found in the work of twelfth- and thirteenth-century German coppersmiths found on armor in the Kunsthistorisches Museum in Vienna.24 There we find examples of armor that feature gilt ornament on a brown ground that resembles champlevé but which is termed Braunemaille in German or émail brun in French and which does not contain vitreous enamel or champlevé.25 Instead, it is a sort of “encaustic” process, practiced in regions along the Rhine and Maas in Westphalia and Hesse, that involves a coat of linseed oil upon red copper that is then charred in the kiln to a deeper or lighter tint of brown.26

Braunemaille is broadly defined as a medieval technique by which oil varnish (or linseed oil) was burnt into metal. It is present in the Corona Lucis at Buckfast Abbey in Devon, and on the funerary monument of Geoffroy Plantagenêt, count of Anjou Maine (1113–1151), where it appears concurrently with champlevé vitreous enameling.27 While this technique did involve heating, it also demonstrated the possibility of achieving color and translucence without vitreous material, and the use of both these techniques together on earlier medieval objects may offer a clue that it was a technique originally employed as a way of achieving a similar or complementary visual effect, since resin and glass can have very similar reflective indices when the resin is freshly laid, although resin darkens over time. While not definitive, this gives us a clue as to when and how non-vitreous enamels were conceived of as potential alternatives to vitreous ones.

That Braunemaille has been found intact on some early modern suits of armor at the Kunsthistorisches Museum further supports a connection with the tradition of encaustic.28 It may have been related to the fashion of etching designs and then blackening them through niello for contrast. A suggestion that this link might have been seen at the time, and this email-encaustic might have survived into the Baroque is hinted at by a line in John Evelyn’s 1662 Sculptura: “Silver, to fill with a certain Encaustic or black Enamel.” This quotation is listed by the Oxford English Dictionary as one of the early usages of the word “encaustic” which has led to its current definition, and it seems to suggest a time when niello, too, was understood as a type enamel because it was a type of coloring applied to metal for decoration.29 This underscores the fact that the early modern concept of enamel encompassed much more than what we think of as “enamel” today—covering paints and encaustic materials, as well as applications to metal—which have since been differentiated into a variety of terms and categories.

The Language of Heraldic Colors and Armor

Painted enamels have also been used to display heraldic colors, such as on wooden shields.30 Fol. 39v lists many colors of enamels and uses the word gueule (“gules”) and the phrase le rouge quon appelle gueule (“the red called gules”). Gules refers specifically to the red color in heraldry.31 Gueule is derived from the Old French goules, which itself comes from the medieval Latin gulae and is given two etymological derivations, meaning “ermine dyed red,” or “throats.”32

In English, precedents for its use as the name of a heraldic color are evident beginning in the fourteenth century, where it appeared in a short English treatise which states that there are two metals and five colors generally used in coats of arms: sable, gules, azure, vert, and purple (with an additional color called “tawny” being used in France).33 The mid-fifteenth-century Bradfer-Lawrence roll of heraldry lists the ordinary colors of arms as sable, silver, gold, azure, vert, gules, and purple.34 These are the same colors of enamel that are listed in enamel recipes in Ms. Fr. 640 that name many colors, such as on fol. 39v. The author-practitioner’s use of the names azur, gules, and verd may suggest that the recipes for those colors specified in Ms. Fr. 640 are meant to be consistent with widely-accepted color representation in heraldry and standards. There is, perhaps, a further connection between the evolution of cold enamel and heraldry: a fifteenth-century German tradition of painting on shields covered with silver foil.35 At the very least, this German technique offers another example of painting on metal in the history of the decorative arts that bears further investigation.

“Esmail” in Experimental Perspective

Excluding the enamel recipes that mention enamel but do not identify specific ingredients and directions for making esmail, the recipes in the manuscript fall into two categories: directions for making either “azure” or “rouge clair” esmail. In order to better understand what is meant by esmail we recreated multiple recipes for both “rouge clair” and “azure” enamels.

Rouge Clair

There are three entries for rouge clair in Ms. Fr. 640, one recipe that requires heat (fol. 124v) and two cold enamel recipes (fol. 6r and fol. 40v). The recipe on fol. 124v only includes instructions on the application of the enamel and not instructions for how to prepare it. However, it is still worth noting because it focuses on how best to pair red enamel with gold, while fols. 6r and 40v—which address cold enamels—allow for both gold and silver.36

This difference between hot and cold enamels is significant because vitreous red enamels were traditionally paired exclusively with gold.37 Davidowitz states that painting was cheaper and required less specialized skills.38 Yet it should be noted that cold enamels could also exceed their vitreous counterparts in versatility, not just in the delicate forms to which they could be applied, but in the substrates they could pair with—perhaps because their material ingredients were less reactive with their substrates or because of the ease in removing them and retouching them if they were damaged, or if their colors changed with age. According to Gaborit-Chopin, it was a common practice in the first half of the fourteenth century to paint the faces and hands of metal statues, and the ease with which paint could be applied meant that these works could be easily maintained through retouching.39

It seems that red and green were the most translucent colors obtained for cold enamels and that they were frequently painted on silver, while blue was not—a significant fact to note since red vitreous enamel was usually paired with gold, and blue vitreous enamel with silver.40 For instance, with hot enamels, the sixteenth-century goldsmith Benvenuto Cellini (1500–1571) finds it impossible to enamel the beautiful red smalto roggio or rogio chlero onto silver or copper, though it can be done on gold. Notably, he warns that if the red enamel is not immediately cooled with bellows, the red color may turn yellow so that it does not stand out against the gold.41 Yet, the author-practitioner provides a similar warning for his rouge clair recipe on fol. 124v, which mentions heating it to restore its color and cautions that it will fade if applied to pure gold because it will seem too pale. He does not give the same warning for the cold enamel rouge clair recipes, saying that they will pair with copper and silver, showing a significant difference in the practices of applying hot and cold enamels in terms of versatility.

In our experiments, rouge clair appeared brightest against gold. Those samples that we applied to silver darkened as they dried so that only the lighter rouge clair, with a higher ratio of resin to pigment still displayed the refractive qualities that vitreous enamels and paint glazes are known for. This is to say, the translucence allows light to penetrate these “enamels,” reflecting on the metal substrate and producing a radiant effect. We only tested this against copper and gilt pieces of silver foil and gold leaf, so we did not see the effect on a three dimensional object; we would assume that on a vertical silver surface the occurrence of a thick pigment layer would be less problematic since the cold enamel layer would have to be applied very thinly to prevent running, or it would ruin the delicacy of the work.

As with sixteenth-century vitreous Limoges enamels, the substrate for painting metal with oil-based translucent paints was often copper.42 The author-practitioner says on fol. 6r that paints take better to copper than silver in general, although it seems azure cold enamels were also frequently used on silver and gold, as was the case in the work of the famed German goldsmith and lifecaster Wenzel Jamnitzer (1507/1508–1585).43

Azure Enamels

In our reconstructions of azure enamel, based on fol. 11r, “Painting esmail d’azur in oil” (Fig. 3), we found that the azure enamels most closely resembled vitreous enamel when mixed with oil (Fig. 4), however the author-practitioner instructs on fol. 11r to take the “most delicate” esmail d’azur “& grind it with ceruse, which binds it.” Our reconstruction showed that the addition of lead white created an entirely different optical effect from the oil binder.44 Esmail d’azur mixed with lead white created an opaque pigment when applied to a copper support (Fig. 5). Such an application is apparently also referenced in the entry “For coloring stamped trunks” on fol 7r:

The stamping of sheets of copper or latten is made on engraved & carved service tree wood, if one wants to spend less. And next, the stamped object is colored with the aforesaid colors of lake, verdet, azur d’esmail, & tempered in turpentine varnish. But in the place you want to azure, lay down fer blanc, which is more appropriate for an azure background.

While azur d’esmail is mentioned as being applied to numerous substrates besides metal, including glass, crystal, wood, and canvas, we observed that the azure cold enamels were brightest on copper.

Lifecasting and Delicate Forms

The esmail described in Ms. Fr. 640 is intended for application to metal surfaces such as gilt, shaped, and cast items, but also to surfaces such as glass and hard stones. While the absence of heating may at first seem strange since the author-practitioner was writing during the peak popularity of basse taille enameling in Limoges, it may be useful to consider this absence in the context of the author-practitioner’s familiarity with lifecasting. While the practice of cold enameling was not limited to lifecasting, cold enamels were often applied to delicate areas that would have been difficult to color with vitreous enamels, in order to enhance the lifelikeness of the object.45 In particular, vitreous enameling requires intense heat that can adversely affect gilding and is usually not suitable for delicate forms that can lose their shape under high heat. Cold enamels, on the other hand, could be applied to very thin and fine works, as can be seen in a table ornament by Wenzel Jamnitzer (Fig. 6).46

Delicacy would seem to be a concern for the author-practitioner as he includes recipes for applying esmail to gold leaf (fol. 136r) and imitation gold leaf (fol. 6r). However, when discussing “A way to enamel very delicate gold rose leaves and others” (fol. 116r), which would seem to be close to the description of the fine vegetal forms seen in Jamnitzer’s table ornament, the author-practitioner seems to suggest another type of esmail since he writes:

After you have cast or beaten in gold the thin leaves of a rose or other things, if you want to enamel them, you need to solder or braze your delicate gold leaves onto silver strips, which will strengthen them to support the enamel. Next, once the thing has been enameled, put the work in aquafortis, which will eat away the silver & leave all the gold with its enamel. For this, the gold needs to be passed with ciment real.

However, the author-practitioner also mentions applying esmail to burnished gold leaf and silver foil surfaces (fol. 6r) which may suggest a flat surface such as a panel painting or altarpiece, like the 1466 Herlin Altarpiece (St. Jakob’s church, Rothenburg ob der Tauber), or a wooden sculpture covered in foil.47 Rather than being a description of a material or a technique, esmail seems to indicate coloring a hard surface by applying paint without ground layers.

At the same time, it appears to have been accepted terminology with regard to ceramics as the equally well-known French lifecasting ceramicist Bernard Palissy referred to his glazes as esmail.48 Although the process of ceramic glazing and firing is not described in Ms. Fr. 640, we should take it into account when considering the range of meanings applied to esmail.

Imitation Gems

Additionally, the author-practitioner presents another use of esmail in relation to imitating and enhancing gems: specifically, in a recipe titled “Enameling a cornaline” (fol. 104r). This practice is in line with a long tradition of dyeing and heat-treating gems in order to enhance their color, which is mentioned at least as far back as Pliny and in the sixteenth century in Giambattista della Porta’s 1558 work Magia Naturalis, in a chapter on the dyeing of stones. Although the recipe in Ms. Fr. 640 is about “enameling a cornaline,” it does not seem to have any ingredients in common with the red and blue esmail that is meant to be applied to metal; instead, it calls for a heat treatment (fol. 104r):

Soak some wheat flower [sic] in white wine, then besmear & cover the whole cornaline with it. And bring the violet kind to heat again, next put it to temper in white wine for one or two hours, then scratch it & leave the part you want to save on it, then reload it a little more on top.

This suggests that within the manuscript, esmail is used to describe processes that may or may not include heat, and that enamel is understood to describe the glass-like visual effect rather than the materials or processes involved.

Taken together with the imitation gem enamel recipes, the esmail recipes imply that the author-practitioner had an interest in hot esmail and transformation for the sake of adornment (either with jewels or with buttons), as well as an interest in producing these adornments through inexpensive and artificial means. He seems to have been more concerned with creating artificial gems that could pass a visual test rather than ones that could stand up to close expert scrutiny. Although the lapidary work of Swiss naturalist and physician Conrad Gessner (1516–1565) is among book titles listed on the first two folios of the manuscript, the author-practitioner’s observations seem less driven by Gessner’s interests in natural philosophy and more similar in tone to the anonymous recipe, “To make stones for rings, that is to say, precious gems clear and of fine color,” found in the Bolognese manuscript “Ad Lapides Anullorum Componendos.”49 There does not seem to be any thought given in Ms. Fr. 640 to the idea that the imitation gems might be tested by weight or hardness, or even rubbed or scratched to see if the color was consistent beneath the surface, leaving the impression they would only have been looked at superficially from a distance, perhaps applied on objects, rather than being scrutinized for value and sale.

As Marco Beretta, Maria Conforti, and Marjolijn Bol have discussed, an imitation was not necessarily a fake or a forgery, but sometimes considered an object made with a higher form of skill because it demonstrated an understanding of nature and the ability to recreate it.50 However, unlike lapidaries, such as that of Persian medieval polymath Al Beruni (973–1048) or German medieval philosopher Albertus Magnus (1200–1280),51 the author-practitioner does not seem to be recording impressions of the properties and uses of different stones. It seems unlikely, therefore, that he believes that he is actually recreating them; rather, he is outdoing nature by obtaining the desirable aspects of the things he is imitating (their visual effects) through different methods on a compressed timescale, circumventing nature’s ponderous course through the cleverness of artifice.52

Conclusions

Esmail was a term both for a material and a set of material techniques that was widely used in the early modern period. The term esmail encompassed many meanings, which, at their most basic, all related to covering a substrate in a colorful substance that would leave it with a glossy and refractive finish on the surface. Cold enameling—a technique imitating the refractive and coloring effects of vitreous enamel—has largely been forgotten because of an evolution in the nomenclature of art categories. Sharing its name with a substance that we now typically think of as vitreous heat-process enameling, cold enameling actually bears strong resemblance to glazes in painting, and it shares many ingredients with varnishes. It may rightly be described as a type of paint; however, that would fail to capture the goal of cold enameling. Although cold enameling may have begun as a way to imitate the refractive indices of vitreous enamels, it surpassed them in versatility, ease of control, and potential for applying a variety of colors to different substrates.

In his recipes, the author-practitioner demonstrates a knowledge of both hot and cold enamels and the methods required for the making of each, while showing a stronger interest and potentially a preference for cold enamels. Regarding the uses of cold enamels, he describes ways to add realism and naturalism to delicate objects, depict colorful stories on glass, and recreate the dazzle of expensive gems, while exploiting the natural advantages of economically less valuable materials. This points to an interest in identifying the essence of visual effects inherent to one material in order to capture them and spirit them across material borders by recreating them in other media.

The author-practitioner’s particular interest in cold enamels supports the possibility that he was a lifecaster himself, potentially of small and delicate works, and affirms his attention to a variety of optical qualities and his determination to recreate them across media. This is a pursuit that may be thought of as an interest in isolating what is inherent to, or definitive of, a particular material’s optical qualities in order to understand what it is that actually produces certain visual effects—a form of “practical optics.” In the realm of esmail, recognizing this interest can help us to better apprehend why the author-practitioner would have considered cold esmail as part of the category of esmail and help us to reconsider how materials were understood and categories were defined in the sixteenth century.
