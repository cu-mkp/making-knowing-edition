Molding Grasshoppers and Things Too Thin

Abstract

The entry “Molding grasshoppers and things too thin” on fol. 142v describes one of the many processes of molding and casting in Ms. Fr. 640 that range from large, heavy weapons such as cannons, to small, delicate objects like flowers, insects, and even paper. This essay focuses on our close reading and hands-on reconstruction of casting “things too thin,” specifically, paper, pansies, and butterflies. We consider the properties of the various materials with which the author-practitioner experimented in order to add thickness to and strengthen delicate elements of these thin things. We also reflect on our own experience of learning to work with these materials according to their own dynamic characteristics.

Introduction

The recipe “Molding grasshoppers and things too thin” on fol. 142v is one of the many processes of molding and casting described in BnF Ms. Fr. 640 that range from large, heavy weapons such as cannons, to small, delicate objects like flowers and insects, and even paper. Indeed, throughout the manuscript, the author-practitioner is preoccupied with the scale of such objects and the challenge of casting them. Such delicate and ephemeral things as grasshoppers, butterflies, and flowers on fol. 142v are associated with transience, and, in the case of butterflies, also bring to mind processes of metamorphosis. Judging by the number of entries on casting delicate, ephemeral objects in metal, the author-practitioner desired to transform and fix them into a more durable form.

In many entries on casting delicate plants from life, he experiments with combinations of sands and binders that transform ordinary materials into carriers of the most delicate impressions. In the entry on fol. 142v, he discusses how to create the models from which to make the mold for metal and how to give these thin things enough substance to be cast, and how to most effectively create molds. These kinds of recipes in Ms. Fr. 640 often focus on medal making and lifecasting, and they constitute a significant portion of the overall work.1 Indeed, according to Smith and Beentjes, both types of casting “involve extensive knowledge of, and experimentation with, mold materials and metal alloys, and both aim for a very fine surface impression that demonstrates the maker’s expertise and virtuosity.”2

In his investigations of sands and binders, and in these particular investigations in the entry on fol. 142v, the author-practitioner found it necessary to coordinate and combine diverse materials at different stages of molding and casting because objects of different sizes and densities require a variety of techniques and molding materials as well as different metals for casting. The author-practitioner’s instructions and observations in this entry reflect his knowledge acquired through experience and hands-on trial and error. Our reconstruction of the recipe for “things too thin” illuminates this underlying workshop rationale.

This essay focuses on our reconstruction of casting “things too thin” on fol. 142v, specifically, paper, pansies, and butterflies. Through our reconstruction of these three things, we consider the role of the materials that the author-practitioner experimented with in order to add thickness to delicate elements and thus improve their “castability.” This particular entry is noteworthy for the fact that the author-practitioner discusses different methods for treating the delicate parts of animals and plants (i.e., wings of a grasshopper or butterfly, and pansy petals) prior to casting from life. This recipe on fol. 142v also connects to other recipes throughout the manuscript in which three particular materials are mentioned that serve to thicken “thin things”: butter, oil, and wax.3 Interestingly, the author-practitioner contradicts himself about the efficacy of butter and oil,4 and this inconsistency within the manuscript caught our attention. Hence, our exploration of its presence and significance informs our own experimental process in our reconstruction of casting the “thin things” from fol. 142v. Thus, this essay investigates ambiguities in the use and description of thickening agents. We explore different characteristics of these thickeners through close reading and hands-on reconstruction of the processes described on fol. 142v for molding paper, pansies, and a butterfly. Our exploration helps us to consider how the author-practitioner sought to reanimate the thin things he casts in metal. Our essay unfolds over three parts: molding paper, molding pansies, and molding a butterfly. Each section has two parts: our close reading and textual interpretation of the recipe, and our reconstruction.

Paper: “a written paper to mold that is too thin”

Interpretation

If you have a written paper to mold that is too thin, after you have made the first cast & it has set, give a little thickness to the reverse of your paper with melted butter, which is the most appropriate means there is....

[Note appearing in upper left margin:] If you write on paper or on common carton & if your letter is of gum, the humidity of the clay slab or the wetted sand for noyau will moisten your letter & undo it. Therefore write with cinnabar wetted with oil on oiled paper & press in.

In these passages from fol. 142v, the author-practitioner offers advice about how to create a piece of writing on paper that can be molded and subsequently cast in metal. The writing needs to withstand the wetness of the molding materials (packed clay or wet sand), and the paper must be given additional thickness. Both of these points address the challenge of protecting the model from the potential dangers arising during the molding process.

We could find no other mention of techniques for molding paper among contemporary sources that address casting, such as Vannoccio Biringuccio’s De la pirotechnia (1540), Benvenuto Cellini’s treatises on goldsmithing and sculpture (1568), and Hugh Plat’s Jewell House of Art and Nature (1594). Given this lack of sources that might explain the recipe, we asked the following questions of Ms. Fr. itself, and particularly of the two entries on fols. 131r and 142v that treat the molding of paper:

  1. Why should writing be made of cinnabar mixed with oil, and what kind of oil should be used?

  2. What kind of paper is used here, and how it should be pretreated before molding?

  3. How can the written paper be molded and cast?

We began with writing on the paper. We reconstructed the two methods mentioned by the author-practitioner: writing with gum and writing with cinnabar mixed with oil. In his note on this section, he rejects the method that uses gum because the wetness of the molding material “will moisten your letter & undo it.” Even so, gum appears to be an important material elsewhere in Ms. Fr. 640. It is mentioned in many recipes throughout the manuscript, most of which are pigment related, and the varieties called for include gum arabic, sandarac gum, gum amoniacum, and tragacanth gum. These different gums primarily function as water-soluble binding agents for pigment, and the term “gum water” (gum diluted with water) is used several times in recipes for pigments.

To understand why the author-practitioner would be opposed to using gum for molding paper, we looked at the other references to gum in the manuscript. From these entries, we inferred that it was most likely the water solubility that made it an inadequate substance for this type of molding. But there is an entry on fol. 131r, “Letters and molded paper” which calls for writing that uses “ancre bien gommé” (well gummed ink). This entry advises writing with well gummed ink, “or with any color which has body & which does not erase once moistened with eau-de-vie.”5

After examining the role of gum, we turned to consider the recipe’s use of cinnabar mixed with oil as a writing substance. What type of oil does the author-practitioner intend the reader to use? Many different oils appear in Ms. Fr. 640, including turpentine oil, walnut oil, linseed oil, and spike lavender oil, among others, which provide possibilities for the kind of oil he refers to on fol. 142v.

We then considered what type of paper to use, and what “oiled paper” might mean.6 We used reconstructed early modern book paper that was already present in the Making and Knowing lab.7

We turned next to the question of how to mold paper. Both entries apparently suggest a two-piece mold: fol. 142v mentions “the first cast”) and fol. 131r instructs to “cast on one side & the other.” They both give instructions to “place your paper on a clay slab” (fol. 131r) and to “press in” (fol. 142v), then to “moisten it with eau-de-vie” (fol. 131r). Fol. 142v adds extensive commentary on adding a thickening agent to thin things: “give a little thickness to the reverse of your paper with melted butter, which is the most appropriate means there is.” We concluded from these comments that the paper with writing on it should be molded in the same way as animals and plants are molded and cast from life, that is in two-piece molds, with a release agent applied to the model (the eau-de-vie), and delicate parts thickened with butter.

This close reading of the entry for “Molding grasshoppers and things too thin” on fol. 142v provided us with information to design our hands-on reconstruction:

  1. Pigment-related recipes in the manuscript can be used to prepare cinnabar mixed with oil.

  2. The oil to be mixed with cinnabar might be found among the plant oils mentioned in the pigment or the varnish recipes.

  3. The oil coating on the paper is intended to make the paper water-resistant and durable.

  4. The paper should be cast in the same way that animals and plants are cast from life.

The first question we undertook to answer in our reconstruction was whether oiled paper can withstand the plaster molding process, and the second question was which oil to use with cinnabar.8

Reconstruction

To determine the difference between oiled and non-oiled paper when it comes into contact with “wetted sand,” that is, liquid plaster, we placed two small pieces of paper, one without oil and one with oil applied to both sides, into wet plaster and left them there for five minutes (Fig. 1). The one without oil was completely wet with plaster clinging to the surface of the paper. In contrast, the oiled paper resisted the wet plaster, and its surface came out smooth with only a little plaster clinging to it (Fig. 2). Not surprisingly, the oil rendered the paper more water resistant.

To determine which type of oil to use for this reconstruction, we investigated varnish and pigment entries and decided to use three types of oil: turpentine oil, wheat oil, and linseed oil. As a comparison, we also decided to try the same combination of ink and gum that Raymond Carlson and Jordan Katz used in their trial reconstruction of the entry on fol. 131r, “Letters and Molded Paper.”9 (Safety considerations necessitated that we modify the author-practitioner’s recommended combination of cinnabar and oil to substitute a Kremer pigment Venice Red for the cinnabar.)10

We drew the Chinese character for Taoism 道 (Fig. 3) and applied to it four different combinations of pigment and binder:

  1. Ink (Fig. 4) and tragacanth gum powder (Fig. 5) (10 ml of ink and 2 tsp of gum powder)

  2. Venice Red (Fig. 6) and Windsor & Newton turpentine oil (distilled turpentine) (2 tsp of Venice Red pigment and 10 ml of turpentine oil)

  3. Venice Red and wheat oil (Fig. 7) (2 tsp of Venice Red and 10 ml of wheat oil)

  4. Venice Red and linseed oil (2 tsp of Venice Red and 10 ml of linseed oil)

Our first experiment with ink and tragacanth gum powder came out precisely the same as Carlson and Katz’s experiment from Fall 2014. The gum is viscous when mixed with the ink. But no matter how much we stirred it, the mixed liquid remained sticky and uneven (Fig. 8). As a result (and just as Carlson and Katz found), it is hard to apply on the paper. Our reconstruction confirmed the author-practitioner’s assertion that this combination is not desirable, since the ink-gum mixture made our rendering of the Chinese character on the paper lumpy (Fig. 9), which we recognized would not cast well.

Venice Red pigment and turpentine oil worked better than ink and gum. The pigment and the oil mixed with each other evenly (Fig. 10). When applied to the paper, this mixture did not rise very high off the paper, and it dried quickly. When dry, it began to flake off the paper (Fig. 11). Based on this, we determined this combination was not sticky and viscous enough for casting.

Compared to the preceding two trials, Venice Red and wheat oil proved better. The pigment dissolved in the wheat oil very well. This mixture proved viscous enough to hold our Chinese character to the paper, and the pigment did not flake off when dry. When stirring this mixture with the paintbrush, we could feel its resistance against the brush much more than with the two preceding mixtures. When we lifted it with the paintbrush (Fig. 12), it clung to the paintbrush for about ten seconds, a sign of its viscosity (Fig. 13). The last mixture of Venice Red and linseed oil came out as well as the pigment and wheat oil combination, with almost the same viscosity (Fig. 14, Fig. 15). We thus concluded that wheat oil or linseed oil are the best options. Their only disadvantage was that the character dried out much more slowly, and oil started to permeate the paper (visible on the left in Fig. 14).

Our reconstructions enabled us to answer two questions: First, oiled paper can resist the wetness of the plaster. Second, pigment mixed with wheat oil or with linseed oil works better than with turpentine oil, and the ink and gum mixture is unsuitable for use in writing on paper for molding. When combined with Venice Red pigment, both wheat and linseed oils showed good consistency and viscosity, and created a convex surface to the writing, which appeared ideal for casting.

Next, we turned to making a two-piece mold. To do this, we laid our paper with the Chinese character on a clay base and constructed a sprue system (to facilitate the flow of metal when it is poured into the mold), then we poured the plaster.11 Forty minutes later, the plaster having set, we stripped off the clay wall and base and turned the mold over to prepare it for the second cast. Before this next cast, we followed the author-practitioner’s instructions on fol. 142v to apply melted butter to the back of the paper in order to add to its thickness. Then we baked the mold, melting out the wax (Fig. 16, Fig. 17). We then opened the mold to clean out the remaining wax in the gate and sprues (Fig. 18), and then luted it together (with a plaster mixture), and, finally, poured the metal (Fig. 19). When we opened the mold, we finally saw the outcome of our casting (Fig. 20). The metal failed to flow through the entire mold, however, it did show proof of concept, namely, that writing on paper could be molded and cast in a two-piece plaster mold by following the process on fol. 142v. This failure was nevertheless productive because it drove us back to the manuscript to look for further information, which helped us in our following trials of molding and casting pansies and a butterfly.12 We reflect on this experience in our conclusion.

Pansies: “For giving thickness to a pansy or other flowers”

Interpretation

For giving thickness to a pansy or other flowers, butter is not good, but rather wheat oil, which is soon dry & holds firm. Wax would not be appropriate here for it is too hot once melted, & it makes the thing to which it is applied contract. But butter is amiable and handleable.

This advice on fol. 142v about molding and casting pansies is part of a larger preoccupation in Ms. Fr. 640 with techniques for the molding and casting of live flowers and foliage. As treated in the essay on molding roses by Carlson and Katz,13 lifecasting was common in sixteenth-century Europe, and extant examples come from the workshops of Bernard Palissy (ca. 1510–1589) and Wenzel Jamnitzer (1508–1585). Palissy mentions in Architecture, et Ordennance that he had molded from life “Scolopendrium, adiantum, maidenhair, polytrichum, polypody and other species of plants appropriate to this affair.”14 Lifecast ceramic works by Palissy and his workshop also include small plants like plantain, sage, rosemary, lavender, wheat, and oats, but Palissy provided no specifics for the technique in his written works.15 The extant oeuvre of Wenzel Jamnitzer, including a bouquet of flowers in a table piece of 1549 (Rijksmuseum, Amsterdam), a writing box with lifecast animals and plants (Kunsthistorisches Museum, Vienna), and a set of individual cast plants attributed to Jamnitzer (Germanisches Nationalmuseum, Nuremberg), are of extraordinary delicacy and speak to his virtuoso craftsmanship; however, he does not discuss his process for lifecasting in writing.16 Hugh Plat’s Jewell House of Art and Nature, a recipe compilation contemporary with Ms. Fr. 640, mentions techniques for molding herbs and flowers, but Plat does not relate techniques for strengthening delicate parts of the plants.17 Ms. Fr. 640 appears to be the sole textual description of how to thicken and strengthen the fragile parts of plants before casting them.18

In Ms. Fr. 640, the author-practitioner mentions three kinds of thickening agents for lifecasting. He explicitly excludes wax, likely because its melting point is between 62 and 65℃, and this heat could cause delicate plants to wilt. As the author-practitioner states on fol. 142v, “wax would not be appropriate here for it is too hot once melted, & it makes the thing to which it is applied contract.” Instead, he regards wheat oil as a proper choice because “wheat oil [...] is soon dry & holds firm.” “Butter is amiable and handleable,” but it is not suitable for pansies and other flowers. He recommends butter, however, to “give a little thickness to the reverse of your paper [...] which is the most appropriate means there is, & for fortifying the wings of either a butterfly or a grasshopper, or some delicate part of an animal to which you need to give thickness.”

To understand better the effects of butter and wheat oil, we consulted several other recipes in the manuscript. In a marginal note appended to a recipe for making “Wheat oil” on fol. 110v, the author-practitioner states,

If you want to mold something delicate, like a pansy, some, to give it a little thickness, more than what is natural, some rub it with butter. But it is best to anoint it with wheat oil, for it has no body, & does not obstruct the small lineaments as much, and makes the flower firm.

The author-practitioner prefers wheat oil over butter because it has no body, and thus does not cover over the delicate details of the flower. A recipe for “Molded roses” on fol. 129r also emphasizes the drying quality of wheat oil, aligning with the comments on fol. 142v:

They [roses] are awkward to mold because the petals are very fine & weak & doubled. But, to obl avoid this, one needs to anoint them with wheat oil, which is very dessicative. And having quickly dried, it firms & stiffens the petals to be able to separate them & withstand the tempered sand. The same is done with flies, with pansies & similar delicate things, with flowers from the caper plant.

In a passage contradicting this on fol. 154v entitled “Reinforcing flowers and delicate things,” however, he states that butter rather than wheat oil should be used to strengthen flowers:

For flowers [&] delicate things & herbage, one does not use wheat oil but melted butter, of which one gives a light coat on the back of the petals of the rose flower & the pansy & other flowers that need it. But one reinforces the little legs of flies & other small animals with wheat oil to strengthen them & make them hold up.

Despite these contradictions, we can conclude that wheat oil dries faster and holds firm, and it does not have the bulk (the “corps,” or body) of butter, thus preserving the delicate details of the plant.19

Reconstruction

For this experiment, we used four pansies from the university campus flower beds to test the effects of butter and wheat oil. We split them into two groups:

  1. Group A: two white pansies to experiment with butter.

  2. Group B: two purple pansies to experiment with wheat oil.

We found it difficult to cover only the back of the petals, because once butter or wheat oil was applied to the back of one petal, the front side of another petal behind it immediately adhered to it. Both butter and wheat oil soaked through both sides of the petals. In Group B (wheat oil), the petals of the purple pansies became heavier after completely absorbing the oil, and they could no longer stand on their own. The petals also crumpled. The wheat oil, which was supposed to “dry quickly and hold firm,” exhibited the opposite effect (Fig. 21). In Group A (butter), the petals of the white pansies stiffened immediately after being coated on the underside with a fine layer of butter (Fig. 22). But this only lasted for 20 minutes, and, just as with the purple pansies, the petals on the white variety also drooped (Fig. 23). Given the time it took for the pansies to wilt, it is possible that the white pansies simply became dehydrated and withered on their own.

Reflecting on our experiment, we hypothesized that our failure might be attributable to the characteristics of this cultivated pansy, which has large floppy petals in comparison to wild pansies. Therefore, for our second experiment we decided to use a different type of pansies with smaller and thicker petals, and we obtained a living plant from a local florist. We cut four flowers from this plant to experiment with and also split them into two groups:

  1. Group A: Two pansies to experiment with butter.

  2. Group B: Two pansies to experiment with wheat oil.

This time we heated the wheat oil a little bit to make it thinner and applied it to Group B when it had cooled to 27℃. Unfortunately, it failed again, and by the time we finished building the infrastructure of sprues, the pansies had completely wilted and curled up (Fig. 24). We decided to give up experimenting with wheat oil and to try melted beeswax on the other pansy in Group B. This also proved problematic, as the beeswax solidified quickly, and we had to keep reheating it. It did not adversely affect the pansies, but it added a rather thick layer underneath the petals, which would cost some details when cast in metal (Fig. 25).

As for Group A, butter worked well at first, and we could see clearly that the petals with a layer of butter were stiffer. But after the infrastructure of gate and sprues was built and a clay wall was rolled out and wrapped around the clay base, the buttered pansies wilted as well (Fig. 26). At this point, we realized that speed would be of the essence for a successful casting, a factor not referenced in the recipe but discovered through our own process of reconstruction.

After our second failure, we decided to save time by not constructing the sprues attached to the petals. Based on our experiences during skillbuilding, metal would flow through the sprues with only three sprues attached to the gate.20 In this way, we were able to apply butter and wheat oil to the petals right before pouring the plaster. Judging from the final result, only the pansy with a wax coating worked effectively (Fig. 27), while the buttered pansy and the oiled pansy wilted and were distorted in the mold (Fig. 28). It could be that butter and wheat oil failed to make them strong enough to withstand the plaster or that the metal failed to fill the entire plant without the additional sprues.

Our reconstruction showed both wheat oil and butter, which the author-practitioner recommended or discussed in different entries in the manuscript, to be unsuccessful. In contrast, wax, which the author-practitioner rejected, turned out to be a good material for thickening the pansy. However, while wax worked well as a thickening agent, it failed to capture the details of the petals, just as the author-practitioner suggested. That might be the reason he rejected wax, since the primary goal of molding thin things is to replicate precisely the fine details of the original object. In this sense, wax is not a good option.

Our second unsuccessful reconstruction raised questions about the suitability of our reconstruction materials: according to the author-practitioner, wheat oil should dry fast when applied to the flower. In our case, however, the wheat oil had not yet dried when we poured the plaster. The author-practitioner recommends butter for the underside of petals, however, for us, it wilted and deformed the pansies. In 2008, however, Pamela Smith had successfully cast a pansy flower in silver that had been coated on the underside with butter (Fig. 29, Fig. 30, Fig. 31).21 Further experimentation is thus necessary to test and understand more fully the instructions of the author-practitioner with regard to wheat oil and butter.

Butterfly: “for fortifying the wings of ... a butterfly”

Interpretation

give a little thickness to the reverse of your paper with melted butter, which is the most appropriate means there is, & for fortifying the wings of either a butterfly or a grasshopper, or some delicate part of an animal to which you need to give thickness. But take heed to apply this melted butter underneath the wing or in whichever place it cannot be seen.

Molding a butterfly appears to have been interesting to the author-practitioner because it poses a material and structural challenge, as this excerpt from fol. 142v and several other recipes for delicate things in his manuscript attest. The creatures he mentions for casting all possess diverse “surfaces.” For instance, he suggests molding lizards, snakes, crustaceans (such as crabs and crayfish), turtles, birds, bats, rats, and such insects as beetles, spiders, flies, and butterflies—he even provides a recipe for casting spider webs. In addition, as Pamela Smith has suggested, many of these creatures inhabited more than one elemental zone within the early modern view of the cosmos—the lizards, snakes, toads, frogs, and crabs live both on earth and in water, while insects and birds inhabited both air and earth. Significantly, many of the animals preferred for lifecasting—flies, beetles, frogs, snakes, and lizards—were also regarded as spontaneously generated from putrefying matter. Because of their liminal place between elemental zones and between processes of coming into being and passing away, these creatures were resonant with meaning in early modern culture.22

The butterfly, too, was a meaningful creature: the butterfly transforms from caterpillar crawling the earth into a colorful winged insect that flies effortlessly in the air. Butterflies live only a short span. They symbolized both metamorphosis and impermanence in early modern Europe. As such, they fit well into the group of meaningful creatures used for molding and casting “things too thin;” however, the author-practitioner mentions none of this, straying explicitly into the symbolic meaning of an animal only once, in an entry for catching snakes on fol. 109r–v, where he notes,

Also, I have noticed that when they [snakes] want to bite or eat something, they do not bite with a direct attack but with sinuous turns & from the side, as do Satan & his disciples. It has a small head but a very long body. The entryway to sin seems small & inconsequential, but the consequences of it are very great.

He quickly moves on from his symbolic musings, however, to further observe of the snake:

It abstains from eating seven or eight days, once it has devoured some frog. It can swallow three or four of them, one after the other, and what it devoured is neither corrupted nor consumed in one go in its stomach, but p some part little by little, that is to say bones and everything. And the remains are found as fresh as when it had devoured them, such that sometimes, when one presses & torments it, it renders up what it has engulfed, parts of which are found totally consumed & others as fresh as if it were alive. It can keep a frog engulfed for two to three hours & renders it up completely alive (fol. 109v).

Similarly, in the entry on fol. 142v, the author-practitioner focuses not on symbolic associations but on very practical techniques for molding those very thinnest of things—the wings of a butterfly. Given the brevity of the information about this subject—a single reference to the use of butter—we consulted related recipes in the manuscript.

The butterfly has hairs on its body and fragile antennas. In addition to thickening the delicate parts, butter and wheat oil are supposed to help flatten the hairy parts of an animal, which can otherwise become difficult to manage. The author-practitioner mentions this several times. On fol. 110v he advises wheat oil “for anointing the hair of a butterfly or similar thing, for this oil is instantly dry & makes the remainder dry out.” It is “necessary that the hair or down of any animal that you want to mold be flat, for being upright, it would elevate the sand & become porous.” On fol. 124r, the entry entitled “Animals with hair and fragile and very thin flowers” addresses the problem of hairy creatures, mentioning butterflies again, and issuing a challenge:

Animals with hair are awkward to mold because the hair is raised & is represented as though mixed up & to clumped together. Starting, one ought to keep it flat with something dessicative & that makes it firmer. And in this, the most singular thing is wheat oil, with which you will anoint it. Once cast, you will be able to repair it. The bodies of butterflies and plants that have a stem & leaves which are wooly with a certain capricious & downy hair, also need to be anointed with the same oil, to keep this down flat; likewise flowers that have very delicate & thin leaves, for dry wheat oil makes them rigid and firm. And if someone brags about molding what is presented to them, give them to mold the downy head of the plant called dandelion or a pappus, which comes from the seeds of burdock & flies away at the slightest sigh of wind.

Similarly, on both fol. 129v in “Spider molded on a leaf,” and fol. 130r , he mentions using wheat oil to lay hairs down flat and make them firmer because they would otherwise become entangled in the plaster. On fol. 152r, in a densely written, full-page entry on “Molding a rat,” he runs through several different alternatives, noting in the margin that “all feathers & all hair is awkward to mold according to its nature.” He first states that

the body should be anointed with wheat oil, which will keep the hair stiff & couched, so that the sand will not raise it by getting entangled in it to prevent coming out neatly. The wheat oil being dry, which will be quick, you will be able to pass a very thin comb to divide it, in order that it appears in the cast. On a small mouse, you can even put some common oil to lower the hair.

But he then turns to the opinions of others:

Some make the rat shed, others anoint it with olive oil, which is amiable, for wheat oil is thick & has too much body. This is why one scarcely uses it, only to give some feature to a fly foot or under a wing to keep them rigid. Others couch the hair with an egg yolk, which is quickly dry & amiable.

In the margin of fol. 152r, he writes, “butter for anointing hairy animals is better e than wheat oil, because it is more amiable.” And, on 154v, in “Reinforcing flowers and delicate things,” he returns once more to the problem:

For flowers [&] delicate things & herbage, one does not use wheat oil but melted butter, of which one gives a light coat on the back of the petals of the rose flower & the pansy & other flowers that need it. But one reinforces the little legs of flies & other small animals with wheat oil to strengthen them & make them hold up.

Despite contradictions, these comments establish some of the desirable characteristics of materials in molding and casting “things too thin”: quick drying is wanted, like that possible using wheat oil or egg yolk. Stiffening and firming is also needed to “strengthen” and make hairs more distinct. Amiability, which seems to mean something like “user friendly,” is also sought. Wheat oil does not seem to be amiable, while butter and olive oil are. In addition to its drying properties, wheat oil is useful for its lack of body, allowing delicate surface details to show through the coat of oil (fol. 142v), but the author-practitioner contradicts this in molding a rat on fol. 152r, where he maintains that “wheat oil is thick & has too much body.”

Our next concern in molding the butterfly was how to mold it: should it be done in a one-piece or two-piece mold? There are no detailed instructions about how to mold a butterfly in Ms. Fr. 640, so we studied other lifecasting entries. Animals, including lizards, crayfish, crabs, beetles, spiders, and flies, are generally cast in two-piece molds. Flowers, by contrast, are cast in one-piece molds. A rose is actually cast in two molds—the blossom in a one-piece mold and the leaves in a two-piece mold.23 A butterfly differs from other small creatures in its planar surfaces; it is in fact similar to paper with raised letters, thus it makes sense that the author-practitioner included it alongside instructions for molding paper on fol. 142v.

Reconstruction

In casting the butterfly, we first tried a two-piece mold. After applying wheat oil to the wings and body of the butterfly, we laid it out on a piece of clay. We pressed the butterfly against the clay to affix it to the clay wall. Then we used four pins to pierce the edge of its wings in order to fix them onto the clay. However, after the application of the wheat oil, the butterfly’s wings seemed unable to resist the wetness of the oil and they became transparent and even more delicate. Moreover, the wheat oil failed to adhere to the clay, making it hard for the butterfly to rest flat on the clay. Finally, the pins tore the now even more delicate wings, and they disintegrated.

After this first failure, we reconsidered the viability of a two-piece mold for casting a butterfly, but we also considered the state of the butterfly itself, as we had used a desiccated specimen from the gift store of the American Museum of Natural History (Fig. 32).

We were forced to think about how the dessicated state of the butterfly might have influenced the cast. The author-practitioner discusses how to catch, keep, and kill animals used for casting, repeatedly making the point that animals should generally be killed just before molding. For example, on fol. 110r, he says about snakes, “one ought to put it to death only a quarter of an hour before you want to mold.” Similarly, flies should be used “as soon as you can after they are dead, because if you were to leave them to dry a lot, their legs would break when you want to spread them out” (fol. 156v). A different strategy had to be used, however, for turtles and rats: for turtles, he notes that “One ought not to mold them as soon as they are dead because they are still too stiff. But on the following day, you will manipulate them & fold their legs as you wish” (fol. 143r), and for rats and mice, he advises on fol. 152r to mold immediately after killing the animal or, if you cannot, hold off for a few days to let the animal soften again:

And before molding these animals (except snakes & lizards), let them rest remain dead one or two days to have them more handleable. For they stay rigid after their death & cannot be well fitted to their true shape until they are a little softened. However, if you mold it quickly after it is dead, or give it its shape before it stiffens, you will do well.

All creatures the author-practitioner uses in his casting appear to be freshly caught. Our butterfly was a specimen, purposely dried out in order to preserve it in its original form. We could not rehydrate it (as we did with beetles and spiders), since its wings were too fragile and brittle. As a result, the desiccated state of the butterfly—stiff and hard to manipulate—could not but adversely influence our casting.

Based on what we learned about the butterfly’s fragility from our first trial, we decided to try a one-piece mold in order to reduce our direct physical contact with the insect. Having built a wax gate on the clay base to serve as both the channel into which the metal would be poured and as a support for the butterfly (Fig. 33), we used a pin to carefully pierce the belly of the butterfly and to fix it on the top of this gate. We then used melted wax to solidify the joint between its wings and body (Fig. 34), since our specimen was rather fragile and the wings could easily separate from the body. Since our preceding experiment of coating the butterfly with wheat oil failed, we tried melted butter both to flatten the hairy part of the body and to thicken the wings.

Our second trial showed that butter was a viable thickening and strengthening agent, and that a one-piece mold reduced the risk of damage to delicate things (Fig. 35, Fig. 36).

Concluding Thoughts

In our last trial with butter, we gained important experience, as captured in our field notes:

Facing the melted yellowish butter in front of me, my mind was actually unsettled. My hands even start to tremble when I recall my last entanglement with butter, when we were molding the pansy. Using a paint brush, I carefully applied the lukewarm butter to the petals of the pansy. Like drawing a painting, brushstroke after brushstroke, the pansy was covered with butter. Then it began to die! The petals failed to withstand the weight of the butter. How frustrated! This time again, I have to face the butter. My last unpleasant encounter with it simply makes me afraid, but I have no other choice. This time, instead of applying the butter brushstroke after brushstroke, which is really time-consuming, I decided to do it fast in order to save time. I dipped my paintbrush into the melted butter for some time and then pulled it out. At the end of the brush there is a droplet of butter dangling and swaying. I then placed the brush right beyond the wings of the butterfly, and waited for the droplet to fall freely on the wings. Since the wings of the butterfly have a slight slope, the droplet of butter just fell and flew throughout the surface of the wings according to the declivity. The only thing I have to do is to blow the butter to flow more evenly through the surface of the wings and then to absorb redundant butter with paper towel at the lower end of the wings. And it works quite well! I was really excited about that. That moment is like a magic moment for me. It is like a sudden enlightenment disclosed through trials and errors. From this experience I learned that melted butter has its own life and trajectory, its own agency and habits. When encountering it, we must try to know more about its life and habits so that we can cooperate with each other.24

In describing our experience, we were using the anthropological technique of autoethnography, widely used to record sensory experiences. By “calling on the body as a site of scholarly awareness and corporeal literacy,”25 autoethnography focuses on becoming aware of and reflecting on corporeal and sensory experiences.26 We realized that to let the melted butter flow of its own accord, following the declivity of the wings, was to work with the physical characteristics and dynamic properties of the materials. At the same time, it permitted keeping physical contact with the fragile wings to a minimum, since the brush did not have to have contact with the wings, which could potentially damage them. In this sense, the butterfly, the wings with their slope in space, the paintbrush with drops of butter on it, and the flowing of the melted butter, all constituted an agentic assemblage, as defined by Jane Bennett:

an assemblage owes its agentic capacity to the vitality of the materialities that constitute it. Something like this congregational agency is called shi in the Chinese tradition. Shi helps to ‘illuminate something that is usually difficult to capture in discourse: namely, the kind of potential that originates not in human initiative but instead results from the very disposition of things.’ Shi is the style, energy, propensity, trajectory, or élan inherent to a specific arrangement of things.27

Our encounter with the melted butter in our reconstruction is an example of following the shi of the disposition and the propensity of materials; it is to listen to the things themselves, and to counteract our anthropocentrism in our interaction with matter. By encountering and interacting with materials on their terms, we start to get a sense of intimacy with them. Indeed, materials in our reconstructions certainly did not feel like dead and lifeless things; rather, as we puzzled over and struggled with them, they came to seem “vibrant matter”—as they also were for the author-practitioner.

In the end, our experiences showed that melted butter could be a good material to thicken delicate things, as long as one works with it and its particular properties. Perhaps our bodily encounter with the melted butter can help to explain the author-practitioner’s seemingly contradictory statements about butter and wheat oil, as he notes in other molding and casting recipes that any material can be rendered good.28 Or, perhaps it explains why he insists on the amiability of butter.

For ourselves, as we followed a cycle of close reading, hands-on reconstruction, failure, close reading, and reconstruction again, we began to get a sense of how making and knowing are intertwined: how knowing can aid or hinder making, and how making can both reinforce established knowledge and bring new knowledge into being.
