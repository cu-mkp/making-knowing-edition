Molding with Cuttlefish Bone

Abstract

Several entries mention cuttlefish bone in Ms. Fr. 640: some use the powder of cuttlefish bones as a method of erasing (fol. 15v), others employ calcined cuttlefish powder as a sand for making molds (fol. 49r), but most of these entries make use of the bone as a rapidly-produced mold into which metals are cast, including fols. 91r–v and 145r–v. These entries provide a wealth of information on preparing the bones for molding, impressing models into them, and, finally, casting metals into them. They also contain descriptions of techniques by which an artisan could test the temperature of molten metal before casting. These entries particularly highlight the sensory and bodily knowledge needed by an artisan. We learned through experience the value of this knowledge in the course of our reconstructions.

Cuttlefish Bone

Several entries mention cuttlefish bone in Ms. Fr. 640: some use the powder of cuttlefish bones for erasing writing on tablets (fol. 15v), others employ calcined cuttlefish powder as a sand for making molds (fol. 49r), but most of them make use of the bone as a rapidly produced mold into which metals are cast (fols. 53r, 72v, 91r–v, 139r, 145r–v, and 157r). These entries give a wealth of detail on the processes of preparing the bones for molding, including how to flatten and sand off the bones, how to make them “imbibe” willow charcoal so they will release the model and then the cast, how to dry them, and how to bind the mold to prepare it for casting. The entries devote much space to the various techniques of impressing the model into the cuttlefish “marrow,” as the author-practitioner calls the soft spongy side of the cuttlefish float bone. He also notes that the “shell which holds the marrow, once calcined, is good for making sand” for molds (fol. 72v). In addition, “A means of goldsmiths for molding hollow,” on fol. 157r contains an intriguing method of using cuttlefish bones to make a hollow casting:

They mold in two cuttlefish bones their animal or other work that they have either already made or modeled in wax & cast in lead. Once it is well imprinted in the two cuttlefish bones, they place between these two bones a paper, & join the bones & cast. The work comes out split in half. And then, with ch a chaple [a type of chisel], they hollow out as finely as they please the two halves cast in gold or silver, and then solder or braze them.

In addition to these techniques of making the mold from the cuttlefish bone, the author-practitioner notes that lead and tin are well suited to being cast into cuttlefish bone, and, while silver and gold can also be cast into them, the cast will not come out as “neat,” by which the author-practitioner means that the metal cast will not contain the desired sharp, crisp details.

Perhaps most interesting, the entries on fols. 91r–v and 139r describe a “paper test” to determine whether the molten metal is at the right temperature to be poured into the mold and is not too hot (which will result both in a poor metal surface and a burnt mold). The “paper test” that the author-practitioner elaborates in these recipes is just one of the tricks of the metalworker’s trade, which, like the “squeeze test” to measure the readiness of molding sand to be packed into a mold, formed essential parts of sensory and bodily training for an artisan in a period before instruments took over the measurement of temperature and humidity.1

No other artisanal manual we consulted provided anything like the level of detail on the use of cuttlefish bones for casting that is contained in Ms. Fr. 640, perhaps because this was a well known and commonly used technique (and is still used by goldsmiths today). Cennino Cennini does not discuss the process, but mentions “cuttle such as the goldsmiths use for casting.”2 Likewise, Vannoccio Biringuccio makes a passing reference to cuttlebone casting in the Pirotechnia, implying that it is a quick method of making molds: “To save time, they cast all metals in cuttlebone, having first molded in halves the object they are to make.”3 It would seem that the technique was so familiar to these writers that they did not see the value of describing it. Why, in contrast to these other authors of practical texts, did the author-practitioner believe a detailed description of cuttlefish mold making and casting was worth such a large amount of space and effort?

Our essay leaves this intriguing question for further research, and instead explores the various bodily techniques the author-practitioner specifies for impressing the object into the mold (with hands, knees, or feet), and reconstructs the author-practitioner’s “paper test” for determining the heat of metals.

Reconstruction

Cuttlefish Bones

We sourced cuttlefish bones from Petco, where they are sold for parakeets.4 The first step was to prepare the bones to serve as molds. After discussing different metals to cast into cuttlefish molds, the author-practitioner notes on fol. 145r that

before casting in them [the cuttlefish molds], dry them, & especially for gold, which does not want humidity. You will recognize that they are dry enough when, after having brought the inside & the imprint of these to the fire, they cry & crackle once brought near the ear.

Fol. 91r also admonishes the reader to “Guard against keeping them in a damp place, for they are very prone to getting moist [...]. Before casting, heat the bones in order to make them lose only the coldness & dampness.” Although we did not have access to a fire, we held the cuttlefish bones over an electric hotplate burner using a thin mesh strainer (Fig. 1). Much to our delight, the bones emitted a noise much like a “cry & crackle” after a few minutes.

On fols. 145r and 91r, the author-practitioner gives instructions about the part of the bone into which the model should be impressed:

usually one cuts the bone in the middle, and the dullest part and that which does not have any half circles is the most delicate and smoothest for molding, & thus one always imprints there the principal [i.e., the model or pattern] (fol. 145r)

***

the bone marrow toward the tail is more delicate & does have not as many stripes & molds more cleanly (fol. 91r).

On fol. 145r, he provides a small sketch of the grain of the marrow (Fig. 2).

We were unsure whether we should cut the bone in half along its length, width, or depth (Fig. 3). We decided it made the most sense in terms of the function of the mold to bisect along the width, and we used a jeweler’s saw to cut the bones (Fig. 4). This was exceptionally slow going as the bones were brittle and broke easily. It became easier with successive attempts as we became more familiar with the resistance of the material and the handling of the saw. We cut a series of molds between one and three inches in height (using one half of each cuttlefish bone for the front and back of each mold), and we made them flat on the ends so they could rest without being held during the pouring. We then sanded the bones to make them flat by rubbing the marrow side of the cuttlefish bone against a wooden board (Fig. 5), following instructions on fols. 145r and 91r: “One smooths & flattens these two halves on some smooth wood” (fol. 145r) and “on a hooked roof tile, quite dry & quite smoothed & covered with pulverized willow charcoal, rub & smooth the two halves of the bones” (fol. 91r). We did not smooth our bones with charcoal, which was meant to cause the mold to “release well” from the cast object, but, instead, applied it later before binding the two halves of the mold together. We only realized after doing this that the author-practitioner advises applying charcoal before making the impression so that the model will also release well (fol. 145r).

Forming the Impression: Fingers, Knees, Foot

Cuttlefish bones proved to be an easy surface in which to make an impression, but, as in sawing them, we failed at our first try because the material is very brittle. As we came to expect—and work with—the resistance of the bone, however, we were able to create a mold of a ring and a cross pendant by pressing each firmly into the bone (Fig. 6, Fig. 7).5

This makes it sound easier than it was; in fact, it took extensive experimentation. The author-practitioner gives three different methods of pressing the model (or “principal,” as he calls it), which in this case is a medal, into the mold:

But because the fingers do not press evenly, for one if you press on the edges, the middle will remain hollow. Begin, therefore, by the middle, & then follow the edges. But to make it better, put on the medal something flat & smooth, or some large square filee, & press with this, for you will press equally, sometimes after l with your knee, other times with your foot with your shoe taken off, & make the bone be on top; thus you l’emprand will imprint it without breaking it. If, on the first go, it is not molded well, return to it several times (fol. 145r)

***

Then, on a counter bone, that is to say a piece of brick evened to the size of your bone, place your medal, & then on this one, place the bone, & press well with some other piece of the same size on the top. And for the second time, mold it, but before, pounce on top with willow charcoal & blow lightly, then press as before, and it will come out neatly [...]. And if the medal has two sides since the two bones are joined [...]. Press, between them your hands joined between your two knees & not with a press, because the bones would crack (fol. 91r–v).

He notes that one must be mindful to work slowly and carefully, or, as he puts it “Moule aussy a laise” (“mold at your ease” or “at your leisure”), and press the model in the two halves “two or three times, until you see that it is pressed quite tightly together & well imprinted” (fol. 145r).

We tried pressing with our fingers, and it indeed proved difficult to apply pressure evenly, then we tried pressing with our bare foot, per the recipe’s instructions, but broke several molds in the process, because it was difficult to be precise in the pressure we applied. The most successful method was the third alternative given by the author-practitioner, and consisted of placing our fingers on one side of the mold, then sandwiching the model between the two halves of the bone, then sitting down in a chair, pressing our palms around the mold at the same time we were pressing our hands between our knees, which made possible a strong and even pressure, while using our hands as guides and moderators of the pressure. Our experience was confirmed by later cohorts of students.6 We found that this method quickly became second nature, and we were soon able to relatively effortlessly make effective impressions in our molds.

The Mold

We then used a razor blade to cut a funnel-shaped gate (or pouring cup) in the top of the mold for the metal to enter, and three or four small air vents on one side of the mold (Fig. 8). We fastened the two halves of the mold together by binding them tightly with galvanized steel binding wire (Fig. 9), following the instructions to “join them & lute the joints with a little clay, & make it dry lightly by the fire” (fol. 145r). We did not, however, attempt to lute the joints of the mold with clay because we were afraid that the clay would not be fully dry when we poured the gold, which could, as the author-practitioner notes on fol. 15r, cause the metal to “spatter, & cause damage.” We had learned from experience during our skill-building exercises that dampness in the mold was dangerous.7

We decided to try casting a variety of materials into our molds. After several unsuccessful attempts with beeswax (the mold was porous and it absorbed the wax) and sulfur (the sulfur was too brittle and could not be removed in one piece) (Fig. 10, Fig. 11, Fig. 12), we decided to try tin.

Measuring the Heat of Metals: The Paper Test

The author-practitioner is very concerned that the 1:1 tin-lead alloy he recommends for cuttlefish bone casting not get overheated, and he provides instructions for a test of the metal’s temperature: on fol. 91r, he mentions that one should “cast moderately hot, which you will know when the bone is about to change its good whiteness. But when it is too hot, it turns the bone very red.” On fols. 139r and 145r, he lays out what the Making and Knowing Project came to call “the paper test”:

One ought not to cast either tin or lead too hot in it, for it would burn the bone & come out lumpy. And to know when it is at a good heat, dip a little piece of twisted paper into it. If it blackens it without lighting, it is at a good heat. But if it burns it & catches fire there, it is too hot (fol. 145r).

***

Note that if you want to cast them [an alloy of tin and lead] in cuttlefish bone, they ought not to be very hot because they will burn the cuttlefish bone. Test for this effect with paper. If it turns the paper red, it is enough, it is good to cast, but if it blackens the paper, it is too hot (fol. 139r) [Fig. 13].

Like other writers of practical texts, the author-practitioner has a variety of sensory tests for judging the state of materials. The heat of metals for casting is especially important, and he notes the signs by which different metals make known to the practitioner that they are ready. All metals give a distinctive sign:

When gold reaches its perfect heat, it is green like an emerald. (fol. 124v)

***

When [the silver] is in this state & when it is very white and polished, shining like quicksilver, prepare yourself to cast. (fol. 128v)

***

You will recognize that [red copper] is hot enough when it is smooth, thin & shiny like a mirror of cu of steel, newly polished, or like melted silver. (fol. 137r)

***

when [lead] is very hot, it becomes blue, let it then pass this color deva & rest a little before casting. (fol. 72v)

***

for the alloyed tin [...], it is necessary that it is very hot & almost red for casting. (fol. 115v)

***

In order to know [pewter’s] heat, first melt it well, so hot with such a degree of heat that it promptly burns in a shiver a paper or straw placed inside. Then remove it from the fire & leave it to rest a little & cast. (fol. 68v)

Because the heat of the mold was also important, the author-practitioner also provides a variety of methods to sense the heat inside the mold.8

Reusing the Mold

Our tin trials were immediately successful. After the cuttlefish mold had cooled, we were able to pop the tin ring directly out of the mold in one piece, without damaging the mold at all. The melting point of tin is so low that the mold was not even slightly discolored, nor damaged in any way (Fig. 14). We understood experientially what the author-practitioner meant for a cast to “come out neatly.” In fact, the mold was so perfectly intact that we were able to cast an identical ring in the same mold (Fig. 15, Fig. 16, Fig. 17).

Our next attempt was at pouring gold. As we discuss in our essay on “Making gold run for casting” (fol. 106r), the verdigris substance in our limited trials functioned well for cleaning the gold and increasing its flow.9 The casting of the gold cross (that gave easy flow to the metal) created a perfect casting of the cross on the first attempt (Fig. 18, Fig. 19, Fig. 20, Fig. 21).

In contrast to our tin casting, however, the gold casting burned the mold entirely and destroyed it for further use. This is not surprising because the melting point of gold (1948°F) is nearly four times higher than the melting point of tin (449.5°F). The molten gold not only burned and blackened the cuttlefish bone, but also, when the gold casting was removed, the mold flaked and crumbled, and parts of the mold came away attached to the casting (Fig. 22). As the author-practitioner had predicted, gold did not come out neatly from this mold.

We next made three attempts at pouring gold into the ring mold, but it was difficult to keep both the gold and the crucible hot enough to ensure that the gold remained liquid, and at the same time to pour quickly enough to make the gold run smoothly into the mold. We thus made two attempts and could not get the gold to flow through the whole mold (Fig. 23, Fig. 24). Our expert practitioner consultant, Dr. Boyd, suggested that we enlarge our pouring cup so that we could pour more quickly and accurately. We also expanded the bottom of the funnel on each side so the gold would be able to flow more easily in both directions around the ring. After a second try using these techniques, we were finally able to create a successful casting of the ring in gold (Fig. 25, Fig. 26).

Conclusion

It became obvious to us in the course of our reconstruction why the author-practitioner preferred using cuttlefish bone for casting metals like tin and lead that have lower melting points, for the cuttlefish bone withstood these metals very well, and it was possible to create several castings from the same mold. Gold, on the other hand, as he had predicted, destroyed the mold with its heat and resulted in a less “neat” cast. That cuttlefish bone molds were nevertheless used by sixteenth-century goldsmiths for casting silver (melting point 1763°F) is evident from extant objects (Fig. 27, Fig. 28).

Our reconstruction also proved the effectiveness of the author-practitioner’s techniques for making molds from cuttlefish bones, and it confirmed his close observation of materials. We came to understand the importance of the material signs mentioned by the author-practitioner, to which an artisan had to be attuned. Our skepticism about instructions that at first reading seemed absurd—bones that “cry & crackle” and impressing molds with our knees—gave way when we learned from experience that these sensory and bodily techniques were in fact extremely useful and effective.
