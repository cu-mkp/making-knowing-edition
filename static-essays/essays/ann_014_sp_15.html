Abstract

“Founders of small works of tin” on fol. 80v contains a detailed description of the technique for making a stone mold and casting metal into it. This entry gives insight into a technique likely used for producing pilgrim badges that is not treated in other contemporary written sources. This essay recounts the reconstruction of this process, and it speculates on how and why the author-practitioner engaged with the practice.

Stone Molds

“Founders of small works of tin” on fol. 80v describes in detail the process for making stone molds and casting metal into them. Stone mold making and casting, like cuttlefish bone casting, does not receive as much attention from the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 as other forms of casting such as sand mold casting and lifecasting (carried out in plaster molds, regarded by the author-practitioner as a form of sand mold). Stone mold casting is alluded to a few times in the manuscript, but only fol. 80v contains instructions for the technique of making the mold.1 A similar technique using cuttlefish bone molds for the casting of metal objects is mentioned three times.2 In contrast, there are at least a hundred sand mold making and casting recipes in the manuscript. Despite being the only set of instructions for stone mold making in the manuscript, the recipe is richly detailed. It describes a mold making and casting process used to produce tin and lead pilgrim badges in large numbers, a procedure for which few comparable contemporary written sources exist. This essay describes our attempts to reconstruct and document the stone mold making and casting technique described in Ms. Fr. 640 and speculates on questions about how and why the author-practitioner engaged with the practice.

Casting metal in stone was first performed in antiquity, and it developed in tandem with other casting techniques, such as sand mold casting and lost wax casting.3 During the Bronze Age, Scandinavian peoples used soapstone molds to cast objects with various types of metal.4 Prior to and during the Middle Ages, stone mold making and casting was carried out in many regions of Europe. From the medieval era and into the early modern period, stone molds were used in the production of pilgrim badges, which were typically cast in lead or a low-quality pewter and poured into a carved stone mold (Fig. 1, Fig. 2).5 The recipe for making stone molds in Ms. Fr. 640 appears to refer to this practice, for the author-practitioner writes in “Casting of lead” (fol. 49r) that “Those who cast those small works that are sold in front of churches, y melt one for one lb of tin a half of lead, & cast in stone.”

Stone Mold Carving

If, as seems likely, the author-practitioner collected the stone mold making information from practitioners, this oral tradition might help explain the lack of recipes in other technical writings. Cennino Cennini (ca. 1360– before 1427), writing at the end of the fourteenth century, gives instructions on how to mold tin foil with stone molds in Il libro dell’arte, but these directions bear little resemblance to the technique described in Ms. Fr. 640.6 In his sixteenth-century treatise Pirotechnia, Vannoccio Biringuccio (ca. 1480–ca. 1539) writes, “indeed, [tin] is an easily melted metal, in common use for the utensils which are made for human needs, it is an art known not only to skilled men but almost to children, wherefore I could proceed without telling of it.”7 As Biringuccio notes, casting metal objects in stone molds was a relatively simple process. First, the practitioner sought out stones of the best consistency to make their carving. This means they should be “close-grained,” without large inclusions of differing hardness.8 Once two stones of adequate density were found, they were ground down, perhaps one against the other, or on a flat surface, until they fit tightly together (a stone used for an open-face mold did not require grinding). Using a pointed tool made from a hard metal, such as iron, the practitioner then carved a design into the stone as well as a venting and gate system, applied a separating agent, and then joined the stones together. Finally, the molder poured molten metal, typically a mixture of tin and lead, into the mold and, once the metal had cooled, separated the stones, and removed the object.

Reconstruction

Thus, the first step in reconstructing this process was selecting appropriate stones.9 The author-practitioner writes:

They engrave their works on stones of which are made the sharpening stones or files of barbers, which are found in great flakes near the mountains, & resemble slate. They are of three colors: reddish, which is not as perfect as the others because it does not last as long in the fire & as, one dard of the color of dark slate, the other whitish.

Determining the type of stone to which he is referring is extremely difficult. Geological maps of the Pyrenees mountains just south of Toulouse show the presence of limestone, sandstone, granite, and other metamorphic rock formations from various geological periods.10 Surviving stone molds from Western Europe from the fourteenth to the sixteenth century are equally diverse, made of a variety of materials including soapstone, limestone, mudstone, and more (Fig. 3, Fig. 4, Fig. 5).11

In order to determine what type of stone to use in our reconstruction, we had to follow the author-practitioner’s approach and consider stones by their material properties rather than by their modern classifications. He specifies that the stones used by tin founders are those “of which are made the sharpening stones or files of barbers, which are found in great flakes near the mountains, & resemble slate” (fol. 80v). The comparison with the materials used for sharpening blades suggests that these stones had a highly uniform texture. And, “close-grained... [and] fine-grained” medieval stone molds do exist.12 We originally intended to use slate since the author-practitioner specifically mentions that the stones he used resembled slate. The slate samples we obtained displayed a uniform texture and density throughout, however, they proved impossible to use because of their awkward shape. Based upon these observations of grain, we decided to use two types of common stone with the finest texture we could find, namely, limestone and soapstone, although limestone is much more granular than soapstone (Fig. 6, Fig. 7).

Following the instructions on fol. 80v, that is, “having first flattened their stones & worn down one against the other [...] to make a circle or a square which joins perfectly,” we first spent much time trying to get the stones flat enough to fit together seamlessly for a two-piece mold.13 But after several sessions of grinding the stones against each other and on a flat surface, they were still not perfectly flush, and we had to continue grinding them during and after carving the design (Fig. 8).

As inexpert stone carvers, we soon discovered that stone is hard, especially when we attempted to carve precise details. At first, our progress was slow, especially with the limestone. It took us over three hours to complete the relatively simple design we chose for this type of stone, using various files, chisels, and brushes (Fig. 9, Fig. 10). This was partially due to our novice skill level, but the hardness of the stone was also a factor. Achieving cuts with any depth in the stone required patience and force. Early modern artisans, as the author-practitioner writes, used specific instruments to do this work: “with a little compass & little matching iron fittings, they engrave their figures” (fol. 80v). Sixteenth-century compasses and calipers were precision instruments.14 While stone molds made with a high degree of artisanal skill are found in museum collections (Fig. 11), many stone molds are relatively simple, indicating that the hardness of the stone may have been a limiting factor then, too (Fig. 12).

We also used distilled vinegar in our attempts to carve, as the author-practitioner suggests in a marginal note on fol. 80v: “Try to etch with distilled vinegar.” The vinegar made the limestone easier to carve, but we did not notice much difference when we used it with soapstone. Limestone undergoes a chemical reaction with vinegar, and it may be that the resulting dissolution of the stone facilitates carving. If this is the case, it would suggest that the stone described in the recipe was likely limestone or a stone chemically similar (Fig. 13).15

Vinegar is used frequently throughout Ms. Fr. 640. In “Counterfeit coral” on fol. 3r, vinegar “gives [colophony] strength & prevents it from being so fragile;” in “Grenades” on fol. 24r, vinegar is used “to temper the [gun]powder;” in “The work done in Algiers” on fol. 52r–52v, rinsing the mouth with vinegar is advised to neutralize dangerous vapors (fol. 52v); and in “For making black goumiche” on fol. 76v, vinegar is used to temper glue. Vinegar is clearly an important material in the workshop, and, within his material imaginary, the author-practitioner seems to have regarded it as an agent that could temper and ameliorate other substances.16

Once we finished carving our designs in the limestone with vinegar, we drilled plugholes with a drill and filled them with tin-lead alloy. The metal forms natural posts which ensure proper alignment and enable repeated use of the molds (Fig. 14, Fig. 15, Fig. 16, Fig. 17, Fig. 18). We ensured that the posts could easily release from their recesses, again following the advice of the author-practitioner on fol. 80v: “Make sure the pegs of your frame enter easily so that in opening the frame will easily open without shifting anything, and that your frames fit well together, & the table is quite even.” Most extant molds in modern museum collections have lost their posts, leaving only the holes as evidence of a two-piece mold construction, as in the pilgrim badge mold shown in Fig. 12. The jewelry mold shown in Fig. 3 retains its posts.

To the soapstone mold, we also added an extraction hole, as described in the manuscript: “They pierce a hole in some place on the medal that is least visible. And then with a borer they pierce the mold on the side of the medal. And if they want their work to stay pierced in any place, they drive in a bit of cork at this place in the mold. And the lead or tin will not attach to it” (Fig. 19, Fig. 20). Generally, the extraction hole worked well—the cork was only slightly singed, and it greatly aided the release of the metal. The use of an extraction hole seems like a very specific detail, suggesting that the author-practitioner likely practiced stone making and casting himself and appreciated the tricks of the stone molder’s trade.

Once the posts had cooled, we applied a separating agent. Following the recipe (“before casting, they rub the mold with tallow” fol. 80v), we heated the molds slightly (using a blowtorch) and applied the tallow with a brush (Fig. 21,Fig. 22, Fig. 23). The heat from the mold melted the tallow, “which has quickly absorbed it because it is hot.”

Our next step was to select the separators. The author-practitioner specifies two different materials to be used as powdered separators in the recipe: quicklime and calcined oyster shells. The quicklime is mentioned in the main body of the recipe: “then, taking fine powder of quicklime in a linen, they pounce the mold while beating with the linen on top, then blow a little on top to prevent it from becoming porous.” This observation may represent the recipe as collected from tin founders or pewterers. The calcined oyster shells are mentioned in a marginal note “Try calcined oyster shells. They are said to be excellent for molding,” which the author-practitioner likely added after the main recipe was written, perhaps as a note to himself or after collecting further information.17 Both recommendations for separation materials appear to be collected from other sources rather than originating from the author-practitioner’s own experience. After considering the author-practitioner’s suggestions and reflecting on our own prior experience of casting with sand molds, we decided to conduct the experiment with three different separators (charcoal, quicklime, and calcined oyster shells) on our two types of molds (limestone and soapstone).18

After applying the separators (Fig. 24), we sealed the molds (Fig. 25). For all metal pours, we used an alloy of 50% tin and 50% lead.19

Once the metals had cooled, we removed the completed badges and the sprues; after this, we performed some work on a few of the badges using a soldering iron (Fig. 26, Fig. 27).

To fully evaluate the differences between the quality of the casts produced using different separators, we first had to understand exactly what a good cast meant for the author-practitioner. He often praises molds for being of good or easy “release,” for example, in “Pewterer” on fol. 30r. Further, on fol. 49r, in “Casting of lead,” he refers to “very neat lead casts.” We therefore concluded that the author-practitioner aimed for the metal releasing easily from the mold (which would preserve the mold for repeated use) and the cast object taking on the precise detail of the impression in the mold with minimal loss. By these standards, charcoal was the least effective of the three separators (Fig. 28, Fig. 29, Fig. 30). It left a grainy texture over the entire surface of the badge, more than the other separators. Pieces of charcoal became embedded in the metal itself and were still present even after repeated cleanings, giving the metal a dark hue.20 It was also associated with the largest failure of all the pourings, where over half of one mold failed to fill at all (Fig. 31).21 By contrast, the quicklime and calcined oyster shells both performed well. These separators made the metal appear brighter and smoother than that with the charcoal. In all cases—charcoal, quicklime, and calcined oyster shells—the metal released easily from the molds, and the molds were left undamaged, ready to be used again.

Our reconstruction of the stone mold technique found in Ms. Fr. 640 is by no means comprehensive. Many questions remain, such as how different stones and metal alloy types might affect the process. In addition, more research is needed on the trade and social status of pewterers. Nonetheless, this recipe adds an unusually detailed description to the extant primary sources on stone molds. Its singular appearance in Ms. Fr. 640 may reveal the author-practitioner’s relative lack of interest in a process of molding that did not involve changing the state of the materials (by mixing different sands and binders) and that resulted in simple and static forms—as opposed to the lifelike nature casts, on which, in other recipes, we see him working so intensively.
