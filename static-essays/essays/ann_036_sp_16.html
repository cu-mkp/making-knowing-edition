Black Color for Dyeing, and the Place of Textiles in Ms. Fr. 640

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/acnf-0e59
← Back to Essays

Abstract

Throughout BnF Ms. Fr. 640, the author-practitioner attempts to create black oils, stains, varnishes, pigments and—in one recipe—dye. Contextualizing the importance of black in the manuscript and in early modern society, this essay explores a short black dye recipe on fol. 38v which uses lead and lye made from quicklime. It then follows the author-practitioner’s instruction to “try other colors with the lye made of lime.” The essay proposes that while this method of dyeing degrades the fibers of wool and hair (and does not work on linens or silks), there is a “material logic” to the author-practitioner’s choice of materials if we place this recipe in context with his other experiments. While there is no substantial discussion of textiles or dyeing in Ms. Fr. 640, textiles make frequent occurrences in recipes as objects of transformation (either to be dyed, or to be used as a source for pigments), as subjects for painters, as tools, as similes, and as the object of useful knowledge. This essay argues that the author-practitioner demonstrates his “textile literacy” throughout the manuscript, including notes on the expense of black textiles, the accounting practices of cloth merchants, and the visual effects of clothing. Given the importance of the woad industry in Toulouse and the economic and cultural value of textiles in the early modern period, such knowledge and interest is understandable, but this essay prompts further questions about how widespread dye experimentation was in this period, the nuanced meaning of color in the region, and even what the author-practitioner might have been wearing as he carried out his experiments.

Introduction: Black in Ms. Fr. 640

“Black colour for dyeing” is not the only recipe in BnF Ms. Fr. 640 that aims to make black. On fol. 46v, the author-practitioner mentions that shoemakers use “oil of sulfur” to paint “a reddish color” on black leather shoes, and gives a recipe for using this “oil of sulfur” to make “writing without ink” that will turn black when heated, and can be used to paint “on stone, black letters.”1 Another recipe, on fol.75v–76r, explains how to color “all woods” or horn black using olive oil and sulfur.2 This is followed by instructions to make a mixture of lampblack, vinegar, and glue that can be applied to wood, presumably to make a similar stain.3 He also gives detailed instructions for a black varnish to cover sword guards and metal chests on fol. 4v and references this recipe again on fol. 97r.4 The desire to make black runs throughout the manuscript, appearing in recipes for oils, stains, varnishes, pigments and—in one case—as a dye.

The author-practitioner understood the complexity of making black. In his instructions for painting “Shadows” he explains, “blacks make different colors, some a reddish black, others tending towards blue, and others towards green, choose those which tend towards yellow.”5 This problem was even more fraught for dyers. Painters could use charcoal or soot to reproduce “true blacks” (rather than using a combination of blue, red and yellow to make black) but before the introduction of logwood in the late sixteenth century, and its mastery by Europeans in the seventeenth- and eighteenth-centuries, pure black textiles were an elusive fantasy for European makers and consumers.6

A number of historians and sociologists have highlighted the nuanced significance and widespread importance of black textiles for use in clothing for various cultures, religious groups, and genders from the ancient world to the present.7 While the author-practitioner does not explicitly state the cultural importance of black textiles as being anything more than related to economics, colorfast and deep black fabrics would have been highly sought-after commodities in his community—amongst artisans as well as members of the court. It is possible to get a sense of the value of well-dyed black textiles, and the social status suggested by those who can afford to wear them, when he notes in an entry on silk, “black is less abundant [for the worker] because it charges much.”8 He also notes that black cloth is made thin “so that the dyeing is inexpensive.”9

The one recipe for black dye, that is probably intended for use with textiles, is at the head of fol. 38v. It follows a seemingly unrelated set of recipes on fol. 38r for sapphire, counterfeit saffron, and amber. It is just two sentences, which appear to be in one hand, neatly written in one sitting. But from the tone of the last sentence, the author-practitioner did not finish his work on this recipe. While he confidently states “Take lye of quicklime & litharge, mix & soak, & you will make a tawny dye, & by reiterating it you will make black,” the second sentence is open-ended, adding to our sense that although this is a fair copy manuscript, the author-practitioner was still in the midst of working through new ideas and experiments during its compilation: “Try other colors with lye of lime.”

Given the importance of black textiles in this period, but an absence of any indication in the recipe as to the substance to be dyed, the first question that this entry raises is what is being dyed black in this recipe?10 That black dye was elusive in the period raises a second question: is this dye recipe successful? In investigating these questions, this essay will also explore the role of textiles for the author-practitioner in Ms. Fr. 640.

Textiles in Ms. Fr. 640

While there is no substantial discussion of textiles or dyeing in Ms. Fr. 640, textiles make frequent occurrences in recipes as objects of transformation (either to be dyed, or to be used as a source for pigments) as subjects for painters, as tools, as similes, and as the object of useful or interesting knowledge.

The author-practitioner is keen to stress the importance of material and visual familiarity with textiles for painters. When he offers instruction as to how to paint “Folds in clothing” on fol. 59v, he states “take care that none are made false,” so the painter should ensure “only that which the natural can do is imitated.”11 He contrasts the supple crêpes, taffetas and silks to “thick cloth” fabrics, which have hardly any folds. Similar attention to the movement, texture, and light of textiles for painters is clear in the author-practitioner’s instructions for “Velvets and blacks” on fol. 63v.12 Here, the painter is instructed to layer the pigment on thick, and add highlights in white, or to use “peach pit black, which is very black” to shade in blue and green velvets. Textile literacy is expected of his reader when, in a discussion of “Molds [...] for delicate things” on fol. 107v, the author-practitioner explains that “Good feather alum is white and as lustrous as white silk.13

Textiles also interest the author-practitioner for their economic value. On the same page as the black dye recipe, two entries suggest that the author-practitioner has been in conversation with local cloth merchants, and is interested not only in the cost of fine velvets, woad, and woolen cloth (dyed and undyed), but also in the accounting practices (double-entry or single-entry) of cloth and dye merchants who sell such textiles and dyestuffs.14 The importance of woad dyeing to the surrounding Toulouse community is hinted at in seven references to woad in the manuscript (fols. 1r, 15r, 32v, 38v [twice], 39r, 76r).15 From the late fifteenth to the mid-sixteenth century, Toulouse was the centre of the woad industry, and pastelier families waxed rich off the spoils of this lucrative trade.16 As the author-practitioner himself mentions in an entry on the cultivation and use of “pastel,” woad was cultivated and harvested in the nearby region of the Lauragais (fol. 39r). Woad or pastel was brought to Toulouse for the rather pungent fermentation and extraction process, but the industry began to decline after the 1560s as indigo became increasingly available in Europe.17 The author-practitioner was also interested in the cultivation of silkworms, which was a tricky but potentially very lucrative industry dominated by the Italians, and which gripped the attention of nobles, artisans and publishers alike in the late-sixteenth and early-seventeenth century (fols. 53v–54r).18

The author-practitioner suggests other dye recipes using laque, vert-de-gris, azur, pastel, alum, brazil wood, copperas, dragon’s blood, crimson, woad, cochineal, and dyes from flowers (fols. 13r, 14v, 32v, 38v, 40r, 44r). He also connects dye to painting and cosmetics when he notes that “Florence lake is better than the kind from Flanders for in Florence the best dyes are made [...] That is why ladies, wanting to color their cheeks, ground Florence lake very finely, then fill a little cotton with it, which they next wrap in a little fabric of Cambray which is clear. And thus they pounce the lake on their cheeks & then, with another clean cotton, they soften it.” (fol. 58r). The author-practitioner’s discussion of textiles and dyes in the manuscript connects to ideas about beauty, fashion, economics, and industry, and gestures towards the social, cultural and economic role that colourful fabrics played in early modern Europe.

Reconstructing the Recipe

The recipe for black dye is comprised of one sentence of confident instruction (“Take lye of quicklime & litharge, mix & soak, & you will make a tawny dye, & by reiterating it you will make black”) followed by a second sentence suggesting further experiment (“Try other colors with lye of lime”). The limited information given—no quantities, no proportions, no timing, no substance or material to be dyed—made for a difficult start. First, it was necessary to work out what was meant by “lye of quicklime.” I dismissed the idea that this was lye made from a combination of quicklime and litharge as these are the only two substances listed in the recipe—if the lye was made from a combination of quicklime and litharge, then there would be no additional substance to “mix” with. Lye does appear elsewhere in the manuscript, three times with no indication of a recipe (fol. 38r—for boiling amber to make it red, fol. 44v—cleaning horsehair sieves, and fol. 51r–51v—for cleaning copper plates), and twice it is mentioned as with ashes (fol. 39v—for “Tracing some history on glass” and fol. 157r—for “Cleaning and degreasing your works cast from tin”).19 The latter of the two lye-with-ashes entries gives the instruction, “Make some lye, carefully passed through good ashes, and since it is recent & not fatty, boil your molded work in it.” This is the most common method for making lye, both in the early modern period and for modern soap- and dye-making, and is simply made by pouring water over wood ash.20 But this recipe clearly stipulates that the lye must be made from quicklime.

Following modern lye recipes, I carefully boiled quicklime under the fume hood for approximately 40 minutes at a ratio of 100ml of water to 60g of sodium carbonate and 60g of quicklime.21 On cooling, this appeared to be a successful lye—liquid separated from the sediment, alkali, and strong enough to partly dissolve a feather (Fig.1 and Fig.2). This raises the question as to why the author-practitioner would use quicklime for lye. When I discovered that quicklime had already been made in the lab by calcining oyster shells, I realized that it made material sense for the author/practitioner to experiment with materials that he was already working with in the workshop (Fig. 3).22 That the lab had this leftover material from former experiments that I was able to use, gave me new insight about what materials were at hand to the author/practitioner. If black dye is so elusive, but the author-practitioner works in an area growing rich from the production and sale of woad, and has close contact with a number of cloth- and woad-merchants, it would be reasonable for him to experiment with materials at hand that might offer new and successful methods for dyeing—whether black or, as the second sentence suggests, “other colors.”

The second material question or challenge raised by the recipe is what is meant by “litharge”? The translation had it as “white lead.”23 For Cotgrave, litharge is “Litargie, white lead; lead mixed with the drosse of silver and brasse.”24 The only other references to litharge in the manuscript are on fol. 32v in an entry on glassworkers (who use it to make “common red”) and on fol. 161r, “Preparation of sand for frames” but neither entry sheds light on what the material is. I decided to experiment with white lead, red lead and yellow lead, all of which seemed like possible candidates, as “golden-” and “silver- litharge” were both commonly referenced substances in early modern texts.25

In the absence of quantities in the recipe, I decided to start with a ratio of 100 ml of lye to 1g of white lead/red lead/yellow lead and resolved to alter the quantities in further recipes, if needed. I hoped to see instant color change in the liquid, but other than a change in the white lead/lye mix to a pale pink/brown color, there was no indication that any of these mixes would make a black dye (Fig. 4). Having made control pots of white lead/water, red lead/water and yellow lead/water, I was able to compare the influence of the lye in the mix, but there was very little color difference between sample and control in the yellow- and red lead lye mixes. The white lead/water control, however, was not the same pinkish-brown tone as the lye mix—it remained white. I left samples of white lead/lye, red lead/lye and yellow lead/lye uncovered, in the fume hood, in case oxygen was required to change the mixture to a dark brown or black, but after two days, the only notable color change was of the white lead/lye mix to a pinkish-brown hue (Fig. 5).

Given the predominance of woolen textiles in Europe in the sixteenth century, I began with the assumption that this recipe was for woolen cloth. While linen was ubiquitous—worn by men and women, old and young, rich and poor next to the skin (as undershirts, collars and cuffs), it was valued for its whiteness, and ease of cleaning and bleaching and would have rarely been dyed. If the author-practitioner was dyeing silk, linen or (unlikely) cotton, this would probably be unusual enough to merit mention in the body of the recipe. Nevertheless, I thought it was worth testing on the textiles available in the lab: silk, linen and cotton. I decided to start testing the dye on textiles and fibers with the white lead/lye mix, as it was the most promising in terms of color change. Having applied it with a pipette to small squares of silk, cotton and linen, held in plastic tubs, I decided to try it on my own hair (as wool was not available in the lab) (Fig. 6 and Fig.7). The linen turned brown-black after about five minutes, as did the hair (which was originally a blonde/red color), while the silk and cotton just took on the pinkish-brown of the solution (Fig. 8). I then reapplied or, in the words of the recipe “reiterat[ed] it” to ensure that the dye was not brown but black. Two days later, the linen had changed from its original grey brown, via black upon immediate white lead/lye dyeing, to a bright powdery white (Fig. 9). The hair remained black (Fig. 10). The dye seemed to be only working on hair so far.

Encouraged by the success of the hair dye, and in the knowledge that hair and wool are similarly composed of keratin, I sourced some 100% pure undyed wool, and scoured it with water and dishwashing soap to remove oils and grease (a necessary step when dyeing wool). Once the wool was dry, I cut small pieces of yarn and tested white lead/lye, red lead/lye and yellow lead/lye solutions (and also tested with a control of lye by itself) (Fig. 11). All three lead/lye solutions turned the wool dark brown after about 15 minutes of soaking (Fig. 12, Fig.13, and Fig.14). I then “reiterat[ed]” the application of lead/lye mix, and after another 15 minutes, the wool fibers were all dark brown/black.

Having left the successfully dyed black wool in the lab over the weekend, I discovered upon return on Monday that the wool had completely disintegrated, leaving behind some shiny thick black sludge (Fig. 15). To see whether it was possible to dye wool without this subsequent disintegration, I repeated the dye process with white lead/lye, red lead/lye and yellow lead/lye solutions, dyeing two pieces of wool black with each of the three mixes. For each lead/lye mix, I took one sample of wool and then removed it from the dye pot, placed it into a fresh pot, and left for two days. I washed the other three wool samples in water, using three pots and dipping and swirling the woollen yarn in each pot (the water turned the color of the lead/lye solution in the first pot, paler in the second dip, and hardly changed by the third submersion). I then placed the washed wool into a clean pot and left for two days. The three woolen samples that had been dyed and then just removed from the lead/lye mixes had all disintegrated into black sludge. However the wool removed from the lead/lye mixes and then washed in water retained the black color and had not dissolved (Fig. 16).

Other recipes

There are similar recipes for black dye, both for hair and wool, which are contemporary to, or pre-date, Ms. Fr. 640. In the 1548 Plictho of Giovanventura Rosetti there is a recipe for “Very beautiful black” with the instructions: “Measure litharge well powdered on the marble, live lime not spent, and put it in common water in proportion and it makes very beautiful black. Also, golden litharge, very strong lye, and make boild what you want inside and it will be black. Also silvery litharge one third, and live lime 2 pounds and make the water boil and it will be a very fine black.”26 This recipe was intended to dye wool, but in many other contemporary recipes, the lead/lye mix is suggested as a hair dye. The 1560 Le Bâtiment des recettes contains a similar recipe that is explicitly for dyeing hair: “Pour faire les cheveux noirs: Prens litarge pilee et broyee et autant de chaux vive. Deslaye le tout en eaue chaude et mesle tres bien de ceste eaue. Lave t’en les cheveux et ilz deviendront noirs.”27 The English translation of The Secrets of the reverend Maister Alexis of Piemont (1595) contains a number of black hair dye recipes, including one with lye from ashes, litharge of gold, black fig tree leaves and sage which is then applied “a combe of leade which you shall anoint with the said juice & so kembe your head and your beard well with it & they will become incompetent blacke.”28 Another recipe “to make also hoare haires blacke, take blacke sope, and quick lime, and some Litharge of gold, and make thereof as it were an ointment, and rub your haires with it.”29 In her La Chymie Charitable (first published 1666), Marie Meurdrac suggested that if you rub on a paste of quicklime powder mixed with silver litharge and rose water at night and comb it in the morning, your hair will turn hair black.30 The Bolognese surgeon Leonardo Fioravanti promised that his recipe for black hair dye, made of strong lye and golden litharge, and softened with almond oil, would make any woman or man look as if they were twenty-five years old again. Fioravanti explained that this recipe worked because litharge is a species of lead, and lead “by nature” (“di sua natura”) makes black. The lye, he claimed, dissolves it (“lo solve”).31

Hair dye recipes using lead and quicklime date back to the ancient Greeks and Romans.32 A version of the recipe (albeit complicated with a much longer list of ingredients) was included in Diderot’s Encyclopedie.33 A similar dye recipe was published in 1858 in Scientific American, demonstrating the longevity and success of this recipe as a method for turning hair black, despite the toxicity and unpleasant nature of the ingredients: “it sticks like mortar, and is a disagreeable, although an effectual dye.”34 But the explanation as to how the lead and lye mix works on hair and wool can be developed even further than Fioravanti’s suggestion: Investigators from the Center for Research and Restoration of the Museums of France (C2RMF), L’Oréal Research, Argonne National Laboratory, and the Laboratoire d’étude des microstructures, experimented with a mixture of lead oxide (PbO), and slaked lime, (Ca(OH)2) following a “formula dating from antiquity.”35 Having successfully made black hair dye, they explain that the blackening of hair is due to the precipitation of galena crystals (PbS), where the lead is provided from the paste (or, in our case, dye) deposited on the hair shafts, and the sulphur involved in the reaction comes from the amino acids of hair (or, in our case, wool) keratins.36 This, they explain, is a form of ancient “nanotechnology”—a chemical reaction that, unusually for hair dye, occurs in the core of the hair fiber: “What is particularly surprising in this reaction is that, despite the structural complexity of hair and its relative chemical inertness, metal sulfide nanoparticles easily crystallize and get organized inside this biomaterial.”37 The study also shows that “a solution with lead oxide and without lime […] provides neither cystine degradation products nor blackening of the hair. In fact, for the dyeing process to occur, high alkalinity of the solution is required.”38 Such an experiment provides a chemical explanation for the dyeing process and its success only on keratin-based fibers. This dye recipe can only work with wool, fur, or hair.

“Try other colors with lye of lime”

While the author-practitioner’s knowledge of the quicklime lye and lead black dye recipe places him in a long tradition reaching back to the ancient world and forward into present day laboratories, his second sentence “Try other colors with lye of lime” is more explorative. Perhaps, following the success of the lead and lime mix in creating black, the author-practitioner sought to experiment with other colors to see if his quicklime lye would produce successful or surprising results. As it was unclear which substances or dyes “other colors” meant in this context, I decided to follow his instruction to “try other colors” using dyestuffs mentioned on the same folio as this recipe. Immediately following the black dye entry is a recipe “Against nose bleed and for dyeing” which suggests “sorrel or lapathum acutum of the sort that is red-veined, which is called dragon’s blood” that is “a strong dye” which “makes beautiful violet.” (fol. 38v). At the base of this page, an entry on “Scarlets,” explains how much an aulne (a measure) costs to dye.39 The author-practitioner explains “whoever wants something beautiful should buy white cloth worth fifteen francs an aulne & have it dyed with pure scarlet pastel woad & a little cochineal” (fol. 38v). He then states, “Black cloth is thin so that the dyeing is inexpensive,” perhaps referring to the black dye recipe using lead and quicklime lye above. Given that cochineal, woad, and dragon’s blood are dyestuffs referenced on the same page, and are substances that we already had in the laboratory for use with other recipes, there seemed to be both material and textual logic for experimenting with these colors “with lye of lime.” Therefore I followed the same procedure as with the lead/lime mix, mixing 100 ml of lye to one gram of dyestuff of woad (purchased already powdered), cochineal (one sample ground in a mortar and one not ground) and dragon’s blood (one sample ground in a mortar and one not ground). The woad, a mid-blue powder, turned deep green when mixed with lye. The ground and unground cochineals transformed from deep red into deep purple. The dragon’s blood samples both changed from a bright orange-red to a brown-red in lye. The unground sample of dragon’s blood in lye was a darker red—almost a brown red—than the sample I had ground in a mortar (Fig. 17). I dyed a piece of unwashed and undyed wool with each of the five colour/lye mixes, by covering them in the dye solutions and leaving for about 10 minutes, and then “reiterating” with a second submersion in dye solution. Following the experience of the black dyed wool dissolving after being dyed, and seeing that it was necessary to wash the dyed wool in three baths of water, I dipped each sample into three pots of water to remove excess lye. While the wool with black dye had survived this process intact, which had preserved the wool and not removed the black color, these samples all but dissolved in the water during this dye process. While the dyed samples left over after the washing process were beautiful and bright colors—the cochineal had dyed the wool a vibrant purple (Fig. 18), the dragon’s blood a bright orange (Fig. 19), and the woad dye a rich moss green (Fig. 20)—the wool had deteriorated into a gelatinous sludge. Cochineal, woad, and dragon’s blood were all used as dyestuffs in the early modern period, but the processes used were more complex than simply combining with a lye. Rather, they usually required boiling, submersion in vats, and a mordant to release and fix the dye color.40 It seems here that although the alkaline lye transformed the dyestuff hues into attractive colors, the lye mix dissolved the wool even more quickly than in the black dye experiment. While this might have been because the wool was left in the dye for too long, and perhaps a shorter “dip” into the dye might have been better, it seems unlikely that this means of dyeing would be effective without damaging the wool irreparably.

Of these transformations, that cochineal turned such a rich and bright purple was something of a surprise, especially as purple was such an expensive color to dye and cochineal such a popular dyestuff in early modern Europe.41 A sixteenth century man’s tunic from northern Chile, woven from camel hair and now held at the Metropolitan Museum of Art, offers evidence that achieving a purple color from cochineal was known to some early modern dyers (Fig. 21). It has a chequerboard pattern of large red and purple squares, but when tested, it was revealed that both sections had been dyed with cochineal.42 As Alejandro de Ávila has shown, cochineal mixed with quicklime was used to produce purple body paint in Oaxaca, and could possibly predate and offer precedent for fiber dyeing.43 Had the author-practitioner experimented with his quicklime lye and these dyestuffs, he would have found that he could transform their colors into new and attractive hues, although he would have had to find a way to preserve the strength of the wool or hair fibre. There is no indication, however, that he ever followed up on his suggestion to “Try other colors with lye of lime.”

Conclusions

Following the reconstruction, and with reference to ancient, early modern, and modern recipes, it seems probable that this recipe was to dye wool black. However, it is possible that the recipe was also known to the author-practitioner as a hair dye, and this might explain the reference in another dye recipe to animal hair (fol. 13r), and to another entry that gives instructions for removing hair on the forehead (fol. 16v).44 If this recipe was to dye hair on animals, perhaps it could be connected to instructions in the manuscript for ephemeral decorative and celebratory materials.45 Given the author-practitioner’s interest in the economic importance of textiles and dyes, and his familiarity with quicklime in other experiments, it is possible that he hoped to transfer the success of the black dye recipe to “other colors.”

His “textile literacy”—understanding the many uses of textile fibers, the methods cloth- and woad-merchants used for accounting, and the visual familiarity with different cloth folds—was not unusual in the early modern period when every individual would have had a close relationship with the clothing that they wore and maintained. Textiles consumed a far greater proportion of income and time than in the post-industrial age, and few clothes were available to purchase ready-made. Early modern men and women would have had to select, handle and negotiate for the fabrics and fastenings required in each garment, discuss styles and shapes with their tailors and seamstresses, and have clothing pinned and fitted onto their body.46

We can only wonder what clothing the author-practitioner would have owned and worn while he was writing the manuscript, and carrying out his experiments. What was the significance of black to him, his local community, and to Philippe de Béthune (the first owner and possible commissioner of the manuscript)?

  13. Fol. 107v Catching snakes and lizards (continued from fol. 106v)
    Good feather alum is white and as lustrous as white silk. It is in pieces long as a finger, & is very breakable & wooly as down. The one made of stone is harder & not so good. The best of the above mentioned quality is brought in Francenear Rouan. The feather alum for our sand is pestled in a mortar, and is further ground on marble, especially since one cannot think of passing it finely through the sieve, for it is so fat & wooly that it would not pass through it. It is this, with its small soft & thin filaments, which gives binding to the sand, in a much more excellent fashion than cloth shearings in the founder’s earth of the founders of great works, because this cloth shearings & cloth waste burns and feather alum resists the fire.↩︎
