Abstract

This essay describes the Making and Knowing Project’s creation of the Digital Critical Edition of Ms. Fr. 640 through interdisciplinary pedagogy and research. It elaborates the various components of the Project, including Text Workshops, Laboratory and Digital Seminars, institutional collaborations, and digital design and development. The potentials and pitfalls of the method of historical reconstruction in a laboratory are discussed, and an introductory survey of insights gained through the student research in texts, objects, and hands-on laboratory work is provided.

This digital critical Edition and English translation of the French manuscript, Ms. Fr. 640, held by the Bibliothèque nationale de France (BnF), presents this rich and unique sixteenth-century technical text in French transcription and English translation for the first time, and situates its contents in their material and historical context. The Edition not only presents traditional archival and paleographic research on the manuscript, but also material reconstructions and analyses of the techniques described in it, through the student essays and their field notes. Thus, it allows users to both read the manuscript and experience it as a record of material practices.

The digital edition of Ms. Fr. 640 makes two important contributions to a broad, interdisciplinary scholarly community made up of historians of craft, art, science, material culture, recipes, and daily life, as well as museum conservators, scholars of the digital humanities, and an even broader audience of history students, makers, artists, re-enactors, and individuals curious about materials and techniques of the past. First, the Edition makes accessible a rich source of information about craft, art, and technical procedures and materials in premodern Europe. It significantly enhances the existing body of early modern technical writing. Second, as the Project’s initial research and dissemination through courses, meetings, and multiple minimal editions has already shown, it engages readers, whether researchers, students, or the broader public, in a new approach to exploring historical texts, one that emphasizes the importance of the material conditions, interpretations, and outcomes that emerge when the written word is realized through making and practice. The manuscript codifies procedures that were not meant to be reproduced solely through the act of reading but are, rather, an invitation to imitate and experiment. Thus, the Edition, through its critical commentary and accompanying resources, invites its audience to read and analyze the text, but also to explore and investigate the materials and processes detailed within it.

A “critical edition” is a scholarly format formalized in the nineteenth century to create a definitive version of a manuscript or manuscript corpus. The present Edition provides an accurate transcription of the manuscript’s middle French text, a lightly-normalized modernized French version, and a literal English translation that provides the clearest interpretation of the text possible, given its contents of often obscure, long forgotten, and incompletely- or confusingly-described technical processes. A List of Entries provides a searchable and filterable index of the almost 930 headings in the manuscript with lists of encoded terms contained in each entry. The accompanying Research Essays and the editorial comments inserted throughout the text of the Edition provide analysis and interpretation of the contents of the manuscript. The scholarly apparatus of the Edition also includes a glossary of technical terms used in the manuscript that was compiled in the course of the Text Workshops, a list of dictionaries and technical encyclopedias used in the Workshops, and the principles of transcription, translation, and encoding followed in the Workshops. The digital format of the Edition has allowed inclusion of multiple media formats and a dynamic design that goes beyond the features possible in a convention print edition.

The Project’s effort to produce this definitive product, an edition of Ms. Fr. 640, exists in tension with one of the greatest potentials of the digital humanities: user-directed, open-ended interactivity that allows scholarship to be built upon, broken down, with data, platform, and methods reused and reassembled by reader-users in new and inventive ways. A recognition of the tension between the Project’s aim of a definitive critical edition product and the digital humanities process we employed came to a head as we sought to produce the Edition through graduate and undergraduate workshops and courses, which necessitated working within the parameters of single-semester courses (although we extended this timeframe by two to three weeks, generally teaching for 16 weeks and editing the essays for several more). More significant, however, was our increasing immersion in the “recipes” and processes of Ms. Fr. 640 as the laboratory seminar students sought to re-make or reconstruct them. We quickly came to see that a text that describes and prescribes material processes is by its very nature not a definitive text—its processes can never be realized in writing, and they are often much harder to comprehend in words than by doing (or by observing, as YouTube has demonstrated). A text is not a particularly good teacher of techniques. Transmission of technique is much more effectively accomplished by watching and following the lead of an experienced practitioner. The analysis and interpretation of Ms. Fr. 640 necessitates extension into material and physical space. As the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 himself appears to have believed, knowledge is best transmitted by long accumulation and transmission from master to apprentice, and he emphasizes the need “to learn from those who preceded you in order to teach to those who come after” (fol. 166r).

Moreover, Ms. Fr. 640 is a compiled text, and it can be broken down and rearranged in any number of ways (as the author-practitioner must surely have planned to do if he meant to publish it). It is thus an ideal text for the reassemblage and reuse that its digital format makes possible. The Project expects that its own repurposing of the manuscript as “data” will in turn come to engender many different uses, and will lead to new interpretations of the text, thus expanding the scope of a “critical edition.” This flexibility and extensibility is in fact characteristic of both recipe collections and digital texts.

Making the Edition

The Making and Knowing Project created the Edition through a series of collaborative workshops, courses, and conferences that have involved students, craft practitioners, artists, scholars of the humanities and social sciences, natural and computer scientists, and practitioner-scholars from the emerging field of the digital humanities.1 Our approach combined a collaborative and iterative research process with a strong pedagogical commitment. In its essence, however, the Making and Knowing Project is an experiment in intensive collaboration, grad- and crowd-sourcing, the integration of pedagogy and original research, the intertwining of matter and texts, and sheer extemporizing and improvisation. Since the Project’s inception in June 2014, hundreds of scholars and students have now used Ms. Fr. 640 in their research and teaching. Although an obscure and anonymous text, it has already proven to be an important source of evidence across a number of disciplines. But the Project’s success in gathering together over 400 collaborators is due primarily to the intriguing text and the anonymous author-practitioner at its center. More information about the text and the possible identity of its author-practitioner can be found in the essays by Pamela Smith (“An Introduction to BnF Ms. Fr. 640”), Marc Smith (“Making Ms. Fr. 640”), Colin Debuiche, and Sarah Muñoz (“Le contexte toulousaine”).2 See also Vid. 1.

Text Workshops

The first stage of transcription and translation of the manuscript was carried out in a series of three-week paleography workshops that brought together graduate students (already in possession of advanced French) to learn to read middle French script by transcription exercises and then by intensive transcription and translation of Ms. Fr. 640 (Fig. 1, Fig. 2). Led by the Project’s Paleography Lead, Marc Smith, Professor of Latin Paleography at the École nationale des chartes in Paris (Fig. 3), and Pamela Smith, Director of the Making and Knowing Project, this phase received essential support from Claire Sabel in the first summer (2014), and from successive Making and Knowing postdoctoral scholars and project assistants, as well as Terry Catapano, the Project’s Digital Lead, and Alex Gil, Digital Scholarship Coordinator, Humanities and History Division, Columbia University Libraries. In 2014, Claire Sabel prepared the basic structure of the Project’s Google Drive, uploaded the digitized black-and-white microfilm copy of Ms. Fr. 640 (at that point, the only copy available from the BnF), and matched those pages in Google documents with the partial transcription and translation that had been commissioned by Pamela Smith from freelance translators Katia Scifo and Alice Bleuzen in segments over the previous several years. Each page of the manuscript was placed in a Google folder with files for each of the four versions of the manuscript that are now available for side-by-side viewing in the Edition’s panes: manuscript page facsimile, diplomatic transcription, normalized French transcription, and English translation. We chose to work in Google Drive despite its proprietary nature (and the fact that it was, in 2014, unfamiliar to almost all our students) because it was the only platform at that time which enabled apparently simultaneous work on the same document. Google also provided a comment function, something other collaborative platforms did not offer until more recently, and this proved essential for our workflow because successive cohorts of paleographers could leave information for the next summer’s campaign.3 Our concern about the proprietary nature of Google Drive remained, however, and in 2017 the Project shifted its workflow entirely to Github.

Beyond translating and transcribing, students also encoded the text according to basic Text Encoding Initiative (TEI) guidelines (with an ad hoc and frequently revised tag set adapted to the manuscript). From 2014 to 2019, seven such Text Workshops as well as a full translation read-through were held, and they were a learning process for all participants (Fig. 4): protocols for the processes of collective transcription, translation, and encoding had to be devised (and developed and revised from year to year). Our set of markup tags was discussed and revised every summer until 2017, which meant many repeated campaigns of encoding. All versions of the text have been encoded with both structural and semantic tags, which provide for another layer of analysis, both by the Project Team and by future users of the Edition. The semantic tags used indicate the Project’s interest in craft and material knowledge, as well as the manuscript’s historical context, and the knowledge base of the author-practitioner. With regard to craft knowledge, the Project chose to tag materials, tools, measurements, temporal expressions, plants, animals, body parts, sensory expressions, and environments. In terms of the historical context, the Project tagged words or phrases related to music, to medicine and health, to arms and armor, as well as trades and professions, coins, personal names (including the many authors listed in the first pages), languages and dialects (including Occitan, Poitevin, Latin, Greek, German, Italian), and place names. All these categories give insight into the author-practitioner’s identity and knowledge base, but we also tagged “author definitions,” that is, when the author-practitioner defines or explains a term (often a clarification about a material).4 These tags can be viewed in the first instance in the Markup View of the Edition (indicated by the </> icon), and, more usefully, in the List of Entries on the menu bar (https://edition640.makingandknowing.org/#/entries). We hope these tags will make possible computational analysis and interpretation of the manuscript in ways the Project has not imagined.

By 2016, we had a returning core group of students who had attended the Text Workshop every year and became instrumental in mentoring the novices and in thinking through best practices for efficient translation and transcription (Fig. 5). These dedicated participants included Heather Wacha, Soersha Dyon, Gregory Champeaud, José Beltrán, and François V. Pageau. We were joined every summer by students who assisted with digital tasks, directed by Terry Catapano (see https://edition640.makingandknowing.org/#/content/about/credits). From 2017 on, with the transcription of the manuscript complete, the focus of the Workshops shifted to translation and encoding. As described by Heather Wacha and Soersha Dyon in their essay in this Edition, “Turning Turtle: The Process of Translating BnF Ms. Fr. 640,” the interpretation and translation of the text went on even as the final touches were being put on the Edition. The collaborative nature of the translation process facilitated unexpected insights into the interpretation of the manuscript: the many perspectives and different expertises of the Workshop participants made the translation process a fascinating and rich journey, but it also led to inconsistency of language usage. Similarly, while digital tools allowed searching for all conjugated instances of single terms and thus could help to correct the inconsistencies, they also raised complex questions about what level of consistency in translation should be sought for a text such as Ms. Fr. 640: this manuscript resulted from the compilation of techniques and knowledge over generations, gathered together from a variety of workshops, which was then, as the marginal additions in the manuscript show, worked over again by its author in multiple campaigns. The fact that Ms. Fr. 640 is itself an unfinished document perhaps makes appropriate that this Edition shows the traces of the work of many hands, and the process of interpreting it will go on even after the Edition site becomes static.

Through this series of Text Workshops, the Project completed grad-sourced diplomatic and normalized French transcriptions and an English translation of Ms. Fr. 640, as well as customized encoding of the text for digital analysis and presentation. This process refined the Project’s methodology for collaborative scholarly editing and use of digital platforms, while simultaneously training approximately 45 graduate students in essential research skills, and involving a dozen computer science students in historical research. As with much else in this Project, the collaborative process and the digital methods also raised many fruitful questions.

Laboratory Seminars

As readers of early modern recipe literature will know, simply having the legible text of a recipe is often insufficient for understanding either the process being described or the desired product. Researchers may thus find themselves in a world of unfamiliar materials, foreign measurements (if any are provided), and peculiar descriptors that leave them bewildered. It was the task of the students in the Making and Knowing Project’s “laboratory seminars” to make sense of the manuscript’s entries (Fig. 6). In turn, their research essays produced in the laboratory seminar often allowed the students to refine the paleographers’ translations. See Vid. 2.

Beginning in September 2014, Pamela Smith, Donna Bilak, Jenny Boulboullé, and Joel Klein began teaching semester-long (15-week) laboratory seminars (Craft and Science: Making Objects in the Early Modern World) in which MA and PhD students (and a few BA/BSc students) made use of the text work of the summer paleographers to critically annotate and explicate the manuscript’s entries. The Edition’s critical commentary about the manuscript’s materials and processes has thus been largely created by student-generated content. In order to create publication-ready critical commentary on the manuscript, the students undertook document-based historical research, object-based museum research, and hands-on reconstruction of the recipes in the Making and Knowing Project’s 1940’s chemistry laboratory (Vid. 3). Much effort by the students and instructors went toward crafting these multimedia “annotations” (as we at first called them) into a historical argument about what the students’ research (both textual and hands-on) revealed about process, materials, sixteenth-century culture and society, and the identity of the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640, among many other topics on the manuscript’s historical and material context. While their essays contain information about their reconstruction experiments and their experience as humanities and social science students doing hands-on work in the lab, step-by-step details of their material reconstructions can only be found in their field notes (included in the footnotes of all reconstruction essays).

Each year, students focused their research on a specific group of thematically related recipes in the manuscript: Moldmaking and Metalworking in 2014–2015 (Vid. 4); Colormaking (including dyes, painting, staining, varnishes, and imitation gems) in 2015–2016 (Vid. 5); Practical Knowledge (including Vernacular Natural History, Medicine, Practical Optics, Perspective, and Mechanics) in 2016–2017, with Tianna Uchacz replacing Boulboullé (Vid. 6). In 2017, Sophie Pitman and Tillmann Taape took over from Bilak and Klein to co-lead with Smith and Uchacz the laboratory seminar on the theme of Ephemeral Art, and, in 2018, on Printmaking, Making Impressions and Inscriptions (Fig. 7).

In all these courses, students worked in close consultation with academic- and museum-based scholars and practitioners, including an intensive two-week period each semester during which the Project hosted an expert maker who helped to hone the students’ skills in relevant techniques. In Fall 2014, we drew upon the expertise of Tonny Beentjes, Programme Leader, Metals Conservation, University of Amsterdam, in sand casting.5 In the next semester, Spring 2015, Andrew Lacey, a sculptor, bronze founder, archaeometallurgist, and independent scholar specializing in the research of Renaissance bronzes, led nature casting techniques from the manuscript.6 Color making experts for 2015–2016 were specialists in technical art history, Marjolijn Bol (University of Utrecht), whose work explores intersections in art, history, material culture, and science, and Erma Hermens (University of Cambridge), whose research focuses on historical painting techniques, materials, and studio practice.7 For Practical Knowledge, Divya Anantharaman, Independent Taxidermist, Friends Forever Taxidermy, led techniques of taxidermy in reconstructing the entry on “animals dried in the oven” (fol. 130r),8 and the Project brought in several speakers to discuss aspects of the variety of subjects that fell under “practical knowledge.” In Fall 2017, Jo Kirby Atkinson, Retired Senior Scientific Officer, National Gallery, London, joined the Project to lead processes in the manuscript that aimed to produce “ephemeral” objects, commonly produced by artists for noble entries and weddings, and festive civic events.9 For Printmaking, Ad Stijnman, printmaker and independent scholar, led the Team and the class in printmaking techniques (which involved the processes in Ms. Fr. 640 for making impressions and inscriptions in a variety of media).

The core Making and Knowing Team (the Postdoctoral Scholars, Pamela Smith, and Naomi Rosenkranz) worked very closely with each student in the research and writing of their research essays, and the Postdocs and Pamela Smith edited each essay multiple times at the end of the semester. In preparation for publication of the Edition, Pamela Smith, Tianna Uchacz, Tillmann Taape, and Clément Godbarge edited the student essays again.

Skill Building in the Laboratory Seminar

Most students coming into the laboratory seminar were MA and PhD students who had been formally trained in the humanities or social sciences but generally had little experience in either a laboratory or an art studio (Fig. 8). Even those students with experience had never encountered nor attempted a historical reconstruction. We thus began each laboratory seminar with six to seven weeks of skill building exercises that introduced students to the subject matter of the theme for the year, as well as the general methods of historical reconstruction (Vid. 7). The students started each semester by working in pairs to reconstruct a historical culinary recipe from the sixteenth or seventeenth century with the express goal of simulating, as far as possible, early modern ingredients, apparatus, and methods. For example, students reconstructed a sixteenth-century “Tart of Hippes” (Fig. 9), a seventeenth-century chocolate drink, the mysterious “fine cracknels,” “Excellent Cake” (Fig. 10), and even some of the few culinary recipes in Ms. Fr. 640, including “Excellent Mustard.”10 Students brought the results of their reconstructions to the following week’s class, when we discussed and digested the outcome. The point of this exercise was to elicit from the students a working template laying out questions, pitfalls, and resources for reconstruction that they could develop and employ in their experiments for the rest of the semester. An important aim of this skill building exercise was to encourage the students to reflect upon the process of reconstruction and its status as historical evidence, for this is a central emphasis of the Project: What degree of authenticity is sought? Which compromises in tools, materials, and time-saving are acceptable in reconstruction, and which are not? What makes a reconstruction persuasive evidence? How should it be employed in a historical argument? Indeed, this last question took at least a couple of iterations of the laboratory seminar to satisfactorily answer, and, as a result, the student research essays vary widely from year to year in aim, voice, and structure of their arguments.

Reconstruction has been used in some fields, such as art conservation, to reproduce a given material as faithfully as possible in order to understand its handling properties, and, while this was certainly the Project’s aim as well, the course of a single semester was not enough to carry out the many experiments necessary to establish such knowledge with certainty. Thus, the goal of most reconstructions in the Making and Knowing lab was more like that of experimental archaeology, in which “proof of concept” is sought about the procedure by which an excavated object was produced. Can a particular process, with the materials and tools present in the environs of the excavation, lead to the production of an object like the excavated one? In their reconstructions, the lab seminar students deciphered materials and processes by searching first for similar recipes in texts composed in the same period as Ms. Fr. 640, and they studied museum objects to try to understand the aim of the entry they had chosen. They then planned an experiment or a series of experiments that attempted to follow the description of the process in Ms. Fr. 640, in order to gain “proof of concept” about whether the “recipe” could (or could not) produce the object or material in the manuscript. Sometimes, quite implausible recipes produced surprisingly effective results (such as breadmolding, marbling with varnish, and a mold made from ox foot bone and rock salt)11, but all experiments would have needed much longer than a semester to be tested thoroughly. In the short span of fifteen or so weeks, students were naturally unable to carry out a full scientific or conservatorial reconstruction, nor could the Project undertake comparative scientific analyses on the results of their reconstructions, although students did seek out literature that analyzed extant museum objects to compare with the results of their experiments. Even in this short period, however, the students’ exploratory reconstructions succeeded extraordinarily well in raising further productive questions, not only about processes and materials, but also about environments, life worlds, and practices of daily life.

Beyond introducing students to basic methods of historical reconstruction, further skill building acquainted the students both with the year’s theme and with methods of data collection and recording of field and lab notes. In the second class, students were introduced to methods for keeping a laboratory notebook by Dr. Joseph Ulichny (Associate in the Discipline of Chemistry), as well as the methods used by anthropologists, presented by Professor Brian Boyd (Director of Museum, Anthropology), for taking field notes. The students uploaded their own field notes to a common platform (a course Wiki in the first years and then Google Drive) that allowed instructors to give feedback and observe student progress but also served as the primary repository for raw data produced in the laboratory. These hybrid field-lab notes are available to users of the Edition through the student Research Essays, where they are linked. All photographs of laboratory work are stored in an openly accessible repository on Flickr.12

Each year, the skill building exercises included recipes from Ms. Fr. 640 that prescribe a simple method for making a quick cast of an object by impressing it in freshly baked bread. Once the impressed bread has cooled and hardened, molten wax or sulfur is poured into the bread mold to give a cheap, easy, and effective sense of how well the original object might be reproduced. If that object is under production itself, breadmolding allows an artist to try out variations before committing to a final design (Fig. 11). The process could also be used to hasten the casting of copies of metal reliefs, such as medals, in gold, silver, or other precious metals (fol. 156r). Drawing on bread’s property of being pliable when moist but then shrinking and hardening when dry, the author-practitioner also suggests breadmolding as a way to shrink a larger object or vary the shape (fol. 140v). The students’ work to research and reconstruct this recipe revealed no other contemporaneous texts that mentioned casting sulfur and wax in bread before the nineteenth century (a source that was not found until 2019 by Min Lim).13 As the author-practitioner gave no instruction about the type of bread he used, the students engaged in extensive research and hands-on trials of possible recipes for early modern varieties of breads, coming to find that recipes with clear instructions for quotidian varieties of sixteenth-century bread are extremely rare. The students began this exercise by spending two weeks learning to bake bread with sourdough starter (after the first year we provided all students with starter), which proved an adventure in itself given the lack of bread recipes. (See Vid. 8) Interestingly, the first recipe for “French bread” that the Team could locate (thanks to Sietske Fransen and Katherine Reinhart) comes from John Evelyn, Fellow of the Royal Society, who sent it to the Society in 1664, entitling it “The several manners of making Bread in France, where by universal consent, the best bread in the World is eaten.”14 The collecting and recording of the processes and recipes of crafts and tradespeople by the “new experimental philosophers” of the Royal Society (founded 1660) shows the connections that these new scientists saw between their own investigation of nature and the techniques and knowledge of craftspeople.15

Like many other entries in Ms. Fr. 640, this simple (and quite effective) process of molding in fresh bread led students to many further material and process investigations: in kneading, rising, and baking the bread for the molds, they encountered the living cycles of the bread induced by leavening—an important component of attitudes to nature and the body in the sixteenth century. In melting and mixing sulfur, wax, and tallow, they observed changes in the states of these materials. Bread molding also demonstrated to us all the existence of previously unknown (and unimagined) techniques. The new vistas of research into unknown objects, techniques, and materials that are opened up by “reading” the text through “doing” the processes described in it constituted one of the unexpected and important outcomes of the Making and Knowing Project’s research on the manuscript. We have learned how fruitful this method of reading and researching a how-to text can be for offering novel perspectives and raising new topics for investigation.

These breadmolding exercises also provided insight into the mechanics of molding in one- and two-piece molds, training that continued in the first year of the Project in molding and casting in cuttlefish bone (Fig. 12), and, after that, in producing “sands” and “binders” to make sand molds and cast metals into them. Other skill building exercises included gessoing a panel (Fig. 13), for which almost two weeks were needed to apply nine coats (including drying and sanding each one), gold and silver leaf gilding, preparing and priming a canvas, then building up the various ground and “dead” layers, “growing” and painting out verdigris, making lake pigments (Fig. 14), grinding blue mineral pigments, and a variety of printmaking techniques. See Vid. 9. We found we could build on the previous semester’s knowledge and work. For example, the research on making sand molds in the first semester was then turned into skill building in the second semester, and the gessoed panels of the first semester were then employed as test panels for the students’ skill building in the following semesters. We found that skills learned in the laboratory were passed on to the next cohort of students, such as the “squeeze test” developed on the basis of fol. 118v for determining the proper consistency of the mixture of “sand” and binder for making sand molds (Fig. 15),16 or the “paper test” for determining the proper heat of an alloy before pouring (Fig. 16).17 In this way, we came to see how techniques, skills, and knowledge quite literally accumulate in the bodies, practices, and spaces of the workshop.

A plethora of insights from the Project’s reconstructions can be found in the almost 100 student research essays, but one of the most important insights from the experience of the Project’s laboratory seminars is the need to formulate a standard of literacy in materials and techniques. An essential part of the historical toolbox for those historians whose objects of study are historical materials and techniques, including historians of material culture, art, and science in the pre-industrial period, can benefit from training in this embodied literacy. The only efficient—perhaps the sole—means of beginning to acquire this literacy is through hands-on work, and the reconstruction of historical techniques and objects can form a first step in acquiring this literacy. While students in the semester-long course can never reach the stage of true literacy or skilled and expert practical knowledge, they begin to appreciate the rigor and time needed to attain it, and they are impelled to ask new questions of texts and objects.

Working Group Collaborations

The Project is fortunate in having many collaborators over the five years of creating the Edition. Foremost are the scholars invited to the annual three-day Working Group Meeting at the end of each academic year (see https://edition640.makingandknowing.org/#/content/about/credits) (Fig.17, Fig. 18, Fig. 19, Fig. 20, Fig. 21, Fig. 22). These participants read and provided critical oversight on student annotations, gave lectures on the relationship of their own research to Ms. Fr. 640, and remained engaged with the Project after the meeting.18 The Project invited Working Group scholars and expert makers to write essays for the Edition that shed light on Ms. Fr. 640 and its cultural context.

Local museum staff in New York City were also crucial participants in Making and Knowing activities. For example, in 2014, when the class created portrait medals by casting into two-piece sand molds, we first visited the Metropolitan Museum of Art, where curators led an examination of relevant medals from the museum’s collection. Back in our laboratory, the group then worked with Tonny Beentjes to reconstruct a method for “Excellent Sand” (fol. 118v) which called for ground plaster and brick dust moistened with a solution of sal ammoniac and brandy in water. This was only one of a remarkable variety of recipes in the manuscript for mold “sands” and binders (Fig. 23). Our students took their sand molds to the workshop of Ubaldo Vitali, silversmith, conservator, and independent scholar, who supervised the process of pouring molten metals (tin and silver) into our molds (Fig. 24).

These first attempts at casting both silver and tin were complete failures in producing nice looking objects, but extremely valuable learning experiences. In our excitement to pour the metal, we neglected the clear instructions in the margin of the manuscript that explain how to dry the molds over a fire. Our molds were thus too wet and the heat from the silver created steam, which resulted in considerable metal flashing and, in one case, a geyser of molten metal escaping from our molds (Fig. 25).19 The lessons learned during collaborations such as these contributed directly to further research, and in this case, we were eventually able to follow the same entry in the manuscript to produce a medal with relative success (see Vid. 10). Process will nevertheless always remain more important than product to the Making and Knowing Project’s research.

Digital Development

Over the past five years, the Making and Knowing Project has proven a crucible of experience for its many participants in the novel modes of working pioneered by the digital humanities—collaborative humanities research, and experimentation in pedagogy, research, and diverse disciplinary methodologies. As noted above, the tension between the Project’s goal of a definitive critical edition and the digital humanities (DH) methods employed in pursuit of that goal came upon us slowly, only revealing itself as the Project co-developed—with Terry Catapano, Digital Lead; Tianna Uchacz, Making and Knowing Postdoctoral Scholar; Steven Feiner, Computer Science, Columbia University; and Dennis Tenen, English, Columbia—new DH courses intended for humanities and social science students (taught 2017, 2019), as well as for computer science students (taught 2018, 2019) at Columbia. These experiments in digital pedagogy included What is a Book in the 21st Century? Working with Historical Texts in a Digital Environment (spring 2017) and Transforming Texts: Textual Analysis, Literary Modeling, and Visualization (spring 2019). In these courses, humanities, social science, and computer science students helped to think through and experiment with alternative formats, design, and visualization of a “digital critical edition” of Ms. Fr. 640. With Terry Catapano, the students in What is a Book for the 21st Century, created a minimal digital edition of parts of the text, organized not by folio, but instead by entry, and encoded with a new set of semantic tags.20

As part of the Columbia University Computer Graphics and User Interfaces Lab (CGUI), Steven Feiner and Carmine Elvezio developed a course, 3D User Interfaces and Augmented Reality, taught in 2018 and 2019, in which computer science students used the Project’s data developed for the Edition to create new visualizations not modeled on the codex format, and an app for taking field notes by employing augmented reality tools.21

The Project’s approach to the Edition’s digital development has been shaped by commitment to long-term use, availability, and sustainability. All decisions and modes of work have been informed by two guiding principles: to generate and preserve all project data in sustainable and open formats while best facilitating the multifaceted collaborative research process, and to ensure commitment to open-access and open-source standards wherever possible. A digital edition that can withstand or respond to changing technologies is better able to remain a sustainable resource to the scholarly and wider community. 

The design and development of the digital environment for the Edition since 2016 has been in the hands of Nick Laiacona, the head of Performant Software, and his staff, with the collaboration of Terry Catapano, the Project’s Digital Lead, and Naomi Rosenkranz, Project Manager and Assistant Director.22

The extremely intensive editing of the manuscript and creation of resources, such as the Glossary and Comments, during the final, intense stages leading up to the release of the Edition was carried out by Assistant Director Naomi Rosenkranz, Postdoctoral Scholars Tianna Uchacz, Tillmann Taape, Clément Godbarge, and Project Director Pamela Smith.

Research and Teaching Companion

Research projects generally result in the publication of research products, while their workflow and methodologies are maintained only in the memory of research team members. The digital humanities have offered new ways of thinking about dissemination and sustainability of scholarly products, but they also provide opportunities for evaluating, refining, and sharing the research process. Despite being defined as modes of scholarship, the digital humanities are often solely represented by products rather than the methodologies employed in their creation, and the publication of these digital products then effaces forever the work that went into their creation, and experiential insights are lost to the makers of future projects.

Moreover, digital projects suffer from problems of sustainability not just through obsolescence of technology, but by lack of discoverability and use. Sustainability is not just a technological problem but also a social one. The Making and Knowing Project’s Research and Teaching Companion aims to ensure the preservation and dissemination of techniques and methodologies that would otherwise be lost in the hidden background and individual history of the Project and its creation of the Edition, as well as encourage use and interaction with the Edition in order to promote discoverability and sustained use. The Companion aggregates the Project’s many resources developed over the previous years, from recipe reconstruction assignments in the lab or kitchen, to syllabi for laboratory and digital humanities courses, to bibliographies for researching and teaching material culture, to case studies of the Companion in use. In addition, from our own experiences and insights, the Project abstracts a set of guidelines and instructions which can be scaled and adapted to the needs of a wide range of prospective digital and research projects and teaching initiatives, beyond the subject matter and institutional context of the Project. This body of experience includes:

  • The use of digital platforms to facilitate a collaborative approach to scholarly editing. The Companion abstracts these approaches into protocols for wide adaptation for print or digital formats.

  • In conjunction with Columbia’s Center for Teaching and Learning, the Project contributed to the articulation of baseline standards and professional competencies in digital scholarship required of graduate students entering the job market, and helped develop a model for digital literacy at the university level.23 The Project has co-developed new course offerings aimed at humanities and social science students (2017, 2019) as well as computer science students (2018, 2019). These experiments in digital pedagogy and their syllabi are also detailed in the Companion.

  • The Project has developed methods of hands-on reconstructions of historical techniques and processes as a form of historical evidence and a tool in the classroom. Reconstruction cultivates a type of close reading that provides insight into the past use of materials, allows the careful deciphering of terminology and technical processes in historical making practices, and illuminates attitudes toward the natural world and human interventions in it. Moreover, it builds hand-skills, dexterity, and bodily familiarity with process to provide experience-driven understandings of embodied forms of knowledge. This is not only a powerful tool of historical inquiry, but also introduces participants to the cycle of trial, failure, replication, and a responsive, adaptive approach to unexpected outcomes. The Companion not only provides protocols for laboratory reconstruction and syllabi for the laboratory seminar, but also lesson plans for skill building exercises.

  • The Project’s mode of sharing knowledge and expertise follows an “apprenticeship” model, as each participant trains others in their specialization, whether as a visiting expert maker leading skill building sessions, or as returning paleography students mentoring and training newcomers in the Project’s methodologies and protocols for transcribing and translating the manuscript. This consolidates knowledge and cultivates the skills of close observation (both of fellow workers at the bench and of the material under investigation), inculcating hand skills and enhancing cognitive abilities of perception and reflection.

  • The Project models a collaborative interdisciplinary approach to the humanities. Deciphering Ms. Fr. 640’s ruby glass recipe, for example, required not only multiple trials in response to unanticipated results, but also the collective expertise of historians, material scientists, geochemists, glassblowers, and students. Such collaboration fosters interaction and knowledge sharing among individuals from various disciplines, facilitated by digital formats and tools. Peer teaching and collaborative learning has been shown to increase understanding and retention of knowledge for tutor and recipient.

  • These multidisciplinary, expertise-directed, process-oriented, and iterative practices undergird the Project’s pedagogy-driven research , through which students acquire skills by generating research as active contributors to the Project’s core goals. Following precepts of project-based group learning, students consolidate their newly gained skills and insights through meaningful practice so they can be transferred to other courses, their own research, and their future careers.

Setting out our experiences and strategies, syllabi, lesson plans, and research methods in the Companion outlines an effective program for using the Edition as well as for research and teaching in the humanities in the digital age, and it will provide the tools and resources for implementing this program in other settings.

This Research and Teaching Companion was conceived as a response to the many requests made to the Project for collaboration. The Project has hosted academic and public outreach events, and successfully adapted its hands-on pedagogy for other scholarly settings (V&A/RCA History of Design postgraduate programme,24 University of Toronto, Science History Institute, University of Glasgow, University of Amsterdam, Rhode Island School of Design); K-12 environments (PRISMS High School, Princeton, NJ; École Secondaire Notre-Dame, Woodstock, Canada); non-traditional academic settings (Nunavut Arctic College); community-based organizations (Genspace Community Biology Lab); and public-facing partner institutions (Metropolitan Museum, New York Public Library, Jewelry Arts, Inc, Sato Sakura Gallery).

This has demonstrated to the Project the importance of building a new interactive culture in the humanities, using digital tools to share not only data and finished products, but also valuable insights into the work behind the scenes, and the integration of research and pedagogy. This involves questioning precisely what it means for a scholarly digital product to be “final,” and to rethink the concept of digital sustainability to include not just the technical sense of static data storage, but also a social environment of continued user involvement and dynamic, interactive project development.

Insights from Student Reconstruction Research

Reconstruction involves both subjective action, self-reporting, and the production of evidence by the historian in the present, a process rife with pitfalls, and the status and strength of its products as historical evidence must always be at the forefront of the researcher’s consciousness. It is useful to think of this method as “historical experiment,” drawing in part on the methods of the natural sciences, as Hasok Chang suggests.25 Where he sees reconstruction as useful in science education, however, the Project uses it as a tool of material exploration and historical research. The students’ reconstruction research combined conventional historical textual research, object-based study, and hands-on experiments to reconstruct the processes and materials of Ms. Fr. 640. All student research had the goal of building material and technical awareness, and of providing information and evidence for research essays; in practice, the essays had diverse aims and outcomes.

Workshop Practice and Daily Life

Through research and reconstruction, we also learned more about workshop practice and the nature of experiential or practical knowledge, as revealed, for example, in the author-practitioner’s use of particular terminology, such as “impalpable,” and his use of artisanal tricks of the trade such as the squeeze test for ascertaining the consistency of molding sand, and the paper test to measure heat. Failures

Students also found evidence of the author-practitioner experiencing failures in his practice, for example in making imitation rubies, or in making sand molds for casting metal. In some of the manuscript’s entries, the author-practitioner appears to have recorded his experiences, perhaps as he was learning, while others seem to be hearsay or observations from other workshops. When students made mistakes and went back to the manuscript to try to understand where they had gone wrong, they sometimes found that the author-practitioner had included a warning about just their problem, as if he too had experienced it. For example, while making molds for lifecasting, we made the clay walls of the mold too thin, and they collapsed under the outward pressure of the plaster “sand” as we poured it into the mold. When we went back to the manuscript, we found that the author-practitioner had warned us about just this problem, saying, “take heed to make [the clay circle] strong and thick according to the size that you want, otherwise it would burst, the sand being inside” (fol. 145v).

Material Imaginary

Especially interesting has been the insight provided by reconstruction into the author-practitioner’s system of knowledge about nature and the behavior of natural materials—his “material imaginary” —including qualitative material categories, such as hot, cold, wet, dry, fat, lean, sweet, and sour; material metaphors based on, for example, the materials of vermilion and sulfur, which provided a means, not so much to signify material meaning (although they also enabled that), but, rather, to enact principles of material process. The author-practitioner’s vocabulary of moldmaking and casting conveys a conception of the material world as animate, such as veins of wax (filons de cire) and breathing holes (souspirails) to vent the mold (fol. 155r). The interactions between materials often demonstrate a view of materials being friendly or hostile. For example, “Latten is the enemy of gold & the friend of silver” (Le letton est ennemy de lor & amy de largent, fol. 121v). His material imaginary also included various areas or activities of emphasis, such as experimenting with the states and phases of materials, or trying to arrest ephemerality in living things through lifecasting, preserving plants and fruits, and taxidermy. In most of the recipes, we see that human utility is the overarching organizing principle in creating (often implicit) taxonomies of natural materials.

The Project has taught its many participants how much information the reconstruction of technical recipes can yield. One of the important results of reconstruction is the new level of understanding of the recipes that it gives. By the constant moving between manuscript and hands-on experimentation, we gained an understanding of the manuscript that would not have been possible by simply reading through it in a linear way (which, given the extreme non-linearity of the text and its contents, would have been near impossible in any case). We have also learned from our hands-on historical research that reconstruction-based investigations can provide another window into artisanal workshop practices, but it can also make us aware of our inevitable “modern framings” or “lenses” through which we tend to look at the material world of the early modern period. Our research in materials suggests the inadequacy of some modern classifications to convey, not only the variety of that early modern material world, but also the ways in which practitioners thought with and through their materials and experiences to form hypotheses, taxonomies, and knowledge systems.

Collaboration and Expertise

One of the challenges faced by the Making and Knowing Project is the lack of expertise among the individual student researchers on the project. As noted above, the laboratory seminars can do no more than give individual students exposure to the experiential knowledge of materials and techniques, and to make them aware of what they do not know. It is thus surprising how much the collective nature of the project and the many collaborators with different levels of expertise make up for this lack of individual expert engagement. The Project’s collaboration with other groups, such as instructors and students from the technical art history and conservation courses at the University of Amsterdam led to progressively more successful reconstructions, for example in the technique for what has been called incuse reverse casting, that is when a portrait, for example, appears on a medal on one side in relief and on the obverse as a concave form (“molding a hollow on one side and in relief on the other”). The Columbia University students deciphered the technique, and achieved proof of concept (Fig. 26), and the University of Amsterdam Metals Conservation programme students were able to build on this to accomplish a skilled product (Fig. 27). In a similar way, the skill building exercises, repeated from semester to semester, demonstrated that an oft-practiced technique can lead to new methods, shortcuts, and the accumulation of knowledge, as in the squeeze test, discussed above. Craft practice is often portrayed as simple, even mechanical, repetition of long established tradition. The constant innovation of science is sometimes contrasted with so-called rote methods of craft, but the Project’s experience indicates much greater similarity and overlap between these two modes and epistemologies of exploring the natural world.

Recipes and Emergent Knowledge

Ms. Fr. 640 is, among other things, a collection of recipes. The research that the Project has carried out over the past five years has demonstrated that the modest form of a technical recipe actually forms an efficient vehicle for conveying active knowledge, and also gives insight into the nature of practical knowledge itself. By their usually anonymous form, recipes assert the collective and cumulative nature of practical knowledge-making.26 Like other written forms of practical knowledge, recipes can point to bodily action, but obviously cannot accomplish that action, nor provide for every circumstance which might bring about necessary variations in procedure. They are not precision instruments, but approximating and flexible tools. In the early modern period, recipes were often listed in series, setting out several slightly varying instructions for the same process or object, often simply labeled “another way.” The recipes thus formed an invitation to action, and, by their serial format, replicated the necessity of repeated hands-on testing and trying to work through the variables and resistances of materials to bring about a successful (and replicable) result. Practical knowledge is very different from propositional knowledge, which is easy to capture in a written proof.27 Practical knowledge always involves emergent phenomena, and its “proof” is brought into being in real time. A recipe, or better still, a compilation of recipes, thus indicates, in abbreviated form, the particularity, variability, and the emergent quality of practical knowledge. Recipes, then, can effectively capture in written form—to the extent this is possible in writing—this characteristic of emergence, as well as setting out a pathway for the acquisition of skill, by means of which emergent phenomena can be channeled and harnessed. Only by engaging in the practice of reconstruction—itself a form of emergent knowledge—would we have recognized this dimension of this remarkable recipe compilation.28

Conclusion

The Project has taught its team, students, and collaborators so many lessons in such a compressed timespan that it is hard to capture them all in the short space of this essay. One central lesson was the striking efficacy and stimulus of collaborative work in the humanities. Another was about the constant experimentation of the workshop, as we came to see that failure, repetition, and “extension” are regular and very important components of learning through experience in the workshop, a useful lesson in today’s high-stakes testing educational regime. Our experiments taught us much both about the unexpected lives (and instability) of materials, as well as their properties. We have come to better understand the author-practitioner’s bodily intimacy with some materials he used, and how this sensory engagement shaped his classification of materials into taxonomies. We have been able to delimit some parts of his taxonomy of materials and his material imaginary to see the ways in which he reasoned and experimented across types of materials in order to hypothesize about useful materials and probable results of his trials. We gained insight into his primary areas of interest, which included imitation of all types, from life casting roses in metal, to sleight of hand tricks, to transforming the state or properties of one material to resemble another, or attempting to alter a material to take on properties that are not native to it, such as altering a brittle material like sulfur by giving it the qualities of a malleable one such as wax. He worked to challenge his materials, manipulating them to take on the often contrary properties of another material in order to transform the rough into the “impalpable,” the lean into the fat, the hard or brittle into the workable, the sour into the sweet, and the elements of earth and stones into the simulacra of gemstones. Over the last six years, we have thought much about why the author-practitioner might compile this record of practice, and we were always interrogating our own practice and both the potentials and pitfalls of using our reconstructions as historical evidence.

After these five years, we have come to think it very odd that historians whose object of study is historical materials and techniques—including historians of material culture, of art, and of science—have generally not seen hands-on engagement with the materials of their historical topics as an essential part of training and research. We have come to see literacy in materials and techniques as an essential part of our intellectual toolbox. Admittedly, the hands-on research into historical techniques and objects undertaken by the Making and Knowing Project cannot bring any of the participants to true proficiency in this literacy, but it can begin to provide such training. It can make all its students aware of what they do not know, even what they do not yet know how to ask. In that spirit, this Edition by no means exhausts the potential of this extraordinary manuscript (and others like it) to inform new histories of science and art, and to spur the practice of hands-on experimental pedagogy in the sciences and humanities alike. The Project welcomes readers and experimenters to this Edition who we hope will use it to formulate new perspectives on our many open questions, including those questions we have not yet thought to ask.
