Painting in Oil on Taffeta I

By , DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/adp7-8568
← Back to Essays

Abstract

This essay investigates the entry “Painting in oil on taffeta” on fol. 42v, a short recipe for a coating that can be applied to taffeta to prepare it to receive oil paint. In aim, the recipe is similar to a more detailed set of instructions on fol. 10v for making a priming coat to prevent oil paint from “running” or bleeding when painted on taffeta; however, the recipe on fol. 42v differs in materials and technique. As the recipe offers no quantities or proportions for the three ingredients, the essay questions whether this information was unknown to the author-practitioner, a workshop secret, or an implicit acknowledgement that quantities might need to be adjusted by trial and error to accommodate different pigments and oil binders.

BnF Ms. Fr. 640, fol. 42v

Ms. Fr. 640 contains very few references to working with fabric but specifies two methods by which one can paint on cloth in oil without the oil running.1 The repetition of this topic between fol. 10v and fol. 42v suggests a demand for painted taffeta, and the simplicity of the respective entries offers an opportunity to determine the necessity and role of each individual ingredient.2 While the recipe on fol. 42v, “Painting in oil on taffeta,” aims to replicate the outcome of the instructions on fol. 10v, “For painting in oil on taffeta without the oil running,” the steps to reach that goal differ almost entirely, warranting further exploration of this second technique.3

Our expectations for the outcome of the recipe on fol. 42v were based on our reconstruction research of the recipe on fol. 10v. Fol. 42v calls for “honey, alum water & starch water” and fol. 10v calls for a layer of “strong glue” (colle forte) “soaked for 24 hours in water then gently brought to boil so that it is hardly strong,” with some honey added. The use of a “strong glue,” probably made from animal skins,4 and honey suggests a rather sticky consistency, so we expected the alum water (made by boiling potash alum in water until the alum dissolves) and starch water to produce a similar consistency. Indeed the viscous wheat starch paste (i.e., our “starch water”) resembled an animal skin glue, while the alum water served to dilute it.

A comparison of the recipe on fol. 42v with other uses of alum water in the manuscript revealed that alum water is also used to fix a dye. A recipe for making gray wood on fol. 73r includes the use of alum water. Potash alum was used in Europe in the sixteenth century to harden “gelatinous sizes.”5 This may suggest that its role in the recipe was to allow the color to retain its strength while speeding the drying.

Equally obscure was the “starch water” (eau d’amydon). Randle Cotgrave’s Dictionarie of the French and English Tongues (1611) translates amydon as a “fine wheat flour steeped in water.”6 Though of a much later date, we found a helpful explanation in a nineteenth-century book of chemistry of the term “amidin,” which derives from the French amydon and is a synonym for “gelatinous starch” produced when ground wheat grains, added to water, expand and form “a transparent jelly” or wheat starch paste.7 We hypothesized that such a paste seemed a likely interpretation of the recipe’s “starch water,” since this paste—or amidin—much like the “strong glue” (colle forte) in the recipe on fol. 10v, would enhance the adhesive quality of the layer.8

Honey, although the most straightforward ingredient in the recipe, retained some degree of mystery. Its particular role in the recipe can be illuminated with other references to honey in the manuscript. Fol. 10v calls for honey to “soften” the glue mixture, though the particular meaning of the word “soften” is not made clear.9 A recipe on fol. 32r for laying gold on distemper is especially enlightening. It references the use of “batture” (also mentioned in the recipe on fol. 10v) and says to add “very little honey, that is to say a few drops to make it stick.”10 Honey is thus revealed to be a thickening agent, and we are furthermore given some indication of quantity, which is otherwise lacking in the recipe.11

Observations and Conclusions

For the reconstruction of the taffeta recipe on fol. 42v, we separated the taffeta into four squares to isolate the effect of the various ingredients that this recipe lists for a “first layer” (couche premiere) to prepare the fabric for an application of oil paint.12 When we applied the first layer mixture to the taffeta, we found that the trials with significantly more alum water than amidin dried with tiny white particles on the surface.

After applying oil paint to the taffeta treated with this “first layer,” it became clear that the “running” of oil referred to the oil spreading beyond the painted area. We used a paint that we composed from linseed oil (with trials of cold pressed and stand oils) and madder lake pigment. As the paint dried, dark oil stains formed around the edges of the paint (Fig. 1, Fig. 2 Fig. 3, Fig. 4). The trials with more alum water retained a deeper red color. This may have been due to the alum water’s function as a mordant, which can help fix the color. The alum water also seemed to facilitate applying the first layer of mixture to the taffeta. Since alum water is mentioned in both taffeta recipes on fol. 10v and fol. 42v, we assumed that it would play an important role in preventing oil from “running.” However, our trials showed that the oil spread even further when oil paint was applied to first layers containing more alum water. Moreover, mixing the pigments with stand oil rather than cold pressed linseed oil caused the oil and paint to spread further (Fig. 5).

A description of such an effect can be found in a copy of Roger de Piles’s book Élémens de peinture pratique. Although the book is not contemporaneous with Ms. Fr. 640 (it was published after de Piles’s death in 1709), it does provide a point of comparison for the recipes. In de Piles’s book, the recipe reads as follows:

One needs to first prepare the taffeta with a gum, the composition of which is as follows. Take fish glue as large as a bean, cut it in little pieces, & soak it for twelve hours in a glass of water. Next melt it on a fire just until boiling, then drain it and let it cool. When one wants to use it, one needs to heat it, & having stretched your taffeta well, apply it quite hot with a sponge, as evenly as it can be. Once the taffeta is dry, you can lay your colors on it, without worrying that they will soak in or that they will spread more than they should.13

It is interesting to compare the different descriptions of what this layer prevents in de Piles’s recipe with the Ms. Fr. 640 recipes. While fol. 10v provides a recipe for a first layer “for painting in oil on taffeta without the oil running,” de Piles’ recipe gives instructions for a first glue layer that prevents colors from soaking or spreading more than they should, which may accord with our experiment using red paint prepared with stand oil.

As mentioned above, neither of the recipes specifies any quantities or proportions (except to say just a little honey), which might suggest that they call for similar amounts of alum and wheat starch powder, or perhaps that the proportions were considered to be common knowledge. We observed significant differences among the four groups with varying ratios of alum water and starch water. Quantity matters in making the first layer because too little alum water will make the taffeta crumple while too much will not prevent the oil from spreading. The absence of proportions, though not uncommon, is noteworthy. As in the other taffeta recipe on fol. 10v, this seems to call for trial and error to achieve the desired effect on fabric. Since the quality of the taffeta, pigments, and oils used could change, our author-practitioner might be implying here that it is up to the person following the recipe to find the proper proportions “by judgement and discretion” (par jugement & discretion), a phrase he uses on fol. 158v in a recipe for “Colors for green leaves.”14 It is also possible that the author-practitioner himself did not know the specific proportions. As we suggested, judging by the brevity of the recipe, the author-practitioner might have copied or heard of the recipe without particular proportions and wrote it down, not (yet) having tested it himself. Further, it could be that the proportions were a workshop secret, with the craftsmen unwilling to divulge this integral piece of information. Finally, we must consider the readers of such a manuscript. If they wanted to perform trials following the instructions of the manuscript as we do today, it might appear inconvenient but probably not unexpected to an early modern reader that the author-practitioner did not record the proportions. As we noted above, this might suggest that the author-practitioner expected that anyone using his recipes would read them as an invitation to experiment to find the optimal proportions, taking into consideration the type, condition, and quality of the oil, starch water, alum water, and taffeta used. This echoes William Eamon’s reading of recipes as “prescriptions for an experiment” in his research on early modern books of secrets.15 Rather than seeing the impreciseness concerning ratios or proportions as a lack of crucial information and an inconvenience to the reader, we could read the taffeta recipes as testimonies to a lively and diverse experimental culture flourishing in the early modern period.
