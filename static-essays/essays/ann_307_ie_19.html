Working (with) the Corps: The Body of Sands, Colors, and Varnishes in Ms. Fr. 640

By , DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/jyms-a974

Abstract

This essay traces the use of the term corps in Ms. Fr. 640, investigating its occurrence, meaning, and translation. While the term—which literally translates as “body”—seems straightforward to understand at first sight, close reading and comparison with contemporary sources and dictionaries reveals its complex web of meaning. Reconstructions underscore this fact and allow insight into the polysemic qualities of corps. Employing concepts from linguistics and anthropology, we argue that the author-practitioner employed corps as a rich linguistic strategy that allowed him to navigate the fields of practice and theory, to document or communicate aspects of artistic practice that would otherwise remain ineffable.

Introduction

In two experiments, one in 2014 and one in 2016, students set out to make “a cast of wax to represent an animal that one has not got,” following the instructions of a handwritten recipe in the French late sixteenth-century manuscript BnF Ms. Fr. 640.1 The students gathered the ingredients listed in the recipe (fols. 139v–140v) for making a “black sulfured wax” especially suited for modelling or casting a wax model that “melts with very little heat and exits without leaving any filth.” The recipe calls for “white wax, which is more appropriate for this work than anything else because it is firmer & does not leave as much filth,” “charcoal, pestled & finely passed through a linen or a sleeve,” and “as much sulfur as a large walnut,” pulverized and melted over a slow fire. The addition of charcoal and sulfur enhance the molding properties of the white wax, as the author-practitioner explains: “This wax is very soft & amiable & pliant as copper, and it is so strong because of the sulfur, which renders it meltable much before the other,” while “the charcoal gives color to the wax, which would otherwise be transparent & the features would not be seen as well.” Apparently as an afterthought, the author-practitioner noted that charcoal not only gives color, but also body to this wax mixture for molding round figures that are burnt in the mold. The addition “and body” (et corps) in superscript is puzzling. How can additives “give body”?

Rozemarijn Landsman and Jonah Rowen (2014), and Charles Kang (2016) explored in several trials the influence of sulfur on wax and the effects of varying amounts of the black colorants soot and charcoal.2 Their experiments confirmed the author-practitioner’s observation about the increased visibility of features when using black sulfured instead of white wax (Fig. 1). However, the addition of charcoal not only affected the visual properties of the wax. Kang’s experiments with substantial additions of finely pestled charcoal resulted in a viscous wax, more difficult to pour into a mold. And black charcoal powder seemingly affected the casting properties of wax in more ways: Kang’s mixture yielded casts with less shrinkage after cooling. Moreover, black sulfured waxes were much harder to carve after cooling and setting than the control samples, made from beeswax to which no “color and body” had been added.3 Reading through the students’ accounts of their reconstruction experiments, even more questions surfaced: What does it mean “to give body” to wax? Is corps, like color, related to visibility? Or does corps relate to the consistency of the mold material, as sulfur strengthened the soft and pliable wax, which was also observed by students reworking another recipe for casting sulfur (fol. 140v)?4 Evidently, the author-practitioner found it important to add the words “and body” (et corps) in his technical description, yet what could he mean when referring to the corps of materials in his sixteenth-century workshop? Searching for more instances in which the author-practitioner mentions corps, we discovered that the term appears with conspicuous frequency in descriptions of diverse artisanal processes throughout Ms. Fr. 640. In this essay we trace the term across the manuscript and examine how the author-practitioner used the concept of corps to convey and communicate artisanal knowledge—not only in casting recipes but also in recipes for color and varnish making. We reflect on the diverse ways in which corps relates to materialities and processes of artisanal practice, as described in Ms. Fr. 640. We furthermore show how systematic textual analysis (word search, tabular presentations of content), applied to the transcription as a whole, can help to map out the rich and complex semantic field of the meanings of corps in the context of the author-practitioner’s writing about craft practices.

Our comparative research suggests similarities between the author-practitioner’s conception of corps and that found in other earlier and roughly contemporary art technological sources, which also discuss the properties of artists’ materials in terms of corps, such as the more widely known fifteenth-century Strasbourg manuscript as well as the early modern miscellaneous collection of art technological notes Ms. Sloane 2052, compiled—mostly in French—by and for Sir Theodore de Mayerne (1573–1655) and often referred to as the “Mayerne Manuscript.”5 Our observations suggest that the author-practitioner and De Mayerne partook in a more widely-used corps terminology to describe artisanal materials and processes. Theodore de Mayerne’s recipe collection, of which the first print editions and translations appeared in the nineteenth century, has a much longer and richer reception history than Ms. Fr. 640.6 This allows us to discuss how other editors of modern editions have dealt with the translation and interpretation of corps in the context of artisanal practices, in particular historical recipes for colormaking, and preparations of paints, pigments, oils, and varnishes. This essay thus aims to contribute to the translation and interpretation of a term that invokes a surprisingly complex field of meanings. 

For our conceptual analyses we combine insights from the history of art, history of science, and conservation studies with related approaches in language studies and cultural anthropology. We draw, for example, on recent research in comparative color anthropology and linguistics, investigating “strategies that speakers have available to manage phenomena or domains of experience that are inexpressible or difficult to express in their language.”7 This allows us to explore constructively how the author-practitioner deals with “ineffability” in his writings and how he works with practice-based conceptualizations to navigate domains of artisanal making that are or appear to be indescribable. We therefore propose in this essay to discuss the author-practitioner’s use of corps as a linguistic strategy with which he manages domains of experience in craft practice that are difficult to express in writing. Discussing corps terminology as a linguistic strategy for navigating ineffable domains allows us to move beyond the much-cited tropes in the historiography of early modern craft writing that tend to remain focussed on the inexpressibility of putting artisanal know-how into words. Consequently, instead of trying to provide a condensed and static definition of what corps means in context of the artisanal recipes we examined, we focus on how the author-practitioner made efficient use of the polysemic concept of corps to point to important perceptual aspects and sensory cues in casting, modelling, and paint, color, and varnish making.

We show that his strategy is grounded in the expertise of a skilled practitioner, with a keen interest in experimental exploration of artisanal materials and techniques. We argue that performative and material engagements with the recipe texts by reconstruction experiments are a vital complement to qualitative and quantitative textual analysis in various ways. Many of the historical concepts that we encounter in recipe texts may appear self-evident to a modern reader, especially those that can be easily translated into modern nomenclature, as in the case of corps that is literally translatable into English as “body.” However, as we try to show here, technical historical concepts can reveal unexpected semantic richness, conceptual conciseness, and observational acuity, as well as partly unfamiliar or surprising meanings and connotations, when they are worked through alongside a combination of textual reading methods and hands-on methods.8 

While we observe that the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 certainly did not set forth a systematic corpuscular theory of colors, substances, or material interactions as they were developed in natural historical and philosophical writings around the same time, we do believe that the author-practitioner’s writings are a testimony to a sophisticated and fine-tuned conceptual framework grounded in a practice-based corps terminology.

Exploring Corps in Ms. Fr. 640

The digital edition of Ms. Fr. 640 gives more than a hundred matches for the term corps.9 Some of these concern bodies or body parts of humans, animals, or things—for example, the many folios populated with corpses and living bodies of animals, including snakes (fol. 109v), lizards (fol. 165r), silkworms (fol. 53v), and rats and birds (fols. 106v and 48r). Things have bodies, too. For example, a distillation recipe calls for placing pre-heated coals around the body of a retort (fol. 40v), and among the numerous instructions related to arms and armor, we can find recipes for casting and tempering a corps de cuirasse (body of a cuirass, fols. 32r and 56r) and instructions for loading an arquebus with a hailshot that “makes for great piercing against a door, cuirass body, and others” (fol. 45r).10 Some instructions pertaining to the human body are concerned with improving its health, while others give us insight into the bodily work of an artisan.11 In addition, some sixty matches are found in recipes in which corps relates to colors, casting sands, and varnishes. These recipes provide a vivid image of the role of corps in descriptions of materials and artisanal practices in Ms. Fr. 640, and they are the main focus of this essay. The author-practitioner uses corps to write about qualities, properties, or affordances of materials, discusses corps modifications, and also includes tests to assess the amount or kind of corps a material possesses. While his use of the term is not limited to a single category of recipes, the term occurs mostly in recipes for painting, varnish making, and instructions for sand casting.12 As evident from the lists in the tables below, a rich collection of materials can be described with corps, and corps properties are broad.

The author-practitioner judges the suitability of materials or varieties of materials for specific purposes. Practitioners choose the material with the optimal degree of corps for their specific needs and applications. For instance, the choice between two types of lead white—ceruse and blanc de plomb—depends on the corps best suited to the work at hand, for example, in the case of small-scale paintings (fol. 61r):

Lead white

Without this, you cannot work on a small scale, for which one needs to soften with great care. But you cannot do it with ceruse because it does not have enough body [corps].

In another example, a recipe for a starch size for gilding on parchment, attention is given to preparing a ground layer with “no body” (fol. 10v):

Ground layer for gold leaf on parchment or paper

Make very clear starch glue & which does not show itself to have body. And make thereof six or five layers, & on the last, once it is half dried, apply your gold.

On the basis of the above text, one might be tempted to think of corps in terms of core features of materials or might assume that the concept connotes intrinsic affordances of specific materials. Yet other descriptions in Ms. Fr. 640 strongly suggest that what constitutes the corps of a material is not necessarily something fixed but rather that it connotes processes of transformation, enhancement, or material manipulation. What makes up the corps of a material can be adapted or changed by certain processes and treatments. Several recipes describe methods to actively modify the corps, such as a recipe for a quick-drying varnish for which the ingredients have to be processed until the mixture has gained the right amount of corps (fol. 60v):

Varnish dry in one hour

Take white turpentine oil & turpentine & mastic, pulverized & passed delicately through a sieve, & boil together, stirring continuously with a stick until it is dry. And put in two liards’ worth of good eau-de-vie. And if you extract the tear of mastic, it will be whiter & clearer. There is no need to put in turpentine, but only its white turpentine oil & mastic pulverized at your discretion, until it has enough body [corps]. +

The author-practitioner added a “footnote” in the left margin, explaining a technique or method for assessing if a material has enough corps:

+ which one knows when, being placed on a knife in the wind, it does not run. This one is excellent for panels and is dry within an hour and does not stick like the turpentine one.13

To evaluate or assess the corps of a material and estimate whether it has enough or too little body appears to be a matter of constant concern, not only for the compiler of Ms. Fr. 640 but also for painters, as documented by Sir Theodore de Mayerne in Ms. Sloane 2052.14 We come across a similar description in De Mayerne’s hand on fol. 85v. This recipe provides instructions for a glazing lake that should be processed until its corps has the consistency of butter: “lake for glazing should be mixed with a little oil and ground as thick as butter so that it can be cut; otherwise it has no body & is worthless.”15

Sometimes much corps is wished for, as in the lead white recipe above, while too much corps may also be undesirable. In some cases combining materials with and without corps works best, as the author-practitioner explains in detail in an entry discussing the merits of “Toulouse sand”16 (fol. 81r):

Before filling in the [casting] frame, you can put in the finest sand you have in a very fine linen, & dry pounce the medal and then the moistened sand that you put on top, which has more body, will grip it.

This example also illustrates how one and the same raw material can be worked by hand into different degrees of corps and then becomes suited to different purposes. Indeed, experiments with a batch of modern Toulouse sand from a region recommended by the author-practitioner (fol. 81r) demonstrate how one can manually work the corps of sand of Toulouse in different ways, thus attaining a “finest sand”—which is pounced onto the surface of the medal in its dry form (without any binding additives) through a very fine linen cloth—and a coarser, moistened sand “which has more body” that is applied on top of this first fine layer. The metal expert Tonny Beentjes taught us in the Making and Knowing lab to prepare a sandcasting mold in this manner, a practice that we could also observe in one of the last extant professional sandcasting metal workshops in New York, which we visited.17 In reconstruction experiments of this recipe, we learned how grinding and sieving modern “Toulouse sand” to different degrees of fineness yields molding sands with different properties. The casts made in this manner show that the procedure described by the author-practitioner worked very well (Fig. 2, Fig. 3, Fig. 4, Fig. 5, Fig. 6).18

Additional terms, used to describe varying degrees of corps, further confirm the high level of precision in the author-practitioner’s corps language: materials can have more (plus de) corps than others, they can take on (prend de) or maintain (retient) their corps throughout diverse manipulations, and there are numerous means and methods to give body (donner corps) to materials and to make body (faire corps). Furthermore, several techniques are described that allow practitioners to navigate the continuous scale of different levels of corps by playing with the amounts of ingredients or by modification of the materials themselves. The author-practitioner in one instance even notes that one can create “whatever body [corps] you want” (fol. 3r). The many collocations (terms that occur next to one another) we found when tracing the use of corps through Ms. Fr. 640 and Ms. Sloane 2052 suggest that the compilers of these manuscripts had a rich vocabulary at their disposal from which they could choose when describing the corps of materials.19 When we look at words that appear in relation to corps in casting recipes, for instance, we find the following list of words that define crucial know-how for casting in sand molds: grippe, liaison, compaction, pour despouiller, soubstenir, subtil, impalpable, and grosset (for better grip).20 Comparing the properties of corps in recipes for paint, color, and varnish making with those in recipes for sand casting confirms that connotations of the term may vary according to artistic medium the author-practitioner is discussing. When reviewing the varnish recipes that mention corps, it may seem that absence of body refers to clarity or transparency and (thin or aqueous) liquidity, while absence of corps in ingredients for casting recipes has different effects that may result in suboptimal release from the mold, less sharply detailed casts, or an insufficient bond of mold materials and thus a lack of structural integrity in the casting mold.

The conceptual importance of corps in conveying material literacy and artisanal expertise became evident when reworking artisanal recipes from Ms. Fr. 640 and Ms. Sloane 2052. To explore the meaning of corps in different art-technological contexts, in the next sections we draw on close readings of recipe texts and on reports of experiences and experimental reconstructions that were performed by students and researchers involved in the project, including ourselves. The following thick descriptions of the term corps in casting, color, and varnish recipes will elucidate how corps operates in the context of different artisanal practices. Interestingly, we observed that connotations of corps can be clarified through corporeal interactions with the recipes, such as those that took place in the reconstruction laboratories at Columbia University and at the Atelier building in Amsterdam, The Netherlands. This highlights the methodological value of such an approach. A closer look at the casting recipes in Ms. Fr. 640 provides a first insight into the complex web of meaning associated with the term.

Corps in Casting Recipes

The author-practitioner’s keen interest in mold materials for casting techniques, in particular his focus on the right sand for metal casting, is evident in the number of recipes devoted to the topic and echoed in the frequency with which it is discussed by researchers and students who have engaged with this manuscript.21 In many instances, the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 makes assertions about the core qualities of molding sands, and the corps of a sand is clearly one of them. In the margins of a recipe for artificial sand (fol. 89v), he notes: “all sand that releases well has body & gives a good hold.” Terms connoting compaction, bond, and cohesion are frequent in the casting recipes. The concept of corps also appears to relate closely to a testing method we came to refer to in the Making and Knowing Lab as the “squeeze test,” used when trying to assess a sand’s quality and readiness to be used for molding. In order to “mold very neatly” with a mixture containing sal ammoniac, the author-practitioner advises to “moisten it in a damp place for two or three days, & so that when you take fistfuls, it holds together, without, however, attaching itself & being pasted to your hand” (fol. 89v).

We also observed a close proximity between corps and two other key concepts used by the author-practitioner to describe the complex process of making mold materials for metal casting and for assessing the quality of mold materials: the concepts “fat” (gras) and “lean” (maigre).22 As explained in other contributions to this Edition, these terms had important explanatory functions in early modern understandings of materials, not only in the context of craft but also, for example, in medical practices.23 In several casting recipes, the author-practitioner describes the desired properties of a good sand in these terms. For instance, in a recipe for sand on fol. 69r–v, he advises not to choose a sand “that is in any way so lean & arid that it does not have bond, like the one from the sea or  desei  from sandy paths dried out by the sun.” Instead, he recommends a local sand: “The sand near my area is very excellent. But one ought to, in order to make an excellent work, take it fresh, which has been hardly put to work. For it dries out after having been cast several times & is lean & has no body [corps].”24 In a marginal note on the same folio, the author-practitioner provides a list of “fatty things” to compensate for a lack of corps in natural sand: “It is good for big work, but for small ones it is troublesome for releasing  Il  because it crumbles. It is good that it be a little alloyed with some fatty thing which has bond such as molded tripoli or  ab burned felt or sal ammoniac or tripoli & similar things.”25

Ameliorating the corps of casting sand is discussed elsewhere in another recipe calling for the “Sand from a mine in Thoulouse” (fol. 84r), which “is excellent in itself,” but can be enhanced for unlimited reuse: “[...] to make it withstand several castings, I mix it with pulverized sandever & moistened, which hardens it, & takes body & bond with it, & makes it withstand more than five as many castings as you wish.”

In another recipe for “Sand for copper medals” (fol. 83r), the author-practitioner gives advice on how to make artificial sands for molding from “hat felt burnt on a covered fire, dross of iron, & burnt bone, all of which ground very finely & pestled & watered with saltwater.” He notes that “burnt hat felt molds very neatly and releases very neatly.” This observation is confirmed in a marginal note, where we also learn that burnt bone “has scarcely any body unless it has iron dross.”

A master mold maker apparently needs not only the skill to recognize when a mold material has enough or the right amount of corps, but in order to produce high quality artefacts and to work efficiently, he also needs to learn how “to give body” to materials that are too lean and to make materials, that otherwise show insufficient resilience,“take body.”26 Masters of this art know how to manage and manipulate the corps of molding sands, e.g., by combining the ingredients with the right amount of corps for artificial sands, by processing sands in specific ways, and by mixing in an additive that, as the author-practitioner explains on fol. 84r, “hardens it, & takes body & bond with it, & makes it withstand” not just five casts, but “as many castings as you wish,” a comment that seems to suggest that corps may also relate to lasting qualities of mold materials.27

Giving Body with Magistra

Students reworked several casting recipes to explore the use, merits, and pitfalls of binding agents, dry and liquid, such as the saltwater, sal ammoniac, and iron dross mentioned above.28 Vanoccio Biringuccio (1480–1539), in his Pirotechnia (1540), refers to such agents as maestra, translated into English as “magistery,” a technical term that appears to be used in roughly contemporary recipes for tin casting and alchemical recipes to describe a (liquid) agent that ensures a desired bonding in naturally occurring sands or in mixtures that often consist of mostly dry ingredients. Magistery is key for making good molding sands. Biringuccio warns that without magistery, the (artificial) sand would not hold its place, retain the dryness of a powder, and therefore, it would not mold well in a casting flask. In addition, he points out that magistery renders the sand molds hard and strong when they are dry.29 Ms. Fr. 640 contains two recipes for Eau magistra on fol. 84r–v. They are discussed in detail in two student research essays.30 On the verso of the same folio, the author-practitioner mentions yet another liquid agent (quelque eau) to produce “Sand for lead, the most excellent of all, for high and low reliefs.”31 The author-practitioner intimates here how he came to know from his experiences that some liquor was necessary to give body and compactness to good sands, thus making them withstand repeated casts (fol. 85r):

This worked very well and I molded a medal of high relief very neatly, without any sticking, which a lot of good sands, such as felt, burnt bone & iron dross, had failed to do on the first try. I reheated it & my mold became hard like marble, and by this I knew that sands for molding high relief should be well moistened with some water, which gives them body & compactness, such as glair, gummed water, ea wine boiled with elm root, &c; and lightly oil the medal, it withstands as many castings as you wish, for it is as hard as glass.

The reports of the author-practitioner’s trials with liquid binding agents convey a close conceptual proximity between the terms corps and magistra. In achieving the right corps when preparing sands for molding, a delicate balance is sought between hardness and malleability. Intuitively, liquid agents seem most appropriate to moisten dry/lean materials such as sands “that have no corps,” “dried out sand,” “sand from the mines, overheated,” and “burnt bones.” However, as experienced makers know, and as we discovered by reconstruction in the Making and Knowing lab, dry hygroscopic materials can serve the same purpose, when placed in a humid environment. Experiments following a recipe for “Sand of burnt ox bones and rock salt” (fol. 89r) show that burnt and pulverized ox bone, which of itself is “very arid & lean” (fol. 84v), can be worked into an exceptional molding sand by folding it with rock salt into moistened paper wrapped in a moist cloth, preferably working in a humid environment like a cellar.32 The author-practitioner writes of its outstanding quality: “And I have not found anything else which releases more neatly than this one” (fol. 89r).

On fol. 87v we find instructions on how Toulouse sand can be processed to yield the right sands for a first and second layer of a mold. The author-practitioner describes how “others” attain, through extensive grinding and gently heating, a sand that becomes so very fine it is “like impalpable.” By adding a liquid agent, this very fine sand can be worked into a mold material that releases very neatly:

Sand of the mine of Thoulouse

[...] Others beat it well in a mortar, in small amounts at a time, & thus it is pressed together and rendered very fine. Then they reheat it moderately, only to dry it. Next, they grind it dry on porphyry. And thus it becomes as if impalpable, and not too dried out, it and it retains the body & the bond of the earth to which it is kin, and is better than when it is so burnt. Once moistened with glair of egg passed through a sponge, it releases very neatly M very neatly in low relief, but not for figures in high relief.

The author-practitioner’s own experiments with extensive grinding and sieving of mold materials and the addition of binding agents are described in several locations, amongst which fols. 84v and 85v.

The entry on fol. 85v, entitled “Experimented sands,” suggests that the author-practitioner preferred local natural sand, possibly related to the previously mentioned distinct qualities of Toulouse sand. He praised it for retaining body and bond (corps & liaison) when rendered impalpable—both properties required for a good mold. In fact, the author-practitioner comments frequently on the necessity of grinding mold material extremely finely, “until it is not felt between your fingers” (fol. 84v), a quality that also characterizes the fine grain of today’s industrially manufactured commercial molding sands.

During the lab seminars we experienced how much manual labor it actually takes to render our sands almost impalpable.33 The technical instructions in Ms. Fr. 640 thus testify also to the laborious embodied practices that are involved in manipulating the corps of sands. Based on such reworkings, we conjecture that the corps terminology in artisanal recipes hints perhaps also at the distinctive corporeal dimensions of the act of artisanal making and of manual transformation of materials, including the intensive and skilled bodily work needed to modify the corps of sands.34

Working through the recipes in the Making and Knowing lab taught us that the author-practitioner employed—and perhaps also devised—conceptualizations that belong to concise technical terminology, grounded in embodied practices and sensory experiences of skilled makers. The term impalpable, mentioned above, is a good example of such a conceptualization: the term denotes a particular softness, perceived by touch. In Biringuccio’s metalworking treatise, “impalpable” (impalpabile) attains the meaning of a concise technical term that defines a key quality of (artificial) mold materials.35 Reconstruction experiments showed that the term is likely used in a similar specific technical sense in Ms. Fr. 640. This informed the decision to consistently translate impalpable as a technical term (using the same word in English). Further below, we make a similar argument for translating corps throughout as “body,” yet this time we seek terminological consistency in the English translation in order to convey the conspicuous multi-layered meanings of body notions (i.e., corps terminology) in art technological descriptions.

Attention to corps is very clear in sand recipes for metal casting. The concept of corps enables the author-practitioner to describe desired properties of sand mold materials, how they can be achieved through sourcing of suitable materials (e.g., choosing sands from the right location),36 by processing (e.g., heating, beating, pestling, sieving), or enriching with particular means and methods (e.g., with dry or liquid additives), much of which is explained in unmatched detail in Ms. Fr. 640. The right corps will ensure good releasing of the mold (fol. 29r: “if it does not release well, mix in more flour until it has enough body”) and will provide strength, firmness, and durability so that the mold keeps its shape (fol. 70r: “enough body to withstand copper;” fol. 84r: “to make it withstand several castings”). Corps is also instrumental in achieving a precise and sharply detailed cast (fol. 70r: “molds very neatly”).37

Methods and agents for ensuring a better release of the cast, like vegetable oils, are also discussed in terms of having/not having corps. For instance, an entry for casting en noyau to make “wax images or lead medals and such like” gives instructions to “anoint your images of wax or of lead with olive oil, very thinly & very lightly, such that the oil makes no thickness nor body [corps] on the medal” (fol. 116r).38 A recipe on fol. 110v provides detailed instructions for preparing a release agent from wheat oil “that is appropriate to anoint” the finest and most delicate parts of an animal, like “the hair of a butterfly,” by heating the oil first. Similarly, the author-practitioner added a marginal note in which he recommends to use wheat oil (huile de froment) for molding “something delicate, like a pansy,” instead of rubbing the petals with butter as others do “to give it a little thickness, more than what is natural” (fol. 110v). He explains that wheat oil works better than butter “for it has no body, & does not obstruct the small lineaments as much, and makes the flower firm” (Fig. 7).39

Note, however, that in the context of casting recipes for “Molding a fly” (fol. 156v) and “Molding a rat” (fol. 152r), the author-practitioner warns against this same wheat oil as an anointing agent, exactly because of its thickness and corps, adding in the margins that butter is better for anointing hairy animals, like rats, “because it is more amiable [amiable]” (fol. 152r). Apparently, preparing delicate things for molding demands not only acute attention to body parts of animals and flowers but also to the corps of release agents. Not only oils and fats but also well-chosen carbon-based blacks are recommended as release layers in casting (fol. 84v):

Charcoal for pouncing makes for a good release, but one finds that the one of willow makes it porous. The one of oak or beech does make it porous well without making it porous.

In a recipe for en noyau casting (fol. 158v), the author-practitioner also attributes the suitability of the smoke of a candle as a release layer to its lack of corps:

Candle smoke

It makes it come out neater, & even if it is a little thick, it does not hold to any place because it has no body [corps]. I have thus fumigated my noyau molds for tin & lead, and have cast in the cold mold, well dried beforehand, and it came out very neat.

We have seen in other recipes that corps is often associated with thickness, implying that a lack of corps may relate to thinness. When discussing the quality of release agents that are applied on top of the surface patterns of casting models, it seems perhaps most likely that a release agent with no corps connotes a material that can be applied as a very thin separation layer. However, the above recipe makes plain that lack of corps is not synonymous with thinness: the author-practitioner observes here that even if the smoke of a candle is “a little thick,” it still works very well as a release agent “because it has no body.” The recipe subsequently makes clear that candle smoke makes for such a good release agent, because “it does not hold to any place” (fol. 158v). A lack of adhesion certainly makes practical sense, as a release layer is applied to ensure that the mold material separates easily from the cast medal. So, while smoke black might have some thickness, a characteristic that could elsewhere classify it as a material in possession of some corps, it is recommended here because it has “no body.” This shows that in the context of casting, at least in the particular context of release layers, no corps may refer to low adhesive properties. This delicate shift of meaning points towards a certain polysemic quality of the term; corps is fluid and takes on different meanings depending on context and purpose.

Polysemy in corps language can also be observed in wax molding recipes. In a marginal note on fol. 153r, the author-practitioner advises to mix wax with black colorants, like the smoke of a lamp, that have “hardly any body, in order that you see the imprint better” when carving a pattern into your wax model, a remark that makes clear that carbon-based blacks can occur in casting recipes as both release agents and colorants: nothing sticks to candle smoke, and the very fine black lamp smoke gives a dark color to molding wax.

Indeed, as we have already seen at the beginning of this essay, the rendering of delicate features in wax is an issue demanding acute attention to corps. Students also explored the author-practitioner’s recommendation for adding another black—charcoal (charbon)—to the wax for making a model for “casting in wax an animal that one has not got” (fol. 139v). In this recipe the author-practitioner explains that tinted waxes are better suited for wax modelling because the colorant reduces the wax’s transparency:40

Take some white wax which is more appropriate for this work than anything else, because it is firmer & does not leave as much filth, as much as you need to mold the animal that you propose, & no more, and half as much charcoal, pestled & finely passed through a linen or a sleeve. The charcoal gives color and body [corps] to the wax, which would otherwise be transparent & the features would not be seen as well.

Only upon close examination of the folio did it become apparent that the addition “and body” (et corps) had likely been made later, suggesting—as mentioned above—that the author-practitioner realized only after writing, maybe in practice, that charcoal black has more affordances besides color, and that working fine charcoal powder into the white wax might enhance or enrich the molding wax in more ways than one (Fig. 8). Perhaps the pestled and finely sieved charcoal also helped—like the addition of sulfur—to give firmness to the wax, which by itself tends quickly to become (too) soft and pliable when worked by hand.41 The author-practitioner affirms these observations in a marginal note of another recipe that calls for black wax mixed with sulfur: “transparent wax is not good, but rather the one which has body [corps], to make an imprint well” (fol. 169v).

These examples show how the author-practitioner—as writer and as maker—expertly navigates various corps variations and connotations, using the flexibility offered by this term to voice and fine-tune intricate details of practice, details that embody physical and workable aspects as well as visual or perceptual ones. The above discussion reveals the rich semantic field related to corps in casting recipes.42 However, to further our understanding of corps connotations in descriptions of artists’ materials, we need to investigate the author-practitioner’s use of this expression within the larger continuum of art technological writings in Ms. Fr. 640—a continuum that stretches from casting to molding as well as to painting, color, and varnish making. Carbon-based blacks, such as the smoke of candles and lamps, soot or chimney smoke, and finely ground charcoal, serve well to illustrate how corps terminology stretches across the disciplinary distinctions of casting and painting, which are separated in art history and conservation studies today.

Corps in Early Modern Color Discourse

Further comparison between the fine-tuned corps conceptualizations that we encounter in the writings of the author-practitioner in Ms. Fr. 640 and De Mayerne’s descriptions in Ms. Sloane 2052 (ca. 1620–1655) support the idea that the author-practitioner was not alone in using “body talk” in color recipes in early modern Europe.43 De Mayerne’s references to corps, quoted above, are not the only ones in this recipe compilation, which contains many references to the bodies of colors and varnishes. Most prominently, perhaps, are those on fol. 18r, where De Mayerne provides a list of “Colors that have no body or very little body.” His list includes smoke black, which is here described as having body, “but not much”:

Colors that have no, or very little, body. The Lake has none of it. The verdigris has none of it. The one and the other serve for glazing. The Scudegrun has very little of it. The white from glass tin [d’Estain de glace]44 has very little of it. The smalt has none of it. Black from smoke, which one must burn, on a stove, or in a crucible before it is ground, has [of it], but not much. Ivory black or bones of sheeps’ trotters is much better has less body [...]. The Indigo has of [it] but not much.45

In an entry on “Ordinary colors for oil” (Couleurs ordinaires pour huile), De Mayerne documents in detail the production and application of ivory and bone black, favored by painters for having “less body” than smoke black:

The strongest black is made with ivory, burnt in a well-closed iron vessel, with Lutum & salt. The black has no body, & is to be placed over another black of lamp as in glazing, & then it is black in extremity.46

Ivory black is used here as a glaze, implying that the pigment retains a certain translucency in oil. Applied as a second layer over a black first layer, the glaze deepens the black tonality. The suitability of colors with little or no corps for glazing is also discussed by the author-practitioner in Ms. Fr. 640 in a recipe entitled “Glazing” (fol. 65v). He adds advice on how to render colors with more body suitable for glazing by adding cristallin—a material described as having no corps:

Glazing

One commonly glazes with colors that do not have body, such as lake & verdigris. However, to use other colors, one mixes in a quantity of calcined & ground cristallin, which also has no body & makes lightens the density of the others.

In both the author-practitioner’s and De Mayerne’s recipes, the lack of corps appears closely associated with clarity and transparency. Note, for example, the correction and the use of the Middle French term esclarcist, which is defined in the Dictionnaire du Moyen Français (1330–1500) (DMF) as “giving light to” (donner de la lumière à), or “illuminating” (illuminer).47 The term is further associated with making “clear” and “brilliant” (rendre clair, brilliant), thus not only carrying connotations of transparency and translucency but also of luminosity and shimmer. The “lightening” effect of calcined and crushed cristallin, due to its lack of body, is described by the author-practitioner as a modifier for the “thickness” or heaviness (espesseur) of other colors.

Similarly, De Mayerne’s recipe collection contains a description of a painting technique for rendering the appearance and texture of “transparent linen,” in which “no body” (nul corps) is closely associated with transparency:

Crêpe or transparent linen. A crêpe is made by trailing one’s white so dry that it does not leave any body to cover all one will see through & at the same time, one dashes off only the traces and soft folds and leaving the rest void.48

Turning back again to Ms. Fr. 640, we encounter the phrasing “clear as water” (cler comme eau) on fol. 58v in a recipe where it is related to an oil that “has no body” (nha poinct de corps) and in a recipe for “scratching satin” (fol. 42v) that describes a method for pouncing a pattern onto satin fabric using flour or chalk and then tracing it with a special sizing “which will immediately make it [i.e., the pattern] clear up like water, & without becoming thick [sans faire corps], it will keep the trace.”49 This phrase was translated as “without becoming thick, it will keep the trace.” However, despite the close conceptual proximity between “thickness” and corps in color and casting recipes, the recurring contrast between clarity and corps in Ms. Fr. 640 becomes lost when substituting “becoming thick” for the polysemic concept corps.

Notably, in De Mayerne’s compilation we can find more examples where corps terminology is used to describe both the different colors for painting and the structural qualities of colorants. For example, in a recipe for damasquinerie, a decorative technique in which a pattern is scratched into a paint layer applied over gold or silver leaf, he added a note, describing how to make a paint that can withstand the scratching of a (metal) point without flaking off. The description testifies to De Mayerne’s own first hand observations of this artisanal technique:

NB I have seen it done. When you grind your color, when it has no or little corps like lake you have to mix in a little chalk to give it corps, to sustain the cut of the point, otherwise it flakes.50

While corps has connotations of thickness and fattiness in both casting and color recipes, associations with transparency appear to be idiosyncratic for color and painting recipes. This is manifest in a note by the author-practitioner next to an entry entitled “Fatty colors” (Couleurs grasses) on fol. 65r. His observations illustrate a close association of a color’s “thickness” (espesse) and its “fattiness” (grasse) with having corps, while “clarity” (claires) seems to denote a lack of corps:

All fatty colors, such as ceruse and minium, massicot, ocher, lead white, are good for making gold color. Every color or thing that becomes dense [qui se rend epaisse] when water is put in during the grinding has body [corps]. But those which do not have it, such as pestled glass, lake, &c, become clear.51

Colors, lacking body, that are worked with calcined and crushed glass, are juxtaposed here to colors that become thick (se rend espesse). The latter is a derivative from the French espesseur, defined in the DMF as relating to a solid body (corps solide).52 Elsewhere, De Mayerne implies a relation between the body of colors (corps de couleur), the binder a pigment is mixed with, and the chromatic appearance of the paint, emphasizing like the author-practitioner that pigments without corps in oil are very suitable for glazing.53

Corps de vernis: Translation Issues

The French term couleur, as it appears in De Mayerne’s above-mentioned listing of “colors that have no, or very little, body,” has been translated as “paint” (rather than “color”) in Donald C. Fels’s English edition of the “Mayerne Manuscript,” a choice for emphasis on the material instead of the coloring aspect of the term. Such editorial choices call for more in-depth research into the semantics of colors/paints/bodies of colors (corps de couleur) in early modern color discourses.54 Disambiguating the rich discourse on corps in varnish making recipes within Ms. Sloane 2052 is also challenging. Take, for example, the entry with the heading Discours sur les vernix, where De Mayerne uses the term corps to describe the consistency of a varnish:

Oils suitable for making varnish are those of walnut, and of linseed; these alone, rendered dessicative with litharge or (which is better) with white calcined copperas, dry on the work, & can withstand any water whatever. If the dissolution of resins is done with these oils, the varnishes will be more beautiful & have more corps.55

The meaning of corps in this recipe remains elusive without knowledge of the other oils that walnut oil or linseed oil are compared to. Corps may relate to properties like fatness or consistency, to layer thickness, or to the gloss of a varnish. And possibly the rich varnishes that were made with nut oil had a bit of each: fattiness, thickness, and glossiness. Ernst Berger chose, in the first comprehensive print edition and German translation of De Mayerne’s manuscript, published in 1901, not for a literal translation of corps into the German Körper, but used instead an explanatory description and interpreted the term as konsistenter, translated verbatim as “more consistent” in the more recently published first English translation by Fels.56 Berger’s choice is as puzzling as it seems unnecessary. Why did both editors choose to abandon the rich connotations of corps for a more restricted—and debatable—alternative term? In another recipe, describing the preparation of materials for “the true a varnish for lutes and viols” on fol. 43v, De Mayerne describes how carabé resin transforms when heated on a fire: “Il se fond en un corps obscure qui semble la colophone,” translated by Berger as “Er schmilzt zu einem schwarzen Körper, der wie Kolophonium aussieht” (“It melts into a black body that looks like colophony”).57 This is another example of a semantic ambiguity becoming lost in translation, as the possible multivalent meanings of corps in combination with the term obscure are reduced to the visual appearance of a black color, while the corps of carabé resin might resemble the properties of colophony in more ways. Fels takes us even a further step away from corps in his English edition, by translating Berger’s “schwarzen Körper” as “black mass.”58 Why such substitutions were considered necessary is unclear. Clearly, a mass and a corps are terms with different connotations, mass putting emphasis on material and bulk, while the multi-layered nature of corps appeals to a range of material as well as immaterial aspects, both visual and perceptual.

There are other instances, in particular in the section with varnish recipes, where Berger and Fels felt tempted to (over)interpret corps in translation. The dated recipe for an amber varnish on fol. 48v, “vernix d’Ambre. 8 Febr. 1631,” testifies to De Mayerne’s difficulties with dissolving carabé resin in spike oil and turpentine oil. The addition of “a little very clear distilled Carabé oil” provided a solution for the issue, as it “instantly penetrated the corps.”59 Again, Berger chose the word Masse (“mass” in English), translated by Fels in the English edition as “substance.” In yet another instance, Berger translated corps as Körperhaftigkeit, subsequently translated by Fels into English as “substantial body” in a recipe for yellow which appears on folio 122v in the “Mayerne Manuscript.”60 Here, De Mayerne remarks upon the nature of a yellow plant-based pigment, known as schitgeel or pinke, noting that it could also “pass for an earth pigment, because its corps is of chalk.”61

Interestingly, there existed a close proximity in meaning between the terms “substance” and corps in other pre-modern art technological writings. This is apparent in a compelling description of the production of artists’ pigment from an early fifteenth-century copy of Alcherius’s short Latin treatise on colors, dated ca. 1398–1411.62 Alcherius gives two recipes for making a green color for painting, one “with body and not corrosive” (cum corpore et non corrosivum), the other a corrosive one “without substance or body” (absque substantia seu corpori).63 The detailed description of the latter shows that lack of body is here, too, closely associated with thinness, transparency, and qualities associated with pigments suitable for glazing. Though a green (pigment) “without body” is further defined as having no substance, the author clearly refrained from simply conflating the meanings of substantia and corpus into one, suggesting that, while synonymic, they are not congruent:

300. To make corrosive green, without substance or body.

To make a green transparent in its nature, and without body, that is, having no substance, such, for example, as is the color of saffron, i.e. of crocus, which does not cover up other colors so as to conceal them, on account of its thinness, transparency, and rarity, owing to which other colors appear through it, wherefore this color as well as the said green color is overpowered, and shows little or not at all, nor can it be much seen over other colors.64

Luckily, our comparative survey of De Mayerne’s original French text with Berger’s German and Fels’s English translations showed that divergent translations of corps terminology are exceptions. In most instances both editors opted for a literal translation of corps as Körper or “body.” Indeed, this policy seems most true to the original term and its polysemic qualities. Moreover, sticking to body terminology invokes the living, changing nature of materials and the corporeal dimensions of working (with) the corps of materials in an artisanal context.

Conclusion: Corps as a Linguistic Strategy to Navigate “Ineffable” Domains

Considering the rich and varied connotations discussed above and the semantic spectrum the term corps covers, conceptualizing a material’s corps appears to have mattered to the writer and maker of the exceptionally detailed document of artisanal know-how that is Ms. Fr. 640. Through attentive reading of the handwritten folios and by executing experimental reconstructions of casting, color, and varnish recipes, we could observe how efficiently the author-practitioner worked with corps in practice and on paper. As a linguistic tool or strategy, corps allowed him to express specific phenomena that were key to mastering artisanal procedures otherwise difficult to express in words. After working ourselves through the corps recipes, we come to the conclusion that the author-practitioner’s use of this concept allowed him to successfully navigate “ineffable” domains of artisanal practice. His conceptualization and use of corps can best be understood when we describe the concept in its different contexts of use and define it dynamically as a linguistic strategy, rather than trying to provide a concise definition of the meaning of corps in early modern artisanal discourse. With this focus on semantic richness and the creative strategies speakers (and writers, in our case) use to express themselves, we followed the approach of linguistic anthropologists and argued against a tendency in scholarship on craftsmanship to emphasize only the shortcomings and impossibilities of spoken and written language to voice the idiosyncratic experiential and material phenomena of craft practices.

The lively and detailed descriptions of the sensory experiences of the author-practitioner furthermore demonstrate how his use of the term corps was grounded in the skilled vision and trained touch of an experienced practitioner. Building on Pamela Smith’s work, our discussion of corps reveals what we could tentatively call an artisanal corpuscular epistemology, i.e., an emergent conceptualization of corps as a technical term to describe manual modifications and transformative processes of materials through an intimately entangled practice of making, knowing, and writing. 

Our comparison between Ms. Fr. 640 and Ms. Sloane 2052 strongly suggests that corps terminology in art technological writings belonged to a more widely shared corpuscular discourse in premodern arts and crafts. Perhaps further research will reveal etymological connections between early modern corps language and current technical terminology in color writing, like the German “Farbkörper,” employed by Berger and still in use today. De Mayerne’s recipe trials and observations, though dated slightly later, provide an interesting comparison on account of the close bodily proximity of writer and workshop. We can discern in De Mayerne’s notes on color and varnish making a similar broad semantic spectrum for corps with a conspicuous variety and fluidity of meaning, indicating material characteristics related to consistency and transparency. Finally, both the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 and De Mayerne testify in their writings to their roles as close observers of contemporary artisanal practices and as hands-on experimenters.

In addition to evidence that the polysemic qualities were recognized and used in art technological sources, a quick excursion to contemporary dictionaries leads to a puzzling observation. Although bilingual dictionaries such as those by Cotgrave (1611) and d’Arsy (1633) include the term corps, they do not necessarily show awareness of the uses of the term as we described them above. D’Arsy’s French–Dutch dictionary translates corps only as a physical body, either human or natural (e.g., “le corps d’un arbre”), and as an assembly of people (“compagnie de gens”); it also includes the term in the context of “body and soul” (corps & coeur).65 Cotgrave gives as the primary meaning “a bodie, a corps,” but follows with terms more closely related to material quality: “the whole bulk, or substance of; also, a volume ... also, (in cloth, or stuffe) substance, tacke, stuffinesse.”66 No description of corps as a quality of a material is included. Godefroy’s Dictionnaire de l’ancienne langue française et de tous ses dialects du IXe au XVe siècle includes several terms that have corp* as their basis (e.g., corpuleux – en parlant de chose, volumineux, corpora – qui a un corps), but also only documents the use of the term corps as le corps d’un object (“the body of an object”) in French language from this period.67 A rare early reference to the modification of corps is found only in the 1606 French–Latin dictionary Trésor de la langue françois tant ancienne que modern, which includes prendre du corps in the corps entry.68 It is only in more recent, late eighteenth- and nineteenth-century dictionaries that descriptions are found related to a definition of corps as a property, quality, or affordance of material.69 And to our surprise, none of the above dictionaries relate corps to opacity or lack thereof, while this essay has demonstrated that this connotation was common knowledge to both the author-practitioner and contemporary practitioners. It is surprising that the wide-ranging and interesting web of meaning for corps which emerges from the artisanal sources discussed in this essay is not reflected in contemporary dictionaries, which emphasize the structural qualities of corps and seem to neglect its implications for transparency. Whether this points to a more general discrepancy between early modern artisanal vocabulary and the codification of language in contemporary dictionaries is still an open question—a topic for further research.70
