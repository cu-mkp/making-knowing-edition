Abstract

The discussion of painting in Ms. Fr. 640 forms a relatively small proportion of its content, but one that is significant. In most sixteenth-century compilations and “secrets” books, discussion of painting is largely limited to recipes for materials, often copied from elsewhere. Some, derived from earlier sources for manuscript illumination, describe how to paint particular details such as faces and hair of young people, or animals and birds. Ms. Fr. 640’s author-practitioner describes aspects of Flemish painting practice fairly accurately, perhaps from information received or from observation, and also mentions details of Italian painting practice, relating to painting faces. His account of materials treats their behavior in oil paint, rather than the list and description of individual pigments commonly found in other texts. This emphasis on practical painting in oil for a variety of purposes, including painting decorative metalwork and glass, is unusual in sources of this date, not found in such detail until the seventeenth century. The use of cristallin glass as a drier is described and, more interestingly, its additional ability to give translucency to opaque paint. The attention paid to the blue glass pigment smalt and its properties is unusual: it indicates some difficulties associated with its use, not discussed in other contemporary sources. Other blues, also green, red, yellow, white and black pigments are mentioned, but the author-practitioner’s particular interest is in translucency, which may relate to his interest in cast metalwork.

The late sixteenth-century manuscript in the Bibliothèque nationale de France, catalogued as Ms. Fr. 640, contains practical notes and comments on a range of subjects, largely concerned with crafts. At first sight, painting materials and the methods for their application appear to occupy a rather small proportion of the content as a whole, a little over fourteen percent; three times as much is devoted to casting. In fact, the instructions and comments relating to painting, broadly speaking, form the second largest category in the compilation, followed by processes relating to metalworking and then varnish making, technologically related to painting, at just under five percent. Paint and varnish had important functions: the creation of images and all kinds of decorative projects, from the painting of scenery and guild banners to painting on glass and the ornamentation of all kinds of objects, from frames and hand mirrors to the cold painting of cast metal items, immediately come to mind. Both paint and varnish could also provide some protection for surfaces or objects to which they were applied and this was an important role. This essay compares the treatment of painting and painting materials by the anonymous “author-practitioner” of Ms. Fr. 640 with that in contemporary sources and then discusses some of the points raised in the manuscript itself. It also places these observations in the context of what is known about actual practice from direct examination of painted works of art from the relevant period.

Paint consists of a colored solid powder, the pigment, mixed thoroughly with a liquid binding material or medium in which it is insoluble and which coats the particles, binding them together to create a coherent film that adheres to the surface being painted. The pigments used during the sixteenth century included ground natural minerals, such as the blue copper carbonate mineral azurite, and manufactured pigments, such as verdigris and lead white. The manufactured pigments also included lake pigments made from natural dyes, obtained from plants and certain insects, precipitated onto suitable substrates such as hydrated alumina, chalk, or even lead white. The binding media used included those that were essentially aqueous—mixed with water—such as gums, glue (made from animal skins and parchment scraps, for example), and egg, as well as oils, at this date principally linseed and walnut oils. These oils were also ingredients in many varnishes.1 A varnish could itself act as a binding medium, or be an ingredient within an oil medium. Many of the effects achieved in painting are due to the physical and rheological (flow) properties of the binding medium used, as well as the interplay between the medium and the pigments used. However, it is important to understand that the paint is much the same, selecting from the same range of ingredients, whether it is being used for painting a portrait on a prepared wooden panel, or a decorative scene on glass, or the details on a cast metal object.

Almost all the individual painting materials—pigments, binding media, and varnish ingredients—mentioned in Ms. Fr. 640 are those found in other similar sources written or compiled at about the same date. One example is a manuscript compiled in the Kingdom of Naples around 1570 (so more or less contemporary with Ms. Fr. 640), and now in the Biblioteca Nazionale Marciana in Venice: Ms. It. III. 10, better known as the Marciana Manuscript from its partial publication in the nineteenth century by Mary Philadelphia Merrifield.2 In some respects the two manuscripts are comparable: instructions for methods of gilding and, interestingly, painting on glass (fols. 39v, 41v, 102v)3 are given about the same amount of attention in each and both contain varnish recipes, although the varnish recipes found in the Marciana Manuscript are more varied and more numerous. However, there is a marked difference between the two in the treatment of painting. The Marciana Manuscript contains a selection of recipes for the preparation or manufacture of common pigments, including lead white, vermilion, and copper salts used as blue pigments, typical of those found in other books of “secrets” produced in large numbers during the sixteenth century; these are not found in Ms. Fr. 640. On the other hand, little attention is devoted to painting methods in the Marciana Manuscript, whereas the French manuscript describes the behaviour of oil paint in practice, including some practical instructions, so that the right combination of oil and pigment can be chosen for the required purpose.

Ms. Fr. 640 (and, to a lesser extent, the Marciana manuscript) has a different approach to the discussion of painting and pigments to that found generally in most “Secrets” books, such as the Secreti of Alessio Piemontese, very probably a pseudonym for Girolamo Ruscelli, an author mentioned in the list of books and authors in Ms. Fr. 640 on fol. 1r. The first edition in French of this very popular book was published in Antwerp in 1557;4 two editions were published in Lyon in 1558 with many following thereafter. Recipes from it were also copied by other authors. Much of the section on painting in Johannes Jacob Wecker’s Les secrets et merveilles de nature (Lyon, 1584)5 consists of recipes copied from Alessio Piemontese, including instructions for “Une couleur bleue Indienne parfaite – ou outremer”—that is, extracting lapis lazuli ultramarine from the stone—and several recipes for copper-containing blue pigments and organic red lake pigments prepared from bourre, the shearings from dyed cloth. These pigment recipes are typical of those appearing in this variety of “Secrets” book, with a wide-ranging content, during the fifteenth, sixteenth, and seventeenth centuries; however none are found in Ms. Fr. 640 and this is unusual. Equally, the details of handling properties of oil paint, mentioned by the author-practitioner, are not usually mentioned in other “secrets” books like those of Alessio Piemontese and Johannes Jacob Wecker.6 The compilers of “secrets” books tended to copy from other sources, however, including other books of “secrets,” whereas, to a large extent, the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 does not appear to have done this, at least as far as practical painting is concerned.

Ms. Fr. 640 discusses the properties of pigments and paint from a practical point of view, in some cases in a detail that is not found elsewhere at this date. One useful source to compare with it is the well-known manuscript compiled by Theodore de Mayerne between 1620 and 1646, “Pictoria, sculptoria et quae subalternarum artium,” MS Sloane 2052 (London, British Library). Although this manuscript is some forty years later, Mayerne gained practical information on pigments, oils, and varnishes from painters—often named—and there are some similarities in the information given.7

A substantial proportion of the comments on painting and pigments are grouped together (with some of the varnish recipes) on fols. 56–67 of Ms. Fr. 640, with a smaller group on fols. 10–11; the remainder are scattered through the manuscript. In fact this manuscript is fairly unusual in that much of the information on painting it contains gives the impression of having been acquired by observation of someone at work, or during conversation with someone trained as a painter. The receipt of information—a particular recipe, for example—from a named person is quite common in this variety of technical literature: an example from the Marciana Manuscript is the advice to use milk as a binding medium for blue pigments in fresco painting, received from Master Andrea de Salerno.8 In Ms. Fr. 640, the sources are unidentified. It is particularly interesting that some of the comments made on the properties or behavior of particular pigments are not found elsewhere, or not at this date.

Some of the more significant comments on painting methods refer to painting practice in Flanders and the southern Netherlands in general. The use of several layers of chalk mixed with a glue binding medium as the preparatory layer—the ground—for a panel was standard in northern Europe. The author-practitioner makes the comment that “Flemish painters have such panels made by the dozen” (fol. 60r), which would certainly have been the case in a sophisticated center such as Antwerp where panel-making was a recognized trade.9 The alternative suggestion for the first ground layer of ashes mixed with the chalk (fol. 57r), while not specific to Flemish painting, is notable as an earlier reference to a practice mentioned in Spanish treatises by Francesco Pacheco (Arte de la pintura, Seville 1649) and Antonio Palomino (El Museo pictórico y escala óptica, Madrid 1715–24). Analytical evidence of the use of ashes for this purpose—they are calcite-rich but have a morphology distinct from that of chalk—was first noted in works by Velasquez and other Spanish artists at the Prado, Madrid, and then subsequently confirmed through identification of specific calcite pseudomorphs at the Metropolitan Museum of Art, New York. It has been suggested that this is a specifically Spanish habit, but the reference in Ms. Fr. 640 suggests it might prove to be rather more widespread.10

“Work of the Flemish” (fols. 60v–61r), a description of painting procedure concentrating on the painting of flesh, appears to be a fairly accurate account of practice in the southern Netherlands. The description begins, “They do all their works in oil with the tip of the pinceau, in the manner of good illuminators, and grind their colors very finely…,” and attention is paid to the handling of the brush, describing the use of the point or the flat of the brush in the painting and highlighting of round objects (Fig. 1, Fig. 2, Fig. 3).11 In a passage not specifically linked with Flemish painting practice, the author-practitioner comments that at least three layers of flesh color are needed when painting flesh in oil, beginning by placing the “black and umber where it is appropriate” (fol. 58r); in other words, by indicating the areas of flesh that are to be in shadow with a dark-colored paint, subsequently applying the flesh color and lead white highlights. This is rather similar to the description of painting faces in destrempe—distemper or glue size—on canvas, wetting the reverse of the canvas with a sponge to allow the paint to flow, which suggests that the canvas was unprimed (fol. 93v).12 Areas of shadow are applied using the brown pigment bistre, followed by the layers of flesh-colored paint. Paintings on canvas in an animal glue binding medium were produced and traded in very large numbers throughout the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, although their fragility has meant that relatively few have survived. In those cases where scientific examination has been possible, it appears that the canvas was primed with animal skin glue and, in some cases, a thin ground layer.13 Particular attention is paid to the painting of shadows on flesh, in tempera and oil, with the use of different pigments being suggested for men and women. For men this includes a black “which tends towards yellow, to make beautiful shadows in oil, for the shadow, especially that of men, is yellowish”: geiete (jet in some form) is suggested, mixed with yellow ocher and white (fol. 65r). For women, blacks “which make a greenish black”—un noir verdastre—are suitable (the author-practitioner leaves a space for a suitable black to be inserted later), mixed with with sap green and bistre; later aspalthe (asphaltum or similar), umber, and red lake are suggested (fols. 58r, 63r, 65r, 66r). Here the author-practitioner also refers briefly to Italian and German practice (fols. 59r, 63r) and makes the perceptive comment that shadows closer to the light need to be lighter and softer than those in darker areas: “The Italians commonly make three shadows, the first one, of light, very clear, the second one darker & the third one quite strong, then blend these three shadows together by hatching them from the darkest to the clearest”—le plus obscur jusques au plus cler (fol. 59r).14 Here translation of the word cler is difficult, relying on the context, but Edward Norgate’s explanation of “Chiar oscuro” is helpful in this respect. He explains that “it signifies nothing else but light and darke, or, if you will, clear and obscure, Sunshine and shadow.”15 The selection of different pigments for the flesh of men and women was common, so the concentration of the author-practitioner on this particular aspect of the work is not in itself unusual.16

An interesting comment, entitled “Double layers,” refers to applying more than one layer of paint: “The Flemish & those who paint panels by the dozen only make one layer & finish at the first go. But the colors soon die. And when they are layered twice, they do not die. One needs to layer them lightly & not very thick, to soften them well” (fol. 66r).17 In Den Grondt der Edel vry Schilder-const, the Introduction to Het Schilder-Boeck published in 1604, Karel van Mander used the phrase ten eersten opdoen to describe painting the picture by the application of a single layer of paint over a thin translucent priming over the drawing, mixing the required shades on the palette, rather than first applying an underlayer (that is, employing two or even more layers of paint). The result was a paint that was not heavily applied, but thin and sparingly laid on, “glowing and clean,” in Van Mander’s words:

“Als dit nu droogh was, saghen sy hun dinghen

Schier daer half gheschildert voor ooghen claerlijck,

Waer op sy alles net aenlegghen ginghen,

En ten eersten op doen, met sonderlinghen

Arbeydt en vlijt, en de verwe niet swaerlijck

Daer op verladende, maer dun en spaerlijck,

Seer edelijck gheleyt, gloeyend’ en reyntgens

Met wit hayrkens aerdich ghetrocken cleyntgens.”18

This was a skillful, rapid, and economical method of painting that developed in northern Europe after the first decade of the sixteenth century, perhaps typified by the work of Cornelis Engebrechtsz. (c.1462–1527)19 and seen later in the work of Pieter Bruegel the Elder (active 1550–51, died 1569) (Fig. 4), Marten van Heemskerck (1498–1574), Joachim Beuckelaer (probably about 1535, died 1575) and many others. The technique was also ideally suited for mass production of panels of a popular subject which could be sold cheaply. As the paint film aged, however, it became more translucent so that drawing or changes in the composition, pentimenti, could be seen—not a desirable feature (Fig. 5).20 This may lie beneath the author-practitioner’s more negative reference to this manner of painting and use of the word “die” (mortes, meurent) to describe the changes in the appearance of the work. In fact, the word “die” is used fairly frequently in sixteenth- and seventeenth-century sources discussing painting to indicate a variety of changes that could take place in the paint film, including fading, darkening, sinking of the pigment within the film so that the oil formed a skin on the surface, or sinking of the oil itself, or increasing translucency, the most likely in this case. The effects of fading, increasing translucency, or, indeed, sinking, would become apparent quite quickly in such thinly painted works. The comment on fol. 66r suggests that the author-practitioner may have had some contact with someone who perceived the disadvantages of this method of painting.

Painting Materials and Methods

The painting materials and techniques described by the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 are those used in one branch of painting or another during the sixteenth century, in both oil and aqueous binding media. Most of the comments are concerned with oil painting.

On the whole, walnut oil was more commonly used in southern Europe, including southern France, while linseed oil was used further north, although this did depend to some extent on the materials being used, the work being carried out, and availability. The author-practitioner makes the interesting comment, rarely mentioned in other sources at this date, that oils dry more quickly in the south, also commenting that there was no better oil than walnut, saying that “it does not dry out too soon nor too late” and is also light in color (fols. 60r, 63r), although no other drying oil is mentioned for comparison. It is, however, true that walnut oil is initially lighter in color than linseed oil and dries slightly more slowly. Allowing the oil (walnut or linseed) to stand in the light would bleach and also thicken it (fol. 60r). In fact, it is quite common to find both of these oils used in different areas of the same painting, with linseed used more generally and walnut in colors where a more yellow color would be a disadvantage, such as whites, mauves, and blues.21

Both linseed and walnut are drying oils; in other words they gradually form a solid film as their constituents, polyunsaturated carboxylic acids, cross-link by the action of oxygen in the air. The oil can be modified, in fact thickened, prepolymerized, in chemical terms, to increase its drying ability or to change its rheological properties by heating, with or without the addition of metal salts—including pigments such as minium (lead tetroxide) or verdigris (copper acetate)—or other substances known to act as driers. Alternatively it can be allowed to stand, usually where it can receive sunlight, covered or uncovered; the thickening would be encouraged by the addition of ceruse (lead white) or other forms of lead white, lead filings, or calcined pulverized glass (fols. 60r, 64r).22 This is the huile gras, or fat oil, described by the author-practitioner as making the colors thick and difficult to work, although in practice thickened oils were commonly used in easel painting as, depending on how the prepolymerization was carried out, the properties the oil acquired enabled the artist to achieve particular effects. For fine or intricate work a more fluid oil might be preferable and at several points in the manuscript the comment is made that the oil should be fresh (so, pale in color) and as fluid as possible (fols. 57v, 64r).

Turpentine spirit, obtained by the distillation of pine resin, and oil of spike lavender could be used as diluents for the oil, for painting on glass and in varnish making, turpentine being mentioned rather more frequently as a varnish ingredient than for use in oil painting, for example on fols. 31r and 99v. The author-practitioner makes the useful observation that oil of spike lavender penetrates oil paint so well that “painters use it to clean their pinceaulx [brushes] when they have become hard, for it renders them soft & clean immediately, penetrating the dry color which encrusts them,” but its powerful solvent activity also meant that care was needed in its use to avoid paint cracking or flaking off (fols. 58r–v, 60v).

The pigments mentioned in the manuscript were widely used in Europe in the sixteenth century. The list includes:

  • Blue: azur in various forms, including cendres d’azur (most generally a synthetic copper carbonate blue, although it could also be a low grade of natural mineral azurite or ultramarine, or even other blues of a more dull color such as the iron phosphate pigment vivianite); azur d’esmail—smalt; florie, or flower of woad, the indigo pigment that collected at the top of the woad dyeing vat; Flanders blue, the same buried for a time in putrefied cow dung.23

  • Red and orange: vermilion; a variety of lakes, including roset (a pigment made using brazilwood or sappanwood dye); minium; dragon’s blood.

  • Green: verdegris (verdigris); verdet; vert de terre; verd d’asur; sap green (the juice of ripe buckthorn berries, a watercolor pigment).

  • Yellow: stil de grain yellow (a yellow lake); orpiment; massicot (lead-tin yellow); yellow ocher; turmeric.

  • Brown: umber and reddish and brown ochers (all iron oxide pigments); bistre (burnt wood containing much tarry material); aspalthe (asphaltum, a naturally occurring blackish-brown substance derived from crude petroleum—which could also be counted as a black, depending on the form available) and similar substances.

  • White: lead white, including a variety of the pigment known as ceruse; chalk.

  • Black: glazier’s black (although only for glass); various forms of charcoal; burnt ivory and other bones; burnt peach stones; lamp and smoke black; geiete (a form of jet); black of charbon de pierre.

No purple pigments are mentioned in the manuscript; indeed, none were available at this date,24 although some of the red lake pigments could be a distinctly purplish crimson (and on fol. 58r, the author-practitioner makes the point that such pigments gave cold flesh tones). Purples and violets were usually obtained by mixing blue with a red pigment, often red lake. In the sixteenth century, however, painters appeared to have developed an alternative method in addition: applying a translucent layer of paint containing a red lake pigment (such a layer is known as a glaze) over a layer containing a blue pigment, as described by the author-practitioner: “Painters make it beautifully by making the first layer of common azur, or better yet azur d’esmail, & next they glaze it with lake” (fol. 10r) (Fig. 6).25

Some of the pigments were rarely used in conventional easel painting, but had particular applications: dragon’s blood, for example, is described as being used over gold where its transparent, red color would be very effective for decorative purposes, but it was rarely used otherwise. Turmeric could be used similarly (fols. 29v, 57r, 165r).26

The author makes some interesting comments about lead white and ceruse. Lead white, basic lead carbonate, was the standard white pigment, known from antiquity and made by suspending plates of lead over vinegar in containers commonly buried in horse manure which maintained a constant (warm) temperature and also generated carbon dioxide as it fermented. The lead acetate formed initially was converted to the carbonate on exposure to the air or carbon dioxide. In most of Europe, including France, ceruse was the name given to a form of lead white with a proportion of chalk or some similar extender added;27 it was therefore slightly less opaque and slightly cheaper. This is borne out by the author’s comments that flesh colors made using ceruse would go yellow in five or six months, whereas lead white did not change (fol. 58r).28 Ceruse also had less body (was less opaque) than lead white (fols. 61r, 65v).29

In practice, the identification of the pigments is not always straightforward; this is the case with some of the blues and greens, for example. However, it is important to remember that the painter would be choosing to use a pigment for its color and for its other properties, including whether it felt gritty or in some other way unpleasant or unsuitable; chemical or mineralogical identity was not necessarily at the top of the list.

Pigments vary in their drying ability when mixed with oil. Compounds of lead and copper, such as lead white, massicot—at this date lead-tin yellow (a double oxide of lead and tin)30—and verdigris (copper acetate) all dried well and, as mentioned above, could be used in the treatment of oil to improve its drying. Lead white and lead-tin yellow are described as the most drying, although they need at least a couple of days (fol. 59v), a useful comment that is rarely found elsewhere.31 The author-practitioner makes the helpful suggestion (and another not commonly found elsewhere) that, if the reader wants to test whether or not an oil will dry, it should be mixed with lead white; if it “produces a crust in a day” (forms a skin), it is a drying oil (fol. 59v). On the other hand, some pigments, including vermilion, lakes, and black pigments, were poor driers, and additives were required to help them. Verdigris was commonly added to black paint to improve its drying and this is suggested in the manuscript (fol. 105r).32 For vermilion, the lake pigments, and the organic brownish-black substances indicated by aspalthe, the addition of powdered cristallin glass is suggested; as far as the lakes, which are translucent pigments, are concerned this would have the additional advantage of not affecting the hue and of remaining transparent once ground with the paint (fols. 57r, 58r, 64r).

Cristallin Glass as an Additive for Oil Paint

Cristallin is mentioned numerous times as a paint additive in Ms. Fr. 640 in different contexts. It is especially helpful in understanding the motivation behind its use, as the manuscript is exceptional in providing explicit explanations of its purpose and what properties it was considered to confer to the oil paint. It is therefore worth discussing it in more detail here. The term cristallin—or the Italian cristallo—is itself ambiguous, since in glass-making recipes the same term is sometimes applied to both the pure silica obtained from crushed pebbles (rather than sand) used as one of the ingredients and for the high quality, very transparent and colorless cristallo glass for which Venice was famous. The latter had been developed there in the fifteenth century and its method of manufacture remained a carefully guarded secret among the glass makers in that city until the technology spread further across Europe during the course of the sixteenth century. By the time of Ms. Fr. 640, Façon-de-Venise glass was being made in various centers across Europe, including Antwerp, Amsterdam, and several locations in France.33

The distinctive step in the manufacture of cristallo developed by the Venetians was the purification of the plant ash that was used as a flux. Treatises mentioning powdered glass, however, more often simply specify the whitest or least tinted.34 Any higher quality colorless glass—whether cristallo or the vitrum blanchum of a slightly lower grade—contains manganese introduced as a separate ingredient or as an impurity in the plant ash, which counteracted the greenish tinge caused by iron as an impurity in the silica source.35 This is of particular interest in this context given that the siccative properties of manganese in oil paint are well known.

Almost every reference to cristallin in Ms. Fr. 640 gives further information indicating that it is high quality glass that is meant rather than crushed silica pebbles. A section on painting (fol. 57r) states that “Lake takes long to dry in oil and for that reason one must grind glass with it. But one needs to choose cristallin because it is cleaner.” The same passage goes on to say “And because it would be too difficult to grind it as it is, one must redden it in the fire, then once completely red throw it into cold water and it will crumble and become powdery and then be ground easily.” This process is encompassed by the term “calcined glass” used elsewhere in the manuscript as a shorthand to indicate its preparation (fols. 60r, 64r, 65v, 68r).

Later it is stated that “It is ground with lake and with aspalthe, which will not dry for a very long time without it” (fol. 58r). The use of powdered glass as a drier specifically for oil paint with red lake and black pigments such as aspalte is also regularly found in the other treatises that mention this practice.36 It would have been better than other siccatives for red lake in particular, since it was translucent (like lakes) so that it would not compromise the optical properties of the paint, especially when used for glazes, and also would not have adversely affected the color of these relatively expensive pigments. As the English painter Marshall Smith wrote in 1692, it would “not in the least Tinge the purest Colours.”37 The earliest treatises in which powdered glass is mentioned as an addition to oil paint do not indicate its function, even if the context suggests it could have been a siccative. Richard Haydocke seems to have been the first to state this explicitly—for orpiment bound in oil—in his 1598 translation of Gian Paolo Lomazzo’s Trattato dell’arte della pittura, scoltura et architettura, although only in a very brief marginal note that does not appear in the original Italian treatise. By the first half of the seventeenth century it was mentioned as a drier more often, and with more detail about the type of glass and how it should be prepared, as well as the circumstances in which it should be used. The earliest manuscript with this more extensive information that had been noted before was that compiled by Theodore de Mayerne between 1620 and 1646 (MS Sloane 2052). The very comparable references to pulverized glass in Ms. Fr. 640 are considerably earlier in date. Mayerne, too, specifically recommends cristallin, as does the more or less contemporary manuscript of 1635 by Pierre le Brun.38

High quality clear glass does indeed seem to have been the type used as an additive in paintings, as is evident from the results of quantitative analysis of glass in paint samples. While the references in treatises in connection to its potential siccative qualities had long been noted—it was discussed at some length already by Merrifield in the mid-nineteenth century—it was only relatively recently that it was possible to prove its presence in painting through technical investigations. Having first been confirmed in Italian works of the sixteenth century, a broader study of more than seventy works in the National Gallery, London, showed that it was also routinely employed in Netherlandish and German paintings (Fig. 7, Fig. 8). Interestingly, among the earliest of these was Jan van Eyck’s Arnolfini Portrait, which, given this artist’s importance in the development of oil painting, perhaps adds significance to this material in the history of painting technique. Although fewer French paintings have been investigated with this material in mind, several examples in works of the fifteenth and sixteenth century have been confirmed.39

In line with what is mentioned in the treatises, including Ms. Fr. 640, the pulverized glass was found mixed with red lake in all of the paintings in the study. Its use in primings is also frequent, with some examples too in blacks and mordants for gilding. A few artists—notably Raphael and Hans Holbein the Elder—employed it much more widely.40 Although historic texts most commonly mention it as a drier, surely an indicator that this was often at least one possible reason artists added it to their paint, there has been much debate about whether this was its primary function or whether its effect on optical and physical properties, including consistency and handling could in fact have been equally if not more important.41 This uncertainty results in part from doubts as to whether the manganese in the glass, while well known to act as a siccative, is sufficiently mobile to interact with the oil; it may be that the alkali content proves to be more important in this respect. Whatever the outcome of the further experiments needed to provide decisive proof that it is effective for this purpose, artists could still have used it in the belief that it could perform this function (at least in oil paintings, rather than the other contexts in which this material is sometimes mentioned). This does not preclude the possibility that they appreciated, in addition, any effect it had on the optical or handling properties of the paint. The study on paintings in the National Gallery, however, indicated a large variability in particle size and it should be remembered that the influence of coarse glass powder and much more finely ground glass will be very different, therefore it is not possible to generalize about its influence on the consistency and viscosity of the oil paint.

Pulverized colorless glass is nevertheless also employed for other purposes in other branches of the arts and Ms. Fr. 640 is especially interesting in this respect. Its use for casting is mentioned in Ms. Fr. 640 (fol. 68r), but more relevant in relation to oil paint is a passage on glazing (fol. 65), where its purpose is clearly intended to increase the transparency of the paint: “One commonly glazes with colors that do not have body, such as lake & verdigris. However, to use other colors, one mixes in a quantity of calcined & ground cristallin, which also has no body & makes lightens the density of the others.” Here the translation is challenging, especially for the last phrase—“esclarcist l’espesseur des aultres”—which seems to express an increase in clarity and transparency concurrent with a decrease in opacity and covering power.42 The repeated statements that ground glass is lacking in “body” are valuable in understanding the meaning of the term in this context and also what properties the author-practitioner considered powdered glass to possess. A note in the margin of fol. 65r states that “Every color or thing that becomes dense when water is put in during the grinding has body. But those which do not have it, such as pestled glass, lake, &c, become clear.” Like sugar, powdered glass appears white when it is dry and its translucency, or lack of body, becomes apparent when it is mixed with a liquid. The phrase in a passage on painting, “Cristallin when ground in water appears to have body, but in oil it does not have any” (fol. 58r), might appear contradictory when taken out of context, but probably refers to a water-based medium juxtaposed against oil where the former, once dry, would indeed appear opaque. The challenges of translation and the idiosyncratic phrasing are again apparent here. Importantly, this passage on glazing is not in reference to paintings but to decoration of cast metallic objects, where paints that allowed the metal to shine through were required. Here it is being advised for use with opaque pigments for a specific purpose, to render the paint more transparent. Conversely, when mentioned as a siccative it is most often for the poor drier, red lake, a pigment that was inherently transparent. Experienced and practical artists and artisans in this period, however, are likely to have had no difficulty in making a distinction between the uses of the same material for different purposes.

Blue Pigments

The author-practitioner’s comments on blues are sufficiently extensive and interesting to merit a separate discussion. As mentioned above, the main blue pigments used in the late sixteenth century were azurite (see Fig. 7), smalt, natural ultramarine, indigo, and blue ashes (cendres d’azur).43 This last term is a color name designating a lower quality of blue rather than a specific pigment and could be various different materials; Samuel van Hoogstraten indeed mentions three different types, saying that “for our blues...we have English, German, and Haarlem ashes.”44 Even so, the name was used most often for the artificial blue copper carbonate (an example is shown in Fig. 16), chemically the same as mineral azurite, but could also be ultramarine ashes (a low grade of ultramarine), or even vivianite, a soft and dullish blue iron phosphate found in peat bogs.45 The term azur is often used in a general way in Ms. Fr. 640 for the color blue. Occasionally, however, it is applied specifically for the mineral azurite, as in the passage that mentions “...mines where ​​azur​​ or ​vert d’azur​ is found” (fol. 11r), which clearly refers to the blue and green copper carbonates usually found together as secondary minerals in areas of copper deposits. A passage on fol. 11r states that “Azure ashes are only good for landscapes because they die in oil. Only true azure holds on.” True azure—azur vray—must also refer to the natural copper carbonate mineral azurite, with the word “true” signifying the contrast with azure ashes, the synthetic equivalent or a low quality grade of the natural azurite. The only possible reference to the pigment ultramarine—Azur d’acre—includes a qualifier that indicates an origin connected with trade from the east into Europe, identifying it unambiguously.46 Azur commun (fol. 10r)—ordinary azur—is advised as a base layer beneath red lake to make purple. In this particular context this is perhaps most likely to mean indigo, since it goes on to say “or better yet azur d’esmail” and neither azurite nor ultramarine could be considered inferior to smalt. Elsewhere florey, which is certainly indigo, is specified.

Over the course of the sixteenth century the blue pigment smalt was introduced onto the artist’s palette and by the second half of the century it was extremely widely used (Fig. 9). It is therefore not surprising that azur d’esmail, the French term for this pigment, is by far the most commonly mentioned blue in Ms. Fr. 640, especially since it was cheap enough to be used not only for high art forms but for paint for all the various decorative purposes with which this manuscript is concerned. On fol. 11r it states that “Those who make it in Germany compound it like enamel, in large pieces which they pestle, & pass through several sieves & wash.” As enamel is a vitreous material this confirms its identity, since smalt is indeed a glass, colored blue with cobalt (Fig. 10). The association with Germany is also significant, since in the sixteenth century smalt was made in only a very limited number of places, including in Germany close to the only cobalt ore deposits in Europe that were being exploited in that period, and in the Netherlands, due to the control they had managed to establish over the trade in cobalt ore from Germany.47 Names associated with Germany such as azzurro tedescho, azzurro della magna, and so on were also used to mean azurite,48 but there is no doubt as to the author-practitioner’s meaning, since smalt is the only blue that could be made “like enamel.”

A limited number of recipes for smalt pigment can be found in historic documentary sources, such as that from the notebooks of the Darduin glass-making family who were active in Murano. It includes river pebbles, which are the source of the silica. Tartar (potassium carbonate) is the flux, added to lower the melting temperature of the glass, although in smalt it is also important in maintaining the blue color and is more effective in this function than the soda ash (sodium carbonate) used in other types of glass. The third ingredient in this recipe, well-calcined or roasted zaffre, was a mixture of roasted cobalt ore and varying amounts of quartz or sand.49 What is important to note is that smalt pigment is always a potash glass containing only potassium as the alkali. This is not the case for true enameling of a blue color found, for example, on Limousin enamels or for decoration on glass or metal objects. There is nevertheless a close association, since smalt could be used as the coloring material, which then needed to be mixed with a white or colorless glass such as the leaded silicate rocaille that melted at the low temperature needed for the enamel so that it did not disrupt the substrate on which it was laid. This is exemplified by the recipe on fol. 31v: “For blue, they take the coarsest azur d’esmail & grind it on shale, & mix in two or three rosary beads’ worth of rocaille or more….” Although entitled “Painting on glass” it is clearly for enameling, since the final phrase states “Then they heat it up.”

Like the equivalent Italian name smalto, the pigment name azur d’esmail can create confusion that makes it difficult to know when to translate it as blue enamel and when as smalt pigment. However, in the manuscript compiled by Theodore de Mayerne (MS Sloane 2052) it is reduced to only the single word email in his list of blue pigments, so it must have been well understood in this period that this designated the blue pigment smalt.50

Many treatises mention the poor working properties of azurite, ultramarine, and especially smalt, which arise from the need for these to be used in a more coarsely ground form than other pigments because they become weaker in color as the particle size decreases (Fig. 11). Aside from making the paint gritty and difficult to use, it meant that the oil absorption of these pigments was high so that they were more likely to suffer from other problems such as darkening or sinking. Various writers, especially in the seventeenth century, therefore give attention to how these shortcomings can be ameliorated, and how these materials could be further processed in the studio to produce a finer pigment.51 This might seem contradictory, but was presumably to reach a compromise between a more intense color and a paint that could be applied and manipulated more easily.

The author-practitioner, too, addresses many of these issues, noting that “every kind of azure is difficult” (fol. 59r), especially smalt (fol. 11r). He repeats the common advice to take “the most delicate” or finest in many places (fols. 11r, 56v, 58v, 59r, 60r, 61v), and suggests trying it on the fingernail or palette to see if it is sandy (fol. 11r). Elsewhere, he comments that painters take their palette to the grocers so they can grind it with some white to assess the intensity of its color (fol. 93v), which was dependent not only on the particle size but also on the amount of cobalt ore used in its manufacture, but at the same time does not neglect to consider its handling properties, saying “The most delicate is the best for working. It is made finer [Il se subtilie] working by washing it.” The final result produced by such a process is indicated by the author-practitioner’s use of “subtil” in the context of other materials, such as molding sand: “to render all of it fine [subtil] & impalpable” (fol. 68v), where impalpable means that no grittiness can be felt between the fingers. An English treatise of 1573 uses similar sensory language for smalt of suitably small particle size: the author advises to choose smalt “which is bright of colour, and not harshe, but softe betwene the fingers.”52 Marshall Smith, too, a century later states “the best Smalt is the deepest and soft like Butter between the Fingers.”53

There are many references scattered through Ms. Fr. 640 to the washing of smalt, first to remove impurities and then as a way of separating the pigment into different fractions of successively finer particles through levigation, as described in the passage on fol. 11r.

The main thing is to grind it well on marble, and before that, to have washed it thoroughly. Some grind it with thoroughly with an egg yolk & then wash it in five or six waters I have experienced that grinding azur d’esmail with egg yolk & next washing it in several waters is good. However, it loses a little of its vividness in the grinding of it. I have also washed it in several waters &, when it had settled a little, I removed the water, still q blue, with a sponge and squeezed it into another vessel thus where it settled, & from the residue I had the ash, flower, and finest part of the azure without grinding it, which is the best, for in the grinding of it, it loses some of its tint. Those who make it in Germany compound it like enamel, in large pieces which they pestle, & pass through several sieves & wash.54

The author-practitioner writes about this in the first person, as if he has experimented himself with the methods that have been recommended to him. The first wash he mentioned would have been to remove any impurities; he explains on fol. 93v that “the filth that can be perceived in the wash water makes it die.” The further washing, as is clear from his description, is instead to separate out the finer pigment through levigation. Like other authors, he discusses the difficulties in processing the pigment after purchase to this end while still retaining a good color. At the end of the above passage he concludes that it is better to achieve a finer pigment without grinding, through levigation, and here as well as elsewhere he notes that with grinding it loses its tint.55 It might, therefore, seem contradictory to start the passage with the advice to grind it well, but in thinking through the practicalities his rather shorthand notes are more comprehensible. Grinding alone will produce a wide range of particle sizes, with the very finest and palest diluting the hue of any medium-fine pigment that would have optimal balance between working properties and color. In washing, however, these finer, lighter particles can be separated off as they are suspended in the liquid. The last sentence mentions that it is sieved during manufacture, but the pigment produced in this way would still be relatively coarse, as it is not effective below a certain particle size. Any further refinement needed to be achieved through washing, and although this was no doubt also done by the manufacturer it may not have been sufficient, perhaps explaining why artists felt the need to process the pigment further themselves.56 The advice to grind the pigment with egg yolk as part of the washing process is also found in a fifteenth-century manuscript in the Biblioteca Casanatense, Rome, Ms. 2265.57 The so-called Ricettario dello Pseudo-Savonarola, dating from the sixteenth century, instead recommends grinding smalt with milk.58 In both cases this may have been to improve the wetting of the pigment in the water.

Having refined the pigment, Ms. Fr. 640 gives various different hints on how to use smalt as a paint, mainly addressing the issues arising from the high oil absorption. Pricking the surface on which it was to be applied, for example (fol. 11r), would have allowed excess oil to sink into the substrate so that, as he notes, the paint would not run. This otherwise seems to have been mentioned only by Karel van Mander in 1604 (Fig. 12) and no evidence has been found on extant paintings that it was part of regular practice.59 The author-practitioner then notes that others deal with this problem by laying the painting flat.60 Similarly, he states “It is better used on canvas, where it is imbibed immediately, than on wood” (fol. 93v).

The addition of turpentine oil, as advised in numerous passages (fols. 58v, 61r) perhaps was able to reduce the amount of oil that was used. It is advice that is mentioned in many other treatises of the period, appearing, for example, more than once in the manuscript of Theodore de Mayerne.61 Grinding smalt with white is also often recommended, including in Ms. Fr. 640 on fols. 11r, 59r, and 61v, and here we can be more certain that this was widely followed. This, too, would have reduced the oil absorption, with the much finer white pigment filling the interstices between the coarse smalt particles. It has been shown in addition that this reduces the degradation of smalt on ageing, a common problem with this unstable pigment.62

Green Pigments

Verdigris was probably still the most widely used green pigment in sixteenth-century oil painting. Its transparency permitted the production of richly colored green glazes over gold or silver (Fig. 13) and, like other translucent pigments, it was ideally suited for painting on glass, as the author-practitioner describes (fols. 39v, 65v). In conventional easel painting this property was exploited in the painting of draperies (see Fig. 7), although, mixed with other pigments, it was also used for landscape. Chemically it is a basic copper acetate,63 but this term covers several forms and in practice it was often sold as so-called distilled verdigris, the neutral salt which had been recrystallized from vinegar.

The identity of the other named green pigments is very much less clear. Verdet is defined by Randle Cotgrave as a form of verdigris and it is certainly a synthetic copper salt. In a manuscript written by Pierre Lebrun in 1635, verdet is described as being prepared from copper or brass filings sprinkled with old urine and sal ammoniac (ammonium chloride), dried in the sun and sprinkled with the same materials until they become green.64 However, Mayerne’s discussion of a verdet distilled for glazing suggests a form of verdigris and the Spanish verdete, a very similar name, appears to have been verdigris.65 Vert de terre (fol. 129r) is described by Cotgrave and Lebrun as a kind of green mineral chalk or sand; this could describe green verditer (a synthetic basic copper carbonate) or green earth (a naturally occurring iron potassium silicate mineral pigment containing glauconite or celadonite, depending on the source). Lebrun says of “verd de terre” that the best comes from Smyrna: “la craye verte ou verd de terre vient de la terre de Smyrne et est la plus excellente”; later he uses the name terre verd for the pigment, all of which suggests green earth, rather than green verditer.66 However, Mayerne describes it as being similar to blue cendres, which is also most often a basic copper carbonate, suggesting that here green verditer is the most likely.67 Cotgrave describes verd d’asur as “a certain blewish greene stone, found in Mines of Copper which be mingled with silver, and an excellent purger of melancholie;”68 this was most probably usually malachite, frequently found in association with the blue pigment azurite—both are basic copper carbonates. However, analysis of materials in paintings has shown that sometimes this can be accompanied by other green or blue-green copper minerals.69 It could also instead be composed mainly of another copper mineral; there is now evidence, for example, of copper sulfates being almost as common as verdigris as green pigments in sixteenth-century Netherlandish painting.70 They have a blue-green hue that would make the color name vert d’azur very appropriate.

Red Pigments

The author devotes rather more attention to the translucent red lake pigments than to the opaque and widely used vermilion, red mercuric sulfide, more widely used in the synthetic form than as the natural mineral cinnabar (see Figs. 4 and 6 for examples of vermilion in paint). Synthetic or natural, the color could vary from a reddish orange—as the author-practitioner says, hardly different from minium—to a dark red. The author-practitioner’s description of it as blaffart et palle—wan or bleak and pale—(fol. 57r) is slightly unexpected for what is usually a strongly colored pigment; perhaps the examples available were rather dull in color, but the pigment was commonly adulterated with minium (red lead), brick dust, or even lime,71 all of which could affect the color. Grinding it on a slab immediately after grinding red lake is suggested (fol. 57r). The addition of a little red lake pigment to give some vibrancy to the color, suggested by the author-practitioner on fol. 64r, is quite often found in practice.

The lakes were manufactured pigments, made by precipitating or adsorbing a dye onto a suitable substrate, commonly amorphous alumina hydrate, but chalk or even lead white could be used; in the last-named case the pigment is not translucent. Strongly colored and translucent in the oil binding medium (Fig. 14), red lakes were commonly used as glazing pigments, as described by the author-practitioner on fol. 65v, applied over red or pink paint, often containing vermilion, to give shadow and structure to drapery folds, or over blues to give purples as mentioned above (fol. 10r). At this time the sources of dye were those used in dyeing: the expensive insect dyes kermes and the New World cochineal;72 lac, also an insect dye, less often used on textiles but widely used in pigments; madder and the soluble redwood brazilwood (the south-east Asian variety sappanwood was probably also available). Of these, kermes, cochineal, and madder lakes were made from clippings or shearings of cloth, extracting the dye in alkali (such as that prepared from wood ashes, essentially potassium carbonate) and precipitating the pigment by the addition of potash alum (aluminum potassium sulfate); lac and brazilwood lakes were generally made directly from the raw material.73

As far as the pigments made using dyes extracted from cloth were concerned, it was not necessarily clear to anyone buying a lake pigment which dye was present, although by the 1580s, in Italy at least, names under which lakes were bought were beginning to be more informative and it seems very possible that lakes described or named as crimson—cremese and similar names—contained cochineal dyestuff.74 In general, however, in most western European languages, most red lakes were simply known as “lake.” They were available in different shades of red, depending partly on the dyestuff present; those containing madder were usually a more orange red rather than the crimsons or bluer reds given by the insect dyes.75 The author suggests the use of as red and as lively a lake as possible for the painting of flesh, as too purple a lake gave a cold color and the very dark variety was unsuitable (fols. 58r, 61r). For violet too, the blue pigment used should be glazed with a lake which “on a polished knife or on glass gives a clear color of red rose, tending a little towards violet” (fol. 93v); the dark-red variety was not as pleasant. On the other hand, a more purple shade was an advantage if the lake was to be used to make purple by glazing over a layer of blue paint: mixing alum with it to make it more violet in color is suggested (fol. 10r). The addition of too much alum while the lake was being made also resulted in a more purple or violet color (fol. 58r). Both comments suggest that the author-practitioner knew, or had been talking to, someone with experience of making the pigment, or had tried it in person; the effect on the color of adding too much (or additional) alum is rarely mentioned at this date. The tendency to become more purple can be observed in the case of lakes containing cochineal dye, which would probably have become common by the 1570s or 1580s.76

The names used for lakes usually referred either to places that supplied good quality pigments, or to some physical property such as the shape in which the dry pigment was supplied. The author writes that the lake from Florence is better than that from Flanders “for in Florence the best dyes are made” (fol. 58r); the comment that it was used as a rouge also suggests that it was not an unflattering bluish crimson. Interestingly, however, the comment is also made that, whereas a good lake will darken immediately if wetted with saliva (light scatter from the surface is reduced by wetting), that from Florence is “too gummed (trop gommee)” (fol. 57r), a comment saying something about the processing of the pigment or the form in which the pigment was supplied.

In the description of painting a cross of the Commanders of Malta in a transparent red over silver leaf (fol. 40v), laque platte d’Inde, which the author thought was made in Flanders (although on fol. 6r he describes it as Venetian), is suggested as an alternative to dragon’s blood. Laque platte refers to the shaping or cutting of the wet paste of finished pigment into flat squares or tablets, which are then allowed to dry and kept for use; this was quite widely done.77 Inde may suggest that this particular variety of lake pigment was made from lac dye; this was imported from India principally in the form of stick lac, the dried, hard, brownish raw material secreted by the lac insect which encrusted the twigs of the tree upon which it was parasitic, either broken from the twigs or still attached.78 Laque platte could also be used to give a transparent red over gold, being more suitable for this than either brazilwood or laque ronde, the name given to lake that was prepared and sold in the shape of drops or balls,79 however, these lakes died (fol. 6r)—in this case, the word “die” probably indicates that they faded.

One red lake that had had its own name for several centuries was that prepared from the dye extracted from brazilwood or sappanwood (the principal dye constituents are the same in both), with the addition of potash alum and, usually, chalk or another calcium-containing material. This pigment, light red or pink in powder or an aqueous binding medium, bright scarlet in oil, was known as roset in English, rosette in Ms. Fr. 640, and very similar names in other languages (see Fig. 22 below). Modifications of the making process gave an ink and other recipes using brazilwood gave other colors, but roset seems to have been particularly popular. The version favored by the author-practitioner with ceruse in the substrate (fol. 63v) would have been opaque and not as suitable as a glazing pigment.80

Yellow Pigments

An important use for yellow pigments was to make green colors, either by mixtures with or glazing over blue (Fig. 15, Fig. 16), or by mixtures with green, particularly verdigris. In Ms. Fr. 640, yellow ocher, orpiment, massicot (lead-tin yellow), and stil de grain yellow are all mentioned for that purpose, as much for painting decorative and molded items (as described on fol. 158v) as for any other purpose. Stil de grain yellow is particularly interesting from this point of view as it is a form of lake pigment, made from a yellow dye with the addition of potash alum and, usually, chalk, in a very similar way to the brazilwood pigment roset. The use of a wood ash or similar alkali as a precipitating agent for making yellow lakes was uncommon at this date. The source of dye was either weld (Fig. 17, Fig. 18), a pigment the author-practitioner describes as being made in Lyon (fol. 10r), or dyer’s broom (fol. 63v).81 In both of these recipes, the use of ceruse rather than chalk is suggested, which would give a more opaque pigment. For decorative purposes, such a pigment was probably quite useful, but examples have very rarely been identified in conventional easel painting. Here, and particularly in the painting of landscape, yellow lake pigments came to be used more frequently from the early decades of the sixteenth century onwards. These pigments were made using chalk or a similar calcium salt, and in an oil medium were translucent, like the red lake pigments. In mixtures to give the darker shades of green or brownish greens, they gave a useful translucency in the mid ground of the painting, useful to convey recession;82 they were also used for conventional glazing purposes.

In practice, the presence of yellow lake pigments in easel paintings is quite hard to confirm, partly because the dyes from which they are made are quite fugitive, but also because even where the color survives its presence is difficult to confirm through analysis. As a result, the only sign that such a pigment may be present in a mixture is the unexpected presence of a marked amount of a calcium salt in a paint layer where a yellow might have been expected.

Unlike the red lakes, the names for yellow lakes vary from country to country in sixteenth-century Europe: for example, arzica and giallo santo in Italy, pincke in England, Schietgelb and schijtgeel in Germany and the northern and southern Netherlands respectively, to name but a few, all with varying spellings. Stil de grain was the recognized name in seventeenth-century France and the evidence of Ms. Fr. 640 shows it was in use earlier. However, in the seventeenth century, the name was usually associated with a pigment made using the juice of unripe buckthorn berries, grains d’Avignon, not one prepared from weld or dyer’s broom.83 This more golden-yellow or golden-brown pigment appears to have been very much less popular at an earlier date; most recipes for the pigments describe the use of weld or dyer’s broom (like Ms. Fr. 640). Perhaps stil de grain was a name for the pigment in France even in cases where the yellow dye used was not that obtained from unripe berries.

The author-practitioner mentions the golden-yellow arsenic trisulfide pigment orpiment in the context of supplying a gold color in place of gilding itself; the related orange arsenic disulfide, realgar, is only mentioned in the context of the founding of soft iron (fol. 16r), not as a pigment. To obtain a deeper, more orange or golden yellow, orpiment could be melted or sublimed (fols. 9v, 99v), and by the late sixteenth century a range of synthetic or modified arsenic sulfides were being produced on a large scale by dry process methods.84

Black Pigments

The blacks most commonly mentioned in sixteenth-century treatises on painting materials are charcoal black, bone or ivory black, lamp black, fruit-stone blacks of different kinds, and black earths.85 The list on fol. 58v in Ms. Fr. 640 is therefore quite typical: “Black of charcoal from the mines, of ordinary charcoal, of burnt ivory, of peach pits, of lamp smoke, of burnt bones of the feet of oxen.” Many of the references in historic treatises make it clear that artists were well aware of the differing hues and working properties of these blacks, and that they chose which pigment to use accordingly. Bone black gives a deep, saturated warm black paint, charcoal black is bluer in tone, while lamp black has less body and can have a slightly greasy quality.86 A passage on velvets and blacks in Ms. Fr. 640, for example, states “For blue & green velvets, you highlight touch the shading with peach pit black, which is very black. For lake, black of pit coal which makes a reddish black on lake for velvets” (fol. 63v). “Soft wood charcoal” is described as bluish and therefore suitable for depicting armor (fol. 63v).

The “black of charcoal from the mines” (noir de charbon de mine) is probably equivalent to black earth. This is not a single material of defined composition, but a class of pigments, which could include a number of different black minerals. The terms black earth, black chalk, and black stone tend to be used interchangeably, both for black pigments and drawing materials. Sometimes good geographical sources are mentioned, or shreds of information about their properties are given, which could provide some clue as to the nature of the pigment. Vasari mentions black chalk from the hills of France, and Antonio Filarete mentions a black earth from Germany for fresco in his Trattato dell’Architettura of the fifteenth century (ca. 1461–4).87 While these could simply be carbonaceous black shales, they could also equate with the black pyrolusite (manganese black) that has been identified in numerous paintings of the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, or the black coal that is in fact far more common. Generally it is only in seventeenth-century treatises where a pigment that is certainly a coal is found. Theodore de Mayerne, for example, mentions charbon de pierre and charbon de terre d’Escosse—both common coal—and it also appears under the names of sea coal and smythe coal (forge coal) in several English treatises of the period. The earliest seems to be in The Arte of Limning by Nicolas Hilliard dating from the beginning of the seventeenth century;88 it is therefore interesting that Ms. Fr. 640 mentions “le noir de charbon de pierre,” translated as “pit coal,” as a reddish black for painting velvets (see above). Later in the century, Edward Norgate too stated that “sea cole makes a Red Blacke,” which is consistent with the warm black appearance of the coal that has been identified in paintings.

Ms. Fr. 640 is unusual in mentioning geiete, jet, which is in fact a low grade coal (fol. 65r), recommended for shadows in flesh paint. Coal blacks of different types and composition have been found through analysis of paint samples (Fig. 19), including in one case a lignitic type that could well be consistent with jet as a pigment name.89 Interestingly, Marshall Smith uses it as a descriptor of color, stating “When Ivory is burnt you must scrape off all the white and brownish Colour, reserving only the Jet Black.”90 Otherwise “geet” can be found in the accounts of materials for the painters at Westminster (1351–2),91 and was bought to be used for painting on glass. William Salmon’s Polygraphice of 1684 also notes jet as a black for painting on glass, together with iron scales or copper scales.92

Iron scale—named as glassworkers black (fol. 41v) or gris d’escaille (fol. 37v)—is also mentioned for painting on glass in Ms. Fr. 640. Lomazzo, however, lists nero di scaglia—scale black—among the pigments that could be used for oil painting. Interestingly he puts this in the category of a black earth, which suggests that the term was being used in a broader sense to mean a pigment with soft, friable earth-like properties. A pigment that could be nero di scaglia of the copper type (copper oxide) mentioned by Salmon has been found in Italian sixteenth-century paintings.

Gilding

Gilding, the application of gold or silver leaf to the surface of an object, was a popular and widely used decorative technique, often applied to painted objects, but also used on its own. Burnished gold—gold that has received a high polish by rubbing—has a gleaming, shiny appearance, but gold can also appear more yellow and with a slightly matte surface if it is not burnished, and the method used to apply the gold was chosen according to the appearance desired (Fig. 20).

The methods for applying metal leaf described by the author-practitioner were fairly traditional, having changed little from previous centuries. If the gold (or silver) leaf was to be burnished, it was applied using a technique known today as water gilding, on a surface that provided cushioning for the leaf and also a degree of “slip,” so that the thin leaf would flatten out and take a high polish as it was rubbed with a suitable burnishing tool, commonly a polished hard stone such as hematite or even the canine tooth of a dog. Traditionally, this surface consisted of a slightly greasy, reddish-brown clay, known as Armenian bole (the color being given by its content of red iron oxide), or sometimes another red-brown earth, mixed with an aqueous binding medium, such as an animal skin glue or glair (the liquid from beaten egg white that has been allowed to settle out), which served to attach the gold laid on top, but also allowed a degree of movement as it was burnished. This was essential: if the gold was held firmly, it could not move and burnishing was impossible. The reddish color of the bole or other red earth gave some warmth to the gold above.93 The author-practitioner describes the procedure very precisely in “For laying down and setting burnished gold and giving red or green or blue” (fol. 6r), first applying four layers of chalk in a distemper (animal glue) medium, followed by a mixture of bole, sanguine (a red-brown earth pigment), a little lamb’s fat, a little ground charcoal and half-burnt saffron, ground and mixed with water; this would have been greasy and brownish in color. It was smoothed with a cloth until it was shiny, wet with a paint brush of clean water and the gold applied and burnished when dry. The author-practitioner goes on to suggest glazing the gold with red lake or verdigris; blue—smalt, azur d’esmail—was better applied over silver leaf.

At the beginning of this passage, the author-practitioner also mentions the other gilding method: mordant gilding, in which the gold is applied over a mordant layer, essentially an oil paint containing pigments such as red or yellow earths and lead-containing pigments: “or mat which is made with oil, mixing it with yellow ocher & mine [minium]… & tempering all of it with oil.” In this case, the gold is held firmly by the mordant and cannot be burnished (Fig. 21).94 The technique was widely used for gilding detailed or complex surfaces where burnishing would be impractical, but also for applying fine gold details or lettering to painted surfaces. This could also be done in an aqueous medium, such as the batture described on fol. 32r, made from “joiner’s glue tempered with water on the fire, moderately clear, mixed with very little honey, that is to say a few drops to make it stick, and with it they form letters, or that which they want to gild, with a pinceau, and immediately after layer the gold.” The author-practitioner goes on to say that sugar candy mixed with a little sanguine gave neater results, however. The most common mordant recommended by the author-practitioner for or mat consisted of walnut oil, thickened by being allowed to stand, with or without the admixture of a lead-containing pigment to improve drying, mixed with several or all of massicot (lead-tin yellow), minium (red lead), yellow ocher, and ochre de ru (a dark yellow or yellowish-brown earth pigment) (fols. 66r, 66v, 100r). The oil and pigment mixture obtained from cleaning brushes was used by some, although the author-practitioner stated that this eventually caused the gold to discolor due to the verdigris present (fol. 66r). The mordant became sticky quite quickly and the gold, either in the form of leaf or as ground or powdered gold, could be applied. Once dry it could be varnished. The author-practitioner describes a decorative technique for frames consisting of a mordant gilded moresque design (a stylized pattern of tendrils and foliage) over black, where the black was painted in a glue medium (fol. 66r).95

Conclusion

The author-practitioner’s discussion of painting in Ms. Fr. 640 is unusual in many ways. If the comments distributed throughout the text are put together, the end result is not the equivalent of an artists’ manual; this is not the aim. The descriptions of how to paint hair, or the faces of old men, or various birds and animals, still almost universal in treatises on painting at this time, are absent. There is some discussion of painting in aqueous binding media, notably in comments on the painting of flesh, but most of the points are on the practice of oil painting, that is, how the various pigments behave when mixed with the oil, their drying characteristics and the properties of the oil. His aims are different to those of other more or less contemporary authors writing on oil painting. In most cases, other authors focus on the work of easel painters, while the author-practitioner writes about the use of oil paint in general. His interest appears to lie as much, or rather more, in the decorative purposes for which it was used, such as painting cast metal flowers (fol. 158v), for example, or applying a suitable priming layer to silk taffeta so that it could be decorated with oil paint without the oil running, a technique that might be used to prepare decorative banners (fol. 10v, 42v).96

The earliest published account of oil painting in French appears to be the chapter on “Platte peinture” in the Jesuit author Etienne Binet’s Essay des merveilles de nature, a very popular book on everything from falconry to the rainbow, written under the pseudonym René François and first published in 1621. Much of this information was copied in Pierre Lebrun’s very much more substantial Recueil des essaies des merveilles de la peinture, written in 1635 but unpublished.97 These are forty or fifty years later than Ms. Fr. 640. Italian examples are more numerous, and some, such as Giovanni Paolo Lomazzo’s Trattato dell’ arte della pittura, scoltura, et architettura (Milan 1584) and Giovann Battista Armenini’s De’ veri precetti della pittura (Ravenna 1587), were written at much the same time by people who were painters and did include practical information, but also a great deal of history and commentary on the works of painters.98 Rather little technical information is given by Giorgio Vasari in the introductory essay to Le Vite de’ più eccellenti pittori, scultori, e architettori (Florence 1550, enlarged second edition 1568), the model followed by Karel van Mander in Het Schilder-Boeck (Haarlem 1604).99

There are interesting similarities between some points made in Ms. Fr. 640 and comments made by Van Mander, not only about sixteenth-century Flemish painting. It seems very possible that the author-practitioner obtained some of his information from someone who had trained in the southern Netherlands, or had a good knowledge of Flemish painting practice. That said, where the author-practitioner is discussing the work of Flemish or Italian painters, one occasionally has the sense that he is describing the practice of an earlier generation; the comment on fol. 61r that Flemish painters “do all their works in oil with the tip of the pinceau, in the manner of good illuminators” might suggest a painter of the early sixteenth century, rather than a contemporary artist. On the other hand, his descriptions of the behaviour of paint—whether a particular pigment dries well or not; how to improve the drying properties of oil paint—are as valid now as they were in the later sixteenth century or earlier, and his descriptions of smalt and its behavior are entirely contemporary.

The other account discussing painting from the point of view of how painters went about their work is Theodore de Mayerne’s “Pictoria, sculptoria et quae subalternarum artium,” which is also at least forty years later, but the emphasis on how the pigments behave is similar. It is also useful for the present-day researcher to read that a comment made on the behavior of a pigment in the 1620s is very similar to one made in 1580 or so.

Some of the properties ascribed to pigments or the paint in which they are used are expressed in words that are found widely: that a pigment or paint has body, for example, or that the color or paint dies, are expressions as likely to be found in the Marciana Manuscript or the writings of Lebrun or Mayerne as in Ms. Fr. 640. The Marciana Manuscript, for example, describes colors as being of two kinds, “one of which consists of those which have no body, and which do not conceal the colors laid under them,” while Mayerne, who comments regularly about the body of pigments or pigments dying under certain circumstances, also writes, “The death of colors is when the oil floating on the surface dries and forms a skin, which blackens in the air.”100 Body usually indicates opacity of either pigment or paint. An interesting example is provided by the discussion of powdered cristallin glass, which as a powder looks white and so as if it has body, i.e., some degree of opacity (discussed above). In practice, once it has been added to oil paint, it does not: the refractive index of the glass is very similar to that of the oil, so it is transparent in oil and provides translucency to the paint (fols. 57r, 58r).

Dying implies an undesirable change: the pigment may fade or change color; fol. 56v provides an example, describing the fading of lake pigments (Fig. 22, Fig. 23): “The lake & rosette of Ghent & others lose their color & die in the air.”

The paint may also die by darkening, or it may “sink” as the oil in the uppermost paint layer sinks down into the layer beneath, leaving the top layer with a matte appearance. A remedy for this was to apply a varnish to the paint, as described on fol. 32r: “As soon as the colors of panels are well dried, the Flemish varnish them so they do not die any further & remain in that state.” A particularly interesting example of dying, also on fol. 56v, relates to the mixing of colors: “In distemper do not mix your different colors together for this makes them die, but use each separately.” In this case dying presumably refers to the risk of a mixture of pigments giving a subdued color, rather than the pure, bright tone one might expect from paint in an aqueous binding medium like distemper.

The author-practitioner is sufficiently interested in the training of painters and the drawing of heads to note down some points (fols 62r, 62v, 64v). Instructions for drawing heads and faces (and bodies) could be found in many books at this time, often illustrated (Fig. 24); an example may be found in Binet’s work, although this is more an explanation of different views of the head.101 However, it is interesting that there was also a context particularly relevant to Toulouse: painting the portraits of the eight Toulouse capitouls, the chief magistrates of the commune of Toulouse who were elected annually, was a privilege given to painters. As the painting of portraits was otherwise restricted to the nobility, the honor of having their portraits painted thus underlined the civic authority of the capitouls. Instructions in a text describing how to paint faces could therefore be useful.102

Otherwise the discussion of painting is almost entirely practical. The author-practitioner’s comments on brushes, both the coarser broisses (made using hogs’ bristles) and the finer pinceaulx (made using the hair of smaller animals such as squirrels and members of the weasel family), including a description of how to make them, are made with a detail rarely found in contemporary texts (fols. 31v, 58v, 66v–67r).103 The author-practitioner describes how to make and apply the paint, not only on prepared panels or textile supports, but also on cast metal or modeled artifacts. The interest in translucent paint is notable: the only pigments or paint where there is any sign of the author-practitioner making a practical trial are the transparent pigments dragon’s blood and azur d’esmail, smalt. The description of smalt and its properties, as well as how to use it, are not found anywhere else in this amount of detail at this date; the very similar comments by Van Mander were written some twenty years later. The suggestion that cristallin, powdered glass, can be used to give a degree of translucency to otherwise opaque pigments, as described on fol. 65v, without affecting the hue of the pigment—quite apart from its use as an additive to improve the drying of paint—is very rarely mentioned in written sources. Perhaps it is in the use of paint to decorate metal items, or perhaps painting on glass, that this technique would be particularly useful.

The discussion of painting in Ms. Fr. 640 is a rare example of one that is based on practice alone, including the preparation of surfaces and the making and application of paint by those who need to use it, whatever the intended purpose. In this it is very different from those contemporary authors writing more on painting as a liberal art and less on the practice; this author is not trying to demonstrate his academic credentials. At the same time it does not reproduce the standard instructions on painting particular motifs (hair, birds) found in earlier works and still appearing in contemporary literature. The author-practitioner may not have quite succeeded in writing a practical guide for the preparation and use of paint for decorative purposes, such as painting glass or cast metal objects—the absence of a logical order to the content of the manuscript suggests he had some way to go to achieve this. However, the content of the instructions he gives is useful and informative, providing a rare practical commentary on late sixteenth-century painting practice.
