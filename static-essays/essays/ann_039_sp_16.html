Gilding on Paper and Parchment

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/a53m-jb79
← Back to Essays

Abstract

Among recipes in BnF Ms. Fr. 640 devoted to gilding, two give instructions for laying gold leaf on paper and parchment, and these recipes distinguish themselves by their reliance on an unusual mordant made from wheat starch (fols. 10v and 29v). By reconstructing these recipes, this essay evaluates the possible ways in which this adhesive could have been used in the context of gilding on paper and parchment.

BnF Ms. Fr. 640 contains three entries on fols. 8r, 10v, and 29v that describe how to apply gold leaf to either paper or parchment.1 While the author-practitioner uses various mordants (i.e., the underlayer that adheres gold leaf to a surface) for gilding, the entries on fols. 10v and 29v differ from other such recipes in their use of mordants derived from wheat starch. While use of this substance as an adhesive for paper or parchment is not particularly unusual—in fact, it is still used by paper conservators today—it is unusual to see it employed as a mordant in gilding. Reconstruction of these recipes paired with historical research on their context can suggest the ways in which the author-practitioner came to employ this adhesive in gilding.

Reconstruction

Wheat Starch Adhesives

Both recipes use amydon, a “fine wheat flower [sic] steeped in water, then strained.”2 The substance resulting from this process is wheat starch, the carbohydrate portion of the grain.3 The starch molecule is adhesive yet non-corrosive, making it an ideal ingredient for glues.4 The recipes on fols. 10v and 29v use different forms of this starch as a mordant. The recipe “Seat for gold leaf on parchment or paper” on fol. 10v calls for colle d’amydon (starch glue) which is fort clere (very clear), while “Layer of burnished gold on paper” on fol. 29v calls for l’eau clere d’amydon (clear starch water). The author-practitioner does not describe how to make either substance, which might imply that the recipes were commonly known. A recipe for flour glue can be found in Cennino Cennini’s Il libro dell’arte:

Get a little pan almost full of clear water; make it really hot. When it is about to boil, get some thoroughly sieved flour; add it, little by little, to the little pan, stirring continuously with a stick or spoon. Let it boil and make sure that it is not too stiff. Take it out; put it in a bowl. If you do not want it to smell bad add some salt.5

Cennini’s flour paste may create the same substance as wheat starch glue, but because the author-practitioner clearly differentiates between farine (wheat flour) and amydon (wheat starch),6 I used ready-made wheat starch to create a paste for my reconstruction of the recipes on fols. 10v and 29v, referring to Cennini’s recipe to judge its proper consistency.7 Cennini does not specify the ratio of starch to water, so I used a 5:1 water to starch ratio, which I found in the instructions of a modern recipe intended for rare book conservators.8 I boiled 100 ml of wheat starch in 500 ml of distilled water. After storage in the fridge, the paste had the consistency of a gelatinous pudding (Fig. 1).9

In the wheat starch recipe for the laying of gold leaf on paper or parchment (fol. 10v), the author-practitioner states that the colle (glue) must be fort clere (very clear), implying that it must have a more liquid and translucent consistency than the paste I had made. To ensure that my paste was clear but still thick enough to be considered colle while still being fort clere, I placed three palette-knife sized scoops of the paste into a beaker and heated it in a bain-marie until it became liquid. With the addition of 100 ml of water it became translucent (Fig. 2).

For the process of laying the gold leaf on paper (fol. 29v), the author-practitioner describes the adhesive not as glue (colle), but as eau d’amydon (starch water). This recipe specifies soaking wheat starch in water (amydon destrempe en eau) and then explains that clear starch water (l’eau clere d’amydon) is a good base for burnished gold. I thus assumed that eau clere d’amydon probably referred to a more liquid and transparent substance than the colle d’amydon of fol. 10v and that this “clear starch water” was likely made mainly of water with only a small amount of starch soaked in it. To make clear starch water, I added one scoop of the cold starch glue from my reconstruction of the Cennini recipe to 200 ml of distilled water—a mixture with more water and less starch than I used for the starch glue. I heated the mixture in a bain-marie with a beaker until it was a cloudy liquid. This mixture behaved more like a watery, runny liquid than the gluey colle d’amydon (hereafter “starch glue”) (Fig. 3).

Gilding

Gilding is a difficult and delicate task. Individual leaves of gold are so light that they can be disturbed by any movement of air, even breath. I practiced gilding before attempting to gild using the two amydon mordants; however, I was unable to lay the gold leaf down perfectly in either instance.10 While I am not experienced enough to produce a flawless result in which the leaf lays down flat and does not roll over onto itself, my trials showed that the gold leaf did adhere to the mordants.

Materials and Tools

The gold leaf and gilding tools I employed in reconstructing the author-practitioner’s recipes are very different from those used in the sixteenth century. Sixteenth-century gilders acquired gold leaf from a goldbeater, who made gold leaf by beating coins into the thinnest possible sheets.11 Unlike other metals, gold can be reduced to a thickness of several atoms without crumbling, allowing for the creation of extremely thin leaves.12 Sixteenth-century gold coins like ducats were alloyed with other metals, making them less flexible and heavier than pure gold leaf.13 Using weights outlined in Cennino Cennini’s Il libro dell’arte, Daniel V. Thompson calculated that a single sheet of sixteenth-century gold leaf weighed approximately 0.54 grams, whereas modern gold leaf weighs approximately 0.20 grams. Sixteenth-century gold leaf was thus likely twice as heavy and less pliable than the 23 karat gold leaf used in modern gilding. Because of the difficulty in sourcing a contemporary gold leaf of thicker proportions and unknown alloy, I used a 0.2 gram gold leaf and worked with a gilder’s set. I cut the leaf on a gilding cushion with a knife and used a gilder’s tip to pick up and apply the leaf. The gilder’s tip is likely a post-medieval invention; medieval gold leaf did not require such a delicate tool because it was heavier and less pliable.14 It is more likely that premodern gilders would have used a small shovel made out of parchment or paper to move the gold leaf.15

The recipe on fol. 10v specifies that starch glue can be used to apply gold leaf to paper or parchment, while the recipe on fol. 29v is explicitly intended for paper. The surface texture of both substrates is very different and affects the surface appearance of the applied gold. Sixteenth-century paper possessed an uneven and fibrous texture. The papers I used are gelatin-sized sheets made of hemp and cotton fiber made by the University of Iowa’s Center for the Book.16

Parchment is generally much thicker and denser than paper and thus behaves very differently. It was made from the hides of calves, sheep, or goats. While the exact process of medieval parchment production is unknown, the procedure involved washing and removing hair from the hide and then stretching and drying it on a frame.17 I used vellum (parchment made from calf) sourced from Henk de Groot, a maker of parchment based in Rotterdam who uses sixteenth-century techniques. Modern parchment makers remove hair chemically using sodium sulphide, whereas sixteenth-century parchment makers would have done so manually. Regardless of technique, the resulting hairless parchment is the same.18

Unlike paper, which is uniform on both sides, parchment differs markedly on the flesh side (the side on the interior of the animal’s body) and the hair side (on the exterior of the animal’s body). The flesh side tends to be whiter and softer while the hair side tends to be yellower and smoother, with the hair follicles visible as bumps.19 Both sides could be used for gilding, but the resulting gilding layers would have different textures. The entry on fol. 10v, however, does not specify which side of the parchment to gild.

Applying the Mordants and Laying Down the Gold Leaf

I taped two pieces of parchment (one hair side and one flesh side) and a piece of paper to a sheet of cardboard in order to compare all three substrates (Fig. 4). I then took a flat paint brush and, following the instructions on fol. 10v, applied five layers of the wheat starch glue (colle d’amydon) to each substrate and then gilded them (Fig. 5, Fig. 6, Fig. 7). I used a piece of cheesecloth attached to a paintbrush to tamp the gold leaf flat (Fig. 8).20

In the recipe on fol. 29v, the author-practitioner mentions using eau d’amydon (starch water) on paper only, not on parchment. In the sixteenth century, paper was made by grinding and soaking rags into a pulp which was then poured into a mold.21 The result was flatter, thinner, and more absorbent than parchment. I painted three coats of starch water, as specified on fol. 29v, on a sheet of historically produced paper taped to a piece of cardboard (Fig. 9, Fig. 10). I then used a gilder’s tip to apply the gold leaf (Fig. 11).22

I waited for the four trials to dry before burnishing them with an agate burnisher. I removed the gilded papers and parchments from their cardboard backings and placed them onto a hard plastic table where I could apply maximum pressure while burnishing. I burnished only the right half of all three types of substrates, leaving the left side unburnished. The recipe on fol. 10v does not specify burnishing the gold leaf on wheat starch glue, but it is possible that the author-practitioner presumed it to be an obvious part of the technique (Fig. 12). He notes on fol. 29v that gold leaf applied to paper with starch water will burnish well. Indeed, after burnishing, the paper became much smoother and more reflective (Fig. 13).

Comparison of Results

The gold leaf laid on wheat starch glue (following the recipe on fol. 10v) adhered well to both sides of the parchment and to the paper. Additionally, the glue was thin enough that the grainy texture of the hair side was visible through the gilding. However, because the modern gold leaf was thinner than that of the sixteenth century, it is plausible that the grain would not have shown through to the same degree for the author-practitioner. Both pieces of parchment became slightly warped after a week. This is common because parchment tends to revert to the living curves of its source animal (Fig. 14). Despite the warping, the gold remained securely attached to the parchment. It is likely, however, that the moisture from the glue caused the parchment to warp as it dried, which would be problematic if it were for use within a bound manuscript. The paper absorbed much of the starch glue (colle d’amydon), making the paper rather fragile during the application of the gold leaf, as wet paper is much more likely to rip than wet parchment. The starch water (of the recipe on fol. 29v) was much more liquid than the starch glue, resulting in an even more fragile paper substrate.

Overall, the thinness of the starch water on paper allowed for a less textured surface than the starch glue on paper, creating a more easily burnished gold surface. However, the starch water was less adhesive than the starch glue, and would therefore likely have been unsuitable for gilding on parchment; this may be the reason that the recipe on fol. 29v does not mention using starch water to gild on parchment. The adhesive strength of starch glue makes it effective on both paper and parchment, however it forms a thick underlayer which makes it less suitable for burnishing.

The burnished and unburnished parchment did not appear significantly different. The hair side was so rough and hard that it was impossible to apply any more pressure. The grain was still visible through both the burnished and unburnished sections (Fig. 15). This was not the case in manuscripts illuminated on the hair side of parchment in the author-practitioner’s lifetime. They were gilded without the grain showing through the gold leaf. Parchment naturally changes shape when wet, similar to my experience when the glue was applied. The slightly raised gilded areas in medieval manuscripts was partly due to the reshaping of the parchment beneath the gold leaf as it became wet with mordant (Fig. 16).23 To counteract the grainy texture and achieve a more unified convex surface, illuminators added powder—mainly chalk or lead—to the glue, which would then fill in the more shallow areas of the grain under the force of the burnisher.24

In contrast to the hair side, the gold leaf on the flesh side of the parchment lacked the coarseness of the hair follicles and rough skin of the hair side (Fig. 17). The burnisher wore through the gold, which indicates that the mordant did not create a sufficiently hard surface. The gold was as reflective and flat as the gold leaf in Getty Ms. 34, fol. 15 (Fig. 18).

The gold leaf on paper burnished well with respect to both shine and texture, both on the starch glue and on the starch water. The gilding with starch water looked flatter than the paper gilding on starch glue, though not considerably. Both were similarly reflective. The grain of the paper was still slightly visible through the gold leaf for both, but this is fairly common with gilded paper. Overall, both the gold leaf applied with starch water and the gold leaf applied with starch glue were of similar quality to gilded works on paper produced during the Renaissance, such as a fifteenth-century illuminated paper manuscript copy of Dante’s Purgatory (Fig. 19).

None of my burnished papers or parchments were absolutely flat or reflective. This is apparent when these pieces are compared to gilded panels with an underlayer of bole, which produces a perfectly smooth, flat surface (Fig. 20). Nevertheless, the paper and parchment gilded with wheat starch (amydon) mordants come close to the flat surface and shine seen in medieval manuscripts. Naturally, I cannot conclude anything about the longevity of my trials in comparison to medieval gilded paper and parchment, much of which has endured for centuries.

A Brief History of Gilding

Ms. Fr. 640 was written at a time when texts were being produced in both manuscript and print. The production of manuscripts declined slowly with the rise of the printing press, and early printers left space for illuminated capitals and gilded images in their printed texts.25 In Europe, parchment had been the predominant writing surface since late antiquity, replacing the less durable papyrus.26 Paper appeared in Europe as early as the late twelfth century but was not used widely until the advent of movable type in the fifteenth century.27 Paper could be produced more cheaply than parchment, as it was made from rags rather than costly animal hides.28 Additionally, paper could be made with a more uniform surface in a mold (unlike parchment which varied with the individual animal), and thus formed a better surface for printing. The author-practitioner’s concern for gilding on both paper and parchment reflects the coexistence of manuscripts and print materials at the end of the sixteenth century.

Prior to the twelfth century, illuminators used a gold pigment that was composed of ground gold and a binder, and which had a grainy character and little luster.29 Grinding gold was difficult because gold can be flattened continuously without disintegrating (a property which allows for gold leaf to be beat extremely thin).30 Gold was used sparingly in manuscripts, mostly as an accent on decorated initials.31

Illuminated manuscripts grew in popularity in Western Europe during the twelfth century as a result of heightened contact with Byzantium during the Crusades, contact that proved influential for Western art and architecture.32 Western European artists used materials associated with the East, such as gold and lapis, to convey luxury and, by extension, the otherworldly and the divine.33 Moreover, Western European illuminators learned Byzantine methods for gilding mosaics with gold leaf and applied these processes to gilding on parchment.34

Many incunables and early printed works continued to use gold leaf as a means of replicating the prestige of illuminated manuscripts. Early printed Bibles, including some produced by Gutenberg, were resplendent with illuminated capitals or painted block prints to resemble manuscripts.35 The invention of printing thereby proved beneficial for some illuminators, as it increased the volume of text to be illuminated.36

By the mid-seventeenth century, printers had devised ways of printing with gold leaf. They applied a “hard varnish” to the type, printed this on the paper, then placed gold leaf on top of the varnish. When the gold leaf was brushed away, it would only remain on the printed text.37 This form of gilding was a printed version of a twelfth-century gilding method, chrysography, whereby gold leaf was laid on top of text written in an adhesive.38 The author-practitioner outlines this process on fol. 8r for “Lettering of gold on paper,” where he instructs how to write with fig tree milk, a sticky sap, and then lay gold leaf on top. He explains that after it has dried, the lettering can be rubbed with a cloth, leaving only the gold lettering.

Mordants used in Gilding

The author-practitioner’s use of wheat starch as a mordant is unusual. Starch is not mentioned in premodern comprehensive texts on gilding. From the twelfth to the sixteenth century in Western Europe, the most common mordants used in gilding on paper and parchment were glair (made from whipped egg white), gums, sizing glues, and gessos.39 In an anonymous fourteenth-century treatise, the Liber de coloribus, the author lists gypsum, hide glue, egg white, “brazil,” ocher, Apulian white, cinnabar, urine, gums, and pine sap as ingredients which, in different combinations, could serve as mordants for gilding.40 He does not mention amydon or starch as a possible mordant.

Starch paste is not discussed in writings on limning, as the painting of portrait miniatures was known in the late sixteenth century. The English limner Nicholas Hillard, in his Art of Limning (ca. 1600), instructs to use gum as a mordant for gold leaf.41 Thomas Purfoot, another contemporary limner, suggests using garlic, glair, gum arabic, fig milk, and snail mucus, but not starch.42 Thus, it is unlikely that the author-practitioner found this recipe in a contemporary source. Wheat starch mordants also do not appear in recipes from the Islamic tradition. Snail glue and fish glue were the two most common mordants for medieval Arabic manuscripts.43 Medieval Islamic illuminators did use starch glue, however, for making inks.44

The author-practitioner also employs more common mordants like glair and bole throughout the manuscript, though not in conjunction with gilding on paper or parchment. In addition, he describes other adhesives used in gilding: on fol. 8r, as mentioned above, he describes using fig milk, and on fol. 32r, he describes “layering gold in distemper” by means of “a batture, that is joiner’s glue tempered with water on the fire, moderately clear, mixed with very little honey.” This latter technique is used by “common painters & scribes” and is “undone in the rain.” Wheat starch glue and water are the only mordants that the author-practitioner explicitly recommends for gilding on paper and parchment. Thus, his use of starch glue—despite access to other mordants popular with illuminators—seems a conscious decision, or perhaps an exploratory process.

Other Uses of “Amydon” in Ms. Fr. 640

While starch glues do not seem to have been used for gilding, they were commonly used to adhere paper to paper or parchment to parchment.45 The French notary and compiler of craft recipes Jean le Begue notes in his 1431 text:

But if you wish to join paper only and not parchment, wheat-flour or powdered bread-crumbs mixed with pure water and slightly boiled is good for paper. But if you mix a little gum-arabic or whipped white of egg with it, it will do for parchment.46

This passage also shows that parchment requires a stronger adhesive than paper, which is consistent with the author-practitioner’s use of starch water on paper but not on parchment.

The author-practitioner did not specify glueing paper to paper with starch derivatives; however, he uses them for several other purposes. On fol. 20v the author-practitioner mentions wheat starch mixed with water to whiten the face, suggesting it in place of grinding a puffball mushroom with water for this purpose. A substance made with water and wheat starch might be ideal in both texture and color for this face-whitener because it retains its tacky texture without over-drying and cracking, and it has an opaque white color that does not cake or appear matte. Recipes for oil painting on taffeta also make use of starch water, for example, on fol. 42v in the recipe “Painting in oil on taffeta,” which suggests using starch water mixed with honey and alum water as an underlayer to prevent the oil “from running.”47 The author-practitioner also uses wheat starch to glue down kernels of grain in a vessel in order to effect a sleight of hand trick of magically moving a bushel of wheat from one vessel to another (fol. 35v).48

Perhaps the author-practitioner’s unusual usage of wheat starch as a mordant for gilding indicates that he viewed starch as an all-purpose adhesive, as these other uses are in recipes requiring an adhesive that is thin yet strong. Moreover, wheat starch adhesives can be made to different textures and clarity by adding water, without complete loss of their adhesive power.

Conclusion

While the author-practitioner’s use of wheat starch as a mordant in gilding appears to be unusual, reconstruction of the recipes on fols. 10v and 29v showed it to be an effective mordant (at least in the short term). After being burnished, the gilded papers and parchments achieved a smoothness and shine at least superficially similar to those in medieval and Renaissance illuminated manuscripts. The author-practitioner was likely aware of wheat starch pastes as adhesives for paper and parchment. Moreover, it may be significant that flour glues were employed by goldsmiths. For example, the twelfth-century text of Theophilus contains instructions for using a mixture of flour and water to hold together gems in gold settings before soldering.49 Being experienced in metalworking, the author-practitioner possibly extrapolated the use of a wheat based adhesive for gold in metalworking to an adhesive for gold on paper and parchment. Thus his use of this unusual mordant could indicate that he did not draw his recipe for this technique from another written source but instead extrapolated or extended processes he used in other domains of practice to a new yet related area.
