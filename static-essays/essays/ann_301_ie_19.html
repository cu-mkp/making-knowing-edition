The Manuscript Seen from Afar: A Computational Approach to Ms. Fr. 640

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/nnyc-gd81
← Back to Essays

Abstract

Using the abundant data made available by the Making and Knowing Project, this essay examines some of the rhetorical features of Ms. Fr. 640, with a focus on dispositio. It argues that, when applied to a single document, careful visualization of statistical data can promote a “synthetic” approach to texts. Applied to Ms. Fr. 640, these computational methods provide readers with tools to assess relations between the manuscript’s constitutive parts and its cohesion as a whole. In doing so, this essay contributes to broadening the scope of computer-assisted hermeneutics in data-rich editions.

The code underlying digital editions raises the prospect of radically new approaches to the text.1 This alluring vision has prompted numerous attempts at incorporating data science into the study of the written word. Indeed, not indifferent to the quantitative trend that transformed many of the social and natural sciences over the past decades, scholars in the humanities have pondered in turn how the statistical data collected from digital editions could inform their approach to textual analysis and, by extension, renew their discipline. The latter motive, in particular, galvanized literary historians to jump on the digital bandwagon and head for the hills, so to speak, from where they hoped to observe sceneries until then never perceived. Franco Moretti and Matthew Jockers are often cited as typical examples of this movement, respectively coining their approaches “distant reading” and “macroanalysis.”2

These approaches were meant, at least at their inception, to propose a radical departure from the tradition of literary criticism by tapping into the potential of digitization and statistics to vastly increase the scale of textual analysis. According to Jockers, the development of macro-analysis is in a sense comparable to that of macroeconomics, insofar as the two disciplines focus on aggregates rather than individual parts.3 Distant readers, in other words, think big. They are drawn to gargantuan corpora made of hundreds of texts, and tend to leave aside the older and more conventional scale of philology, namely the single text or the oeuvre. According to Franco Moretti, dealing with extreme scales has become the very signature of the digital humanities.4

Such taste for extremes can be misleading, however, as the notion of scale is relative and not always well defined. The literature of the Spanish Golden Age, for example, would make an indisputably lengthy corpus to examine. But in no way would a fraction of that corpus, say one limited to the works of Lope de Vega, the period’s most prolific author, be necessarily less daunting or difficult to apprehend by an individual using conventional methods. His comedies alone extend over fifteen volumes, and his complete works would no doubt fill an entire shelf. In fact, the main difference between the two examples is less in the length of the corpora than in their alleged scope: one would authorize scholars to discuss a single oeuvre, whereas the other would entitle them (assuming they devise a statistically representative sample of texts) to make claims about much broader literary and historical categories. This distinction between length and scope would suffice if one assumed that characters, words, and sentences work as reliable units of measurement across different textual media.

However, this assumption overlooks a third dimension of textual data, namely, its density.5 The density of a digital edition can be easily understood if one compares it to digital images. Some digital images use more pixels per square inch and record a wider spectrum of colors than others. Similarly, while some digital editions are made of unstructured plain-text files, others are formatted in a language that enables corpora to integrate much more information about the text, its material, linguistic, semantic, and genetic aspects, such as layout, structure, variants, marginal notes, paper, ink, watermarks, fonts, part of speech, lexical fields, etc.6 Crucially, variables such as density, scope, and length do not necessarily form a proportional relationship, as data-rich corpora can be smaller in scope, and/or length and yet provide considerably more information to process. As a consequence, many of the invisible patterns, trends, and structures that distant readers seek to uncover experimenting with very large corpora can also be pursued at smaller, more “anthropocentric” scales.

The question remains, however, as to what kind of scholarly insights statistical methods may yield at lesser magnitudes. Using the abundant and expertly-curated data made available by the Making and Knowing Project, this essay argues that, when applied to a single manuscript, visualization of statistical data promotes a synthetic approach to the text, by virtue of which readers can fathom the relations between the document as a whole and its constitutive parts. In this regard, BnF Ms. Fr. 640 is particularly germane, since the manuscript document is hard to grasp as a coherent ensemble. At first sight, the information the author-practitioner apparently compiled looks fragmented and randomly distributed. Yet, is this apparent lack of structure a sign of rhetorical neglect? In other words, is Ms. Fr. 640 approachable as a literary object: with its voices, intentions, addressees, and persuasive strategies? To answer these questions, I will first briefly situate early modern technical literature in its broader rhetorical context. Second, I will show how visualization methods based on the edition’s own metadata enable a synthetic approach to the manuscript that addresses some of the questions raised above. Third, I will argue that, while data-rich editions greatly facilitate this kind of synthetic interpretation, they should not rely on editorial markup or on algorithms alone but rather on a dialectic relation between the two.

Rhetoric, Tacit Knowledge, and the Development of Technical Literature

How does one communicate technical knowledge effectively? For many sixteenth-century practitioners, book learning may have appeared unsuited or at least poorly equipped to transmit know-how, since words alone can hardly convey the many intricacies and contingencies of practice.7 Even today, in practice-oriented settings, verbal instruction is rarely considered sufficient in and of itself if disconnected from the sensorial experience of the workshop: apprentices tend to learn the appropriate gestures from masters by seeing them in action, and practitioners like to supplement their understanding of materials with a visual, tactile, and olfactive approach.8 However, as ineffable as practical knowledge may be, the desire to lay it down on paper and to share it with a broader public has persisted across the centuries. In the early modern period, the development of the printing press only exacerbated these literary aspirations, accelerating the development of a best selling genre: the book of secrets.9 In Ms. Fr. 640, the author-practitioner provides some clues as to why such secrets should ultimately be set down in words. In the commonplaces he gathered with an eye, most likely, to composing the preface of a book, he explains that, by sharing the techniques he tested in his own workshop, his hope is to transmit this knowledge to new audiences and to benefit from a “common commerce of letters” with like-minded interlocutors.10 In the author-practitioner’s mind, then, the written word remains, in spite of its limitations, the primary channel through which a community of practitioners can thrive over time.

What looks like a publication project, however, never came to fruition, and the extant manuscript, on which the present edition is based, while probably containing most of its building blocks, hardly resembles a finished book. Indeed, contrary to most of the printed compilations of secrets consulted for this essay, whose copia is visibly contained by thematic boundaries, the entries of Ms. Fr. 640 do not seem to be arranged in a systematic way.11 A quick browse through the facsimile gives the reader a sense of how the entries contrast between one another, and follow in quick succession without reason or rhyme. On fol. 104v, for example, the author-practitioner deals with topics as varied as curing a dog’s mange, wax casting, goldsmithing, and building a musical instrument.

This seemingly nonchalant approach to the arrangement of ideas and arguments (dispositio), one of the central tenets of rhetoric, may reinforce the impression that practitioners, because of their uncertain social extraction, or because of their often limited exposure to the studia humanitatis, were unconcerned with questions of eloquence. The potter Bernard Palissy, in the preface to his Recepte véritable, makes a claim in this direction when he defines his own plain style as unrhetorical:

I am no Greek, Hebrew, poet, or rhetorician; just a simple and illiterate artisan. However, for that very reason, the topic at matter has no less virtue than if it were told by a more eloquent man. I prefer saying the truth in my rustic language, rather than lies in a rhetorical language.12

Disdainful rejections of rhetoric such as Palissy’s should be taken with a pinch of salt, however. For a start, and contrary to a persistent belief, the art of “saying well” (ars bene dicendi) was not entirely reserved to the privilege of an elite. Many of its principles had long permeated the different strata of society, as manuals of eloquence were widely available in the vernacular and provided a very practical kind of knowledge in the contexts of letter writing, preaching, or sustaining polite conversations, to name but a few.13 In many parts of Europe, furthermore, verbal jousting and poetic eloquence were sources of civic pride. In cities like Toulouse, where the author-practitioner lived, these arts were celebrated in yearly contests and festivities by a diverse population who regarded them as major sources of entertainment.14 Probably influenced by Pierre de la Ramée’s radical reformation of rhetoric, Palissy’s captatio benevolentiae uses the conventional display of modesty that one would expect in a preface, but he does so to present rhetoric, not as the ancient art of civic conversation that many humanists cherished, but as a technique of verbal embellishment at best or, at worst, as an elitist art of deception.

Not all technical writers viewed eloquence this way, however. Girolamo Ruscelli, for example, author of immensely popular pseudonymous books of secrets, was also deeply committed to promoting vernacular language and literature.15 He composed several books on the subject and edited important collections of lyric poetry, such as the complete works of Vittoria Colonna.16 He also took part in lively controversies regarding the questione della lingua; a debate regarding the place of vernaculars in culture and society.17 Another example is Leonardo Fioravanti, an empirical doctor who relied extensively on the printing press to promote his unconventional approach to medicine, inspired by alchemy.18 Fioravanti was keenly aware of the rhetorical challenges posed by the transmission of technical knowledge. In his Capricci medicinali, a book of medical secrets, he explains that the subject at hand necessitated “a new way of writing” in which “beautiful proofs” have to be complemented with indisputable ones drawn from “hands-on experience.”19 Likewise, Leonardo da Vinci’s well-known emphasis on experience should not be read as an outright rejection of all things literary, but rather as the observation that ancient models of eloquence are seldom adapted to technical and empirical matters, and that these matters ultimately require new wordsmiths. In one of his oft-quoted manuscript prefaces, he warns readers against presumptuous naysayers who:

will claim that, since I do not have a literary background, I am incapable of saying well what I want to cover. Yet these people don’t know that my writing is drawn from experience rather than from someone else’s words. Experience has been the teacher of those who used to write well, and thus I choose her as my teacher, and I will plead to her every time.20

Although Leonardo recognizes that he is no expert in classical culture, he does not turn his back on literature per se, nor does he renounce his ambition to communicate effectively. If Leonardo relied on literary models, these belonged mostly to the vernacular tradition and the mercantile world, which included the Tuscan ricordanze books, records of important things merchants and their families ought to remember, and the zibaldoni, working notebooks containing miscellaneous topics and commonplaces.21 Leonardo’s manuscripts suggest, in fact, a great familiarity with the short form: writings characterized by their brevity and portability, such as poems, jokes, riddles, recipes, fables, prophecies, and aphorisms. These short forms circulated orally, in letters, and in printed compilations. Readers would also write down selections in their own commonplace books, and in this sense such books are at the crossroads of public and private writing. On the one hand, they do not seem to follow a predetermined order nor a clear logical structure, since their main vocation is to record diverse things as they come to hand, mostly for mnemonic purposes. Yet on the other hand, they may contain fragments of potential publications, experimental writings, including, in the cases of Leonardo’s manuscripts and Ms. Fr. 640, draft prefaces for prospective books. They betray, sometimes, attempts at organizing information according to a certain logic. In the section below, I will show that a data-driven approach to the text lets us visualize this textual ambivalence, and that this visualization ultimately helps to understand the manuscript, not only as a random repository of short forms, but potentially also as an organic whole.

Visualizing Copia

Taking a step back from Ms. Fr. 640, one can accurately picture the way information is distributed in the document. Scatterplots, in this regard, offer a straightforward method to represent the manuscript (Fig. 1).22 Primarily used to establish a correlation between two variables, and eventually to draw predictive models, scatterplots are also well suited for the more modest purpose of producing basic visual abstractions of written documents in a familiar and legible format. In this Cartesian coordinate system, the x-axis represents the manuscript’s folios from 1r to 170v. The y-axis indicates the length of each entry, which is measured in words. Fig. 1 shows that the majority of entries average a length of less than 134 words. They constitute, so to speak, the “norm” in this manuscript, and typically consist in brief procedural descriptions. More importantly, while the “norm” is rather stable throughout the manuscript, the proportion of exceptionally longer entries increases toward the last third of the document, especially those between the average (134) and maximal (2,721) word count per entry. This trend, while not impossible to guess reading the text in a conventional way, would be hard to communicate without this type of visual evidence. Scatterplots offer, in this sense, a standard visual language to represent and discuss some of the structural features of a text. By adding a third variable, moreover, they allow scholars to test some hypothetical correlations. Fig. 2, for example, highlights with blue triangles entries that have been categorized as “Casting” by the Making and Knowing Project editorial team. This scatterplot shows that entries about casting remain for the most part grouped into distinguishable clusters—particularly around fols. 12r–v, 28v–30r, 80v–93r and 106r–170r.

The same can be observed for categories such as “Painting” (Fig. 3), which concentrates around one main cluster (fols. 56v–66r) and three more smaller groups (fols. 6r–13v, 29v–32r, 39v–43r), and “Arms and Armor” (Fig. 4), which also gathers in three major groups (fols. 8r, 17r–28r, 43v–45v).

Smaller and more dispersed categories such as “Varnish” are more difficult to visualize with the same type of scatterplots. A logarithmic scale on the y-axis, however, enables us, here again, to identify smaller groups (fols. 3r–4v, 73v–74v, 77r–78v) (Fig. 5).

The clusters mentioned above, which represent together about 75% of the manuscript’s word length, disprove that Ms. Fr. 640 is entirely devoid of structure. The author-practitioner, in fact, disposed most of these entries around specific themes.23 Admittedly, several entries are scattered for no apparent reason. Part of this fuzziness is due, perhaps, to the inherent difficulty for modern readers to categorize these entries according to criteria that would entirely match those of the author-practitioner.24 Another reason is that the author-practitioner’s concern for thematic coherence is likely to have been counterbalanced by more practical matters during the different stages of composition of the manuscript, such as the changing availability of his sources or, even more prosaically, the lack of space on a given folio. These scatterplots, in fact, further highlight the dual nature of Ms. Fr. 640, both a commonplace book recording different techniques from different sources, and a publication in the making.

This leads us to another important third variable one can visualize, this time with a bubble chart (Fig. 6). This variant of the scatterplot displays, in addition to the size of each entry, the proportion of marginal annotations for each entry. Marginal annotations concern a relatively small part of the manuscript (27% of entries). In some cases, entire entries (>90%) are on the margin (on fols. 24v, 64r, 83v, and 106v, for example). These have been added subsequently, often because of their thematic proximity with neighboring entries. But in most cases, bubbles between 25% and 80% signal the inclusion of additional instructions and information; details about specific procedures, instruments, methods and ingredients, but also feedback from previous experimentation. Interestingly, these mid-sized bubbles are, once again, situated for the most part in the last third of the manuscript. “Casting,” in fact, is not only the dominating topic in Ms. Fr. 640, but also the one that attracts the greatest number of annotations, as 42% of entries related to this topic include marginal notes, with an average proportion of 25%—more than any other category. Therefore, the reason that “Casting” entries tend to be lengthier than other entries is, in all likelihood, that the author-practitioner subjected them to a more elaborate process of composition.

Word count, position in the manuscript, frequency and proportion of annotations, all these variables concur in highlighting the particular status of casting techniques. Yet, how does “Casting” exactly compare with other categories?

Seeing Voices

While scatterplots are effective in isolating specific categories, they do not perform as well in more comparative perspectives. Treemapping, on the other hand, excels at visualizing the whole text with its constitutive parts (Fig. 7). In this diagram, each category is represented as a rectangular surface sized proportionally to its respective word count. In addition to displaying the relative size of each category, this particular treemap also indicates their average length with scaled colors. In doing so, the figure gives an immediate glimpse of the manuscript’s themes, their proportion, and their respective degree of fragmentation. It reveals the important contrasts that separate some of these categories in one quick glance. “Printing” and “Arms and Armor,” for example, distinguish themselves for their particularly lengthy entries, which respectively average 299 and 226 words, whereas entries related to “Medicine,” average a mere 45 words.

Correlation matrices shed a more comprehensive light on the formal and semantic contrasts between categories (Fig. 8, Fig. 9).25 Represented as a heatmap, the matrix shows the correlation coefficients between two families of variables: the tags that the editorial team included in order to signal the most important semantic, material, and linguistic features of the text; and the categories to which entries were assigned. The heatmap indicates, for example, a strong correlation between the lexical fields of weaponry and measurements (Fig. 8). This lexical interdependence is of course to be expected, since entries devoted to warfare consist in detailed descriptions of a wide variety of guns and cannons, of their caliber, range, and weight.

Yet, besides stating the obvious, the matrix also makes clear that entries categorized as “Arms and Armor” are likely to include more “author definitions” and more references to professions than any other category (Fig. 9).26 Therefore, entries about “Arms and Armor” not only tend to be grouped in specific sections of the manuscript, as I have shown above with Fig. 4, but they also display a unique combination of formal and semantic characteristics, as if they constituted a voice of their own. Scholars have in fact noted that, while such entries provide a precise characterization of the weapons that arsenals of the time could produce, they seldom dwell on the details of their manufacturing process. According to Pascal Brioist, this probably suggests that access to the arsenal of Toulouse was denied to the author-practitioner.27 Yet, that these entries are more descriptive than prescriptive also provides some clues as to what audience the author-practitioner may have been targeting. Indeed, Colin Debuiche and Sarah Muñoz argue that such descriptive entries would likely be addressed to notables from the city of Toulouse, or to political advisers working for the King. Quoting Philippe de Béthune, the first known owner of Ms. Fr. 640, Debuiche suggests that these entries met the requirements of an audience concerned with developing “political prudence” with a better understanding of the technical and productive aspects of war and, more generally, of the kingdom’s economy.28

While “Arms and Armor” is characterized by an extensive nomenclature and a descriptive tone that fulfills the expectations of a particular audience, other categories imitate long established genres of technical writing.29 This is the case of “Medicine” which, in addition to its concision, greatly contrasts with the categories mentioned above. In the matrix, “Medicine” appears as a neat category that predictably develops the semantic fields of body parts, plants, and to a lesser extent, of time. More interestingly, the semantic field of medicine is not correlated with deletions, while its corresponding entries are completely devoid of marginal annotations (Fig. 8).30 These peculiar characteristics are owing to the fact that many of these recipes had already been employed by the author-practitioner himself, or sourced from some authoritative acquaintance.31 Medical recipes circulated from person to person in a wide variety of social contexts, their form was expected to be short enough to remain portable. The concision and relative spotlessness of these entries suggests that the author-practitioner did not depart from this rule, preserving their transactional quality, sometimes even at the expense of comprehensiveness.

In contrast, entries about “Casting,” as the treemap attests (Fig. 7), stand out for their expansiveness. The matrix indicates, moreover, that this category has the greatest propensity to include deletions (Fig. 9). Such correlation is consistent with my observations regarding annotations in the previous section of this essay (Fig. 6). It provides visual evidence that entries about casting are the result of a particularly wrought writing process, one that implied back and forth movements from the manuscript to manual activity in the workshop. “Casting,” in other words, may be regarded as the author-practitioner’s core contribution to that “commerce of letters” he evoked in his tentative preface; not only because of the preponderant place of the entries in the manuscript but also because their redaction implied a greater investment in the subject matter and its verbal expression.

Also worthy of attention is “Varnish” which, contrary to the examples mentioned above, does not denote any significant correlation with other annotated features of the manuscript (Fig. 9). This does not mean that, as a category, “Varnish” is unremarkable, but rather that the selection of variables used for this research may not suffice to reveal—both quantitatively and qualitatively—its particular features, and how it connects with the rest of the manuscript.

Toward a Fusion of Digital Horizons

As I have shown in the treemap (Fig. 7) and in the logarithmic scatterplot (Fig. 5), “Varnish” represents a relatively small and dispersed category in the manuscript. As a consequence, its salient traits are difficult to delineate. Categories like “Varnish,” in fact, may very well show the limits of an approach exclusively based on the edition’s own curated metadata.

If, as I explained in the introduction, the editorial annotation of digital texts presents indisputable advantages over the automated approach privileged by distant readers, the former still benefits from the latter. According to this complementary approach, the edition’s metadata is temporarily foregone in order to resort to a different dataset produced this time by Natural Language Processing algorithms. Although imperfect, these algorithms have the potential to annotate every single word in the text.32 As a result, one can focus on a wider range of linguistic patterns: trends regarding specific uses of a word, or of any kind of lexical fields, spellings, and parts of speech.
Such trends are easy to visualize on a cumulative frequency plot, as they are based on the same Cartesian coordinate system used in the scatterplots shown above. The main difference is that the x-axis does not represent the manuscript’s folios but its cumulative word count. The y-axis, on the other hand, records the cumulative number of occurrences in the manuscript. Cumulative frequency plots reflect consistent uses of words with smooth positive slopes—comparable to linear functions. Conversely, they can signal irregularities with sharp rises or sudden plateaux. Through this method, one can detect subtle evolutions in the way authors express themselves. For example, the cumulative frequencies of singular masculine and feminine determinate articles, le and la, signal diverging patterns that would have been otherwise imperceptible without the aid of a computer (Fig. 10).33

The same method can also reveal abrupt and unexpected changes such as the irruption of alternative spellings (Fig. 11). The pronoun ung (84 occurrences), for example, a variant of the much more frequent un (1,329 occurrences), appears sporadically in the manuscript (notably with the hands C and D, on fols. 20r and 73r–79v). This is also the case of aveq (37 occurrences), an alternative spelling of avecq (1,059 occurrences). Fig. 11 shows that significant changes in orthography often correspond to a change of hands in the manuscript.

This would make sense, after all, as scribes presumably wrote what was being orally dictated to them using their own orthography, like verny instead of the prevalent vernis, or like the phonetic spelling of the Latin terra merita into terroy merita (hand E on fol. 73r).34 Yet these scribes not only used different orthographies and calligraphies, but sometimes also addressed the reader as vous, instead of the second-person singular tu (the dominating personal deixis with 1,250 occurrences) and it is unclear why, if they were merely writing down what was dictated to them, they would also change personal deixis.35

Perhaps more importantly, all the aforementioned anomalies intersect in a way that is clear and statistically significant on fols. 73r–79v, and, while the entries from this particular passage belong to a wide variety of smaller categories, all of them seem to converge under a broader theme, as many species of the same genus. Indeed, be they categorized as “Varnish,” “Wood and its Coloring,” “Metal Process,” “Wax Process,” “Corrosives,” or “Painting,” these entries ultimately are related to the application of color to surfaces. They constitute a thematic cluster in its own right that my initial approach had not detected.

In that respect, besides corroborating the idea of a thematically structured manuscript, this method sheds light on another factor of thematic coherence that may explain in part some of the stylistic and formal divergences between categories, namely the intervention of specialized contributors in the manuscript: in this case, perhaps a collaborator from the author-practitioner’s workshop who addressed the latter as vous, an acquaintance in the city of Toulouse who specialized in painting and who was invited to share some of his techniques for the occasion, or a particular written source read aloud by the author-practitioner to his amanuensis.

Crucially, when used in combination, the two methods enable us to make the most of data-rich editions, while remaining aware of the hermeneutic nature of the enterprise. Indeed, like any other human endeavor, the digital annotations provided by the editorial team fall into an interpretive horizon. The automated approach I used in this part of the essay actually helps to refine this horizon, adapting it to specific research questions.

Conclusion

Ms. Fr. 640 may look like a chaotic and fragmented zibaldone. However, by cross-referencing the edition’s semantic, structural, and formal tags, my essay shows that this fragmentary feel to the manuscript can be misleading. The different themes and styles, the diversity of spellings and personal deixes, the particular distribution of deletions, annotations and handwritings, all denote a polyphony in the text, based on a plurality of sources, be they written or oral. Yet the manuscript is not exactly a zibaldone, but rather a compilation of entries that gather first- and second-hand information, for the most part around some clear thematic clusters. This first attempt at organizing entries may indicate, along with the prologue draft, that the manuscript ultimately constitutes the basis on which a printed book might have emerged. Admittedly, some parts of the manuscript are less polished than others, sometimes to such a degree that one may wonder whether this compilation did not itself suffer, at some point, from a process of “zibaldonization,” so to speak. The fact that most deletions and annotations appear in the last part of the manuscript may reinforce this impression; as if the author-practitioner, caught up in the difficulties, not only of the matter at hand but also of its verbal expression, was progressively bogged down by it and eventually stumbled at the finish line. Hypotheses of that nature are facilitated by the holistic perspective that data visualization makes possible. This type of perspective, which I named here “literary synthesis,” is particularly useful in the study of complex manuscript sources like Ms. Fr. 640. Contrary to literary analysis, which tends to rely on a patchy and fragmented approach to the text, literary synthesis ponders the relations that such fragments maintain with each other and with the document as a whole. Conventional literary analyses tend to overlook synthetic questions, since the global trends and patterns that data-driven approaches to the text reveal are often invisible to the naked eye. They can, admittedly, be intuited, and a scholar may use his or her authority to communicate this impression. Yet data visualization lets us engage critically with this intuition, and confront it with empirical evidence. It also enables us to communicate this perspective easily and to convey difficult notions to a wider audience. Such visualizations remain an interpretation of the text, however, as the data provided by the editors of Ms. Fr. 640 originate from a specific horizon. It is therefore important to keep framing such data-driven approaches as a form of computer-assisted hermeneutics, and not as an objective science. The dual technique I exposed in the final part of the essay allows us, in this sense, to establish a dialogue with this particular horizon in order to refine it according to our specific, but evolving, research questions.
