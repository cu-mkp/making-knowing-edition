The Drawings in BnF Ms. Fr. 640

Sietske Fransen

Introduction

Ms. Fr. 640 is a manuscript that contains drawings that could be called technical images—as we can also see, for example, in Leonardo da Vinci’s notebooks with drawings of machines. Leonardo used his notebooks as thinking spaces to draw from life, from observation, but also to experiment on paper with machines that did not yet exist (Fig. 1). As various scholars have argued, Leonardo saw a sheet of paper, a page, as a unity, and he used its entirety to experiment on paper in a combination of images and words.1 The texts in his manuscripts sometimes annotate the drawings, and sometimes the texts are actually unrelated to the images. Other early modern artists, such as Albrecht Dürer, Maarten van Heemskerck, and Michelangelo kept notebooks in which they jotted down visually their observations and thoughts about their daily lives. These highly skilled artists created sketchbooks, filled with drawings and sketches from which (art) historians have been able to learn a lot about the drawing and learning practices of artists.2 However, it was not only artists who kept notebooks; all kinds of practitioners and students kept them, and all these practices can be bundled together as part of an early modern note-taking tradition. Putting observations, experiences, and teaching notes onto paper in private notebooks was a new practice in the early modern period: paper was more readily available than before, and the literacy rate had gone up exponentially after the late Middle Ages.3 Note-taking was more involved than the word might assume. Note-taking in the early modern period did not just mean to write down the first thing that came to one’s mind; it often included, in some way or another, the collation or organization of information. Recipe books and how-to books can thus also be characterized as notebooks. Notebooks are often full of words but may also include drawings, tables, and diagrams—in the margins or in the main body of the text.4

How, then, can we think about the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 and his writings about artistic and even specifically painterly practices? The manuscript is part of this note-taking tradition in which we find sketches and drawings as part of a larger written narrative. The author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 not only wrote his text, he also drew images in his manuscript. In this essay I will consider several questions about these images: How can we characterize these images? What was the aim of these illustrations, how do they relate to the texts and practices captured by the author-practitioner, and how can we place them in the context of other late sixteenth-century illustrated manuscripts and printed texts? And can the images tell us something about the manuscript and its maker that the text on its own cannot?

Epistemic Images

The drawings that we find in Ms. Fr. 640 were not meant to be artworks, they were meant to be somehow utilitarian. Images of this kind have been called many things: epistemic, technical, knowing, scientific. None of these terms is perfect as they are either anachronistic (scientific), too abstract (epistemic), too restrictive (technical), or assume too much agency (knowing). Since the 1990s, a growing literature has addressed this type of image.5 While the initial discussion focused on the connection and break between our modern understanding of art versus science, the discussion shifted to categorizing these images in the early 2000s.6 This has resulted in introducing a whole new corpus of images to historians of art and science while categorizing them as a genre. Over the past ten years, scholars have moved beyond these initial explorations of the topic by looking at practices around image making in the context of knowledge production.7 The Making and Knowing Project fits very well with this latter approach, attempting to understand the images of the manuscript not just as free-standing entities but how they functioned for the author-practitioner in recording his recipes and his experiences. This approach prompts us to ask questions such as: what did these illustrations add to the information captured in words? When did the author-practitioner add illustrations, and what kind of information did they represent or collect?

A General Description of the Images in Ms. Fr. 640

Ms. Fr. 640 contains many drawings. Of the 170 folios, ca. 90 leaves carry images, with a total of approximately 150 images. This means that about a quarter of the bifolia in the manuscript contain images. They are not evenly divided over the manuscript and differ significantly in size, as we will see in more detail below. Taken all together, this signifies that the manuscript is not a highly illustrated manuscript. We find the first image on fol. 3v (Fig. 2), the following one on fol. 6v, another one on fol. 14r, and another on fol. 16r. Then we find a high concentration of mainly diagrammatic images on fols. 19v–28r. The total number of images depends on how they are counted. I took, for example, the small drawings of tools and equipment on fol. 94v as one image. Apart from images, there are also various signs throughout the manuscript that show where to continue reading (for example on fol. 114r) that I have counted as symbols rather than images and of which there are nineteen. The last symbol on the verso side of the last folio of the manuscript does not refer to anything, hence the unequal number of symbols.

Most of the drawings are made in ink of the same color as the text, making it likely that the drawings were made at the time of writing by the same person. This would indicate that the drawings were not made by a separate draftsman or artist but by the author-practitioner of the manuscript himself. Despite the fact that there are different hands in the manuscript, the drawings appear on pages of the main scribal hand and thus seem to be connected directly to the author-practitioner. Even the drawing of a pipe on fol. 77r (Fig. 3) is in a part of the text written by the author-practitioner, even though it falls within a larger section (fols. 73r–79v) that was written in another hand. All of this shows that the practice of drawing alongside the text was of particular interest to the author-practitioner for the understanding of his text.

The Position of the Drawings

The images are equally spread over the recto and verso sides of the pages. The images occur as part of the main text as well as in the margins. Overall, we find slightly more drawings in the margins than in the main body of the page, but there is a shift in the manuscript: while there are more drawings as part of the main body of the text in the first half of the manuscript, marginal drawings become more abundant in the second half. According to Marc Smith’s observations, the author-practitioner used a set of pages that was already bound as the manuscript/book we can now read.8 When looking at the place of the text on the page in the digital edition, we see no difference between a recto and a verso side in terms of the mise-en-page. Practically, this means that the text is always positioned a few centimeters from the left edge of the page, continuing to the far right edge of the paper. This leaves the broader margin always on the left side of the text, unrelated to whether it is on the front or back side of the page. There are an equal number of drawings in the margins on recto sides as there are on the verso sides, so there does not seem to have been a preferred side of the paper for the author-practitioner. This might imply that the author-practitioner was right-handed, as a left-handed draftsman might have had difficulties drawing in the left margin of the recto page if the sheets were already bound. The distribution of the images might also indicate that the manuscript was not strongly bound, as noted by Alexis Hagadorn, and thus easy to open completely.9

On the basis of the placement of the drawings, we can also say something about the order in which Ms. Fr. 640 was written. Take for example fol. 113r (Fig. 4), where we find three small drawings. Two are placed within the main text, and the third one can be found in the middle of the marginal text on the left side of the page. However, this figure, “un petit instrument recourbe,” or “a small curved instrument,” is mentioned in the main text just under the other two images. The text in the margin has nothing to do with this third image that is located there. This means that the main text and its drawings, including the one in the margin, were written and drawn first and that the marginal note was written later, around the already present images. A similar example, where the drawing in the margin is also directly connected to main text and was drawn at the same time, can be found on fol. 135r (Fig. 5). Although the image seems to be part of the marginal note, it has nothing to do with the content of the text in the margin.

The opposite is the case on fol. 125r (Fig. 6), where the small drawing seems to be part of the main text. However, the marginal note is so long that it runs both above and below the first paragraph of main text and even entirely around and across the heading of the next section (“Plastre”). The drawing here is actually part of the marginal text and was drawn together with that note. If you look closely, you can also see a slight difference in color of ink between the main text and the marginal text and drawing. This indicates a difference in ink and, therefore, most likely different moments in which the author-practitioner wrote the main text and the marginal note plus image. The author-practitioner even drew a box around the first paragraph of the main text; he must have realized it was hard to distinguish the text from the comments.

In short, the position of the images does not make them more or less important in their own right. And as readers of Ms. Fr. 640, we have to be careful to interpret the images correctly as either part of the main text or the marginal notes. The position of the text and images can tell us something about the order in which the author-practitioner compiled his manuscript and used the spaces on his pages.

What Do the Images Do?

In her essay on the images in the manuscript, Sophie Macomber has connected the existing images to the topical categories of Ms. Fr. 640 (Fig. 7).10 Of the twenty-six categories recognized by the Making and Knowing Team (see Smith, “An Introduction to Ms. Fr. 640 and Its Author-Practitioner”; and “Entry Categories” in the Resources section), twenty categories have images.11 About half of the images are drawn to accompany entries on casting.

By discussing some of the images, I will try to understand how the images could have functioned for the author-practitioner and/or his readers. Images in how-to texts can have a variety of functions, as I have shown elsewhere on the basis of the Royal Society’s seventeenth-century archive of drawings.12 Images can, for example, form an integral part of an argument (which is especially true for mathematical diagrams), function as records of events (abnormal phenomena, observations, etc.), be references for size or likeness, or form parts of instructions given in texts.

The images in Ms. Fr. 640 depict a whole range of different things, from cannon balls, to tools, to mathematical figures, as well as all kinds of preparations for molding and casting. More than eighty percent of the figures in the manuscript are figurative, meaning that they depict something that is derived from a real object and thus represents this object in some way. The quality and detail of these representations vary, as we will see below. A small number of images is more diagrammatic—for example, in the entries about drawing mathematical figures and the entry about the sizes of cannon balls. A handful of images show a structure—for example, the structure of cross-hatching or the structure of dragon’s blood on fol. 29v (Fig. 8).

Diagrammatic Images

The manuscript contains several entries about drawing and printing techniques—for example, how to draw perspective. A small related entry is the one on drawing “Mathematical figures without ruler and compass” on fol. 19v. The author-practitioner writes:

fold your paper such that the fold gives you a straight line, at the required distance from which, trace a point or a line & saturate it well with ink. Then fold again the paper & rub it on the back & it will print however much you have made. In that way, A is the line without ink which the paper fold gives you, B is the line you have traced, C is the one which is printed. (Fig. 9).

As Fig. 9 shows in detail, the four images or diagrams accompany the text to exemplify the description. In all images, “a” is the line along which the paper should be folded, “b” signifies the well-inked line that is to be traced by the image maker, and line “c” then shows the copied or printed line that should appear after pressing it on the wet ink. It is interesting to see that the author-practitioner has clearly not used his own method of making the figures here, as the two sides are in all cases very different and were not printed from the wet ink by folding the paper. The function of these images is in this case instructive, and they accompany and exemplify a textual description. The author-practitioner makes clear reference to the images in the text, and the images are really an extension of the textual description. It is worth noting that, although the form is mathematical and abstract, we do not learn anything about the use of the mathematical figures in themselves.

A second example of a set of diagrammatic images is the many cannon balls depicted by the author-practitioner on fols. 22r and 26r–27v (Fig. 10).

Sarah Muñoz has already provided some context about the artillery interests of the author-practitioner within the local context.13 In the text on fol. 24r, we find unpleasantly detailed descriptions of the effects of cannon balls when they are made according to the method described:

The metal of a large bell is made with 3 quintals of rosette & twenty or xxv lb of tin. Each grenade must weigh four to six lb. And in order to throw them, it is necessary that they be full of powder mixed with coarsely pestled glass. And for a quarter lb of powder, one needs half an ounce of glass which is put with it, in order that on the face or places where the powder will have its effect, the wounds will be all the worse for it.

The metal (i.e., metal, a particular copper alloy) for the cannons needs to be sourced, and apparently church bells are especially convenient. With a large bell weighing 20–25 lb and a grenade 4–6 lb, one can make four to six balls out of one church bell. While the weights are mentioned in the text, as are the lengths of several types of cannon, the sizes of the balls are shown in the images drawn. Fol. 22r carries a “cannon ball, weighing 40 lb, having 12 lines, king’s foot.” The image, however, has been discounted by the author-practitioner as “a little too big,” and he points the reader to the following one, several folios further, where the text indeed helps the reader to understand that “this is the true measure of the caliber, but the borer always takes away a little more” (fol. 26r). It looks like the author-practitioner wanted to indicate very precisely the sizes of cannon balls for the various cannons and guns that were used at the time. The circles are abstract and simple representations of the broadest circumference of the ball, which could then be used, presumably, as the size for a mold for making metal balls. On fol. 27v the author-practitioner goes further in not only indicating the sizes of the balls by the names of the particular guns and cannons they are for but also naming equivalents—for example, a “falconet of 4 quintals, alternatively passe-volant, is 8 pans long”—thus including the different names for the types of artillery that were used around his place of working so that there might not be any confusion.

The size of cannonballs was important precision work. Special tools for measuring the caliber of cannons and firearms as well as shot existed—see, for example, a caliper compass (Fig. 11) and a gunner’s rule (Fig. 12) both from Museo Galileo in Florence, Italy. From several early modern books on artillery practices, we can deduce that the problem of having the diameter and needing to calculate the circumference was a common one. In John Seller’s The Sea-Gunner: Shewing the Practical Part of Gunnery, as it is Used at Sea (1691), for example, in the chapter on “Arithmetical problems appertaining to the Art of Gunnery,” the first problem reads, “The diameter of a Circle being given, to find the Circumference.”14 Hence, part of a gunner’s practical knowledge, and certainly also part of a founder’s, was to be able to use a given diameter for the making of a cannon ball. In most printed books on the subject from the seventeenth century, calculation tables were published to help artisans quickly find correct sizes and measurements.15

In Ms. Fr. 640 the depictions of the diameters of cannon balls seem to have a visual function only, to recognize sizes. Apart from the text in the circles themselves, the author-practitioner does not refer to the images in any other part of the text.

Figurative Images

In contrast with the images of cannon balls that are not referred to in the text, in the entry on “Fountains,” on fol. 80r, text and image go hand-in-hand (Fig. 13).

If you wish to make water rise higher than its source, make a pipe descend as in A to B. in order to give it push, then from B to C make the pipe ascend again, which should not quite attain the height of the source, which is represented to you by this horizontal line, then make the pipe descend again from C to D, then raise it again, higher than the line showing the height of the source. And do this successively until you have reached the desired height, heeding nonetheless that the length of the descending pipe should be twice as long as the ascending pipe. 

If we read the text without its references to the image, it is as follows: “If you wish to make water rise higher than its source, make a pipe descend. In order to give it push make the pipe ascend again, which should not quite attain the height of the source, then make the pipe descend again, then raise it again, higher than the height of the source. And do this successively until you have reached the desired height, heeding nonetheless that the length of the descending pipe should be twice as long as the ascending pipe.” An experienced aqueduct builder would probably understand immediately how the mechanism functions, but for any other reader the illustration is needed to visualize the points made by the author-practitioner. Turning this around, the image can be read without the text to get the general idea of the mechanism, however, the final sentence about the lengths of the descending and ascending pipes (essential for the process) is not visible in the drawing. We can thus say that, in this case, image and text are interwoven and need each other to be fully understandable.

The additional image immediately to the left is a representation of what is now called Robert Boyle’s perpetual flask. Not only is its modern name misleading, clearly post-dating its invention, but this image—in contrast to the image just discussed—gives the reader no clear indication of how to use it. The description reads: “This cornet folded back on itself also shows you a perpetual fountain which you can fit into some rock, pulling the water with which it is filled by the tip marked E, by sucking & breathing in.” Here the image is also described and somewhat explained in the text, but without reading the text, even less of the image can be understood. Hence, both images work hand-in-hand with the text and, as such, image and text in these cases should be interpreted together.

Let us now consider the illustrations in the many entries on lifecasting, the largest topic in Ms. Fr. 640. The images that are part of the entries on making molds for casting are all figurative, and they contain information about the making of clay slabs and walls for plaster molds, the arrangement of dead animals on the clay for lifecasting, the positioning of casting channels in the mold, etc. All these images are highly pragmatic in character. Several of the entries for cast making have been reconstructed by students and collaborators on the Making and Knowing Project. As we can see, for example, in essays by Pamela Smith and Fabien Noirot, the images provide essential information for the process of making casts.16 The images illustrate the casting infrastructure, showing the gates one needs to add to the mold so that the material that will be used to make the cast can enter the mold (Fig. 14, Fig. 15, Fig. 16).

The accompanying text does not give any information on how to position the gates in any of the three cases shown in Fig. 13. The author-practitioner gives details about the size of the gates—“very wide […] [but] not be too thick” (fol. 127r), or “Your gate must be very thin at the entrance of the animal & of the thickness of a knife” (fol. 124v)—but the actual placing of the gates can only be seen in the pictures. With a third of the manuscript filled with information about making casts, it seems that these images are especially valuable for the author-practitioner: they register information that is difficult to capture in words but is essential for the success of the process. In these images we can see how text and image work together in a very efficient way, not duplicating information but, on the contrary, adding details that can only be captured in an image.

Conclusion

All the images in Ms. Fr. 640, whether figurative or diagrammatic, help the reader to do things: to easily draw diagrams, to measure cannon balls, to make beautiful casts. The images were not drawn to visually impress, or for purely aesthetic reasons, but rather as information carriers, to give the author-practitioner and his readers more information than could be covered in only a narrative text. The images are thus an integral part of the manuscript and its practical entries.

We could say, therefore, that the author-practitioner was a person who took notes in both verbal and visual form, someone who related to information in a variety of ways. And as we have seen in the entries in Ms. Fr. 640 and in the many reconstructions that the Making and Knowing Project has made and registered in Secrets of Craft and Nature, he was pragmatic, and he was learning, collecting, and understanding information about processes of making during his writing. In his manuscript he included as many tiny images as he needed to capture all these parts of the puzzle. This was much more possible in manuscript than it would have been in print at the time, thus his manuscript gave him the freedom to include visual tools and reminders to himself that no printed book would have given him.
