Abstract

In the entry “Varnish resistant to water” on fol. 31r of BnF Ms. Fr. 640, the author-practitioner describes what conservators today recognize as two types of varnish: spirit-based and oil-based. Moreover, he claims that the former resist the rain while latter do not. An experiment was designed to test these claims, informed by previous canvas preparation and varnish making experiments in the Making and Knowing lab. A test canvas, painted with oil paints and covered with four varnishes arranged side by side, was exposed to the elements May 30–Oct 18, 2018, and then compared to a control version kept indoors. Preliminary results suggest that the spirit varnishes are indeed better able to withstand rain than their oil-based counterparts; however, an unvarnished strip of the test canvas faired nearly as well as the spirit varnish, suggesting that the “Varnish resistant to water” may not have been intended to cover oil paint but another medium—perhaps distemper. Additional experiments are needed.

In sixteenth-century Europe, civic life was characterized by spectacle and display. Artists found reliable work in the preparation of vibrant ephemeral architecture, floats, banners, placards, pennants, puppets, and other materials that could embellish a city and its citizenry on special occasions and across multi-day celebrations1 (Fig. 1). Some of these creations were so acclaimed that they were put in storage after use in order to be repurposed in future celebrations.2 Durability was a concern, and one of the biggest threats to durability was the weather. Despite what some “official” records might suggest, many a parade was rained upon, and sometimes heavily.3 This unfortunate fact seems to underpin the entry “Varnish resistant to water” on fol. 31r of BnF Ms. Fr. 640. In its entirety, the entry reads:

Flanders varnish, made with turpentine & oil of turpentine or mastic, can come off and does not hold up in the rain. But that made with white walnut oil, as you have done, holds in the rain [and] is very clear & beautiful & dries soon. This is why one uses this one for painted banners & signs that one carries in the rain.

These few sentences convey several important points. First, water resistance was a desired feature of some artworks. Second, painters could achieve water resistance by selecting an appropriate varnish as a top coat. And third, “Flanders varnish” is undone in wet weather but varnish made from white walnut oil holds up. Water-resistant varnishes reappear as a subject of interest elsewhere in the manuscript. A marginal note on fol. 3r also compares two varnishes for their storm-worthiness, mentioning that “mastic varnish does not resist rain, whereas that of oil and rosin does.”

We might be tempted to read such varnish comparisons as simple declarations about what resists the rain and what does not. After all, there was an established need for some painted wares to be ready for all weather. Nevertheless, read in the context of the history of art materials and technologies, these comparisons may be more significant. The author-practitioner may be moving toward a larger, categorical claim. The mention of “oil of turpentine” as a key ingredient in “Flanders varnish” on fol. 31r reflects a contemporary artisanal innovation: the advent of spirit-based varnishes. And the marginal note on fol. 3r about the rain-vulnerable mastic varnish is embedded in an entry on another spirit-based varnish. It may be that a central aim of “Varnish resistant to water” was to help define the limitations of the new and increasingly accessible category of spirit-based varnishes. Nevertheless, questions remain. What exactly was this “Flanders varnish,” and how was it made? What, exactly, was the one “made with white walnut oil”? And did they really behave in the rain as reported?

On its own, “Varnish resistant to water” addresses varnish types and their properties rather than provides a recipe for making them. By consulting other entries in the manuscript, we can arrive at plausible conclusions about the nature and composition of the varnishes. The varnishes can then be reconstructed with some degree of confidence—enough at least to test, however preliminarily, the author-practitioner’s claims about their water resistance. Reconstruction and testing of these varnishes can also provide some initial insight into the emerging shift from oil-based to spirit-based varnishes in sixteenth-century Europe.

Varnish

A varnish is a transparent, protective coating most often made from resins in solution that, once applied to a surface, dries into a hard film.4 When applied to a painting, a varnish can help seal water-soluble and fugitive pigments, bring uniformity to the outer surface of the artwork, and impart an overall gloss.5 Until the sixteenth century, the most common varnishes applied over paint were oil-based or “fat” varnishes, made from resins of various kinds (e.g., mastic, sandarac, juniper resin, turpentine oleoresins, and colophony) dissolved in a heated drying oil, such as linseed, hemp, or nut oil.6 Such varnishes could have a yellow or green tint, depending on the oil that was used, and this tint could alter the perception of the painted colors beneath. This color alteration is often addressed in artist treatises, including in Ms. Fr. 640 on fol. 3r, where a recommended varnish “is appropriate for panel paintings and other painted things without corrupting the colors or yellowing.” Over the course of the sixteenth century, spirit-based or “lean” varnishes were introduced into artisanal practice. These were composed of resins dissolved in alcohol or a volatile oil (such as spike lavender oil).7 They became common among painters because their underlying solvents imparted less color to the varnish. They were also easier and safer to make, requiring lower temperatures in their production. They also tended to dry quickly and have less viscosity or body, making them easier to handle.8 In this period, there also existed a third, intermediary category of “mixed” or “semi-fat” varnishes, produced from oleoresins dissolved in alcohol or volatile oil.9

In “Varnish resistant to water” on fol. 31r, the author-practitioner contrasts two types of varnish: “Flanders varnish,” a term that he applies to two different material preparations, and an unnamed varnish made with “white walnut oil.” The following sections attempt to unravel the composition of each in order to develop working recipes for experimental reconstruction and outdoor testing. In these sections, we lay out our thought process and rationale—sometimes rather exhaustively—in order to draw attention to the irresolvable ambiguities and uncertainties of interpreting historical technical texts. In the 450 years between the penning of Ms. Fr. 640 and our engagement with it, materials have changed (e.g., flax and walnuts have evolved or been genetically modified), processes have changed (e.g., industrial production has reduced product variability), and meanings of words have changed (e.g., turpentine). We believe that it is critical to be as transparent as possible about our interpretive process.

Interpretation: “Flanders Varnish”

The entry “Varnish resistant to water” notes the existence of a “Flanders varnish, made with turpentine & oil of turpentine or mastic…” but mentions little else other than its vulnerability to rain. But what exactly is this “Flanders varnish”? Is it one varnish or is it a general category of varnishes commonly used in Flanders? The greatest difficulty with interpretation arises when trying to reconcile the grammar of the sentence with what we know of varnishes and their composition (i.e., resins dissolved in either a drying oil or in an alcohol or volatile oil).

Three materials are mentioned: turpentine, oil of turpentine, and mastic. The grammar suggests that mastic is either being given as a synonym of oil of turpentine or as an alternative to it. Since “oil of turpentine” is materially distinct from “mastic”—that is, not synonymous—we can assume that the author-practitioner is referring to the existence of two alternative versions of “Flanders varnish.” The first and most clearly discernible version is made from turpentine and oil of turpentine. The composition of the second version is less clear. Given the grammar, the most obvious reading would propose a varnish made from turpentine and mastic, but as we will see, this is not the likely meaning. A second reading would propose a varnish made simply of mastic, but mastic, a resin, needs to be added to a drying oil or a volatile oil to become a varnish proper, so this possibility can be dismissed. A third reading would propose a varnish made from turpentine and oil of mastic, but such a material does not appear in any other varnish recipes.10 A fourth reading would propose any varnish that is made with mastic and some other ingredient, prepared in a manner that the reader is assumed to already know. But, if this mastic varnish is somehow comparable to the “turpentine & oil of turpentine” “Flanders varnish” (wherein a resin or oleoresin is dissolved in a volatile oil), then the mastic varnish is presumably also made with a volatile oil.

Our reading, however grammatically awkward, would propose that the second “Flanders varnish” is made of oil of turpentine and mastic. A quick digression to clarify the meaning of the term “turpentine” can shed light on why this reading is the most viable.

Turpentine is a term applied, often without adequate precision, to describe the first two of these three closely related materials:

  1. The sticky sap or oleoresin exuded from coniferous trees, especially from different genera of the Pinaceae family. This sap is also known as balsam, turpentine gum, or gum thus, though it is sometimes simply referred to as turpentine, particularly in historical sources.11

  2. The volatile oil produced by distilling this sap. This product is also known as turpentine oil, oil of turpentine, spirits of turpentine, or turps, though it is sometimes simply referred to as turpentine, particularly in modern and industrial contexts where it is often used and marketed as a paint thinner.12

  3. The solid, resinous residue that remains after distilling this same sap. This product is also known as rosin, colophony, or Greek pitch.13 Such residues sometimes constitute the resins in varnish recipes.

With this in mind, we interpret the “turpentine” of the “Flanders varnish” to refer to the oleoresin or sap from a conifer because “oil of turpentine” unambiguously refers to the volatile oil produced from the distillation of an oleoresin.

The mention of “oil of turpentine” and the absence of any drying oil such as linseed oil or walnut oil in the description of “Flanders varnish” is revealing. This suggests that “Flanders varnish” is not a “fat” oil-based varnish; rather, it is likely a “lean” spirit-based varnish or, possibly, a “mixed” varnish of oleoresin and spirits. The author-practitioner’s first version of “Flanders varnish” (i.e., the one “made with turpentine & oil of turpentine”) is most likely made from a mixture of oleoresin (i.e., sap or balsam) and turpentine oil, yielding a “mixed” varnish. The second version of “Flanders varnish” is most likely made from mastic (a resin) and turpentine oil, yielding a “lean” varnish. Thus, in our interpretation, the “or mastic” of the manuscript entry functions like an afterthought, by which the author-practitioner makes a material analogy between the oleoresin turpentine and the resin mastic as materials that can be dissolved in oil of turpentine to produce a varnish. The author-practitioner does not seem to make the same fine-grained distinction between “mixed” and “lean” spirit-based varnishes that art conservators do today.

In conclusion, the phrase “Flanders varnish, made with turpentine & oil of turpentine or mastic…” seems to refer to two spirit-based varnishes made by dissolving turpentine and mastic respectively in oil of turpentine. We can surmise that “Flanders varnish” was a category of varnish characterized by the use of volatile oils, and it may have been more common in Flanders at the time than it was in France. Regardless, a plausible recipe for making either of these “Flanders varnishes” can only be gleaned contextually from the rest of the manuscript.

Turpentine Varnish

According to our interpretation above, the first version of “Flanders varnish” is a “mixed” turpentine varnish combining the oleoresin turpentine with the volatile oil known as turpentine oil. The entry “Varnish resistant to water” (fol. 31r) does not indicate the precise materials and quantities needed for making turpentine varnish.14 The entry “Frames of the Germans” (fol. 61v) gives ingredient ratios for the making of a “clear turpentine varnish,” “namely half turpentine oil & almost half turpentine.” Unfortunately, the entries on fols. 31r and 61v do not specify the variety of turpentine to be used, in contrast to a great number of other varnish recipes in the manuscript that require “Venice turpentine” in particular.15 For instance, fol. 3r gives a recipe for “Varnish for panels” using Venice turpentine, recommending a ratio of two parts balsam to one part turpentine oil. Given the general availability of larch (Venice) turpentine16 and silver fir (Strasbourg) turpentine17 in early modern Europe, and the attestation of their use in picture varnishes across several historical recipe texts, both are strong candidates for use in the “Flanders varnish.”18 Rather than arbitrarily choose one over the other, we decided to prepare two varnish samples, one using each turpentine following the proportions given on fol. 3r, acknowledging that we may need to adjust those proportions by feeling for the “body” or consistency of the varnishes.19 This kind of adjustment is described in the entry “Varnish” on fol. 88r, which counsels to “test on a very clean knife, & you will know then if it has enough body & if it does not run too much.”20

Mastic Varnish

We interpret the second version of “Flanders varnish” in the entry “Varnish resistant to water” (fol. 31r) to be a “lean,” spirit-based mastic varnish made from the resin mastic dissolved in turpentine oil. There are several competing recipes for spirit-based mastic varnishes across the manuscript in addition to those for oil-based mastic varnish and varnishes that include mastic as one additive among several.21 Proportions between mastic and oil of turpentine vary: “Mastic varnish dry in a half hour” on fol. 97v gives a ratio of 4:1, while “Varnish” on fol. 99v gives 2:1. Both recipes suggest adding eau-de-vie, and the former recipe also adds turpentine balsam. Nevertheless, these additives appear to be optional; “Varnish dry in an hour” on fol. 60v notes that “There is no need to put in turpentine, but only its white turpentine oil & mastic pulverized at your discretion, until it has enough body.” As for the desired consistency, “one knows that this varnish does not have body enough when it does not take well on a panel in oil, for it is like water” (97v); it has enough body “when, being placed on a knife in the wind, it does not run” (60v). The varnish can be made thicker by adding more mastic and thinner by adding more turpentine oil (97v).

Interpretation: Varnish Made with White Walnut Oil

The author-practitioner contrasts the two spirit-based “Flanders varnishes,” which purportedly fail in wet weather, with “that made with white walnut oil, as you have done,” which “holds in the rain.” The phrase “as you have done” suggests that either 1) a reader of the entry is presumed to already possess the knowledge of making varnish and can use their go-to recipe for walnut oil-based varnish for the purpose of waterproofing; or that 2) a recipe for the relevant white walnut oil picture varnish lies somewhere within Ms. Fr. 640. This second suggestion is misleading; no such recipe exists.22 There are varnish recipes made with walnut oil, but they concern coloring objects with varnish: a red varnish is used to imitate coral (fol. 3r), black varnishes for the decoration of armor (fol. 4v), black varnishes as ground layers for etching (fol. 4v), and a pseudo-varnish for armor that is produced by using heat to polymerize walnut oil applied directly to metal (fol. 96v). The closest thing to a recipe for water-resistant varnish comes in a marginal note on fol. 3r: “mastic varnish does not resist rain, whereas that of oil and rosin does.” This off-hand comment at least suggests to us that the principal constituents of the water-resistant white walnut oil varnish are likely walnut oil and rosin (rousine). In another marginal note on fol. 3r, the author-practitioner writes that “colophony is nothing other than recooked rosin (rousine), and on fol. 3v, he identifies colophony as the residue left behind after distilling oil of turpentine.

From these contextual details, we interpret the water-resistant varnish made with “white walnut oil” to be a “fat,” oil-based varnish made of walnut oil and colophony.23 However, one particular difficulty here is the meaning of “white” (blanche) in “white walnut oil.” We have not found reference to white walnut oil in any other art technical source. We propose three possible meanings of “white”: 1) lacking or having transparency, 2) lacking color (no obvious tint or hue), and 3) lacking impurities and inclusions.

On fol. 60r, the author-practitioner writes that “walnut oil extracted like peeled almonds is very white,” and on fol. 57v, a distinction is drawn between walnut oil that is clear and walnut oil that is white: “4- or 5-year-old walnut oil which is clear (cler) is the best color, it keeps off dust. The kind which has recently been drawn with the press in the manner of almond oil is white (blanc), especially if the walnuts’ skin is removed.” It is noteworthy that the first of these two sentences is written by a scribe and the second is in the author-practitioner’s own hand. Cold-pressed walnut oil, when freshly pressed, is mildly opaque, though it becomes lighter and clearer with filtration and/or time.24 This freshly-pressed state might be what the author-practitioner means by white.

Complicating our understanding of the author-practitioner’s use of “white” is a recipe on fol. 67v for an essential oil distilled from a mixture of equal parts of walnut oil and turpentine. There is no qualifier for the walnut oil (e.g., white, clear, fresh), and the word “walnut” itself is a later addition. The essential oil of walnut-turpentine that results from the distillation “renders an essence whiter than common water” (plus blanche que leau commune). Given that water is not white in any conventional understanding of the color, nor are essential oils typically opaque,25 the meaning of “white” walnut oil remains unclear. This qualifier may be referring to the clarity and transparency of the oil, with a desire for oil to be as transparent as water (or even more transparent than “common water”).

A second interpretation of “white” that is also supported by fol. 67r is a description of the walnut oil’s color. While water may not be white, it is comparatively not yellow, an important characteristic of the new spirit varnishes.26 Oil-based varnishes are known to be yellow, changing the colors of any painting to which they are applied.27 The author-practitioner discusses the advantage of this new class of varnishes on fol. 4r: “One did not use to varnish the landscape of a panel when linseed varnish was in use, because it yellowed the landscape. But with that of turpentine, one varnishes everywhere.” This comment is followed by an entry dedicated to “removing varnish from an old panel that is yellowish & varnishing it anew.” Another comment on fol. 97v advises the reader to apply varnish with one’s finger to ensure a “lean layer” and prevent the layer from being too thick and thus yellowing. Even before the widespread adoption of spirit varnishes, artists began to replace linseed oil with walnut and other nut oils specifically because they were less yellow, and Ms. Fr. 640 “seems to confirm what the scattered evidence from recipes that started appearing in the first decades of the fifteenth century already suggested; painters and other artisans were looking to replace traditional linseed oil-resin varnishes with materials that were less yellow.”28 The description of white walnut oil in “Varnishes resistant to rain” may be an oblique comparison between oils, given that nut oils are known to have a less yellow hue than linseed oil. Compared to the deep yellow of linseed oil, the paler yellow of walnut oil could be described as “white,” signifying a lack of color or distinctive hue. Admittedly, there is no mention of linseed oil in this entry, however, and the use of “white” in any comparison between linseed and walnut would be redundant.

The third possible meaning of “white” lies somewhere in the middle between transparency and colorlessness. It could signify the purity of the walnut oil, being free of any impurities or other inclusions. The need for purified materials is treated at length by Marjolijn Bol, who discusses the author-practitioner’s detailed account on fol. 97v about obtaining mastic, another varnish ingredient, that is “white and purified” (blanc & purifie).29 When mastic is collected in small tears, “dirt and dust and black dross” (fol. 97v) can still be present, and these impurities will be noticeable in any varnish made with mastic that has not been purified or cleaned. Mastic is, after all, an organic material collected from plants; inclusions and contaminants are bound to happen. The author-practitioner even provides additional steps a reader can take to further clean the mastic. Bol describes the state of this mastic as “clear (or purified),” suggesting the meaning of “white” to be a combination of transparency and purity. The discussion of mastic, however, could also support the interpretation of lack of color: like walnut oil, mastic has a slight yellow tint (see Fig. 4 for the color of walnut oil and Fig. 7 for the color of crushed mastic), and it can appear cloudy or contain inclusions. “White,” then, may be closer in meaning to “purified,” referring to lack of impurities, while also describing the clarity and color of the material.

Upon reflection, we interpret the “white” of the “white walnut oil” in “Varnishes resistant to rain” to mean transparent and colorless. The Dictionnaire du Moyen Français supports this possibility.30 Additionally, numerous varnish recipes in the sixteenth-century artist’s manual known as the Marciana manuscript call for “clear nut oil” and “clear linseed oil,” while white nut oil or white linseed oil never occur.31 Other recipes in the Marciana manuscript advise ways to make oil and varnish clearer. The debate, however, is not settled; the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 uses the term cler, which can mean both “bright” and “transparent,” to describe walnut oil on fol. 64r, so his vocabulary allows for this precision, even if his meaning is not entirely evident on fol. 31r.

In the absence of any obvious recipe in the manuscript for the “varnish of white walnut oil,” we decided to base our protocol on the reconstruction of historical varnishes in the recent work by Tirat, Echard, Lattuati-Derieux, Huerou, and Serfaty supported by the Fondation des Sciences du Patrimoine, Université de Cergy-Pontoise.32 One reconstructed recipe uses a ratio of 4 parts linseed oil to 1 part colophony, heated for 100 minutes, between 170 and 250°C. While the work of Tirat et al. focuses on linseed oil varnishes, the historical recipes they cite call for both linseed and walnut oils.33 Both oils are characterized as drying oils and have somewhat similar flash and boiling points. We rationalize that we can substitute walnut oil in place of linseed, especially as this was a substitution undertaken by Renaissance artisans.34

Experiment Overview

Interpretation of “Varnishes resistant to rain” on fol. 31r has led us to identify three varnishes of interest, one of which we intend to test in two permutations.35 The four varnishes (2 turpentine, 1 mastic, 1 walnut oil), once prepared, will be applied side by side over painted patterns (dots, lines, and feathered tails) made in oil paint on stretched canvas that has been primed with three successive layers (rabbit skin glue, burnt sienna, then lead white). The canvas will be left outdoors, on a low roof outside the Chandler building of Columbia University, exposed to the elements for several months to see how well the two varnish types (spirit v. oil) hold up. An identical sample canvas will be left indoors in the Making and Knowing Lab as a control.

Support, Ground, and Oil Paint

Three identical canvases were prepared, with one intended for outdoor testing, one serving as a control, and one for planned indoor water testing that was never undertaken.36 Following a procedure approximating the preparation of sixteenth-century paintings on canvas and previously undertaken in the Making and Knowing Lab in consultation with Erma Hermens (then Senior Researcher, Rijksmuseum),37 we used raw, unprimed canvas (Blick Unprimed Belgian Linen #135) stretched over a wooden frame, fastened with staples, and primed with the following successive layers, slightly offset in their application so that each preparatory layer is partially exposed as an uppermost coating:

  • 2 thin coats rabbit skin glue, prepared following a procedure previously undertaken in consultation with Marjolijn Bol38 (RSG)

  • 1 thin coat burnt sienna in linseed oil (BS) (Fig. 2)

  • 1 thin coat lead white in linseed oil (LW)

After priming, each canvas was painted using three primary color oil paints composed of blue verditer, madder lake, and yellow ochre respectively, each ground in linseed oil (see Table 1). These pigments were chosen to represent a range of colors and types of pigment:

  • blue verditer: a mineral pigment of copper carbonate prepared as a synthetic version of the naturally found ore, azurite

  • madder lake: an organic pigment that is the result of precipitation of dye on a substrate,39 typically a translucent pigment used as a “glaze” in oil painting

  • yellow ochre: a natural earth pigment which is durable, inert, and lightfast,40 typically very opaque and resulting in a more matte finish41

The initial ingredients, which varied in state and nature (Fig. 4, Fig. 5, Fig. 6, Fig. 7) yielded four varnishes of distinct colors (Fig. 8). The varnishes also ranged in consistency. The Silver Fir varnish, submitted to the author-practitioner’s “knife test” (Vid. 1) appeared to be slightly thin. The mastic varnish was so thick that it poured from its vial with exceptional slowness (Fig. 9), while the Venice Turpentine and Walnut Oil (Fig. 10) varnishes were closest to the ideal consistency. We considered adjusting the ingredient quantities for the sake of achieving a similar consistency across the varnishes; however, we decided against this, since it would complicate our ability to know the ingredient proportions and quantities with as much precision as we did. We decided to apply the varnishes as made, with the understanding that this first iteration of the experiment need not be the last.

The varnishes were applied with brushes to each canvas in horizontal bands so that the varnishes could be compared side by side (Fig. 11). Application required successive sweeps with the brush to even out the application (Fig. 12). The canvases were prepared so that the effect of the varnishes could be seen over each preparatory layer used on the canvas as well as the painted layer itself. In effect, the sample canvases were divided into a 4 x 5 grid to distinguish the horizontal bands of four varnishes and one unvarnished band across the four surface preparations (oil paint, lead white, burnt sienna, glue), as illustrated in Fig. 13 and schematized in Table 3.

Weather Exposure

In order to test the performance of the varnishes in the rain, one of the prepared canvases along with a glass rain gauge were mounted to a wooden frame and placed outdoors on a low roof accessible only via the Making and Knowing Lab window (Fig. 14). The frame was weighted down with a cast iron pot filled with pieces of scrap metal to ensure it would not blow away (Fig. 15).45

The mounted canvas was left outside from May 30 to October 18, 2023. The canvas was checked and the rain gauge emptied at inconsistent intervals over that time. Historical weather data suggests a cumulative rainfall of 27.6 inches of precipitation over the period (Fig. 16).46 Some of the rain fell with particular force (Vid. 2).

A variety of effects were observed over the test period. A June 6 comparison between the tautness of the outdoor canvas (Vid. 3) and that of the control (Vid. 4) suggests that weather exposure was causing the canvas to slacken. White scum began to appear in the two turpentine varnishes (i.e., Venice and Silver Fir), and the blue oil paint began to run beneath the mastic varnish (Fig. 17). The scumming was particularly widespread and apparent by later June (Fig. 18), though it did fade over the summer. Small dirt particles adhered to some varnishes, especially the Silver Fir (Fig. 19), and the mastic varnish gradually took on a more yellow tone over time and began to ripple and crack, likely due in part to the thickness of the varnish when it was applied (Fig. 20). A final comparison between the outdoor canvas and the control canvas that was kept indoors (Fig. 21) shows the effect of nearly five months of exposure.47

Conclusions

The author-practitioner had drawn a comparison between two types of varnish: spirit-based varnishes (those made with either turpentine balsam or mastic dissolved in oil of turpentine) and oil-based varnishes (rosin dissolved in walnut oil). He asserted that the former were undone in the rain while the latter were not. Our experiment, however preliminary, suggests that his assertion was correct. The varnish made of walnut oil and colophony appears to have withstood five months of outdoor conditions (including 27.6 inches of rain) better than any of the spirit-based versions. The mastic varnish performed the worst by far, but it was also notably more viscous than any of the other varnishes, and its resultant thicker application across the canvas likely affected the outcome. It is worth noting that the unvarnished control strip along the very bottom of the canvas fared nearly as well as the strip coated with the walnut oil varnish.

Given that the unvarnished yet painted section fared comparatively well when exposed to the elements for five months, we question whether “Varnishes resistant to water” were actually intended to cover oil paints; they may have been intended to protect another medium, distemper perhaps. It is possible a substrate other than canvas, such as paper, would be preserved more fully by this varnish. Future iterations of this experiment should attempt to produce varnishes with more uniform consistency and should compare the performance of the varnishes over paint of different binding media on a variety of substrates.

Due to their ephemeral nature, the “painted banners & signs that one carries in the rain” described by the author-practitioner in “Varnish resistant to water” on fol. 31r are not abundant in museum collections and archives, nor are they the subject of intense scholarly study. This entry in Ms. Fr. 640, however, highlights the practical considerations of artisans in creating works of art to be used out of doors in civic and religious celebrations and activities. It sheds light on the lost art pieces that decorated festivals, processions, and royal entries. “Varnish resistant to water” also marks an important technological shift in the Renaissance craft workshop: the advent of spirit-based varnishes. While the advantages of spirit varnishes and their preferred use over oil-based varnishes are attested in Ms. Fr. 640, other technical manuals, and in the material record from the later sixteenth century onwards, the older oil-based varnishes may still have been superior in one way: waterproofing.
