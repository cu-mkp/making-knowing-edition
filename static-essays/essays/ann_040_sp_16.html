Abstract

A reconstruction of the entry “Varnish for distemper” on fol. 56r of BnF Ms. Fr. 640 shows that this particular varnish was not intended as a finishing layer to protect the paint and give it a sheen, as was customary in the sixteenth century. Instead, it functioned as an ingredient in the process and as a means of creating a specific optical effect—that of imitation marble.

Imitating Marble: Historical Context

Since at least the first century CE, paint has been used to imitate colored hard stone such as marble or porphyry. Wall paintings dating from this period in Pompeii, for instance, have survived and show that the taste for these stones as decorative elements was not dependent on the use of the material itself.1 Later, in fourteenth-century Italy, painted marble emerged as a popular decoration for the back of panel paintings, a practice that gained popularity in northern Europe in the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries. Numerous examples are extant on the reverse side of panel paintings in museum collections and on the exteriors and integrated frames of small winged altarpieces such as Hans Memling’s 1480 Triptych of Adriaan Reins (Fig. 1) and a diptych of a Franciscan friar by Jan Provoost (ca. 1465–1529) (Fig. 2), both in the Sint-Janshospitaal in Bruges.

Given the widespread employment of this technique to decorate the backs and frames of panels, it is perhaps surprising that detailed methods of creating an imitation marble pattern are not present in well-known early modern written treatises and books of secrets.2 A method for creating a marbled appearance on painted columns with oil paints does make a brief appearance in Eraclius’ tenth-century De coloribus et artibus Romanorum,3 however, the recipe for imitation marble in Ms. Fr. 640 on fol. 56r appears to be unusual among known texts on techniques. This recipe seems to lay out a method for producing an imitation marble or porphyry pattern on panel using layers of red lakes and a varnish made from larch resin (Venice turpentine) and spike lavender oil. Most of the over thirty entries in Ms. Fr. 640 devoted to the making and application of varnish employ varnish as a protective coating or as an optical enhancement, but this recipe uses varnish as an ingredient in, and integral part of the technique to create the visual effect of marble. In a comparison of the manuscript’s varnish recipes, a number of useful details come to light not only about the use of varnish in the late sixteenth century, but perhaps even some clues about the author-practitioner himself.

The recipe on fol. 56r for “Varnish for distemper” (Vernis pour destrempe) presents an intriguing puzzle because it appears to describe a common technique, but its ambiguous wording posed a challenge for reconstruction, and its title raised the question of why it refers to the ingredients rather than to the product (imitation marble). Thus, analysis of this recipe necessitated clarifying both the materials and process.

Reconstruction

To reconstruct the recipe on fol. 56r, it was first necessary to understand its purpose and intended final product, and to establish the identity and availability of the materials described by the author-practitioner. His statement that with this recipe “you can make marble with distemper” (Tu peulx fayre du mabre a destrempe) suggests that this was a method for creating a type of painted imitation marbling like that found on the exterior or reverse of fifteenth- and sixteenth-century panel paintings, especially small-scale ones. Such paintings were often portable, rather than being attached to walls, and the reverse of the main panel was typically decorated with an illusionistic image such as grisaille figures or a painted pattern mimicking marble or porphyry. Painting or otherwise decorating the reverse of the panel also provided protection against the elements and lessened the likelihood of warping.4 Imitation marbling was often used in furniture and architecture as well as paintings. It first gained popularity in Western Europe in trecento Italy, where the technique was probably based on imported Chinese and Arabic marble-patterned paper.5 By the sixteenth century, painted marbling was a common decoration for altarpieces, frames, and panel reverses throughout Europe and especially in the Netherlandish tradition.6

Materials

The types of varnishes mentioned in Ms. Fr. 640 can be divided into two categories, identified by Marjolijn Bol as those made with drying oils and those made with essential oils.7 From the fifteenth through the sixteenth centuries, the predominant method of varnish-making involved the combination of a dessicative oil with a resin, which had to be melted using heat. This varnish was applied by hand, and was typically dried in the sun for a long period of time. In the sixteenth century, varnishes made from essential or distilled oils such as spike lavender oil (also known as aspic oil) abruptly appeared in Europe. This type of oil could dissolve the resin without heat. This is described on fol. 56r, where spike lavender oil is used to dissolve Venice turpentine, a larch resin, into a clear liquid. These “cold varnishes” not only dried much faster than heated varnishes, but could also be applied thinly with a paintbrush. The result was a more brittle varnish that appears to have been mainly used on small-scale objects.8 The reason behind this abrupt change is unclear, as essential oils were certainly available in Europe prior to the sixteenth century. Lavender oil, for example, was being produced through distillation in Burgundy since at least the fourteenth century.9 However, the specific lavender oil used for varnish making, distilled from the spike lavender plant, is believed to only have become common in the second half of the sixteenth century, although it is mentioned in Hieronymus Brunschwig’s 1512 Liber De Arte Distillandi de compositis along with oils of turpentine (referred to as Balsams) from juniper and rosemary.10

In Ms. Fr. 640, the comparison of the thirty-four entries on varnish reveals that the author-practitioner was equally familiar with hot and cold preparations of varnishes. There are sixteen recipes for making varnish with heat, and twelve that describe cold varnish (the remainder of the recipes are either ambiguous as to the production method, or omit instructions for making). There do not appear to be discernible differences in the author-practitioner’s treatment and specific knowledge of hot and cold varnishes.

The recipe on fol. 56r does not give the impression of firsthand experience. It is located in the main body of text, following a recipe for “Excellent tempering bath for cuirass bodies” and preceding the instructions for the “Painter” on fol. 56v. Fol. 56r contains no marginal annotations or additions, in contrast to the folio’s verso, fol. 56v, which is heavily annotated. The recipe “Varnish for distemper” is written clearly and presents a relatively straightforward method for the reader who has understood the process—to the first-time practitioner, the instructions are not clear. This could indicate that the author-practitioner was familiar with the process, or that it was a commonly known technique, which is confirmed by the many imitation marbled surfaces that survive from this period.

Other recipes in Ms. Fr. 640 such as “Mastic varnish dry in a half hour” on fol. 97v and “Black varnish for sword guard, bands for trunks, &c” on fol. 4v, which are extensively annotated with details and further instructions, suggest that the author-practitioner wrote from personal experience.11 In comparison, “Varnish for distemper” includes clearly described steps, but the lack of detail about the intended outcome of the process raised many questions for my reconstruction. First among these was the meaning of specific terms. The most difficult was destrempe, a word found throughout Ms. Fr. 640, and indeed throughout early modern artistic literature, that can have a number of possible meanings.12 As Cleo Nisse shows in her essay in this edition, destrempe is associated with what today is understood to be a distemper medium, which can include any protein and water-based binding medium (including egg or gum Arabic), but is most commonly used to mean an animal glue-based pigment.13 I therefore chose to use rabbit skin glue in my reconstruction. Ms. Fr. 640 contains another recipe on fol. 79v, “For making varnish” that calls for a glue, probably as an isolation layer, before applying a spike lavender oil varnish. This recipe instructs: “Take some mastic, sang daRage darac, gum arabic & spike lavender oil, as much of one as the other, & make them melt all together, & before coating it, lay a coat of glue quite clear, & let it dry.” I therefore chose glue as a binding medium for the first red lake and chalk layer to make “distemper marble” (mabre a destrempe).

Similarly, laque, the French word for red lake pigments, refers broadly to any red organic colorant employed in the early modern period primarily for dyeing textiles.14 Up until the eighteenth century, “lake” could refer to any dark red pigment. Cennini defines lacca as a manufactured red pigment and recommends buying it ready-made. Lacca was typically made from textile clippings, but this kind of red lake could be greasy and faded quickly. Another kind of red lake noted by Cennini is that derived from the resinous secretions of the Kerria lacca insect native to India and Southeast Asia. He described it as “dry, lean, grainy, so that it almost looks black and has a blood color. This can only be good and perfect.”15 The grainy resin-lake needed to be mulled well before use and was recommended for panel paintings and fresco. Although “lake” is now used to denote a dye that has been adsorbed onto a substrate to make paint pigment, in the early modern period the term “lac” or “lacca” specifically referred to a red colorant that could be derived from a number of sources and techniques. The term lac or lacca is also found referring to red organic dyestuffs in general, encompassing kermes, madder, and sappanwood lakes as well as several other sources.16

One of the most challenging materials to determine in this recipe was rose de gand, for which I have been unable to find any additional references in contemporary sources. Although this ingredient was not ultimately included in the reconstruction (since it was only suggested as an alternative for red lake), I attempted to identify the pigment, which was doubly intriguing because of its geographic reference to gand, meaning the city of Ghent, in light of the popularity of imitation marbling in Flemish painting. The author-practitioner’s use of the word rose likely referred to the pigment type roset, which was a bright pink pigment made from brazilwood (or sappanwood), with additions of alum and chalk.17 This is very close to the use of this pigment as specified by the author-practitioner in this recipe, which also calls for rose to be mixed with chalk. There is historical evidence for a specific, high quality red lake manufactured in England and traded through the Low Countries in the fifteenth century that could plausibly be the rose de gand to which the author-practitioner refers.18 According to Susie Nash, Jo Kirby, and Joanna Cannon, rose referred in general to a red lake manufactured from brazilwood shavings.19 Roset similarly was a term used in reference to the red lakes employed in textile dyeing, with the brazilwood lakes noted for their pink color.20 Bernard Guineau’s glossary of historical color terms does contain an entry on rose de Flandres, which seems the most likely equivalent for the mysterious rose de gand of Ms. Fr. 640; in this glossary, however, rose de Flandres is identified as a madder lake.21 For the purposes of my reconstruction, I chose to use brazilwood lake as it seemed that the author-practitioner was most concerned with achieving a rosy pink color in this first destrempe layer with chalk, and brazilwood lakes are noted throughout both contemporary and modern pigment literature for this quality. In the late Middle Ages and Renaissance, lakes made from brazilwood were the most common and also the most inexpensive red pigments. Red lakes from this material are found in the majority of panel paintings and manuscripts in the fifteenth century and were used particularly to manufacture marbled paper.22

More common materials such as tourmentine claire de venise (Venice turpentine) and huile d’aspic (spike lavender oil) are well known and are still manufactured today. Venice turpentine is used in three recipes for varnish in Ms. Fr. 640 and refers to turpentine derived from larch resin. It was a common varnish ingredient by the sixteenth century.23 Larch turpentine was produced since antiquity; it is mentioned in the writings of Vitruvius, Dioscorides, Pliny, and Galen. In the Middle Ages it was regarded as one of the highest quality balsam resins, and, as previously noted, in later centuries writers such as Hieronymus Brunschwig remained very interested in balsams. Larch balsam gained the name “Venice turpentine” or “Venetian turpentine” in the fifteenth century, when it was distributed mainly through the Venice drug market.24

Spike lavender oil, mentioned in three Ms. Fr. 640 varnish recipes, is an essential oil derived through distillation of the spike lavender plant.25 On fol. 56r the use of spike lavender oil seems to be particularly important because of its “penetrating” effect (the dispersal of pigmentation similar in appearance to the addition of water to oil) on the second red wine lake paint layer, crucial to the production of the marbled pattern.

Technique

The recipe “Varnish for distemper” specified three steps. First, the distemper layer made with lake and chalk must be applied to the support. With the understanding that this was a recipe for imitation marbling such as that found on the back of panel paintings, I decided to use an oak panel for the support. Unsure at first of whether or not the backs of panels were typically gessoed, my first attempt at reconstruction used an unprimed oak panel (Fig. 3). The second step of the recipe called for a red lake mixed with wine to be painted over the first distemper layer, so that the entire area appeared a solid red (Fig. 4.). The final step was the application of varnish (Fig. 5).

First, the varnish had to be made by combining Venice turpentine and spike lavender oil. Then this varnish was to be applied to the red lake wine layer, revealing light and dark areas: “And all will appear red, but the varnish you will put here, which will penetrate, will make appear dark & light that which ought to be thus” (fol. 56r). The recipe did not specify how the application was to be done, what tools were to be used, nor what the final product should look like. Instead, the recipe ends after the above description with a note about the use of spike lavender varnish, cautioning the reader against its use as varnish for panel paintings (probably referring here to a varnish over paint on the obverse of the panel, and not the reverse or exterior wings): “Pure spike lavender oil varnish is not good for panels, for spike lavender oil is too penetrating & makes colors sparkle, unless it was made long ago” (fol. 56r).26

As I was unsuccessful in finding any comparable contemporary instructions for creating imitation marble, I turned to modern conservation reports for clues as to how the marbling effect was achieved in paint and varnish. Jill Dunkerton’s report for the conservation of Jan van Eyck’s portrait of his wife Margaretha, restored at the National Gallery in London in 2008, proved extremely helpful (Fig. 6). Dunkerton found that the varnish on the marbled rear of the painting was comprised of a heat-bodied linseed oil and pine resin mixture, different materials from the larch balsam turpentine and spike lavender oil that the author-practitioner calls for (Fig. 7).27 Van Eyck used a black base (where the author-practitioners used a red lake and chalk distemper layer), and then topped this base layer with a spattering of distemper (probably glue-based) of red lake mixed with vermillion and chalk, as well as black, greyish white, and bright vermillion. Whereas the recipe in Ms. Fr. 640 specifies a coat of varnish as the final, catalyzing layer (or what I assumed was a final top coat), Dunkerton believed that Van Eyck applied varnish in an intermediate layer between the first and last distemper-spattered paints.28 Van Eyck appears to have used varnish to add depth to the paint, rather than to “penetrate” the paint layer as the recipe on fol. 56r prescribes.29

The vagueness of the Ms. Fr. 640 recipe for “Varnish for distemper” and the apparent dearth of contemporary recipes engendered several rounds of experimentation before I finally achieved a result that plausibly matched the description “marble with distemper.” I first tried painting the layers onto an unprimed oak board.30 Since technical analysis revealed that the back and front of Jan van Eyck’s Margaretha panel had been primed with gesso, I then undertook the steps outlined on fol. 56r on a gessoed panel.31 Since the recipe mentions that the light rose distemper layer needed to dry before the dark red wine lake layer was painted over it, it was notable that it did not specify whether or not that layer should dry before the varnish was applied to it. Therefore on both unprimed oak and gessoed board, I tried applying varnish to one panel when the dark red wine lake was completely dry, and to one panel immediately after painting out the lake. In both trials I had applied the varnish over the two lake layers with a brush in a single coat and was disappointed in the apparent lack of any visual effect resembling marbling. I could tell, however, that the varnish applied to wet lake seemed to affect the color and light-dark contrast of the two lake layers much more dramatically than when it was applied to dry paint (Fig. 8). It seemed that perhaps the problem was my application of the paint and varnish, rather than the support or the ingredients.

In two recipes in Ms. Fr. 640, “Varnish for panels” on fol. 3r and “Mastic varnish dried in a half hour” on fol. 97v, the author-practitioner stipulates that the varnish should be applied with a finger. This application method was standard in the use of heat-made varnishes to panel paintings in the fifteenth century.32 Applying varnish with a finger might allow for a swirling motion that could produce a marbled pattern, especially since it was clear in the panels to which I had applied varnish with a brush that the brushstrokes were clearly defined and provided the patterning for any dark-light differentiation that was occurring.33 In Dunkerton’s report on the Van Eyck panel, the artist had achieved the marbled pattern through a spattering of paints in successive layers. I therefore decided to try dribbling large drops of varnish onto the lake layer, and then spreading and swirling it with my (gloved) finger.

My second attempt at reconstruction of distemper varnish was more successful than the first.34 This time, I mulled each of the red lake mixtures for approximately thirty minutes (rather than only a few minutes in my first trial). The longer mulling time, and the use of the granite slab rather than a glass surface, resulted in much smoother paints with brilliant, saturated jewel tones, which stood in stark contrast to the grainy and greyish paints I had produced the first time around (Fig. 9). I also experimented with the thickness and concentration of the first light rose distemper and the second dark red wine lake layers. In “Varnish for distemper” the thickness of each paint layer was not made clear, though single layers were strongly implied. On oak panels 3 and 4, I applied thick, opaque rose distemper layers as well as dark red wine lake layers (Fig.10). On gessoed panels 5 and 6, I applied thin layers. I also noted the presence of bubbles that, when dried, allowed the white gessoed ground to show through (Fig. 11). Whereas I had previously applied the paints thickly and took care to eliminate bubbles, I allowed them to remain in this second trial, thinking that it might be beneficial to the formation of the marbling pattern.35 Over the course of my reconstruction attempts it was becoming clear to me that the color contrast enabled by translucent and imperfect paint layers was probably an important element of this technique, as earlier trials with more uniform and opaque paint layers showed significantly less patterning after the application of varnish.

The author-practitioner’s specification that the varnish made from Venice turpentine and spike lavender oil should “penetrate” the paint layers was especially intriguing in regard to the prescribed appearance of areas of dark and light, as I did not observe any major changes to the red lake layers when I applied a layer of varnish with a brush. Recall that the passage reads: “And all will appear red, but the varnish you will put here, which will penetrate, will make appear dark & light that which ought to be thus.” In a recipe for “Varnish of spike lavender oil” on fol. 4r, the author-practitioner similarly notes in the margin, perhaps based on his own observations, that this varnish is “penetrating” and is not recommended for paintings because “Varnish of spike lavender oil is not as appropriate for colors as that of turpentine, for spike lavender oil makes them die eats away at the colors since it is too penetrating.”36 Spike lavender oil is also used in two varnish recipes in fol. 73v (one made with heat, the other unspecified), but these entries contain no details of application or effect. Interestingly, on fol. 79v, the author-practitioner recommends applying a glue layer prior to this spike lavender varnish.37 Presumably this glue isolation layer is necessary to prevent the varnish from attacking the paint layers. This is significant in relation to “Varnish for distemper” on fol. 56r since the spike lavender varnish is intended to penetrate the wine lake layer but not the glue distemper layer underneath.

I was finally able to successfully achieve a patterning that plausibly resembled marble or porphyry on gessoed panel 6 (Vid. 1). In this instance, I had applied a layer of distemper in which I allowed bubbles to settle and also did not correct variations in the thickness of the paint, meaning that when the distemper layer dried there were irregular areas of white ground showing through. I had found through previous trials that the varnish applied to wet wine lake appeared to produce a better effect (particularly in regard to light-dark contrast). On gessoed panel 6, I dribbled varnish onto the wet wine lake layer, and immediately witnessed the “penetration” of the varnish through the wine lake layer to reveal the lighter pink distemper layer beneath it. With a gloved finger, I made only one or two light strokes on the surface to achieve a marbled pattern, otherwise leaving the spotted dark-light pattern made by the varnish as it fell from the brush. The final result looked like something that could be called an imitation marble or porphyry (Fig.12), providing proof of concept that the method described by the author-practitioner could be effective in producing the desired result.38

Conclusion

The recipe, “Varnish for distemper,” on fol. 56r stands out among the many varied types of varnishes in Ms. Fr. 640. The majority of the thirty-four varnish recipes contained in Ms. Fr. 640 seem intended for use as a finishing layer to provide protection and give gloss to objects. There are also numerous recipes for colored varnishes, such as “Color of gold without gold on silver” on fol. 29v. In contrast, “Varnish for distemper” integrates the varnish into a process for achieving a specific visual effect—changing, not simply enhancing, the paint layer beneath it—rather than functioning as a protective layer, or as a coloring or binding agent.39 This marbling varnish also represents an example of a sixteenth-century recipe for a “cold varnish” made with an essential oil.40 After the emergence of cold-prepared varnishes in the early sixteenth century, varnishes were specified for different purposes, as, for example, when the author-practitioner cautions against the use of some essential oil varnishes on paintings, since they could corrode the paint layer.41 In the marbling recipe on fol. 56r, the corroding or “penetrating” property of the spike lavender oil varnish is creatively manipulated to achieve a visually compelling effect, using less costly means to imitate the surface appearances of heavy and expensive stones.
