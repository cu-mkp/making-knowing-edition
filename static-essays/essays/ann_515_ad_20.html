Making Colored Wood in Ms. Fr. 640

By DOI: https://doi.org/10.7916/atee-xn84
← Back to Essays

Abstract

Five entries on fol. 76r of Ms. Fr. 640 offer instructions for dyeing wood, without detailing the type of wood in question or the use to which the dyed wood is to be put. These entries are part of a larger cluster on the coloring of wood, bone, and horn that spans fols. 73r to 79r. This essay reconstructs the recipes for dyeing wood red and green, and it situates them in the context of wood inlay and marquetry, decorative techniques in vogue in early modern Europe.

Introduction

A cluster of entries in Ms. Fr. 640 on techniques for coloring wood starts on fol. 73r and ends about halfway down fol. 79r. The recipes in the section include dyes for coloring wood, bone, and horn, colored varnishes to use on wood and other surfaces, various techniques for coloring metals, colored “gums” (goume) made from wax and resin, and goumiche (seemingly a colored glue), methods to gild wood and other materials, as well as a few pigment recipes, including one for vermilion and an unusual recipe for making “very beautiful color of gold & of little expense” that calls for mixing grated orange peel with sulfur.1 Interestingly, except for three entries on fol. 77r, this entire section is written in a scribal hand—Hand D—in contrast to most of the rest of Ms. Fr. 640. The recipes in this section are very clean, containing almost no deletions, interlinear additions, or marginal comments. This hand also appears sporadically on fols. 57r–58r. In both sections where this hand occurs, the scribe seems to have been uncertain about some terms: on fols. 57v, 58r, 73r, 75v, and 79r, he left gaps for words presumably to be inserted (with one term inserted in a different hand on fol. 73r), and on fols. 77v, 78r, and 79v, the scribe clearly had trouble with the word sandarac. He made a couple of tries, writing Sang de Rai and Sang da Rag which was then corrected in a different hand (probably that of the author-practitioner) to Sang Da Rac (and Sangdarac on fol. 79v). On fol. 78r, the scribe also wrote trois (three) for bois (wood).

These scribal mistakes indicate that these recipes were almost certainly taken down by dictation rather than by copying from another source. The gaps and mistaken terms might have been caused by the scribe taking dictation in a noisy workshop environment or by a general unfamiliarity with common materials such as sandarac. It is hard to guess what might be the import of this somewhat unified section of Ms. Fr. 640 being written down by a scribe, thus this essay leaves aside speculation about this point. Instead, it discusses possible uses for the dyed woods and gives an account of reconstructing some of the recipes on fol. 76r for coloring wood.

Intarsia

By the late sixteenth century when Ms. Fr. 640 was compiled, sophisticated forms of inlay work employing artificially colored materials were common throughout Europe. While woodworkers had used the natural shades of various woods to produce inlay art since antiquity, by the late fifteenth century, artisans in Italy began to experiment with dyes and stains to obtain new chromatic effects or to imitate the appearance of expensive or otherwise difficult to obtain woods.2 Concerning the masterful intarsia work of Fra Damiano da Bergamo in the sixteenth century, Sabba Castiglione wrote, “I even think that the colours of these woods are more vivid, brilliant, and beautiful than those used by painters, so that these most excellent works may be considered as a new style of painting without colours… [by] dyeing woods in any colour that you may wish, and in imitation of spotted and marbled stones” (Fig. 1).3 In 1568, Giorgio Vasari expressed a similar sentiment regarding the highly pictorial intarsia work of his time in the preface to Lives of the Most Eminent Painters, Sculptors, and Architects, stating that “the art of inlaying and making pictures with coloured woods… is painting.”4 Examples of Renaissance intarsia such as the Studiolo, ca. 1479–82, originally from the Ducal Palace in Gubbio, Italy (Fig. 2, Fig. 3) and the so-called Eglantine Table, ca. 1567, originally from Hardwick Hall, Derbyshire, England, show how the combination of natural and colored woods could produce complex, naturalistic depictions of scenery and material things.5

These and other “paintings in wood” provide a historical material context for this essay’s reconstruction of two recipes for coloring wood on fol. 76r.

Coloring Wood

In the loosely related recipes stretching from fol. 73r to 79r, several recipes contain instructions for coloring wood, bone, and horn—materials that were used in inlay work. These recipes give no hint, however, of any purpose or use for these colored materials. As in other entries in Ms. Fr. 640, many of these recipes are brief, containing no information on types of wood to use, nor on how to prepare it for coloring. Among the larger group of recipes, five on fol. 76r make up a unified series on coloring wood. Beginning with a method “For making wood green,” all recipes in the sequence use the same protocols for mixing, heating, soaking, and boiling established in the first entry, with ingredients and proportions varying according to the different colors being produced (red, blue, violet, yellow).

Although the use of artificially colored woods in intarsia was increasingly common in the sixteenth century, there is little textual documentation of the ingredients and procedures used in dyeing from this period. Even as late as 1774, nearly three centuries after Fra Damiano began coloring woods for his intarsia, André-Jacob Roubo recorded that artisans and workshops tended to keep their methods of dyeing secret, and as a result the quality and visual properties of finished products varied widely according to the place of production.6 It is thus not surprising that early recipe collections, such as the manuscripts of Jehan Le Begue (compiled ca. 1431) or the Brussels Manuscript (1635), contain only a few recipes for coloring wood, bone, and horn.7 Conservators have remarked, however, that recipes for dyeing woods are often similar to those for dyeing textiles and may have been developed by artisans in the textile industry.8 The use of textile dye methods may help account for the lack of wood dyeing recipes before the eighteenth century.

Reconstruction

The first challenge I faced was the type of wood to source for the reconstruction.9 The author-practitioner makes no mention of it, and objects in museum collections are often of little help, as a single article of elaborate inlay might contain many distinct species of wood, hence many catalog entries for marquetry furniture simply record the materials as “various woods.” Beyond the difficulty of identifying wood species, the visual and material properties of veneers produced from the same log would vary as a result of the method of manufacture (e.g., sawing thicknesses and cutting angles) as well as post-cutting treatments. According to furniture curator Pierre Ramond, wood veneers were exclusively produced by skilled workers using large frame saws to cut sheets as thin as 1–2 mm until the nineteenth century, but, with the advent of industrialization, new mechanized methods such as slicing or rotary cutting came into wide use.10 These new manufacturing methods allowed for quicker sheet production; however, they also required extensive pre- and post-treatments, including steaming or boiling logs for varying lengths of time according to the hardness of the wood as well as open air or kiln drying after cutting. The sawed veneers of the sixteenth century required no such treatments, and they are still considered to be of superior quality because the natural color and fiber of the wood has not been altered by the treatments.11 Due to lower manufacturing costs and faster production times, the majority of veneers produced today are sliced or rotary cut. In addition, modern treatment methods of steaming, drying, and seasoning cause changes in the levels of tannic acids in the wood that can affect the dyeing process.

For the purpose of my reconstruction, I used three different hardwoods commonly used in marquetry and furniture making, all varying in hardness and natural lightness of color: basswood (also called lindenwood), mahogany, and cherry, with all sheets cut to a thickness of 1/16” (roughly 1.6 mm). I assume the sheets were manufactured according to modern methods, and thus quite different from those of the sixteenth century.

After sanding the veneers (Fig. 4), I considered how to prepare the wood for the dye bath, as the author-practitioner gives no direction on this. Conservators Maarten van Bommel and Enrica Fantini, in their attempts to recreate early synthetic dyes from the nineteenth century, note that the acidity and porosity of wood will affect the final dye result; therefore, they soaked their woods in either plain water, a solution of 5% ammonia, or salt water.12 With this in mind, I divided each type of wood into two groups and soaked one group in water for a period of 48 hours before dyeing and left the other group dry to be added to the dye bath (Fig. 5). For woods being dyed before nineteenth-century methods of manufacture, one probably would have followed instructions such as those of William Salmon’s 1675 publication, Polygraphice, that instructs to boil in alum water all materials to be dyed, or Godfrey Smith’s 1770s compilation, The Laboratory, or, School of the Arts, which directs that “the wood is first to be thoroughly soaked in alum water.”13 After soaking half the wood for 48 hours in water, I briefly wiped them down and allowed the excess water to absorb on a paper towel about 30 minutes before adding dye.

The next challenge was presented by the instructions to “put the pot in a dung heap for fifteen days.” Because thermophilic bacteria colonize manure, keeping the pot in a manure pile would be an effective method for keeping it at a relatively warm, stable temperature for a long period of time. Procuring a sufficiently large pile of composting manure in New York during the winter proved impractical, necessitating an alternative means of incubating the soaking wood. I found that my crockpot maintains water at a temperature of roughly 66°C when set to “warm,” which is within the upper bounds of temperatures expected in composting manure, thus I kept the wood in its dye bath in this covered pot (Fig. 6).14

For Making Wood Green

The entry “For making wood green” on fol. 76r instructs:

One needs to take one quart of white vinegar & one ounce of green & one ounce of glass alum, all mixed together, and you will put your substances into a leaded pot over the fire without taking air, & then put the pot in a dung heap for fifteen days, and once it is out, you will boil your substances for the space of three hours.

I added 14 g of verdigris (copper acetate) produced in the Making and Knowing lab to approximately 475 ml of white wine vinegar.15 Prior to the experiment, I tested the solubility of the verdigris by adding 0.5 g to 20 ml of white wine vinegar in a beaker. While some of the verdigris dissolved and gave the vinegar a light green tint, after ten minutes of soaking and stirring, most of the copper acetate failed to enter into solution (Fig. 7). Briefly heating the beaker on a hot plate, however, almost immediately caused the rest of the verdigris to dissolve in the solution and changed the color of the liquid to a darker hue (Fig. 8). Stirring constantly, I brought the vinegar and verdigris solution to a temperature of around 60°C to ensure no crystals remained and slowly added 14 g of potash alum (Fig. 9). The alum was slower to dissolve than the verdigris, but after ten minutes of stirring with a glass rod, no hard crystals remained. The dye was heated to about 80°C for a period of ten minutes, then added to each of the two 6-oz mason jars containing the pre-soaked and dry wood samples. The jars were then set in the hot water bath (Fig. 10, Fig. 11). After fifteen days, the jars were removed from the bath and the contents boiled in separate beakers for a period of three hours. The wet veneers were then dried for 48 hours and, finally, lightly sanded with 1000 grit sandpaper.

For Making Wood Red

The entry “For making wood red” instructs to “take one quart of old urine and three ounces of madder & one ounce of glass alum, and you will put all in your pot, & do as with green” (Fig. 12). I added the alum to the heating mixture of stale urine and ground madder root, and it effervesced (Fig. 13).16 The dye solution was heated to about 80°C for ten minutes and then added to two 6-oz mason jars, one containing the pre-soaked wood and the other the dry veneers, which were then promptly placed into a warm water bath preheated to 66°C, and left for fifteen days. The jars were then removed from the bath and the contents boiled in separate beakers for a period of three hours. The wet veneers were then dried for 48 hours and then lightly sanded with 1000 grit sandpaper.

Results

Red

On removing the wood from the dye bath, I realized I should have strained the ground madder root from the dye before adding the wood.17 Other than slight variations caused by particles of madder root affixing to the wood during the final boiling and drying process, there was no discernible difference between the veneers that were added to the dye without soaking and those that were soaked in water for 48 hours prior to dyeing.

The basswood veneers, which had the lightest natural color of the three wood varieties, took on a brighter red color than the mahogany and cherry woods, which both turned a darker shade of red. Contrary to my expectations, there was no difference in the dye penetration between the two sample groups. In fact, upon cutting away a 1 mm strip from the edge of each veneer I was surprised to see how shallow the penetration of the dye was for each veneer. While I had certainly succeeded in producing red wood, there was no trace of pigment past the outermost layer of each sample, and the samples seemed no different than if I had brushed a stain over the surface.

Green

The verdigris dye reconstruction failed to produce green wood.18 While the color of the dye was dark green, upon removing the jars from the hot water bath I immediately saw the liquid had turned dark brown. A dark, rust-like material had formed around the rim of the lids, which made unsealing the jars very difficult, and after emptying the contents into beakers for boiling, I noticed more brown precipitate at the bottom of the containers. Instead of green, the veneers all became a rich shade of brown, again with no difference between the pre-soaked and non pre-soaked wood. In contrast to the madder-dyed veneers, however, the inner layers of these samples did appear slightly darker than the original wood color, possibly indicating that some of the pigment penetrated past the surface layer.

More experimentation would be necessary to determine the cause of failure in the green dye trial. In the sixteenth century, green was apparently difficult to obtain through dyeing and staining. As Flavia Perugini Philip and others have noted, verdigris oxidizes and changes from varying shades of green to dark brown when exposed to light or air, which may explain the presence of dark brown leaves and vines occasionally seen in examples of floral marquetry.19 There are examples, however, of extant inlay work from early modern Europe that still contain vivid green wood. Recent conservation work on furniture, wall panels, and musical instruments containing green inlay reveals that from as early as the fifteenth century, artisans may have avoided the problems of fading or oxidizing with verdigris-based dyes by using wood infested with the fungus chlorociboria aeruginascens.20 This fungus, which infects a wide variety of hardwoods from various regions and climate zones, produces the pigment xylindein, which locally stains the wood of the colonized tree a brilliant shade of green to blue-green, depending on the species of wood.21 The Gubbio Studiolo is a prominent example of Chlorociboria-stained wood marquetry (see Fig. 3).

Conclusion

An obstacle to advancing our knowledge of early modern wood coloring techniques is the inherent difficulty of analyzing the materials used in intarsia or marquetry works. Many of the dyed woods in extant marquetry objects have either completely faded or degraded to other colors. While performing conservation and restoration treatments on three pieces of furniture with extensive wood inlay from the late eighteenth century, Wilma Roelofs noted some of the fine coloring in the wood was only visible on the reverse sides of removed pieces of inlay, the only face “protected from the influence of light.”22 It is often impossible to discern which pieces of the inlay were originally dyed without removing large sections of the design and viewing the back side of the veneers.23 Colorfastness may be affected by a number of variables in the dyeing process, such as “quality and quantity of ingredients… quality of woods to be dyed… duration of the dyeing process… and any additional requirements such as heating up the dye of boiling woods in it.”24

Beyond variables that might arise during the dyeing procedure itself, the treatment of inlaid surfaces over time has a great impact on color retention in dyed woods. While varnishing could protect certain pigments from fading or oxidizing due to air exposure, the application of certain varnishes could also accelerate loss of color. The shallow penetration of dye into the veneer also causes problems. Because exposure to air and ultraviolet light results in fading and degradation of colors, inlay artists and furniture conservators alike have often attempted to restore the original vibrant colors of wood by scraping away the outer layers to expose the color below.25 Repeated scraping can destroy the marquetry if the veneers become too thin, and this problem is magnified in the case of dyed materials. Yannick Chastang has noted that as few as three scrapings may be sufficient to almost completely erase delicate inlay work, but if dyes are unable to penetrate past the very surface of a veneer, it is likely the color could be lost after a single scraping treatment.26

The reconstruction of historical dye recipes could help shed light on gaps in current scholarship and open up new ways of understanding artisanal practices and material culture. For example, while VIS spectroscopy is an accurate and non-destructive process for analyzing the chemical composition of dyes used in inlay work, this method requires reference samples to compare with material being tested.27 We can never be completely certain of how any particular work looked at the time of making or of the original color palette. In their recent work on nineteenth-century synthetic wood dyes, Van Bommel and Fantini noted that simply identifying dyes through chemical analysis could not help them determine the original color of the work being studied, writing “methyl violet gives an intense purple colour while malachite green gives a strong green….[but] how purple, and how green?”28 To answer these questions, conservators and other scholars might be assisted by experimenting with the reconstruction of historical wood dyeing recipes. Reconstructing different dye recipes roughly contemporaneous with the work being conserved could provide reference samples and thus might help to identify ingredients used to color the wood and give some hints about the color palette.
