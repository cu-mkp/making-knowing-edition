Black Varnish for Armor

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/5qhq-ky36
← Back to Essays

Abstract

Historians know little about how armor was made black. This essay starts to fill the lacuna by examining a sixteenth-century recipe, “Black varnish for sword guard, bands for trunks, etc.,” on fol. 4v of Ms. Fr. 640. What was black varnish for armor? How was it made and applied? What ingredients and techniques were used? What purposes did it serve? Using a combination of reconstruction and textual research, this essay suggests that “Black varnish for sword guard” was used as an etching ground, a rust-protection coat, and a finish to impart luster and brilliance to armor and metals. The reconstructions underpinning this essay give insights into the varnish ingredients and show that success (or failure) in making and applying black varnish depended as much on the properties of the ingredients as on an artisan’s knowledge of heat, metals, and tools—as well as the skill of working with them.

Introduction

Seventeenth-century paintings like Jan Brueghel the Elder’s Milan Allegory of Fire (1608) (Fig. 1) or the portrait Holy Roman Emperor Charles V copied after Titian (Fig. 2) show that early modern black armor was not uncommon. Yet, while we have evidence of black armor’s existence, primary sources are silent on the making process, and historians have no knowledge of how armor was made black. This essay aims to start filling this lacuna in the history of arms and armor, and that of black armor in particular, by reconstructing a sixteenth-century recipe for a “black varnish for sword guard, bands for trunks, etc.,” described in BnF Ms. Fr. 640. This detailed recipe, which outlines the preparation of a varnish and its application to metal, is part of a larger cluster of entries devoted to the making of black varnishes that collectively cover fol. 4v of the manuscript. These seem to be the only sixteenth-century recipes for varnishes for armor now known to scholars. For later periods, sources show that varnishes for metal were not uncommon and that recipes for armor varnishing were circulating among metalworkers.

What was black varnish for armor? How was it made and applied? What ingredients and techniques were used in the process? What purposes did it serve? In the absence of written instructions on how to make armor black, I attempted to answer these questions by conducting two closely related exploratory experiments: first, the reconstruction of the “black varnish for sword guard, bands for trunks, etc” detailed on fol. 4v, and second, the reconstruction of a sixteenth-century German sword guard based on a model in the Wallace Collection in London and the subsequent varnishing of that guard according to the instructions on fol. 4v (Fig. 3). These two reconstructions and the insights gained from them are supported by textual research, particularly into sources from the history of arms and armor in later, better documented periods, as well as from sources that record early modern practices as diverse as locksmithing, bronze sculpting, painting, and even woodworking. This recipe thus sits at the intersection of a myriad of making processes.

Although the reconstructions for this essay did not yield definitive answers to the many questions raised by the recipe “Black varnish for sword guard,” my experiments afforded important insights about the recipe’s ingredients, the process of preparation and application, and its possible purposes. These insights open a path for further research and reconstructions—both of the varnish central to this essay and of others in Ms. Fr. 640.

Possible purposes of “Black varnish for sword guard”

The entry “Black varnish for sword guard” describes a process of preparing and applying an oil-based varnish. It can be divided into three steps. First, the oil is boiled with garlic, hog’s fennel, and bread crusts to degrease it. Next, the author-practitioner adds black pitch and grains of wheat and continues boiling it. Finally, when the pitch is melted and the oil has body, the varnish is applied on iron by heating the metal and painting the varnish on with a feather or a brush.

“Black varnish for sword guard” is one of three entries on fol. 4v devoted to varnishes on metal with marginal notes suggesting further variations. These entries all describe the making of black oil-based varnishes (using linseed, walnut, or spike lavender oil), but, in contrast to the varnish central to this essay, the other two are prepared without heating and one is intended as an etching ground. A marginal comment on fol. 4v directs the reader to the “chapter on furbishers,” specifically fols. 96v–97r, where the author-practitioner refers back to the black varnish on fol. 4v and includes a recipe for “Yellow varnish.”1 Although these entries do not shed light on the question of why armor was varnished black, they do tell us more about the different ingredients used in metal varnishes and that their method of preparation and purpose could vary. Moreover, they demonstrate the author-practitioner’s interest in varnishes for metal, its various types, and the furbisher’s craft.

Etching ground

Although “Black varnish for sword guard” is the only known sixteenth-century recipe for metal varnishes, a later source illustrates that such recipes were not exclusive to Ms. Fr. 640. In his La fidelle ouverture de l'art de serrurier (1627), the French locksmith Mathurin Jousse (c.1575–1645) also speaks of a vernis de fourbisseurs (varnish of furbishers).2 While historians of metalworking are familiar with Jousse’s work, and it has been discussed by, for example, the metalworker and historian of science Cyril Stanley Smith, the chapter in which Jousse records the varnish has not been studied by historians of arms and armor.3 Jousse’s discussion of “varnish of furbishers” is important not only for what it can tell us about the recipe on fol. 4v, but also for what it reveals about the possible transmission of knowledge among early modern metalworkers—in this case, locksmiths and furbishers—and the organization of some entries in Ms. Fr. 640 more generally.

Varnish of furbishers is mentioned in chapter ten of Jousse’s La fidelle ouverture, which opens with instructions on how to color iron and steel.4 After you have colored and heated your metal, Jousse explains, you can varnish your pieces—for the purposes of making fueillages (branchwork) or escritures blanches (white writings)—with a mixture of lead white and yellow wax or, alternatively, with varnish of furbishers, “made with walnut oil, white of leek, and galipot, which is a gum that one gets from the druggists [droguistes], all boiled together.”5 In contrast to the author-practitioner, Jousse does not apply the varnish of furbishers to armor and does not distinguish it as black. Nevertheless, there are important similarities between Jousse’s recipe and “Black varnish for sword guard” of Ms. Fr. 640. First, both recipes involve making oil-based varnishes and list the same ingredients: walnut oil (suggested by the author-practitioner as a cheap alternative to linseed oil), white of leek (similar to the garlic and onions), and galipot (recorded by the author-practitioner in the margin and proposed as an ingredient to use in “five or six lb of oil”). Second, they are both hot (as opposed to cold) varnishes, meaning that they are prepared by boiling the ingredients over a fire, that are then applied to heated metal. The similarities between the two sources give the impression that the “black varnish for sword guard” of Ms. Fr. 640, just like the varnish produced by Jousse’s recipe, could be used as an etching ground. The fact that the author-practitioner includes another entry devoted to “Varnish of iron etchers” on the same page (fol. 4v) adds weight to this assumption.6

Exactly how the locksmith Jousse learned about a furbisher’s varnish is unclear, yet evidence in Ms. Fr. 640 points to collaborations between furbishers and locksmiths. On fol. 95r, the author-practitioner explains that “the first thing the furbisher does when he gets his new blades is to draw out their tang, which he does himself, or has done by locksmiths or farriers, giving him some liard [i.e., a small-denomination coin] for his charcoal.”7 The author-practitioner’s remark and Jousse’s discussion of a furbisher’s varnish suggest early modern collaboration or outsourcing among various types of metalworkers. Jousse may have worked in this way with furbishers and shared varnish recipes. Be that as it may, Jousse’s knowledge of the varnish of furbishers illustrates that recipes like “Black varnish for sword guard” were circulating among early modern metalworkers and could have been used for metal objects other than armor.

Rust protection

While Jousse’s work leads us to think of the “black varnish for sword guard” in terms of etching practices, another set of sources related to arms, armor, and metalworking strongly suggests it could have served as a rust-protection layer. The process described in “Black varnish for sword guard” is essentially that of blackening armor. The concept of noircir (to blacken) in the context of armor making is defined by A. Paulin-Désormeaux in his Manuel de l'armurier, du fourbisseur et de l'arquebusier: ou Traité complet et simplifié de ces arts (1832): “To blacken: to heat pieces [of metal] after they have been forged and polished properly and to rub them with ox horn when they are very hot, so as to protect them against rust.”8 While Paulin-Désormeaux’s definition does not refer to varnish, and the author-practitioner does not use ox horn, both methods involve rubbing or applying a substance—ox horn or varnish—onto hot metal in order to blacken it. Moreover, though Manuel de l’armurier dates from the nineteenth century and, therefore, has no direct bearing on Ms. Fr. 640, when brought into conversation with earlier sources, Paulin-Désormeaux’s work becomes a later piece of evidence of a long-standing concern about protecting metal from rust. As Giambattista della Porta (1535–1615) notes in his widely disseminated text on natural magic, this concern can be traced as far back as Pliny.9

Artisans experimented with a variety of materials to protect metals from rust; oil, animal fats and proteins, and resins dominate the textual sources. In a discussion of iron, Della Porta writes that “many have laboured” to protect their iron works against “that poysoned corruption of rust,” and he listed a number of different methods:10

Pliny saith, That Iron is preserved from rust, by Ceruss, Gip, and liquid Pitch. But he shews not how Ceruss may be made: Yet those that know how to make Oyl from Ceruss without Vinegar, Iron being smeered therewith, is easily preserved from rust. Some anoynt the Iron with Deers suet, and so keep it free from rust, but I use the fat substance in the Hoofs of Oxen.11

Applying oil and animal fat to iron or steel appears to have been a common technique for metalworkers. The locksmith Jousse, for example, recommends the use of “sheep, or goat, or ox horn” and oil or tallow to blacken locks and prevent them from rusting:

Then you hold [the metal] over the fire and heat it so hot, that in putting sheep, or goat or ox horn on it, it burns and forms a black filth that attaches itself to the iron: after it has almost cooled you will apply a little bit of oil, or tallow, which will prevent the rust from being welcome there.12

In a subsequent chapter, Jousse also advises applying boiled and desalted olive oil to heated locks.13 While others also mixed lead filings with oil, Jousse argues that plain rosin “will preserve [locks] much longer than oil.”14 The author-practitioner also makes note of the locksmiths’ technique of applying rosin to metal on fol. 98r: “they heat [tinned nails and other works] & pass some rosin over the top, which coats it with a shiny skin like varnish.”

Yet, while it was common for metalworkers to apply oil, animal fats, and rosin to their works, Della Porta also reports that Pliny suggests liquid pitch. The work of the eighteenth-century chemist Pierre François Tingry (1743–1821) provides insight into the possible meanings of pitch and the possible purpose of “black pitch” in the author-practitioner’s “black varnish for sword guard.” Tingry’s detailed section devoted to this material, entitled “Black solid pitch with a smooth shining surface,” is worth quoting at length:

Black smooth pitch has often been confounded with naval pitch; and these denominations have even been considered as synonymous. Black pitch is a constant object of commerce; but naval pitch is prepared in the sea ports at the time when it is employed. Black pitch, which is transported in casks, is of a darker or lighter colour. It appears to be dry and friable, but flows and extends itself when exposed to a mean temperature. It is believed to be a compound of the more solid part of tar mixed over the fire with resin. Black pitch of a good quality, when broken by a sudden stroke, ought to be smooth, pulverulent, and exceedingly brilliant on the fracture. This kind of pitch is that used by locksmiths for varnishing articles which they wish to preserve from rust, such as locks, latches, and bolts destined for the doors of cellars, and of other places exposed to moisture. For this purpose, nothing is necessary but to heat the article, and to rub over it a piece of this pitch, partly wrapped up in paper, that it may not stick to the hand. The heat of the metal liquefies the pitch; and as soon as it dries the resinous part that has been applied, the result is a black, durable, and shining varnish.15

Tingry’s rich and instructive description bears a number of important similarities to the recipe on fol. 4v, most strikingly in the reference to “black pitch.” Like the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640, Tingry also speaks of a varnish (although not including oil) which is black, contains pitch, is applied to heated metal, and is dried over the fire. Most significant for this essay is that Tingry’s black varnish, which so strongly resembles that of Ms. Fr. 640, was used to keep metal “articles” free from rust. Indeed, varnishing metal was a well-known technique by which early modern metalworkers battled corrosion. According to sculpture historian Frits Scholten, the Dutch bronze sculptor Adriaen de Vries (1556–1626) applied oil-based varnishes to his metal sculptures to protect them from rust and add to their durability.16

In sum, by reading the entry “Black varnish for sword guard” in tandem with the various later sources, it seems probable that the recipe indicates an early modern metalworker’s larger interest in rust prevention on iron and steel.

Luster and brilliance

While the above-cited sources suggest that the author-practitioner’s “black varnish for sword guard” functioned as an anti-rust coating, they also imply that materials like black pitch and oil imparted lustre and brilliance to a varnish, thereby creating a visual effect on any armor to which it was applied.

As Tingry describes, applying black pitch to hot metal results in “a black, durable and shining varnish.”17 Similarly, the author-practitioner notes on fol. 98r that locksmiths rub rousine (rosin) on their iron works to create a “shiny skin like varnish.” Additionally, on fol. 95r, the author-practitioner remarks that furbishers sometimes applied “a drop or two of oil [on a sword] which they spread with the finger to give it luster.” Moreover, while Adriaen de Vries mainly used oil varnishes to protect the metal of his bronze sculptures, Scholten explains that “the combination of the already highly reflective surface of the polished metal and an additional sense of depth imparted by the varnish created the typically ‘lacquered’ look.”18 Likewise, in 1559 the sculptor Benvenuto Cellini (1500–1571) wrote that “there was still some gold missing [on his celebrated bronze Perseus], and it needed varnishing here and there and a number of other things that are really an intrinsic part of a statue’s finish.”19

Yet oil and black pitch were not the only ingredients that could impart a brilliant and lustrous quality to the varnish. A number of sources indicate that bread crusts, garlic, and onions could be used to enhance this effect as well. This might help us interpret the author-practitioner’s intention in “degreasing” (desgraisser) his oil by adding garlic, hog’s fennel, and bread crusts: “Take linseed oil or more cheaply, walnut oil, and rid it of grease with garlic & onions +hog’s fennel, some also add bread crusts.”20 My initial assumption that “degreasing” meant to rid the oil of fat, similar to the modern-day preparation of cold-pressed linseed oil that rids the oil of fatty acids, was informed by texts on the use of garlic and onions in oil varnishes. The Spanish painters Francisco Pacheco del Río (1564–1644) and Antonio Palomino (1653–1726) both mention the use of garlic in “drying” fat oils.21 Helpfully, Pierre François Tingry explains the effect of garlic on oil: “garlic ... possesses of itself a very drying quality. The garlic alone, or the juice of garlic, employed in the proper dose would render oil exceedingly drying.”22 Drying oils, such as linseed oil, are often used in varnishes to make them dry more quickly.23 As an alternative additive to the oil, practitioners sometimes appear to have used onions—which, like garlic and leek, belong to the allium family and thus likely possess a similar drying quality.24 Such a similarity could account for the author-practitioner crossing out “onions” and keeping the garlic in his recipe: one drying additive may have sufficed to degrease or dry the oil.25

Additionally, two lines into the entry on fol. 4v, in a sublinear addition written directly below the instructions to “rid [the oil] of grease with garlic & onions +hog’s fennel,” the author-practitioner mentions that “some also add bread crusts.” The position of this addition on the page—that is, the structural arrangement of the recipe—and, more specifically, its proximity to the instruction to degrease the oil, led me to assume a direct and meaningful relation between the garlic, hog’s fennel, and bread crusts—in other words, I took all ingredients to be “degreasers.”

What such “degreasing” could do for a varnish is laid out in nineteenth-century cabinet maker Albert Albrest’s L’art de l’ébéniste (1828). Albrest explains that adding burned, carbonized bread crusts to boiled linseed and turpentine oil would purify (purifier) or discolor (décolorer) the liquid. Applying an oil thus prepared to a piece of wood would “better fill [its] pores, [and render] the varnish that it [had] to receive more brilliant and more smooth.”26 Based on this textual research on the properties of the ingredients, it is thus plausible to argue that the garlic, onions, hog’s fennel, and bread crusts would not only rid the oil of grease and render it faster drying when applied, but, in doing so, simultaneously—like the oil and black pitch—add to the luster and brilliance of the varnish.

By bringing the recipe “Black varnish for sword guard” into conversation with other period sources, we can conclude that it might have served three possible purposes: first, it could have been used as an etching ground; second, it might have served as protection against rust; and finally, it may have added luster and brilliance to metals, thus enhancing their visual effects.

While textual research allows us to make plausible claims, a reconstruction of the varnish can test these hypotheses. The next section of this essay recounts my reconstruction of the black varnish for armor on fol. 4v, its application to a reproduction sixteenth-century German sword guard that I made, and the historical insights gained from both. While this practical labor did not yield any definitive answers and many uncertainties still surround the entry “Black varnish for sword guard,” my experiments afforded insights about the recipe’s key components, such as the ingredients and the processes of preparation and application. They also strengthen the textual insights about the function of the varnish.

Reconstructing “Black varnish for sword guard, bands for trunks, etc.”

The following account of my reconstruction is divided into three parts: the making of the “black varnish for sword guard” following instructions on fol. 4v, the reconstruction of a sixteenth-century sword guard (Fig. 3), and the application of the varnish to it. This varnishing process, in turn, consisted of four separate trials: the first three tested different ways to heat and varnish flat pieces of metal, using either a hot plate or a blow torch, and the fourth trial applied the varnish to the reconstructed sword guard.

Making the varnish

The ingredients listed on fol. 4v are linseed oil, black pitch (I used colophony), and wheat grains.27 Following the instructions, I boiled the linseed oil and then added the colophony and the wheat grains. I then heated the metal sheets (and later the sword guard) and applied the varnish. This process gave me mixed insights into the question of the “black” in “black varnish for sword guard,” suggesting that the color resulted from both ingredients in the varnish and the application of heat to metal.

Ingredients

My assumption prior to making the varnish was that the colophony (used as an approximation of black pitch) would serve as a black colorant.28 When I had pounded the colophony in a mortar, however, the resulting powder was of a light yellow tint, which led me to reconsider my hypothesis (Fig. 4, Fig. 5, and Fig. 6). My reconstruction confirmed this doubt: when I added the colophony to the hot oil, there was little change in color visible and the liquid only turned from a light to a dark amber at best, instead of to the expected black (Fig. 7 and Fig. 8). It was unclear at this point if this was due to a mistake I had made in the amount (i.e., more colophony was necessary), or if I should have chosen a different material for black pitch.

The addition of wheat grains to the oil mixture was dramatic. While heating the mixture, and after having thoroughly mixed in the colophony, I gradually added the grains. Upon contact with the oil, the wheat grains popped, like popcorn, and produced a yellow-golden foam that swiftly rose to the top of the beaker and, after a few seconds, if no further grains were added, slowly descended again (its color darkening in the process) (Fig. 9, Fig. 10, and Fig. 11). The popped and carbonized grains did not appear to disintegrate. Rather, they floated in the golden foam layer atop the oil mixture (Fig. 12).29 Intriguingly, and to my surprise, almost immediately after adding the first grains, the oil changed color: it no longer looked translucent and became increasingly blacker—probably as a result of carbonized grain particles mixing in with the oil and other ingredients. None of the texts I examined mentioned wheat grains, thus I did not (and still do not) know what role they play, although my experience shows that they impart a black color to the varnish.

Oil may have been an additional material factor in generating a black varnish. A modern technique for blackening armor employed by blacksmith Jeff Wasson is to rub a layer of olive oil onto the cold surface of a metal plate and heat it from both sides with a blow torch (Vid. 1). The result is a black finish (Fig. 13).

This technique is similar to that described by the author-practitioner in his recipe for “Yellow varnish” on fol. 96v:

One whitens very strongly & one cleans the guards well. Next, one applies walnut olive oil all over them with a feather while they are cold, and next turns them over a good charcoal fire that is on the furbishers’ furnace, without them touching the charcoal or the ash. And when they do not smoke anymore, it is done.

Like Jeff Wasson, the author-practitioner also applies oil to cold metal and heats it over the fire. Given the similarities in technique, it is likely that the varnish the author-practitioner describes here will also turn black when heated. The “yellow” in the title may refer to the color of the varnish—i.e., of the oil—before it is heated over the fire. The difference with “the black varnish for sword guard” on fol. 4v might be that the latter would already have been black in and of itself.30 Speaking of yellow as opposed to black varnish might have been a useful way to distinguish between two varnishes that would both turn black over the heat. This technique of using heat to blacken oil applied to metal suggests the possible multipurpose nature of the oil in the varnish recipe on fol. 4v, but it also points to another component that could help color the varnished metal black: heat.

Heat

The four trials devoted to heating the metal and applying the varnish revealed the key role of heat in producing a varnish with a black finish. In the first test, a metal piece was heated over a hot plate, removed from the heat source prior to applying the varnish, and not reheated after (Fig. 14). Because the sheet was held over the hot plate with pliers, only the tip of the metal was in full contact with the heat source and the piece was thus heated unevenly (Fig. 15). As a result, the metal soon displayed the entire gamut of colors that results from heating steel—ranging from yellow or gold where it was coolest to purple and blue where it was in direct contact with the heat (Fig. 16 and Fig. 17).31 Most importantly, after having applied the varnish, the resulting coat was not black but rather translucent and irregular, and the colored steel was still visible through it (Fig. 18). So while the grains helped blacken the varnish in the process of preparation, they apparently did not suffice to ensure a black coat once applied to the metal.

Trial two determined that heat was instrumental to blackening the varnish. In this experiment, pieces of sheet metal were heated lying flat in a cast iron pan. The pan was kept on the heat source both prior to and after the metal pieces in it had been varnished (Fig. 19). As in trial one, the metal plates changed color in the process of heating. Likely due to the more direct and even heat, the metal did not show a rainbow of colors all at once, but instead revealed them more or less one by one during the heating process (compare Fig. 19, Fig. 20, and Fig. 21). The coat of varnish was also affected by the more direct and even heat. It gradually darkened in the heating process and the coat was no longer translucent and irregular, as in trial one, but had turned into a dark brown, smooth, and shiny finish that did not reveal the colored metal underneath (Fig. 22, Fig. 23, and Fig. 24). Nevertheless, the varnish, although noticeably darker than in trial one, was still not black.

The third and fourth experiments revealed that what made the varnish black was the intensity of the heat used. In trials three and four, a metal plate and the sword guard were heated over the flame of a blow torch, held with tongs and a clamp, respectively, and were only briefly removed from the heat source for the purpose of varnishing but quickly reheated thereafter (Fig. 25 and Fig. 26). After only a few seconds (as opposed to minutes with the hot plate), the steel changed color—a fact that illustrated the difference in temperature between the flame of a blow torch and the heat radiating off a hot plate (Fig. 27). Only seconds after I had applied the varnish and begun reheating the metal with the torch, the varnish turned from a yellow-brown translucent liquid into a dark, opaque, and shiny black coat (Fig. 28, Fig. 29, and Fig. 30).

In sum, my trials determined that heat, and probably also the wheat grains and oil, were key in producing a black varnish. In the next section, I will show that the techniques and tools by which the varnish was applied to the metal, and the knowledge of how to use them, were equally important in producing black varnished armor.

Applying the varnish - Techniques

Towards the end of the recipe on fol. 4v the author practitioner writes: “Then, to varnish, place your iron over a low charcoal fire & apply your varnish with a feather or a brush. And when you see that it no longer smokes, it is done and your varnish is dry.”32 Although these instructions are straightforward, they do not explain to the reader exactly when to apply the varnish, i.e., when the metal is hot enough to paint the varnish on it. Jousse considered the general question of “knowing when the metal is hot” of such importance to the practice of locksmithing that he devotes one of the opening chapters of his La fidelle ouverture to the subject, arguing it was “the thing which one must learn the first.”33 Although this chapter does not actually tell us how to recognize when metal is hot enough to be varnished, the central role that Jousse gives to heating the metal does help us make sense of the incomplete instructions on fol. 4v. Perhaps the author-practitioner took such knowledge for granted, for it was essential to any metalworker’s craft. Speaking about the metallurgy of early modern swords, Alan Williams notes that “there would have been no accurate method for measuring time, and only empirical methods for measuring temperature” in the heating of metal.34 The French cabinet maker André-Jacob Roubo (1739–1791) describes one of these methods: “then [the metal piece] will be heated on a steel plate placed on a forge, observing that it is heated evenly everywhere, and to such a degree of heat that one cannot easily touch it with the top of the hand.”35 Roubo here instructs his readers to employ their bodily senses—vision, to see if the metal is heated evenly, and touch, to feel the temperature of the metal to determine if it is sufficiently hot to varnish. Jousse uses a similar technique to heat, color, and varnish metal:

after you have polished your work, you take hot ashes, and passed first through the crible, putting your work inside, and you will let it heat there until it takes a color that seems good to you ... If you do not have ashes to give it the color you want, heat some rather large iron, and put your polished work on top. Soon you will see it take the colors that I have said above ... After your iron has been turned blue, violet, or another color, you take varnish, then you heat your iron a little and apply a little of your varnish on top, and let it cool off a bit.36

Like Roubo, Jousse also suggests using hot iron (as an alternative to hot ashes) to heat metal pieces for varnishing and ascribes an equally important role to observation of the changing color of metal as an indicator of heat.37

Such sensory techniques, and especially the color change, proved useful to my reconstruction. While the author-practitioner does not inform us exactly when to apply the varnish, he does explain on fol. 4v when it is done: “And when you see that it no longer smokes, it is done and your varnish is dry.” I reverse engineered his instructions and reasoned that the metal plates would be hot enough when I could see smoke coming off the varnish upon applying it to the metal. My first trial showed that the varnish first began to smoke when the metal started to turn blue (Vid. 2). That color, rather than a temperature taken using a laser thermometer, was then used as an indication of temperature for the remaining three trials. Yet, even when the varnish in trial one had stopped smoking, it was not dry—as the author-practitioner says it would be—but rather tacky (Fig. 18). As with blackening the varnish in the previous section, heat proved instrumental here, too. While in trial one, heat was only applied before varnishing the metal, in trial two, the metal was heated prior to and after varnishing. When I reheated the varnish after it had been painted onto the metal, it not only produced more smoke, but importantly, when it stopped smoking, it was no longer tacky (compare Fig. 18 and Fig. 30; Vid. 3), and the result was a shiny, smooth film (Fig. 30). The two trials with the blow torch, furthermore, revealed that the intensity of the heat source does not just blacken the varnish, but produces more (or less) smoke (Fig. 31, Fig. 32).

This outcome illustrates how reconstructions give insight into qualitative statements that indicate material states or stages in the process. When the author-practitioner says “it is done and your varnish is dry,” he means it is no longer tacky. Thus, my reconstruction afforded proof of concept, since the varnish was indeed dry when it stopped smoking. It is important to note that control of heat, which Jousse held to be central knowledge for the metalworker, was crucial in my reconstruction process. Not only did heat change the color of the metal and darkened the tint of its varnish, it also “dried” the varnish layer.

Applying the varnish - Tools

The tools and their use in applying varnish also contributed to the finished appearance. The instructions on fol. 4v specify to varnish using “a feather or a brush.” In the first trial, I used a feather to apply the varnish (Fig. 33). In experiments two to four, I employed a 1-inch paint brush (Fig. 28, Fig. 34, and Fig. 35). Although the heat of the metal liquified the varnish (illustrating another function of heat), which made it easier to apply, I struggled to spread the mixture evenly and smoothly across the steel plate with the feather. This could have been due to the fact that I simply had too little experience to control the feather or that the varnish was too thick.

The use of the brush in my following three trials facilitated the application. When I ran the brush over the hot metal, I felt more in control, and it was easier to cover parts of the sheet with varnish. Yet, while the brush noticeably simplified this process, the resulting coat showed occasional traces of brush strokes, which caused an irregular finish (Fig. 36). Although we do not know what the varnished surface of a period sword guard would have looked like, it seems unlikely that an uneven, patchy finish bearing clear traces of tools or techniques used in the application process would have been desirable (both from an aesthetic and a practical point of view). Tingry’s note that locksmiths’ black varnish made with pitch has “a smooth shining surface” supports this assumption.38 Since the author-practitioner does not comment on this particular part of the process other than by saying one needs to use a feather or brush, and I only noticed the brush strokes after the varnish had dried, I could only hypothesize about what caused the strokes and how to prevent them in future trials. My assumption was that the varnish was still too thick or viscous, and I had not added enough oil or boiled it for too long.

Roubo’s instructions for varnishing metal with a brush offer one other possible clarification of (and solution to) this problem. Moreover, they illustrate that producing a smooth varnish was a common practical problem. After having outlined the process of how to prepare the “Varnish suitable to varnish and gild copper and other metals” and the method of heating the metal pieces, Roubo explains how to apply the varnish:

then one puts the varnish in a small vase, in which one dips a brush of very soft badger hairs, and after having wiped it a little on the edge of the vase, one swiftly carries it over to the piece [of metal] without leaning on it too much. One has to do it rather quickly so that the reprises do not appear, and there are no waves or marks on the varnished piece.39

That such “waves” or “marks” were undesirable is shown by the fact that Roubo also describes a method for removing them, in case they were still visible in the varnished piece.40 In light of Roubo’s instructions, I may have used the wrong brush, been too slow in applying the varnish, or exerted too much pressure (or a combination of all three).

Although repeated testing might help identify the cause of the problem, my struggle to produce a smooth varnish coat vividly illustrated an important characteristic of how-to recipes—the omission of important steps. In Ms. Fr. 640, this might suggest that the author-practitioner was not familiar with potential problems in making or applying black varnishes, or that he assumed enough basic knowledge or embodied expertise in his readers to adjust accordingly. My practical struggles, rather than close reading or textual research alone, provided insight into what kinds of knowledge and skills it took to varnish a sword guard.

Varnishing different types of metal

In the previous section I discussed the importance of mastering the heating of metal as well as the skilled use of tools to the production of a smooth, shiny, black varnish. This section will argue it also required understanding of how to heat different types of metal.

The sword guard I reconstructed consisted of two separately made pieces that were joined together at the center by rivets (Fig. 37, Fig. 38, and Fig. 39). These parts were made of two different types of metal of varying degrees of thickness. The clam or shell shape was rather thin (16 gage) made of 1050 modern mild steel, cold-worked (that is, not heat-treated) and unpolished.41 The iron cross guard was significantly thicker, heated in a forge several times, and roughly polished. These differences in thickness had important consequences for the heat treatment and varnishing process. Whereas the thin steel clam swiftly turned from golden to blue, it took much longer for the iron cross guard to reach the desired temperature for varnishing—even with the intense heat of the blow torch.

While the thin clam was quickly heated, this caused problems when applying the varnish. Even before it had stopped smoking, the varnish began to blister and scale off and some parts of the clam started to show the metal underneath (Fig. 40). Unlike the results of trials one and two, the varnish in this fourth experiment turned black, but its finish was highly irregular and quite rough. By contrast, when I varnished the thick iron bar, its black surface appeared more uniform than that of the clam (Fig. 41). According to Jeff Wasson, this might be because thick metal, although taking longer to reach a high temperature, retains heat and cools more slowly, which might have been advantageous to the process of varnishing.42

That solving (or preventing) this problem was a matter of experience, and required skill and knowledge of how best to heat metal of varying degrees of thickness, can be shown by comparing the finish of my varnished test plate from trial three (Fig. 42) with that of the varnished guard (trial four) (Fig. 40). It was because of my failure in trial three to produce a smooth black surface that I realized I should probably keep the metal plate further away from the heat source, reduce the length of the heating process, and turn off the blow torch perhaps even before the varnish stopped smoking. I then tried to put these insights into practice in trial four, when I varnished the guard. Even though the surface of the varnished guard looked less blistered than that of my test plate from trial three, it was still irregular and parts of the varnish had nevertheless been burned off.

My struggles revealed that varnishing a sword guard was not—as I thought when reading the instructions—a procedure of simply heating your metal and applying the varnish. Rather it was a complex process that required highly specific knowledge about heating metal: the proper heat source, the appropriate length of time to heat the metal (both prior to and after varnishing), how far to hold the metal from the heat, and how different types of metal and the varnishes applied to them respond differently to heat. While all such components are necessary, the essential factor to successfully produce a smooth, lustrous, black varnish is to combine all that knowledge and their corresponding actions through skilled practice that can only be attained through repeated experience.

Varnishing different metal shapes

The heating of the sword guard, with its peculiar form, also raised important questions and posed problems that had not arisen when I heated the metal sheets. Unlike the relatively flat test plates, the sword guard was more three-dimensional: it consisted of a hollow or curved clam and a flat iron cross guard (Fig. 37, Fig. 38, and Fig. 39). Even before the start of my final reconstruction, it was clear that it would be problematic, if not impossible, to heat and varnish the various surface areas of this complex structure simultaneously and evenly—a factor which had proven key to producing a smooth black finish. One of the questions this process thus raised was how artisans would have heated their sword guards, as the author-practitioner instructs on fol. 4v “over a low charcoal fire.” The use of tongs proved problematic: the weight of the guard made it difficult to hold at a specific angle; gravity kept pulling it down, making it harder to heat the metal evenly and effectively. While I ultimately managed to heat and varnish half of the guard (one side of the clam and one side of the cross bar) by locking it into place with a clamp (Fig. 26), this process still required tremendous control over my wrist and arm—and thus bodily strength and dexterity—to hold up the guard and facilitate the heating process.

As my reconstructions showed, it was crucial to heat the metal evenly in order to produce a smooth black varnish. Similarly, as noted above, Roubo warned his readers to “observ[e] that [a metal piece you want to varnish] is heated evenly everywhere.”43 The tongs hamper this process, as illustrated by Fig. 43 and Fig. 44, where the differences in color surrounding the tongs indicate that the metal was not heated evenly. It is unlikely tongs would have been used, however this leaves open the question of how artisans could have held the armor over the heat and painted on the varnish. A closer look at the meaning of foeu legier de charbons (low charcoal fire) in the recipe on fol. 4v, might help answer this question. To heat and varnish the guard, the author-practitioner instructs the reader to “place your iron over a low charcoal fire.” From a modern perspective, we might interpret “fire” here as indicating flames. However, it would have been difficult for artisans to control the flow of heat from such a fire and direct it toward the metal, especially with flames moving in all directions. Accordingly, “charcoal fire” cannot mean fire of flames. Roubo and Jousse support this claim, as both instruct their readers to use either hot steel or ashes (cendres chauldes), as opposed to a flaming fire to heat, color, and varnish metal. Based on this textual evidence and my practical experience, I argue that the “charcoal fire” on fol. 4v is equivalent to but not identical to hot steel or ashes.

While hot ashes are easily imagined, hot steel might require some explanation. The anonymous author of Von Stahel und Eysen, a German booklet on metallurgy, published in 1532 “for all armorers, goldsmiths, silversmiths, engravers of seals and dies, and all other skilled artisans who use steel and iron,” describes such an equivalent in a recipe for softening iron: “take the piece that is too hard and carefully hold it over glowing charcoal until it has become hot. Rub it with tallow and let the tallow dry away on it over the heat; it will then acquire the correct hardness.”44 The word this text uses is glut (glow). My work with Jeff Wasson showed that iron or steel can also be heated until it becomes glowing red-hot, much like glowing charcoal (Fig. 45).

Based on these examples I would argue that by “place your iron over a low charcoal fire,” the author-practitioner means over glowing charcoal. The legier in foeu legier de charbons may refer to the intensity of the heat source and imply that the charcoal was still glowing but no longer red-hot—and thus of a low, but still sufficient heat (compare the color of the metal in Fig. 46 with that in Fig. 47).

One problem remains: the means to support a sword guard over a glowing charcoal fire. Whereas Roubo instructs his readers to place the metal directly onto the heat source (in this case, the hot steel plate), the author-practitioner, advises the reader not to do so in his recipe “Yellow varnish” on fol. 96v, which resembles “Black varnish for sword guard” in its method of application:

One whitens very strongly & one cleans the guards well. Next, one applies walnut olive oil all over them with a feather while they are cold, and next turns them over a good charcoal fire that is on the furbishers’ furnace, without them touching the charcoal or the ash. And when they do not smoke anymore, it is done.45

My reconstruction showed that too much direct heat was detrimental to the varnishing process and caused the varnish to blister, supporting the advice of the author-practitioner. Although this practical problem seems to imply that tongs (or other instruments) might still be required to hold the guard over glowing charcoal (as opposed to placing it on top as Roubo suggests), the example of the Italian metallurgist Vannoccio Biringuccio (c.1480–c.1539) shows that this could be done without tools. In describing a method of making steel in his De la Pirotechnia (1540), Biringuccio explains a technique in which an “empty space is filled with charcoal and around it is made a circle of stones or other soft rocks which hold up the broken iron [that one wants to melt].”46 Although Biringuccio is not concerned with heating metal for the purpose of varnishing, it is nevertheless likely that armor makers would have used a similar method to heat and varnish guards, i.e., by resting them on stones or bricks encircling glowing charcoal. Such a method would probably also help isolate or contain the heat radiating off the charcoal and direct it upwards (and thus control it). While such a method may have worked for heating a sword guard with a complex shape, it still seems puzzling to the inexperienced reconstructor how artisans would have varnished guards with an even more complex shape.

The recipe “Black varnish without fire, without disassembling the harnesses or removing the bands from trunks,” also on fol. 4v, provides a possible answer to this question. As the title of this entry suggests, artisans might have varnished multi-shaped sword guards by disassembling them first, removing the rivets that joined them, and then heating and varnishing the various pieces of metal separately. However, the phrasing of the title also suggests that such taking apart of armor would have been problematic—perhaps because it was time consuming and difficult to put armor back together after it had been varnished—thus cold varnishes might have been a useful alternative because they could be applied more easily, without disassembling anything. Indeed, the fact that furbishers would varnish armor after it had already been assembled suggests it might have served as a maintenance technique and that varnishing could be repeated as needed. Roubo’s statement that “if at any time one would want to varnish pieces which, because of their form or any other reason, could not be heated, one would varnish them cold,” solves the problem of how to heat complex shapes.47

Cold varnish raises questions of how and to what extent fire affects the properties of a varnish, especially since, as we saw in the example of Jousse, heat caused a material like animal horn to “[attach] itself to the iron.”48 By contrast, it seems that the cold varnish would form a layer of paint sitting on top rather than attaching to the metal. Do the different ways in which these hot and cold black varnishes interact with the metal have an impact on the functions they were meant to serve? While this question exceeds the scope of this essay, it is worth exploring further.

Insights from reconstruction: possible purposes of the “black varnish for sword guard”

A steel plate from trial two, which was half-varnished, heated in the cast iron pan, and ended up with a dark brown finish, was subjected to a rust test by placing it outside in the rain for three days (Fig. 48). While the uncovered metal had begun to corrode and showed rust stains, the varnish held up in the rain and appears to have protected the metal underneath (Compare Fig. 49 and Fig. 50). Although more experiments are needed—especially with the black varnish produced in trial four—this outcome certainly adds weight to the hypothesis that the varnish was used to protect metal from rusting.

(Vid. 4) shows that the varnish is highly reflective and refracts light, which may indicate that “black varnish for sword guard” could have been applied to armor for its visual effects as well.

Conclusion

This essay has explored the purpose, making, and application of the “black varnish for sword guard, bands for trunks, etc” described in Ms. Fr. 640. Textual research shows that this recipe was not unique, and that black varnishes for metal were used beyond the context of armor as well. Primary sources suggest that “black varnish for sword guard” could have been used as an etching ground, a rust-protection coat, and a finish to impart luster and brilliance to armor. My reconstruction gives support to the last two hypotheses, but additional reconstructions are needed to test the black varnish of Ms. Fr. 640 as an etching ground. Furthermore, my experiments have shown that the wheat grains served as colorants, have helped clarify the meaning of sec (dry; i.e., not tacky), and provided proof of concept for the indication “when you see that it no longer smokes, it is done and your varnish is dry.” Further research, however, is needed to determine the precise meaning and composition of the ingredient “black pitch” and the meaning and purposes of desgraisser (to degrease) in the recipe. Additional reconstructions, using glowing charcoal or charcoal fire for instance, could also help clarify the meaning of foeu legier de charbons (low charcoal fire) on fol. 4v.49

Above all, my reconstructions show that success (or failure) to make a smooth, shiny, black varnish cannot be reduced to a single component—that is, ingredients, materials, tools, or heat. Each of these components, when used in the right way and to the right degree, could contribute to the finished character of the varnish. For example, although heat could potentially help blacken the varnish, “dry” it, and make it smooth and shiny, this outcome seems to have depended just as much on the inclusion of oil, wheat grains, and black pitch. Moreover, insufficient heat left the varnish brown and tacky, while too much and too direct heat caused it to scale off. More direct heat, however, was needed to varnish thick metal, whereas thinner plates had to be heated and varnished with more care. Similarly, although a brush made it easier to apply the varnish, the process of application also depended on the temperature of the metal. Even with sufficient heat, the application could leave brush strokes if the varnish was applied too slowly or with too much pressure.

In other words, my reconstruction demonstrated that making a smooth, shiny, black varnish for sword guards was not a straightforward, linear accumulation of steps consisting simply of mixing ingredients, adding heat, and applying a liquid onto metal. Rather, it was the outcome of a complex set of techniques and practiced actions that had to converge in just the right way. Making “black varnish for sword guard” and applying it to armor depended as much on the properties of the ingredients as on an artisan’s knowledge of the requisite heat, metals, and tools as well as the skill to work with them.
