To Corrode and Dissolve: Making Aquafortis in Ms. Fr. 640

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/z4f5-5m60
← Back to Essays

Abstract

This essay explores the ambiguous meanings of aquafortis, of its constituent ingredients, and of the units of measurement listed in “For making aquafortis” on fol. 75r. By situating the recipe within a broader historical context, aquafortis can be understood as a substance with the ability to interact with solids, and perform actions such as dissolve or corrode metals. Comparison of the recipe in Ms. Fr. 640 with contemporary textual sources gives insight into the variety of uses for aquafortis and the various ingredients for making it. This indicates that the term copperas refers to any sulfate (vitriol), and solle refers to salt (NaCl) rather than saltpeter. The genres of texts containing aquafortis recipes reveal that the recipes in Ms. Fr. 640 bear comparison with a variety of literary traditions: alchemical manuscripts, metallurgical treatises, treatises on arts, and books of secrets. Finally, the recipe for aquafortis on fol. 75r was reconstructed, which showed the efficacy of the recipe and its correspondence with contemporary expectations about aquafortis.

Aquafortis, or “strong water,” has been commonly interpreted by historians to mean nitric acid. In fact, the term “aqua” was used in the medieval and early modern period to denote any kind of liquid; thus the meaning of aquafortis is often ambiguous, and does not solely denote nitric acid, as this essay makes clear.1 Aquafortis eludes precise categorization due to its variety of uses, as well as the variety of recipes for making it.

Numerous entries in BnF Ms. Fr. 640 require the use of aquafortis (eau fort). These include “Color of damascene steel on knives” (fol. 11v) which describes rubbing steel with aquafortis mixed with clay; recipes “For eating away and dissolving entirely pure gold” (fol. 104r) and “Augmenting the color of gold” (fol. 166v); “Very light and hollow work of gold,” (fol. 156r), in which the aquafortis should “eat away” silver, but leave pure gold unharmed; and even as a suggestion for how not to kill “snakes for molding” (fol. 109v). The use of aquafortis was common in the laboratory of the medieval and early modern alchemist, as well as in the workshops of many practitioners working with metals.

On fol. 75r, the author-practitioner includes a recipe for aquafortis, which instructs to “take a half-septier of vinegar with one ounce of verdigris, & one ounce of copperas & one ounce of solle, then put them together in a glass vial.” The point of departure for this essay is an investigation of the materials contained in this aquafortis recipe by comparison with other contemporary sources. These include alchemical manuscripts, books of secrets, and metallurgical treatises, which reveal a wide variety of recipes, uses, and literary genres that incorporated recipes for aquafortis. By placing the recipe within a broader historical context, we can make better sense of aquafortis and its ingredients in the manuscript and enrich our understanding of the manuscript as a whole.

The second part of the essay describes the reconstruction of the recipe for aquafortis in the manuscript. In order to determine the efficacy of the recipe, the reconstruction connects with other entries in the manuscript. “Color of damascene steel on knives” (fol. 11v) in particular provides an internal reference to test the reconstructed aquafortis. It describes the application of aquafortis on steel, allowing us to assess the qualities of different kinds of aquafortis through their effects in comparison with their presumed purpose.

The recipe for aquafortis on fol. 75r requires a demi-septier of vinegar, an ounce of copperas, verdigris, and solle. These materials raise several questions both independently and in relation to one another. What the author-practitioner refers to as “copperas,” for example, is ambiguous, and the meaning of the word solle, which does not repeat again in the manuscript, is unclear. Other ingredients such as saltpeter, common in the making of aquafortis, are missing. Furthermore, the interactions between these materials raise the question of what the resulting aquafortis is. No less a matter for investigation are the units of measure the author-practitioner used in the recipe.

As units of measure varied regionally, and even within regions, during the ancien régime, the meaning of the measures “demi-septier” (demy sestier) and “ounce” (once) on fol. 75r pose a difficulty.2 The units that came into use in the French-speaking regions of Europe were derived from the Roman Imperial system of once and livre, translated into setier and muid.3 The setier was used both in liquid and solid measurements, which were traditionally separated due to the difference in weight between water and grains of the same volume.4 Contemporary practitioners were aware that this could be a problem. For example, Vannoccio Biringuccio, in his De la pirotechnia (1540), admonishes his readers to adjust their weights and measures to the local custom.5

The distinction between septier and demi-septier is made clear in Randle Cotgrave’s Dictionary of the French and English Tongues (1611) where septier is given as the equivalent of eight Parisian pintes.6 The demi-septier de vin was not, however, half of a septier as its name suggests. As Cotgrave further explains, the demi-septier de vin is equivalent to half a chopine, which in turn is a Parisian half-pint, meaning that the demi-setier is roughly 250ml.7

The oldest known recipes for aquafortis can be found in the Liber fornacum and in the Liber de inventione veritatis which survive in thirteenth-century Latin manuscripts from Italy.8These texts contain a variety of recipes which have been understood to produce nitric acid.9 They refer to aquafortis as aqua nostra dissolutiva (“our dissolving water”), reflecting its properties as a dissolving substance.10 These recipes, however, differ markedly from the author-practitioner’s instructions. The Liber fornacum and Liber de inventione veritatis provide not only the first written recipes for aquafortis but also a practical context.11

A recipe from the Liber fornacum, understood to produce nitric acid, includes instructions to distil one pound of red mercury mixed with one pound of saltpeter and one pound of vitriol.12 The recipe in the Liber de inventione veritatis calls for distilling one pound of vitriol of cyprus mixed with half a pound of saltpeter and a quarter pound of alum.13 Here, vitriol of cyprus refers to copper(II) sulfate, also known as blue vitriol for its blue color, and famously found in rich copper mines in Cyprus.14 This last recipe for “water that can dissolve” metals can be made even “stronger” by adding sal ammoniac.15 Nonetheless, variations of aquafortis made with alum (often a substitute for vitriol) and red mercury (most likely referring to mercury oxide, HgO, which produces a dry red powder) were less common. The two other ingredients—saltpeter and vitriol—were far more common in many subsequent recipes for aquafortis, though saltpeter is not used in the recipe on fol. 75r.16

The recipe for aquafortis in the Liber de inventione veritatis is comparable to another recipe in the Liber fornacum. The recipe in the Liber fornacum instructs to distil “filings of Venus” (copper(II) sulfate) with common salt and calcined alum in vinegar.17 Both the “filings of Venus” and an alternative, “filings from Mars,” are found in the Liber de inventione veritatis, where they refer to copper(II) sulfate (Venus) and iron sulfate (Mars), both of which are called vitriols. These two compounds in the Liber fornacum recipe provide important clues for our understanding of the materials in the recipe for aquafortis on fol. 75r in Ms. Fr. 640.

The use of salt and the absence of saltpeter, as in the entry in Ms. Fr. 640, fol. 75v, occurs in a variety of other sources, such as in Georgius Agricola’s De re metallica (1556), a treatise on mining and metallurgy which emphasizes direct observation and experience in combination with ancient sources.18 In the tenth book of De re metallica, Agricola outlines numerous recipes for aqua valens (“vigorous water”), distilled for the purpose of parting gold and silver. In the first recipe, “the use of which is common and general, there is one libra of vitriol and as much salt, added to a third of a libra of spring water.”19 This chemical mixture would, in fact, not produce nitric acid, but rather hydrochloric acid, due to the lack of saltpeter.20 Another recipe for aquafortis in De re metallica differentiates saltpeter from salt and “contains two librae of powder of baked bricks, one libra of vitriol, likewise one libra of saltpetre, a handful of salt, and three-quarters of a libra of spring water.”21 These two recipes, one containing only salt and the other including saltpeter, confirm that “salt” did not necessarily mean “saltpeter” in aquafortis recipes such as the one in Ms. Fr. 640. Furthermore, the different solutions produced in the process of distillation allow us to begin to see how aquafortis did not refer to any one specific chemical compound.

Biringuccio discusses the distillation of aquafortis and also proposes a recipe for an aqua acuta (“acute water”)—an acid for parting gold and silver that calls for one part well-refined saltpeter and three times as much well-washed rock alum.22 He adds that some recipes might vary the quantities of the saltpeter, or add vitriol or arsenic, and alchemists might even add salt.23 These ingredients are distilled, and the process of distillation as well as the workings of an alembic (a device with two vessels for distillation) are all outlined, similar to the entry in the tenth book of De re metallica.

Agricola’s vitriol requirement is ambiguous. Vitriols were mainly distinguished according to their colors: the green powder of iron(II) sulfate gained the name green vitriol, the blue powder of copper(II) sulfate was labeled blue vitriol, and the white powder of zinc sulfate was called white vitriol.24 Biringuccio establishes these very same color divisions for vitriols in Pirotechnia and defines vitriol as a mineral substance found in sulphur and used to make oils and corrosive acids.25 He distinguishes between those vitriols he mentions in the chapter “concerning vitriol and its ore” and a vitriol “found reduced to its ultimate purity by Nature herself without art. This is a very powerful vitriol and is not called vitriol but copperas.”26 Copperas, according to Biringuccio, is therefore a type of vitriol in pure form, bringing us closer to the meaning of copperas in Ms. Fr. 640.27

The author-practitioner himself makes a similar distinction in “A way for molding excellently with water” (fol. 83v), in which he writes “do the same with vitriol & copperas, which, once well calcined, reduce in water,” thereby differentiating vitriol and copperas but employing the two substances for the same purpose. Whether he meant them as overlapping terms or whether he conceived of copperas as being clearly distinct from other vitriols remains unclear. It seems that when the type of sulfate is pertinent, the type of vitriol is directly mentioned. The author-practitioner does this in the recipe for “For making water for disease of the eyes” that calls for “white copperas” (fol. 79r), which we can now understand as zinc sulfate.

Vitriols were used in many different contexts, one of which is mentioned in a manuscript compiled by Friar Giovanni Andrea in 1562 in Lucca, titled Libro dei Secreti e Ricette (Book of secrets and recipes), also known as the “Gesuatti Manuscript.”28 The manuscript, featuring about 400 pages of remedies, illustrates the method of preparation of oil of vitriol (sulfuric acid) used in several of the remedies, noting that the starting material is vitriolo romano, which can be identified as copper(II) sulfate.29 Equally interesting is the description of oil of vitriol being composed of the two Aristotelian elements, water and fire, since it was a liquid that burned things.30 However, complicating the positive identification of Roman vitriol is a sixteenth- or seventeenth-century Hebrew manuscript that provides a recipe for solidifying quicksilver, in which, it has been suggested, Roman vitriol can be identified as iron(II) sulfate.31

A fourteenth-century manuscript from Bologna contains two recipes that shed more light on the use of vitriols in recipes for aquafortis. The first, for aqua corrosiva (“corrosive water”), notes that aquafortis “reduces the weight of the body brought about from the salt of ammonium (saltpeter) and of copperas in equal proportions [when] distilled in still water.” The second, for aqua solvens (“dissolving water”) to dissolve “silver and indeed other metals,” is made from “vitriollo romano and saltpeter in equal parts, and this water dissolves iron.”32

The two recipes from the Bologna manuscript describe the use of two sulfates, vitriollo romano and coperosa, in two different recipes for aquafortis.33 According to Biringuccio, Roman vitriol “is not well vitrified and clear and is not of the same lovely blue-green color” as Cyprian vitriol and that it is “blackest, most beautiful, and the best,” with the exception of Massa vitriol.34 Nonetheless, Biringuccio indicates that both Roman vitriol and Cyprian vitriol (and Massa vitriol) are comparable and were used by alchemists to make strong corrosive acids.35 The ambiguous nature of the term Roman vitriol in the early modern period stresses the importance of the indication of the color of the vitriol. This might indicate that by omitting the type of vitriol needed in the recipe, the author-practitioner was not assuming tacit knowledge but allowing the reader to choose from the vitriols available since any of them would work.

Roman vitriol is also mentioned in a recipe for aquafortis in Benvenuto Cellini’s Treatises on Goldsmithing and Sculpture published in 1568.36 Cellini instructs his reader to take “8 pounds of burnt rock alum, an equal quantity of the best saltpeter, and 4 pounds of Roman vitriol, and put them together in an alembic.”37 The recipe closely follows the one found in Biringuccio’s Pirotechnia, but adds Roman vitriol. Biringuccio mentions that some prefer to add Roman vitriol to aquafortis, further highlighting the diversity of recipes and the difficulty in defining aquafortis in terms of chemical composition.38

As this overview of recipes shows, aquafortis could be many things and made of many different ingredients. The author practitioner seems to have been aware of this: in a recipe for “Augmenting the Color of Gold” (fol. 166v), he specifically recommends using “aquafortis of vitriol,” suggesting that he was aware of the different varieties of aquafortis, and perhaps even of the employment of different recipes for different uses.39

The entry on fol. 166v resembles another recipe for a similar purpose prescribing aquafortis. Leonardo da Vinci’s Codex Atalanticus (ca. 1480) provides a recipe for “tinging works of gold,” with instructions to put vitriol, verdigris, and saltpeter in a crucible with the object one wants to tinge.40 The process is similar to the entry in Ms. Fr. 640, which says to heat aquafortis of vitriol and to “cover the bottle, that is to say, the mouth, with a tile, then unstop it & thick fumes will come out; put the gold on this and it will take on a high color” (fol. 166v).

William Newman points out that a similar recipe is given in an alchemical manuscript by a fifteenth-century Neapolitan printer named Arnaldus de Bruxella, thus further widening the scope and geographic expanse through which we can begin to understand Ms. Fr. 640.41 The manuscript written by Arnaldus is a compendium of practical recipes for metalwork and glass making, alongside theoretical discussions.42 William Jerome Wilson observes that within the context of Arnaldus’s manuscript there is no general term for liquids, and the word “water” (aqua) stands in for any liquid, while “aquafortis” stands for any liquid that has the particular ability to dissolve and interact with solids.43 Arnaldus’s manuscript (f. 74b–f. 76a) provides a recipe “to make aqua fortis and the virtues of it, (things) which can be performed with it.”44 The recipe for aquafortis in Arnaldus requires “vitriol, sal nitrum (saltpeter), and cinnabar,” to be ground and put together in a distillery, and lists one of its virtues as “coloring silver as gold.”45

Among the virtues of aquafortis in Arnaldus’s manuscript was its use for “the killing of an animal.”46 The author-practitioner also includes a recipe for “killing snakes for molding,” stating, however, that it is not effective:

some put a drop of aquafortis in the mouth, that which stuns them well, such that the head & the part behind seem to be dead, but the mouth remains alive, and when you pierce it with a needle to fit it to the mold, it moves & spoils & undoes everything. Therefore, to put them to death entirely, put it in a bottle of good vinegar & some eau-de-vie. And do thus with lizards & other similar beasts (fol. 109v).

The author-practitioner, in contradicting Arnaldus by suggesting not to use aquafortis for this purpose, provides advice seemingly rooted in experience and relevant to a specific artisanal purpose: molding snakes.

Application of Aquafortis on Metal

The uses of aquafortis thus ranged widely, as can also be seen in Ms. Fr. 640.47 An entry in Arnaldus on fol. 109b to enrich the color of gold calls for a paste made of “vitriol, sal armoniac, verdetto, and alumen gonci, distempered with strong red vinegar.”48 This paste is to be applied to gold, which is then placed on glowing coals; the process should allow a reaction with the fumes produced from the paste, enriching the surface of the alloy, similar to the process and recipe provided by da Vinci.

The Summa perfectionis provides another interesting example of a paste used to enrich or purify gold (or at least its surface). The recipe includes instructions to mix vitriol, sal ammoniac, “and other sharp penetrating substances, and make a paste and spread this upon the thin plates of the metal; lay them on the iron grating in a closed earthen vessel over a fire.”49 The many similarities this recipe has with those mentioned above connect it to the entry in Ms. Fr. 640 for “Color of Damascene Steel on Knives” (fol. 11v) in its mode of application of the sharp paste.

In order to test the author-practitioner’s recipe for aquafortis, I selected the entry for applying the color of damascene steel on knives (fol. 11v). There, the author-practitioner tells his reader to mix aquafortis with earth and rub it on the steel, and then to rub the steel with ashes or sand. He suggests that gold and silver will behave on the resulting surface as they would on a touchstone.

For the purposes of this essay, I will only consider the materials in the recipe and not the color application process or meaning. As we have established, the fundamental property that binds together the category of aquafortis is the substance’s potency to act on lesser metals than gold. The recipe called for a demi-septier of vinegar with one ounce of verdigris, one ounce of copperas, and one ounce of solle, mixed together in a glass vial. Since the entry on fol. 11v describes a knife, I used three plates of bloomery steel.50

Since the author-practitioner did not specify the type of copperas, I used two vitriols in the reconstruction: blue vitriol (copper(II) sulphate) and green vitriol (iron(II) sulfate). I also tested nitric acid (50%) based on previous historical reconstructions of aquafortis recipes.51 For the vinegar, I selected red wine vinegar (~8% acetic acid), a substance that was also known in the early modern era for its ability to interact with metals.52 For salt, I interpreted solle as common salt (NaCl). Dupuy’s notes on terms used in the literary works of Bernard Palissy, a sixteenth-century French artisan, also confirm this translation.53

After crushing thirty grams (one ounce) of verdigris into fine grains, I poured them slowly into a beaker containing 250 ml (demi-septier) of vinegar (Fig. 1) while constantly stirring the liquid.54 The verdigris dissolved into the vinegar, turned the compound slightly more blue, and left some residue undissolved (Fig. 2). Next, I added 30 grams of iron(II) sulfate to the liquid. The liquid remained the same blue color. Once 30 grams of salt were added, upon a couple of stirs, the liquid immediately turned yellow/brown, a color characteristic of iron(II) chloride (Fig. 3). I then put the mixture aside.

For the second reconstruction of the same recipe on fol. 75r, I followed the same formula as the previous one, but I used copper(II) sulfate instead of iron(II) sulfate. There was no apparent reaction between the copper(II) sulfate and the salt, in contrast to the iron(II) sulfate, but the solution did turn more blue due to the color of the copper(II) sulfate (Fig. 4).

A great number of earths and sands are mentioned in the manuscript. Earths in the manuscript include “founder’s earth” (fol. 92r), “tanner’s Earth” (fol. 81r), “potter’s earth” (fol. 41r), “clay earth” (fol. 83r), “fuller’s earth” (fol. 12r), “Cendrée earth” (fol. 70r), and “terres boulvènes” (fol. 69r). The term “earth” thus often refers to clayey substances in Ms. Fr. 640. This is corroborated through other contemporary sources such as Cipriano Piccolpasso’s Three books on the art of pottery (1548), and Cellini’s treatises, which make use of the word terra in Italian (or terre in French) to denote clay.55 It was necessary to select the correct type of earth, based on its reaction with the aquafortis in the recipe.

Much like in the recipe in the Summa perfectionis for testing gold, the earth was likely used as a binder to make the aquafortis into a paste that could then be rubbed onto the steel.56 For my reconstruction, I selected low-fire pottery clay, which I flattened and dried for a week, and then pounded it into fine powder. I put two tablespoons of the clay powder in a bowl and found that seven tablespoons of copper(II) sulfate aquafortis were adequate to saturate the powder with enough liquid to keep it from falling apart.57 I mixed the substances together to make a malleable paste, which I then applied to the surface of the bloomery steel plate and rubbed into the surface with my fingers for 5 to 7 minutes. The steel reacted almost immediately; it noticeably turned red underneath the paste (Fig. 5). Next, I rubbed the paste off the bloomery steel plate using sand—I chose commercial play sand based on its abrasive and absorbent qualities—which helped clean the surface of the steel quickly and efficiently. I then repeated the process with the aquafortis containing iron(II) sulfate, and with 50% nitric acid, the latter of which produced a noticeable amount of heat. These pastes were also applied to modern medium carbon steel (0.29%–0.54% carbon, with 0.60%–1.65% manganese). The nitric acid paste immediately bubbled on applying it to the modern steel (Fig. 6).

All of these solutions produced an immediate and lasting reaction on the surface of the bloomery steel (Fig. 7). The intensity and hue of the surface effects varied, but all of the compositions reacted with the steel. Chemically, this was likely due to a subtraction of material from the original steel plate due to leaching of electrons from the surface into the surface deposit.58 This result corroborates what the historical research discussed above already suggested: that copperas could refer to a variety of sulfates that could be used in the recipe to similar effect.

Conclusion

The comparison of historical recipes reveals that there is no standard recipe for aquafortis and no standard compound to which it refers. The author-practitioner’s recipe for aquafortis does not produce nitric acid, yet it is still a corrosive, thereby reaffirming the multiplicity of meanings of the substance and its ingredients, inflected by tradition, tacit knowledge, and ad-hoc solutions. The reconstruction and historical contextualization of the recipe for aquafortis allow us to better understand the materials invoked in the recipe for aquafortis on fol. 75r, as well as to recognize that aquafortis was a category, rather than a specific compound in the medieval and early modern period.
