Varnish for Lutes

By DOI: https://www.doi.org/10.7916/zd0f-yv91
← Back to Essays

Abstract

Among the several entries devoted to varnish in Ms. Fr. 640, the one on fol. 98r is exclusively about varnish for lutes. Yet what makes for the specificity of this varnish? What is the purpose of its distinctively red color? By reconstructing the recipe “Varnish for lutes,” this essay examines the properties and the meaning of varnish for stringed instruments.

Twenty-first-century stringed instruments, often constructed using modern industrial techniques rather than in an artisan’s workshop, are prized for their brilliance of tone and volume. In the early modern period, however, instruments were quieter. They employed different strings and performance techniques, and they were invariably made in workshops by skilled craftsmen. The recipe for a “varnish for lutes” on fol. 98r in BnF Ms. Fr. 640 is an intriguing inclusion in a manuscript otherwise mostly preoccupied with the arts of mold making, casting, and painting. That the varnish described in the recipe above is for a red varnish makes it even more interesting, as red was not a common color for instruments, and it had, moreover, strong cultural connotations and technical implications.1 From a technical perspective, modern luthiers insist that the composition of a varnish can change the sound of an instrument—the German master Sourène Arakélian, for instance, wrote in 1981 that “the student violin of today, factory-made, with dry hard varnish, can only give out strident and sour sounds […] The function of the varnish is not only to preserve the instrument against humidity and manual perspiration, but also to improve its tone.”2 Though Arakélian claims that the influence of varnish alone has been exaggerated, and that its qualities work in tandem with those of the wood to produce the instrument’s tone, color remains an important aesthetic decision, one that the luthier ultimately makes to “suit his taste.” Intriguingly, a violin left unvarnished is described by Arakélian as “white,” a color that apparently has its own aural qualities.3

Our goal for this essay is to better understand the skills necessary to make or decorate a musical instrument today and also to open a discussion on the nature and experience of color in early modern artisanal practice. While our experiment protocols and use of certain ingredients clarified the former, the latter is still a topic very much up for debate.

Varnishes and the History of Musical Instruments

A European or Near-Eastern lute is a plucked stringed instrument with a deep, rounded back and wide neck. Its origins are classical, and archival sources indicate that it was established in Spain no later than the ninth century, with the first known written music for it appearing around 1500 (Fig. 1).4

It shares features with several other instruments which have survived to the present day (albeit often in highly specialized performance environments) including the oud, the theorbo, the archlute, and the kobza, among others. A fantastic variety of sizes, shapes, and string numbers is obvious in any collection of pre-high classical instruments; it is possible to find lutes of the period of 1600–1800 with anywhere from six to twelve strings in a variety of tunings.5 The lute was also a common subject in contemporary paintings, possessing a range of symbolic meanings, and engravings show it in relation to the instrument maker’s craft. Titian, for example, included a lute player in a painting of Venus dated 1565–1570 (Fig. 2) and Albrecht Dürer engraved a draughtsman at work on constructing a perspectival drawing of a lute (Fig. 3).

Determining the social status of instrument makers and musicians in the sixteenth century is often difficult.6 Little information survives beyond the biographical details of certain master composers, most of whom were artists working in a similar patronage system as painters, attached to courts or nobles (this system would persist for at least 150 years after the probable date of Ms. Fr. 640; J.S. Bach (1685–1750), for instance, spent most of his life in the service of individual or civic patrons. It is, however, possible to find evidence of the look and habits of non-stipendiary musicians for hire in early modern engravings, especially in Germany (Fig. 4, Fig. 5).

There are dozens of mentions of various varnishes in Ms. Fr. 640. The majority of these are varnishes meant for the surface of paintings, such as those listed on fols. 4r-v, 31r, 57r, and 60v. Varnishes can also be applied to objects other than paintings, such as “boxes covered with painted paper” (fol. 67r), engraved iron (fol. 4v), and sword hilts (fol. 96r). Varnishes seem to function both as protection, when they completely cover and finish an object, and as a conscious aesthetic choice on the part of the artisan.7 They are likewise a common material in other contemporary sources, although neither Vannoccio Biringuccio, nor Cennino Cennini, nor Hugh Plat, authors of other technical writings, mention varnishes for decorating musical instruments.8 Likewise, general historical works on the development of musical instruments in the period, although often concerned with the physical form of stringed instruments, including those in the viola da gamba family, the lyra da braccia, the violin, and the crwth, have traditionally ignored the question of how a varnish might affect the production of music, either aesthetically or scientifically.9

More recently, however, there have appeared works of historical reconstruction, akin to those we of the Making and Knowing Project. Scholars have conducted (and published) experiments, for example, on the stripping and examination of “the earliest surviving set of English string instruments” that had been made by a craftsman named William Baker.10 A reconstruction of a lute from scratch using early modern illustrations and instructions likewise has much to say about the preparation and carving of wood, but unfortunately little to say about varnishes, other than that an egg wash might have been used to give a “blond” finish to an instrument.11 Elsewhere, scholar Frank Della Torre has briefly mentioned that “cheap” varnishes were preferable to expensive ones in the making of violins in early eighteenth-century Italy; from this he concludes that certain imported varnishes may have “damp[ened] out the upper harmonies” or that it was the use of a particular type of varnish which might have produced that ever-famous Stradivarius sound.12 Despite these mentions, it is far more common to see varnishes for paintings described in art historical literature. All this research, however, carries a common theme: a varnish’s composition can fundamentally alter the aesthetic of a work of art, and, if one is to extend that to musical instruments, an instrument’s sound as well.13

Reconstructing Varnish for Lutes

Many of the manuscript’s varnish recipes—including those meant for painting canvases or nearly any other object apart from lutes—use similar ingredients (mastic, amber, turpentine, or aspic oil) and have overlapping or complementary techniques, such as heating the base liquid. The recipe on 98r is little more than a list of ingredients: “They take a little turpentine, & oil of turpentine or of spike lavender, & amber pulverized & passed very subtly, & make like that of mastic, & add in a little dragon’s blood to color it and make it reddish, and others some terra merita for yellow.”14 These materials—turpentine oil or spike lavender oil, ground amber, and “dragon’s blood” resin (which could be obtained from a number of different plant genera including Croton, Dracaena, Daemonorops, Calamus rotang or Pterocarpus)—are familiar from other recipes.15 “Terra merita” was more difficult to pin down, but according to modern art suppliers, it appears to be the same as turmeric powder.16 Finally, we had to determine what type of wood we wanted to use to paint out our varnishes. We searched the lutes in the online collection of the Metropolitan Museum of Art and were able to pull up a list of ninety-seven instruments which were both of European origin and built between 1600 and 1800. Even a cursory examination of their details showed us that by far the most common wood out of which they were made was spruce.17 We therefore obtained spruce “patching boards” from a site catering to guitar luthiers.

In establishing our experiment protocol, we found that the recipe titled “Varnish” on fol. 71v was most helpful in laying out a set of steps with which to tackle the recipe on fol. 98r. The former states: “put in 4 ℥ of sandarac & mastic subtly pulverized. And first, boil your oil in a pot on a chafing dish, and then mix in little by little the aforesaid gums, stirring continually with a small stick split and quartered at the tip.” Combining this information with our initial recipe, we determined that we should heat our base liquid and ground amber together. Another varnish recipe on fol. 73v provided us with a more concrete ratio of ingredients to test (“two ounces of spike lavender oil & one ounce of sandarac”), thus, for our recipe, two parts liquid to one part amber. Some recipes mentioned merely that this liquid combination should be “heated,” while others mentioned “boiling” (for example, “chaufer” in the entry “Varnish of spike lavender oil” on fol. 4r vs. “bouillir” in “Black varnish for sword guard, bands for trunks, &c” on fol. 4v). We decided to “heat” our mixture. The original recipe did not mention specific shades of color, so we decided to take our heated mixtures off the heat and stir in however much coloring agent (turmeric powder or powdered dragon’s blood resin) we felt gave the varnishes enough color.18 Given that we had two liquid bases and two coloring agents to try, we ran our experiment four times:

  1. Turpentine oil and turmeric powder → yellow

  2. Spike lavender oil and turmeric powder → yellow

  3. Turpentine oil and dragon’s blood → red

  4. Spike lavender oil and dragon’s blood → red

Our first step was to grind our solid ingredients as finely as we could. The dragon’s blood resin crumbled quickly and easily, and it did not need to be sieved to become very fine (Fig.6). In contrast, grinding the amber pieces was very hard work, and the resin never quite reached a truly fine state despite being sieved several times; there were always larger, coarser pieces visible in the final powdered form (Fig. 7, Fig. 8). Our turmeric powder was a store bought foodstuff. We then heated a hot plate under a fume hood (to help deal with the strong smell of our oils) to what we thought would be a boiling temperature (a setting of 4 out of 5) and started our first experiment with turpentine oil. We combined 3 ounces of turpentine oil with 1.5 oz of ground amber and stirred continuously with a handmade tool of iron wire. Almost immediately, the turpentine oil—which has a boiling point of ~307°F19—began to smoke and boil, and the amber started to solidify into a dark brown sludge in the turpentine. Within moments, the clearly too-high heat had boiled away the turpentine and left us with a crusty mess of foul-smelling amber dust. An acknowledged failure (Fig. 9)!

For our subsequent attempts, we decided to keep our hot plate on its minimum setting and to monitor the temperature of the liquids with an infrared thermometer. On our second attempt with the first mixture, the turpentine rose rapidly to a temperature of about 125°F, at which point we poured in the 1.5 oz of amber and began to stir; its temperature eventually stabilized between 140 and 150°F. Though we stirred the mixture for several minutes, the amber did not seem to be incorporating into the oil,20 and it did not change its sludgy brown-black color (Fig. 10). We therefore decided to strain the mixture before calling it complete. After more than five minutes without changes, we took the varnish off the heat and started to stir in the turmeric, stopping when we felt we had a robust yellow color. We then strained the varnish through cheesecloth to remove most of the amber pieces and stored it in a glass jar (Fig. 11).

The remaining three experiments proceeded almost exactly as above, with the only difference being the variations in color produced by similar amounts of coloring agent (and our two red varnishes were stored in tin cans rather than in glass jars). Each was heated to approximately 150°F and stirred for several minutes, colored, and strained. Our results were as follows:

  1. Turpentine oil and turmeric (5/8 teaspoon): muddy, rich brown-yellow (Fig. 12).

  2. Spike lavender oil and turmeric (1/2 teaspoon): bright, nearly neon yellow (Fig. 13).

  3. Turpentine oil and dragon’s blood (3/4 teaspoon): muddy, dark red (Fig. 14).

  4. Spike lavender oil and dragon’s blood (3/4 teaspoon): brighter red; this was also the most liquid of all four; it was heated for the shortest amount of time and only barely broke 140°F—perhaps this lower heat, not reaching boiling, was the key (Fig. 15)?

On the advice of luthier Gabriela Guadalajara, we looked through the manuscript for clues as to what might be used as a sealant for the instrument’s wood before the varnish was applied to it.21 The manuscript mentions glue dozens of times, again frequently in reference to painting, but has very few actual recipes for it beyond that of fish glue on fol. 159r.22 We therefore looked for synonyms and focused briefly on mastic, a resin obtained from the mastic tree (Pistacia lentiscus) and commonly known as “arabic gum”—not to be confused with “gum arabic” or “acacia gum” (Sengalia senegal or Vachellia seyal).

Due to time and price concerns, we decided to seal our sample piece of spruce with liquid gum arabic instead, which can be used to thicken inks and paints and was already present in our lab.23 On fol. 3v, we found mention of different varnish applications. The author-practitioner explains,

Turpentine varnish does not need any glue because it is fatty & viscous & it is not absorbed in the wood like that of spike lavender & sandarac. Also, that of spike lavender does not require any glue on iron & similar things that do not absorb. But on wood & on colors which have do not have gum or distemper glue, it is necessary to lay one coat of the said hide glue & to let it dry & to varnish.

We decided to test all four varnishes on one piece of sealed spruce, and then test all four on a piece of unsealed spruce. (This would not be modern practice; Guadalajara told us one would never varnish without sealing beforehand.)

We briefly sanded each piece of spruce to take off a slightly rough top layer before we painted one with a single layer of gum arabic and let it dry for half an hour (Fig. 16). After labeling, we then painted each of the four colors in straight lines on both spruce pieces. To our disappointment, although the “red” varnishes had looked quite red to our eyes when they were in the jars, they appeared barely brown when used to paint and were practically invisible on the piece of sealed spruce. The yellow varnishes showed color much more obviously but were still rather weak. By far the strongest color was the spike lavender oil and turmeric mix, which, as we had already noticed while mixing it, appeared as near-neon also on the wood. All four varnishes spread quickly and widely across both pieces of wood despite only being painted in narrow strips. In fact, the varnishes spread even further on the sealed piece than the non-sealed piece; we suspect this may have been because gum arabic is water-soluble, and the sealant may not have been completely dry (Fig. 17).

We painted three coats of each varnish, one on top of the other, to observe how the colors might change or accumulate with repeated coats (each given approximately thirty minutes to dry).24 Only the neon spike lavender oil & turmeric varnish showed strongly on the sealed spruce. On the unsealed spruce, the neon mixture was practically garish, but the turpentine and turmeric, turpentine and dragon’s blood, and spike lavender and dragon’s blood varnishes began to display more pleasing traits. Though not red, the two dragon’s blood varnishes eventually approximated a typical brown one might see on many stringed instruments today. Likewise, the turpentine and turmeric mixture created a far more palatable golden-yellow shade, which would not look too out of place on a lute or an oud. All four varnishes continued to spread rapidly across the wood although we had painted them using very little liquid. The author’s point about turpentine oil taking better to unsealed wood was well demonstrated: on the spruce, these varnishes both displayed better color and spread less than the spike lavender oil varnishes (Fig. 18, Fig. 19).

Questions and Suggestions

Our reconstruction sought to answer two main questions. Our first question was about the workshop context of building, decorating, and perhaps maintaining early modern stringed instruments. The overlap of materials among the multiple varnish recipes in the manuscript suggests that a painter, used to preparing and finishing canvases, would be equal to the task of varnishing an instrument, likely having the necessary resources at hand. The varnishes might also have been made in a luthier’s workshop. The procedure for making the varnishes, assuming we designed our protocols correctly, was simple enough that they could be made quickly and easily, provided the materials were available.

Our second major inquiry was to determine the steps necessary to produce colors suited to the decoration of an instrument. This is a subjective question: what, after all, are the characteristics of a color that make it aesthetically pleasing on the body of an instrument? If the experience of color is a subjective experience—both visually and emotionally—how are we to decide what shades are appropriate for a given situation, or indeed whether we have even approximated the shade intended by the author-practitioner? In the end, we discovered that our liquid varnishes, although they appeared to be strongly colored, immediately weakened in brilliance upon application. These experiments may shed light on the processes involved in instrument making and the making of colored varnish, but whether we can define them as a success or a failure remains open to debate. Further research could include investigating possibilities for sealants; using less ground amber to improve color, or experimenting with grinding more finely to see whether that improves its incorporation into the heated liquid base; using more dragon’s blood to try to strengthen the “red” varnishes; and continuing to investigate the coloring capacities of “terra merita.”
